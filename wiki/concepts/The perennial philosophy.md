## Definition / Characterization from Source

Aldous Huxley's name (borrowing Leibniz's *philosophia perennis*) for the claim that beneath the doctrinal differences of the world's religions lies a **single, universal metaphysics, psychology, and ethic** — rediscovered by mystics and sages in every age and culture. Its core tenets: there is a **divine Ground** of all being; this Ground can be **directly known** (not merely inferred) through a faculty deeper than the discursive intellect; the human self has a double nature, ego and an eternal spark identical with the Ground (**"That art thou"**); and humanity's final end is the **unitive knowledge** of the Ground, attainable only through self-naughting, humility, and love.

Huxley presents it as an empirical, cross-traditional fact — the mystics of Hinduism, Buddhism, Taoism, Christianity, and Sufism describe the same reality in different idioms. It is the mystical/contemplative counterpart to the experiential and post-materialist consciousness material in the wiki, and the framework behind his psychedelic writings ([[The Doors of Perception (Huxley)]]).
