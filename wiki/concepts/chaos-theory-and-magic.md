## Definition from Source

The *Psychonaut* section of [[Liber Null & Psychonaut (Carroll)]] closes with a chapter Carroll
added after the first editions, noting that "chaos theory came to the fore as a scientific
discipline" after initial publication and "represents a further development of the principles of
catastrophe theory."

Carroll summarizes the core finding: "Many systems in the world that seemed either random or
impossible to predict were found to show extreme sensitivity to initial conditions." The classic
illustration is the butterfly effect — a butterfly changing direction may determine whether a
hurricane occurs on the other side of the world months later.

### The Magical Inference

Carroll draws a direct parallel between this scientific finding and the sorcerer's practice:
"Such extreme sensitivity to initial conditions reflects the way the sorcerer strives to effect
large outcomes by well-intuited inputs in advance."

He notes that some chaos theorists speak of *deterministic chaos* — arguing that apparent
randomness is only a function of incomplete knowledge of initial conditions. Carroll rejects this
as an adequate account: "sensitivity to initial conditions goes right down to the quantum domain
where the position and momentum of quanta cannot simultaneously have definite values as well as
outcomes which remain truly random." Chaos theory therefore does not eliminate genuine randomness;
rather, it reveals "the ladder by which the underlying indeterminacy of the quantum realm climbs
easily into the macrocosm."

### Practical Consequence for Magic

"The realization that we inhabit a fundamentally chaotic and random universe leads to profound
consequences for the practice of magic. The future and the past remain mutable. Despite that they
will often want to do otherwise, magicians need to 'enchant long and divine short.'"

This encapsulates a key operational principle: magical intention (enchantment) should be applied
well in advance at sensitive choice points, while divination — which reads a mutable future — is
only reliable in the short term.
