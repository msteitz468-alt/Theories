## Definition from Source

Docetism (from Greek *dokein*, "to seem") is the theological position that Christ's physical body and earthly suffering were apparent rather than real — that the divine Saviour only *seemed* to suffer, or that a substitute body or another being endured the Passion while the true spiritual Christ remained untouched. Two tractates in the Nag Hammadi corpus present explicit docetic Christologies: the Second Treatise of the Great Seth (Codex VII) and the Apocalypse of Peter (Codex VII).

## Description from Source

### The Second Treatise of the Great Seth

The Great Seth presents the Saviour (speaking in the first person as Christ) explicitly denying that he truly died or suffered on the cross. The archons and their instruments could not harm the real Christ because they were blind to his true nature. He states:

> "I did not die in reality but in appearance, lest I be put to shame by them because these are my kinsfolk. I removed the shame from me and I did not become fainthearted in the face of what happened to me at their hands. I was about to succumb to fear, and I \<suffered\> according to their sight and thought, in order that they may never find any word to speak about them." (CG VII.2; trans. Bullard and Gibbons)

The treatise then specifies a substitution at the Passion. It was not Christ who drank the vinegar, or bore the cross, or was crowned with thorns — a series of others stood in his place:

> "It was another, their father, who drank the gall and the vinegar; it was not I. They struck me with the reed; it was another, Simon, who bore the cross on his shoulder. I was another upon Whom they placed the crown of thorns. But I was rejoicing in the height over all the wealth of the archons and the offspring of their error, of their empty glory. And I was laughing at their ignorance." (CG VII.2)

The Saviour laughs from on high at the archons' futile assault on a body that was not truly his. He further explains his descent: "I was altering my shapes, changing from form to form. And therefore, when I was at their gates, I assumed their likeness." The spiritual Christ passed through every gate of the archons in disguise, unharmed and unrecognised.

### The Apocalypse of Peter

The Apocalypse of Peter gives the docetic vision in dramatic visionary form. Peter, watching the arrest and crucifixion, is confused: he sees two simultaneous figures — one being nailed and one standing apart. The Saviour explains:

> "He whom you saw on the tree, glad and laughing, this is the living Jesus. But this one into whose hands and feet they drive the nails is his fleshly part, which is the substitute being put to shame, the one who came into being in his likeness. But look at him and me." (CG VII.3; trans. Brashler and Bullard)

A second explanation follows:

> "He who stands near him is the living Savior, the first in him, whom they seized and released, who stands joyfully looking at those who did him violence, while they are divided among themselves. Therefore he laughs at their lack of perception, knowing that they are born blind. So then the one susceptible to suffering shall come, since the body is the substitute. But what they released was my incorporeal body. But I am the intellectual Spirit filled with radiant light." (CG VII.3)

The crucified figure is described as "the first-born, and the home of demons, and the stony vessel in which they dwell, of Elohim, of the cross, which is under the Law" — that is, a product of the lower archonic creation, not the true divine being.

### Theological logic

The docetic position in these texts is not arbitrary: it follows directly from Gnostic cosmology. If the material world is the defective creation of a lesser, ignorant demiurge (Yaldabaoth and his archons), then the supreme spiritual Saviour — who comes from above the archons — cannot be truly imprisoned in matter, truly suffer under archonic power, or truly die. To claim that he did would be to concede the archons a victory over the transcendent. The "death" is therefore an appearance staged for the archons' benefit, while the real Saviour stands apart, laughing at their blindness.
