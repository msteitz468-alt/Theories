**Definition/Characterization from the Source**
As presented in J. B. Craven's account of Michael Maier's *Themis Aurea* (1618), the Fraternity of the Rosy Cross is governed by six laws attributed to its unnamed Founder: gratis practice of medicine, no fixed habit, an annual meeting on "the Day C," each brother's choice of a successor, use of the letters "R.C." as seal and cognisance, and one hundred years of concealment. The Brethren are portrayed as learned, temperate, secret physicians who possess "the true Astronomy, the true Physics, Mathematicks, Medicine, and Chymistry," dispensing cures gratis out of piety and love of mankind rather than personal gain, and following "only lawful and natural remedies" and the study of "Natural Magic" — described as "the highest, most absolute, and divinest knowledge of Natural Philosophy," provided it does not degenerate into the Diabolical.

**Notes on the Letters "R.C."**
Themis Aurea explains the letters were adopted so the Brethren "may not be without Name"; popular usage read R. as "Roses" and C. as "Cross" ("Rosie Crucians"), but the Brethren themselves declared the letters symbolically denote the name of their first Author rather than a literal rose-and-cross emblem. Maier further offers alternate readings ("R. signifies Pegasus, C. Iulium"), treating the letters as a hieroglyphic cipher in the manner of other orders' emblems (the Rhodians' double cross, Burgundy's golden fleece).
