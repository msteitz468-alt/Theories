# Greek demons (Bane)

## Definition from Source

According to Bane's *Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures*, ancient Greek demonology is the foundational matrix through which much of Western demonic terminology passed. The Greek word *daemon* (daimon) originally referred to a neutral tutelary spirit — a small, nearly insignificant divine influence assigned to each person at birth, charged with guiding and protecting them. The philosopher Plutarch (46–120 A.D.) calculated that demons lived ten times longer than the phoenix; Hesiod (700 B.C.E.) described them as the spirits of departed heroic humans from the Golden Age.

Bane notes that through the spread of Christianity, the neutral *daemon* was converted into a purely evil being: "No longer a neutral force that could be swayed one way or the other, demons, as they were now called, were considered to be beings of pure evil under the influence and control of the Devil himself." The word *daemon* entered Latin as *daemonium* and eventually English as "demon," carrying this Christian valence.

Greek demonology contributed several distinct categories of malevolent being to the Western tradition. The Cacodaemons (kakodaimon, "bad demon") were the evil counterpart of the guardian daemon, described by Bane as fallen angels banished from Heaven who settled in the space between earth and the stars; they attach to specific persons at birth and control their impulses. The Furies (Erinyes) were ancient demonic goddesses of vengeance. Female demons — Lamia, Gello, Mormo, Empousai — preyed on children and men. These Greek figures were heavily absorbed into later Christian and grimoire demonology.

## Notable Demons

**Daemon (Daimon)** — Originally, a tutelary spirit in Greek mythology assigned to each person at birth, bound to protect and guide them throughout life, unable to act directly but able to provide insight and urges. Each person was born with two: the Agathodemons (good) and the Cacodaemon (bad). The word's original neutrality was entirely erased by Christian adoption, which converted all daemons into evil spirits.

**Cacodaemon (Kakodaimon)** — The evil personal spirit counterpart to the benevolent Agathodemons; in later usage applied to all fallen angels banished from Heaven who settled in the space between earth and the stars. Hostile by nature, they take pleasure in revenge and injury, and attached to a specific person at birth, influencing their impulses and acting as messengers between mortals and gods. Described as large, powerfully built humanoids with dark skin, or as a swirling black mass.

**The Furies (Erinyes)** — Three demonic goddesses of vengeance in Greco-Roman mythology: Alecto ("she who does not rest"), Megaera ("the envious one"), and Tisiphone ("avenger of murder"). Originally the ghosts of murdered people, they evolved into demonic personifications of curses, preying on those who broke sacred oaths or committed crimes against family and kin. Described as blood-covered, with eyes weeping blood and living snakes for hair; in older myths with the body of a dog and wings of a bat. Aeschylus said they were born daughters of Nyx; Hesiod said they sprang from the blood of Uranus when he was murdered; Sophocles called them children of Darkness and Gaea.

**Lamia** — A demon from Greek mythology born the daughter of the king of Libya, Belus. Because of her beauty she became Zeus's lover; when his wife Hera discovered the affair, she stole Lamia's children. Driven insane by grief, Lamia became a hideously ugly demon, murdering the children of her kingdom. She could shape-shift back into a beautiful woman to lure men into isolated places, have sex with them, and drain their blood. She was aligned with a group of demons known as the Empousai, the wicked children of the goddess Hecate.

**Empousai (Empusa)** — Daughters of the witch goddess Hecate, red-headed and mulelike in their legs, shod with bronze shoes. Described in Greek classification as "any creature born in another world but with the ability to appear in the human world as a being of flesh." The word empouse in Greek translates as "vampire" and they are considered by some scholars the oldest recorded vampire myth. They used shape-shifting and persuasive abilities to seduce men, drain them of life, and occasionally eat their flesh.

**Gello** — Originating in Graeco-Byzantine and Sumerian mythology; the ancient Greek poet Sappho wrote of her. According to legend, Gello was a maiden from the isle of Lesbos who opposed divine law and died without descendants, causing her to become a demon. At night she would return in maiden form, play with a female child, then consume it. It eventually took three angels — Senoy, Sansenoy, and Semangeloph — to defeat her.

**Gorgons** — Three demonic creatures from Greek mythology: Euryale ("the far-springer"), Medusa ("the queen"), and Stheno ("the mighty"). Born daughters of the sea gods Phorcys and Ceto and originally priestesses of Athena; transformed into monsters by Athena after Medusa's sexual encounter with Poseidon in her temple. Cursed with boar tusks, bronze claws, razor-sharp teeth, pockmarked faces, snakes for hair, and leathery wings. So hideously ugly that a mortal looking directly at them would be turned to stone. Medusa was the only mortal Gorgon; her blood from the right side could revive the dead, blood from the left was an instantly fatal poison. The remaining two Gorgons live in the Underworld or on an island far at sea.

**Alecto** — One of the three Furies; described by Aeschylus as daughters of Nyx, by Hesiod as daughters of Gaea born from the spilled blood of Uranus. Her name translates as "she who does not rest" or "unceasing in anger." Bane notes that she is invoked in Christian demonology and the Ars Goetia as well.

**Agathodemons** — Benevolent counterpart to the Cacodaemon; part of the pair of personal guardian spirits assigned to each person at birth in Greek belief. Represented by the sign of a coiled serpent; considered one of the seven stars in Hermetic-influenced systems. Depicted as a snake with a human head, or occasionally as a young man holding a cornucopia. Offerings of wine were made daily to the Agathodemons.
