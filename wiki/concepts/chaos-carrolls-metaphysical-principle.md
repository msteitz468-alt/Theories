## Definition from Source

Carroll introduces Chaos as the metaphysical first principle underlying all existence. It is
"the 'thing' responsible for the origin and continued action of events." He distinguishes it
carefully from religious conceptions: "It could as well be called God or Tao, but the name
'Chaos' is virtually meaningless, and free from the childish, anthropomorphic ideas of religion."

Chaos performs two functions in Carroll's system:

1. **Origination and causation.** The dualistic mind perceives cause and effect but cannot
   explain how things came into being in the first place or how one thing finally causes another.
   Chaos is the answer: "things have originated and do continue to make one another happen" because
   of Chaos.

2. **Complexification.** "Chaos is also the force which adds increasing complexity to the universe
   by spawning structures which were not inherent in its component parts. It is the force which has
   caused life to evolve itself out of dust and is currently most concentratedly manifest in the
   human life force, or Kia, where it is the source of consciousness."

Chaos cannot be known directly; it operates through [[The Aether (Carroll)]], a realm of
half-formed, probabilistic substance that acts as an intermediary between Chaos and solid reality.
Carroll writes: "Space, time, mass, and energy originate from chaos, have their being in chaos,
and through the agency of the aether are moved by chaos into the multiple forms of existence."

The magician's access to Chaos is through [[Kia (Carroll)]]: to the extent that Kia can become
one with Chaos, "it can extend its will and perception into the universe to accomplish magic."
Similarly, in enchantment, "the magician's will becomes the will of the universe in some
particular aspect" by matching the Chaos that upholds a given event.

A lower anthropomorphic aspect of Chaos, described as the "great mad goddess Chaos," appears in
the example invocations of Liber LUX, invoked "for her ecstasy and inspiration."
