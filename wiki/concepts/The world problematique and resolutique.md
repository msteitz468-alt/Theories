## Definition / Characterization from Source

The Club of Rome's signature conceptual pair, as defined by King and Schneider. The **problematique** (the Club's "trademark") is "the massive and untidy mix of interrelated difficulties and problems that form the predicament in which humanity finds itself" — political, economic, social, cultural, psychological, technological, and environmental problems considered in their *interactions* rather than in isolation. The term dates to the Club's founding and underlay *The Limits to Growth* (1972), which used Jay Forrester's system-dynamics modelling at MIT to project the interacting elements quantitatively.

The **resolutique** is the authors' coined counterpart: "a coherent, comprehensive and simultaneous attack to resolve as many as possible of the diverse elements of the problematique." It is not a "grand attack on the totality" but a simultaneous attack on the main elements, each weighed for its "reciprocal impact" on the others — "to grasp a thousand nettles at once." The authors argue traditional problem-by-problem, country-by-country approaches "can only worsen the situation."
