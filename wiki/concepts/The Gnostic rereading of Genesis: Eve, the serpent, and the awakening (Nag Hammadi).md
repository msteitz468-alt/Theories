## Definition from Source

The Gnostic tractates of [[The Nag Hammadi Library]] systematically **invert** the orthodox reading of Genesis. The archons (not God) create Adam from dirt and cannot animate him; a spiritual Eve awakens the inert Adam; the archons attempt to defile the true, spiritual Eve; the serpent is identified as the vehicle of the "female spiritual principle" — a *liberating* instructor who leads Adam and Eve to the Tree of Knowledge against the prohibition of a jealous, blind creator. The expulsion from the Garden is an act of archonic punishment, not divine justice.

## Description from Source

### The Archons Create But Cannot Raise Adam

The **Hypostasis of the Archons** (Codex II — "The Reality of the Rulers") narrates: the rulers said "Come, let us create a man that will be soil from the earth." They modelled Adam "wholly of the earth … after the image of God that had appeared to them in the waters." But they failed:

> "he breathed into his face; and the man came to have a soul (and remained) upon the ground many days. But they could not make him arise because of their powerlessness."

Adam lay inert until "the spirit came forth from the Adamantine Land; it descended and came to dwell within him, and that man became a living soul." This divine spirit came from **outside** and **above** the archons' creation — it is not their gift.

### The Sleep of Ignorance — Eve as Spiritual Principle

The archons then "caused a deep sleep to fall upon Adam." The text immediately glosses: "Now the deep sleep that they 'caused to fall upon him, and he slept' is **Ignorance**." They extract a rib and replace it with flesh, leaving Adam "endowed only with soul." From his side comes the "spirit-endowed woman," who speaks to him first: "Arise, Adam." Adam's response acknowledges her as his true source of life: "It is you who have given me life; you will be called 'mother of the living'."

### The Archons' Attempt to Defile Eve

When the archons see the spiritual Eve speaking with Adam they are "agitated with great agitation" and lustful: "Come, let us sow our seed in her." The true Eve escapes their assault by a stratagem — "she became a tree, and left before them her shadowy reflection resembling herself; and they defiled it foully." The "carnal woman" (the shadow/image) is violated; the spiritual Eve escapes unharmed.

### The Serpent as Liberating Instructor

The female spiritual principle then enters the serpent as an *instructor*, and the text makes explicit whose side the serpent is on:

> "Then the female spiritual principle came in the snake, the instructor; and it taught them, saying, 'What did he say to you? Was it, "From every tree in the garden shall you eat; yet — from the tree of recognizing good and evil do not eat"?'"

The serpent's argument to the "carnal woman" is:

> "With death you shall not die; for it was **out of jealousy** that he said this to you. Rather your eyes shall open and you shall come to be like gods, recognizing evil and good."

The text confirms the serpent's characterization of the chief ruler: the archon forbids the tree *out of jealousy*, not for humanity's benefit. After imparting the teaching, "the female instructing principle was taken away from the snake, and she left it behind, merely a thing of the earth." The serpent itself is only a vehicle; the divine feminine principle is distinct from it.

### Punishment as Archonic Rage

After Adam and Eve eat and their "imperfection became apparent in their lack of knowledge" of the spiritual element, the "arrogant ruler" does not come as a loving father but in anger. He curses the woman, curses the shadowy reflection of the serpent (not understanding that it was "a form they themselves had modeled"), and expels the couple from the Garden. The narrator is explicit about the rulers' motive:

> "they threw mankind into great distraction and into a life of toil, so that their mankind might be occupied by worldly affairs, and might not have the opportunity of being devoted to the holy spirit."

### Cross-Tractate Confirmation

The **Sophia of Jesus Christ** (Codex III) reinforces this reading. Yaldabaoth's "bond of forgetfulness" was imposed "by the will of Sophia, that the matter might be revealed through it to the whole world in poverty, concerning his arrogance and blindness." The Saviour tells the disciples to "tread upon their graves, humiliate their malicious intent, and break their yoke" — reversing the archons' expulsion.

The **Gospel of Philip** adds a complementary note: "When Eve was still with Adam, death did not exist. When she was separated from him, death came into being. If he enters again and attains his former self, death will be no more" — a statement that reinterprets the Fall as a separation to be *repaired* through spiritual reunion, not a moral transgression requiring punishment.
