## Definition and Characterization from the Source
In Éliphas Lévi's *Transcendental Magic*, the "Baphomet" is the name given to the horned, mitred, hermaphrodite goat-figure depicted on the frontispiece of his "Ritual" and identified with the fifteenth Tarot Key ("The Devil"). Lévi traces this single figure through a chain of historical names — "the Ahriman of the Persians, the Typhon of the Egyptians, the Python of the Greeks, the old serpent of the Hebrews... the Baphomet of the Templars, the bearded idol of the alchemist, the obscene deity of Mendes, the goat of the Sabbath" — and argues that the figure has been worshipped, and is worshipped still, but is misidentified by outsiders as the Christian devil when it in fact represents the pantheistic Universal Agent (the Astral Light), equated by Lévi with the god Pan and even, in one formulation, with "the Christ also of the dissident priesthood." He derives the goat as one of "three symbolical animals of Hermetic Magic" (with the bull and the dog), tied to Judaic sacrificial symbolism of the immolated and emissary (scapegoat) goats of Yom Kippur, and reads every physical attribute of the frontispiece figure as an allegorical cipher: the Pentagram on its forehead signifies light; the torch between its horns, universal equilibrium; its androgyne arms pointing to the white and black moons of Chesed and Geburah, the concord of mercy and justice; its caduceus, eternal life; its scaled belly, water; its feathered legs, volatility; its human breasts, humanity.

In alchemical terms (Chapter XIX, "The Mastery of the Sun"), the same goat's head is identified as the sages' hieroglyph for Sulphur — paired with Hermanubis's dog's head for Mercury — making the Baphomet simultaneously a Sabbath idol, a Tarot trump, and an alchemical operator within Lévi's Great Work.

Lévi additionally offers a disputed etymology, reading "Baphomet" backwards kabalistically as three Latin abbreviations, TEM. OHP. AB. = *Templi omnium hominum pacis abbas*, "the father of the temple of peace of all men," and cites a sculptured coffer disinterred from an old Templar Commandery, bearing a bearded, female-bodied figure holding sun and moon in chained hands, which he treats as an authentic Baphometic artifact (though the antiquarians who examined it judged it "obscene and diabolical").

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's editorial footnotes dispute nearly every element of Lévi's Baphomet thesis: the backwards-Latin etymology has "no reason assigned or assignable," produces "incredibly bad" Latin, and "has no application to Templars"; there is "no such demon in the pictorial emblems of Hermetic Philosophy"; and "no Hermetic Masonry is older than the second half of the eighteenth century," undermining Lévi's claim of a continuous initiatory transmission back to the Templars.
