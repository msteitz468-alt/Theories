## Definition from Source

**Room for error** — which Housel equates with Benjamin Graham's **margin of safety**, and also calls *redundancy* — is "the only effective way to safely navigate a world that is governed by odds, not certainties" (chapter 13 of *[[The Psychology of Money (Timeless Lessons on Wealth, Greed, and Happiness)]]*). It is "the gap between what you think will happen and what can happen while still leaving you capable of fighting another day." Graham's own summary, which Housel quotes: "the purpose of the margin of safety is to render the forecast unnecessary."

## Key Points

- Illustrated by blackjack **card counting**: players bet on odds, never certainty, and keep enough capital ("at least a hundred basic units") to survive bad swings.
- It is *not* mere conservatism: conservatism avoids risk, whereas margin of safety "rais[es] the odds of success at a given level of risk by increasing your chances of survival." Its purpose is **endurance**, which lets you stay in the game long enough for low-probability gains to arrive.
- Practical applications Housel gives: volatility tolerance (can you mentally survive a 30% decline?); retirement-return assumptions (he assumes returns ⅓ lower than historical average); Bill Gates keeping a year's payroll in the bank; Buffett's pledge to run Berkshire with "more than ample cash."
- Coupled with **avoiding ruin**: "Russian roulette should statistically work" syndrome — never take a risk that can wipe you out, no matter how favorable the odds, because leverage turns routine risks into ruin.
- Coupled with **avoiding single points of failure** (German tanks at Stalingrad disabled by field mice; airplane redundant systems). The biggest single point of failure with money is "a sole reliance on a paycheck... with no savings."
