**Definition (from the source)**

The "cattle mutilation phenomenon" (extending to horses, dogs and other animals) is, as presented in *The 37th Parallel*, a decades-long pattern of livestock found dead with a distinctive set of wounds that [[Chuck Zukowski]] and others treat as the calling-card of an unexplained agency rather than ordinary predation.

**Characteristic signature (per the source)**

- **Bloodless / exsanguinated** carcasses — no blood at the wound, on the ground, or pooled in the body (a ~1,200-lb horse should hold ~4 gallons); a Duran-case vet found a "collapsed heart," indicating the blood had been drained.
- **Precise, "surgical," seemingly cauterized incisions** — straight cuts removing tongues "far back in the throat," eyes, internal organs, and (often) the reproductive organs/udder via clean ovals or circles.
- **No tracks, no struggle, no scavengers** — closest animal prints ending several feet away, no footprints/tire tracks, no defensive marks; other animals refusing to approach the bodies.
- **Anomalous EMF readings** — see [[Animal mutilation sites show anomalous electromagnetic-field readings]].

**Scope and history**

The book traces the modern phenomenon to the 1967 horse "[[The 1967 horse 'Snippy' was the first widely publicized animal mutilation|Snippy/Lady]]" in Alamosa, Colorado, and asserts [[More than 10,000 unexplained animal mutilations span the western US over fifty years|more than 10,000 incidents]] across Colorado, New Mexico, Arizona, Utah, Kansas and elsewhere. [[Robert Bigelow (aerospace entrepreneur)|Bigelow]]'s [[National Institute for Discovery Science (NIDS)]] generally held mutilations outside its charter (no witnesses, little evidence) but was drawn to the Utah/Skinwalker cluster.
