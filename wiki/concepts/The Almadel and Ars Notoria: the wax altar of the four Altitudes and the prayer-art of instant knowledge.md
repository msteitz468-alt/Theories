## Overview
Parts IV and V of the Lemegeton present two very different final methods for Solomon's operator to reach beyond ordinary human limits — not by commanding demons, but by petitioning benevolent angelic powers directly.

**Ars Almadel** (Part IV) is a short, physically concrete rite: the operator constructs a wax tablet (the "Almadel") and matching wax candles, consecrated to one of the "4 Altitudes" (Choras) that rule the four corners of the world — East, West, North, South, each further divided into three, so that twelve princes in all govern the year in rotating thirty-day terms. The angels of the Altitudes are not goetic demons; per the text they cannot be prayed to for anything "contrary to the nature of their office, or against god and his laws," and they grant natural increase, wealth, and instruction rather than servitude. The rite closes with a lengthy Invocation addressed to the ruling angel "N" of the Chora currently in season.

**Ars Notoria** (Part V) — called by the text "the oldest book of this art" and said to have been "revealed to Solomon by the holy angel of God called Michael" — is not a summoning rite at all but a devotional discipline: a sequence of orations (in mixed Latin, Hebrew, Greek, and "Chaldee") to be read at fixed lunar and daily hours, accompanied by *notae* (figures/diagrams) contemplated during or after prayer. Successful practice is claimed to instill supernatural memory, eloquence, and mastery of the seven liberal arts and sciences "in one Moneth."

Together the two books represent the Lemegeton's non-goetic pole: instead of binding lesser spirits with threats, the operator here approaches ranked angelic hierarchies (Almadel) or God directly through sanctioned prayer-formulae (Notoria) to receive gifts of knowledge and providence.
