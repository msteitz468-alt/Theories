## Definition / Characterization from Source

Paul Wallis's term for the entities he believes the Hebrew word **elohim** originally denoted: not a singular God but a plurality of "Powerful Ones" (the root meaning of *elohim* being "powers" or "powerful ones"). He reads the plural noun, its occasional plural verbs/attributives, and the first-person plurals of Genesis ("Let us make… in the image and likeness of ourselves," "the human has become like one of us," "let us go down and confuse their language") as literal evidence of a group of physical, creative beings.

Wallis describes them as a fractious **"Council"** (he rejects "heavenly council" as too benign): beings who are "not God and not human," some of them "spirits," each with their own wills and agendas — including "the accuser" (Job 1) and the "deceiving spirit" (1 Kings 22). The Council fashions humans, manages and culls them, fights an internal war, and includes the dissenting member Wallis identifies as the Snake. He suggests the word *elohim* is "a word in transition" — a simple plural in the oldest sources that the redactor "J" later equated with the singular Yahweh, producing the morally incoherent "God" of the received text.
