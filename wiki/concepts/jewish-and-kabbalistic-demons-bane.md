# Jewish and Kabbalistic demons (Bane)

## Definition from Source

Bane characterizes Jewish and Kabbalistic demonology as one of the richest and most systematized in the ancient world, drawing from the Hebrew Bible, the Talmud, the Zohar, the Kabbalistic Qlippoth, the Alphabet of Ben Sira, the Testament of Solomon, the Sefer ha-Zohar, and numerous mystical and rabbinic texts. Demons in this tradition are generally understood as mortal beings — according to the Talmud, demons can die — though key arch-demonesses like Lilith and Agrat-Bat-Mahlat are said to continue until the Messianic age. Many Jewish demons are fallen angels who chose to rebel against God, took human women as wives, and fathered the Nephilim.

Bane identifies several distinctive structural features: the Kabbalistic Tree of Life provides an organizing framework with the Qlippoth (the "shells" or "husks," the demonic mirror of the Sephiroth) each governed by a named arch-demon; there are four named "demons of prostitution" who are simultaneously the four wives of Sammael/Satan (Lilith, Naamah, Agrat-Bat-Mahlat, and Eisheth Zenunim); and the dybbuk tradition represents a specifically Jewish form of demonic possession by restless spirits. Jewish demonology also formed a primary conduit through which Babylonian and Mesopotamian demonic figures (especially Lilith, Asmodeus, and Nergal) entered Christian and Islamic traditions.

## Notable Demons

**Agrat-Bat-Mahlat** — An arch she-demon and succubus from Jewish mysticism; her name translates from Hebrew as "Spirit Daughter of Uncleanness." One of the four demons of prostitution and four wives of Sammael/Satan. She commands 180,000 demons of her own and drives a chariot around the world on Wednesdays and Fridays. According to Talmudic lore, she and Lilith will continue to plague mankind until the Messianic day when God finally extirpates uncleanness from the earth.

**Asmodai (Asmodeus)** — A demon mentioned in Christian demonology, Jewish folklore, Persian mythology, Talmudic text, and Zoroastrian demonology. His name in Avestan translates as "wrath demon." In the Book of Tobit, he falls in love with the human woman Sarah and kills each of her seven husbands on their wedding night. He lusted after Bathsheba; he tricked King Solomon into giving him his ring and assumed the throne of Israel for years. A final tradition names him as the husband of Lilith the Younger, daughter of Sammael and Lilith the Elder.

**Dever** — The demon of plague, mentioned by name in Psalm 91:3 ("the deadly pestilence"). Also called "Pestilence That Walks by Night" and "Terror by Night."

**Dybbuk** — A restless, vampiric evil spirit from Jewish demonology and lore; said by some sources to be one of the children born of Lilith, and by others to be created through an act of sorcery. The dybbuk cleaves to and possesses a living person. The earliest belief held it to be a full demon; later traditions shifted toward calling it the soul of a dead sinner.

**Eisheth Zenunim** — Named in the Zoharic Kabbalah as one of the four wives of Satan; an arch she-demon and succubus, one of the four demons of prostitution, patron to prostitutes and whores.

**Elder Lilith** — In Kabbalistic lore, the original wife of Adam and the "shell of the moon," mother of Lilith the Lesser; her demonic husband is Sammael. A thirteenth-century text, the *Treatise of the Left Emanation*, distinguishes her from her daughter Younger Lilith, noting that Sammael is aroused by both and that on holy days Elder Lilith and Younger Lilith hold screaming contests.

**Lilith** — The oldest and most extensive entry in this tradition. In the Alphabet of Ben Sira, Lilith was Adam's first wife, created from filth and mud; she refused subjugation, left Adam, and aligned with the demon Sammael near the Red Sea, producing the demonic race called the Lilim. In Sumerian lore she is "Kiskil," named in the Epic of Gilgamesh as a "maiden who screeches constantly" with dominion over the moon. In Assyrian lore she is Lilitu, a demoness with wings and a hairy body. In Hebrew texts she appears as the "terror of the night" (Psalm 91) and the "night devil" (Isaiah 34:14). She is the Queen of Hell, Chief of the Succubi, Princess of Demons, and has dominion over the infernal hierarchy Gamaliel. She and Sammael are said by some traditions to have plotted the downfall of Adam and Eve.

**Naamah** — One of the four wives of Satan and one of the four demons of prostitution in Jewish mysticism; associated with Lilith, Agrat-Bat-Mahlat, and Eisheth Zenunim.

**Nephilim** — The hybrid race born from the union of the fallen Watcher Angels and human women; the Hebrew word for giants, with the root meaning "to fall." Their violent nature led to the destruction described in the Book of Enoch, where Sammael incited wars among them knowing they would kill one another. Their creation was one of the primary crimes of the Watchers.

**Samael** — Named in numerous sources as the chief adversary, arch-devil, and husband of both Lilith the Elder and, through a chain of descent, the overall commander of the demonic hierarchy in Kabbalistic thought. He is simultaneously called the Angel of Death, the demon of poison, and the SATAN figure within much of the Talmudic and Kabbalistic literature.

**Shedim** — Guardian demons in Assyrian and Hebrew demonology; in ancient Assyrian and Babylonian lore the schedim could be summoned as either a beneficial or destructive force.

**Younger Lilith (Lilith the Lesser)** — In Jewish demonology and Kabbalistic lore, born the daughter of Lilith and Sammael; the mate of Asmodeus. Described in the *Treatise of the Left Emanation* as beautiful from the waist up and made of fire from the waist down; her intentions are never good, as she seeks to incite war.
