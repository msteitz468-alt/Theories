## Definition from Source

A thought-experiment parable introduced by Sagan in Ch. 10 of *The Demon-Haunted World*. Someone
claims a fire-breathing dragon lives in their garage. Every proposed test to verify or falsify the
claim — flour on the floor to capture footprints, infrared sensors to detect heat, spray-paint to
reveal the body — is countered with a special escape clause: the dragon floats, the fire is heatless,
the paint doesn't stick because she's incorporeal.

Sagan's conclusion: **"What's the difference between an invisible, incorporeal, floating dragon who
spits heatless fire and no dragon at all?"** If no conceivable experiment could count against a claim,
the claim carries no verifiable content. Your inability to disprove it is not the same as proving it
true. Claims immune to disproof are "veridically worthless", however emotionally compelling they may
feel.

## Description from Source

The parable is Sagan's canonical statement of Karl Popper's falsifiability criterion, made accessible
without technical jargon. Key moves in the argument:

1. **Each auxiliary escape clause is itself unfalsifiable**, so the cumulative claim becomes
   permanently insulated from evidence. This mirrors the way UFOlogists in Sagan's view explain any
   absence of physical evidence ("the aliens rendered themselves invisible to bystanders").
2. **Tentative rejection is the rational response.** The open-minded listener should not outright
   declare the claim false — they should place it on hold and await compelling physical evidence that
   resists all alternative explanations.
3. **If physical evidence does appear**, the rational posture changes: footprints in flour that form
   before the observer's eyes, an off-scale infrared reading, paint revealing a shape in mid-air —
   then the claim earns provisional acceptance.
4. **Magic requires the audience's collaboration.** Sagan applies the parable explicitly to alien
   abduction testimony: the lack of any unfakeable physical evidence (no retrieved alien technology,
   no anomalous isotope signatures in alleged implants, no peer-reviewed metallurgical studies of
   landing-site soil) means the invisible dragon has "left no unfakeable footprints."

The parable also encompasses the Barnum-style rhetoric common to channellers, psychics, and
faith-healers, who in Sagan's view build explanatory escape clauses into their systems before
skeptical scrutiny can arrive.
