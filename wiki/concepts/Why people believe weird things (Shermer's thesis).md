## Definition from Source

The umbrella thesis of *Why People Believe Weird Things*: belief in pseudoscience and the paranormal
is not mainly a failure of intelligence but a predictable product of how the human mind works — a
pattern- and meaning-seeking **"Belief Engine"** that finds signal in noise, plus identifiable
**errors of reasoning**. Shermer argues people arrive at weird beliefs for emotional and cognitive
reasons and *then* rationalize them — which is why **smart people can believe weird things** (they are
simply better at defending positions they reached for non-smart reasons).

## Description from Source

The book operationalizes the thesis through tools and case studies (each on its own page): the
**[[How thinking goes wrong — 25 fallacies of pseudoscience (Shermer)|25 ways thinking goes wrong]]**;
case debunkings of **[[Alien abduction and recovered memory as belief, not evidence (Shermer)|alien abduction and recovered-memory accusations]]**;
**[[Creationism's arguments and why they fail (Shermer)|creationism]]** and
**[[Holocaust denial as pseudohistory (Shermer)|Holocaust denial]]** as case studies in pseudoscience
and pseudohistory; and the capstone **[[The Belief Engine — why people believe weird things (Shermer)|Belief Engine]]** plus
**[[Why smart people believe weird things (Shermer)]]**.

As a skeptical source, this and its sub-pages stand alongside
[[Science and skeptical inquiry as a candle in the dark (Sagan)]] as the wiki's explanatory
counterweight to its UFO, occult, and conspiracy material.
