## Definition from Source

Olmsted argues that the explosion of alternative 9/11 conspiracy theories after 2001 was not primarily a sign of mass irrationality but the predictable product of a "culture of deceit" created by decades of real government conspiracies and official lies. The Bush administration's own demonstrable deceptions — manufacturing the Saddam–al Qaeda link, fabricating the WMD case for Iraq — eroded exactly the trust that might have insulated official accounts of 9/11 from alternative interpretations. The result was a vicious cycle: the more the government lied about things Americans could eventually verify, the more plausible it seemed that the government had lied about 9/11 itself.

## Description from Source

**The official conspiracy theory and its weaponization:** The Bush administration initially promoted a relatively limited conspiracy (al Qaeda, bin Laden, the Taliban). Within months, Cheney and others expanded the conspiracy to include Saddam Hussein, citing a "pretty well confirmed" meeting in Prague between hijacker Mohammed Atta and an Iraqi intelligence officer. CIA analysts knew from the start that the identification was wrong; U.S. intelligence "never offered any analysis that supported the notion of an alliance between Saddam and al Qaeda." Nevertheless, by August 2002, 53 percent of Americans blamed Saddam for 9/11; by September 2003, 70 percent believed the administration's conspiracy theory.

**The deceptions unravel:** No WMDs were found. Chief UN inspector Hans Blix found no evidence of a revived nuclear program. David Kay told Congress in January 2004, "We were almost all wrong." Former Ambassador Joseph Wilson's 2003 op-ed exposed the fraudulent Niger uranium claim; the administration retaliated by outing his CIA wife, Valerie Plame. Former counterterrorism czar Richard Clarke testified that the administration ignored al Qaeda and was obsessed with Iraq. The Downing Street memos (leaked May 2005) documented that British intelligence reported to Tony Blair that "the intelligence and facts were being fixed around the policy." A majority of Americans came to believe their president had lied them into war.

**Alternative theories fill the vacuum:** The Jersey Girls (four 9/11 widows from New Jersey) forced an independent commission, then rejected its findings. By 2006, 36 percent of Americans believed the government either helped the terrorists or took no action to stop them; 16 percent believed explosives rather than planes brought down the towers. The "9/11 Truth Movement" divided into LIHOP ("let it happen on purpose") and MIHOP ("made it happen on purpose") camps. The documentary *Loose Change* (2005), produced for $2,000 by a twenty-two-year-old, was watched by more than ten million people. Operation Northwoods — declassified in 1997, the 1962 Joint Chiefs plan to stage attacks on Americans and blame Cuba — became the conspiracists' go-to proof that government-sponsored false-flag terrorism was historically documented.

**Olmsted's structural argument:** The Bush administration followed the same three-part pattern as its predecessors — promoting its own conspiracy theory, running actual covert conspiracies, and suppressing dissent — and produced the same result: intensified popular distrust. The "culture of deceit" is not a pathology of the margins but the predictable response of citizens who have learned, repeatedly and correctly, that their government lies.
