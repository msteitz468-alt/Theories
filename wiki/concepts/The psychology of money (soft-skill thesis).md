## Definition from Source

**The psychology of money** is Morgan Housel's name for the soft skill of *behaving* well with money, which he argues matters far more to financial outcomes than intelligence, education, or technical knowledge. The thesis is stated in the introduction of *[[The Psychology of Money (Timeless Lessons on Wealth, Greed, and Happiness)]]*: "doing well with money has a little to do with how smart you are and a lot to do with how you behave. And behavior is hard to teach, even to really smart people."

## Key Points

- Finance is, in Housel's framing, unique among fields: someone "with no college degree, no training, no background... massively outperform[s] someone with the best education" (Ronald Read vs. Richard Fuscone), which is "impossible to think of" in fields like surgery or engineering.
- Housel attributes this to finance being **"not a hard science"** but a soft skill — "how you behave is more important than what you know."
- The book criticizes the dominant pedagogy: money is taught "too much like physics (with rules and laws) and not enough like psychology (with emotions and nuance)."
- The thesis is the frame that organizes all 20 chapters; each chapter isolates one behavioral feature (luck/risk, "enough," compounding, room for error, etc.).
