# Plant correspondences (Kynes)

Part of the [[Magical correspondences (Kynes)]] system compiled by [[Sandra Kynes]].

See also sibling pages: [[Mineral correspondences (Kynes)]] · [[Animal correspondences (Kynes)]] · [[Magical intentions and powers (Kynes)]] · [[Deity and spirit correspondences (Kynes)]] · [[Astrological and time correspondences (Kynes)]] · [[Elemental, color, and number correspondences (Kynes)]]

## Definition from Source

Chapter Two of [[Llewellyn's Complete Book of Correspondences (Kynes)]] covers the Plant Kingdom, organized into two major sections: **Trees** and **Herbs, Garden Plants, and Shrubs** (which includes flowers). Kynes treats plant correspondences as inherited from Pagan and Wiccan folk tradition — each plant accumulates associations across many axes: zodiac signs, planetary rulers, elements, directions, energy polarity (yin/yang), chakras, runes, ogham letters, sabbat celebrations, deities, magical beings, and a master list of "Issues, Intentions & Powers."

Kynes notes that different parts of a plant carry their own symbolism: **leaves** share the whole plant's energy and represent growth; **seeds and nuts** embody beginnings and duality (life/death, light/dark); **fruit** symbolizes fruition and abundance; **roots** provide grounding and stability; **bark, nuts, and thorns** convey protection and purification. Botanical names (with synonyms where nomenclature has shifted) are included throughout to reduce confusion from overlapping folk names. Kynes explicitly invites practitioners to explore additional species beyond those listed, following personal magical intuition rather than exhaustive prescription.

The chapter reflects the same logic underlying [[The Principle of Correspondence (Kybalion)]] — that natural objects encode relationships across planes — applied to plant material in an operational, practice-oriented key.

## Organizational Notes

Kynes provides botanical synonyms for every entry and notes where multiple species share a folk name. Entries vary considerably in depth — well-known ritual plants (oak, ash, rose, rosemary) receive extensive multi-axis tables, while rarer entries may list only one or two attributes. Seasonal sabbat associations (Samhain, Beltane, Ostara, etc.) are cross-indexed alongside zodiacal and planetary data, making the chapter useful both for calendar-based ritual planning and general magical work.
