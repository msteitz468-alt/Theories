## Definition from Source

The culminating concept of *The Forbidden Universe*: the universe is not a blind mechanism but the thought or mind of God — as stated in the Corpus Hermeticum, Treatise XI — and that modern science, culminating in John Archibald Wheeler's "participatory universe" and the quantum observer effect, has independently arrived at this same conclusion, vindicating the Hermetic worldview.

Picknett & Prince trace the concept across multiple scientific disciplines:

- **Cosmology:** cosmic fine-tuning implies a designer; the multiverse is the only scientific alternative, but it is untestable and arguably more metaphysical than design.
- **Biology:** life as a "cosmic imperative" (Christian de Duve, Louis Allamandola), the directionality of evolution (Simon Conway Morris), and the inexplicable emergence of DNA, eukaryotic cells, sex, and ageing all suggest "some ongoing creative factor, something somehow capable of comprehending the bigger picture."
- **Quantum physics:** the observer effect (Feynman's double-slit experiment), Wheeler's delayed choice experiment, and the Global Consciousness Project (Dean Radin, Roger Nelson) show that consciousness has a measurable effect on matter at the quantum level.
- **Wheeler's participatory anthropic principle:** observers are not just products of the universe — they bring it into being retroactively. "Observership is the mechanism of genesis." The universe needs conscious beings to exist.

The authors argue this is precisely the Hermetic picture: "God is the universe, and vice versa. Intelligent beings are part of God, and also, as their minds help shape the universe, they enjoy a special role in creation." They cite Corpus Hermeticum Treatise XI directly: the cosmos is held in God's mind "like thoughts within himself." They also note that Austrian astrophysicist Erich Jantsch reached the same conclusion: "God is not the creator, but the mind of the universe" — and traced this idea explicitly to Hermetic philosophy.

The concept represents the book's concluding synthesis: that the Hermetic vision of a living, minded cosmos, expelled from science in the seventeenth century, is being empirically rediscovered.
