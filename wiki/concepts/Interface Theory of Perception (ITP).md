## Definition from Source

The **Interface Theory of Perception (ITP)** is Donald Hoffman's claim that each species' perceptions constitute a *user interface* — like the desktop of a computer — shaped by natural selection to hide objective reality and guide adaptive behavior, not to reveal the truth. Hoffman names it in chapter 5 of *[[The Case Against Reality (Why Evolution Hid the Truth from Our Eyes)]]*, calling the name "a bit rich for a mere metaphor" but one he tries to pay off.

The governing analogy: a blue, rectangular icon for an email file on a desktop. The file is not blue, not rectangular, and its bits are not located where the icon sits. The icon's purpose is not to depict the file's truth (circuits, voltages, software) but to *hide* it and let you act usefully. "Ignorance of reality can aid command of reality."

In ITP:
- **Spacetime** is the desktop — a species-specific data format.
- **Physical objects** (spoons, stars, neurons) are *icons* on that desktop — data structures we create on the fly to describe fitness payoffs and how to get them, and destroy when we look away.
- The language of space, time, shape, hue, texture, taste, etc. is the right language to describe fitness payoffs but the *wrong* language to describe objective reality.

## Key Points

- ITP follows from, but is logically distinct from, the [[Fitness-Beats-Truth (FBT) Theorem]]: the theorem proves selection favors fitness over truth; ITP is the positive metaphor that makes the result intuitive.
- ITP redefines **illusion**: not a departure from truth (since no perception is veridical) but "a perception that fails to guide adaptive behavior" (e.g. a Necker cube, whose seen shape cannot be grasped).
- ITP anticipated by the biologist **Jakob von Uexküll** (each species has its own *umwelt*), though von Uexküll rejected natural selection as its shaper.
- ITP is independent of [[Conscious Realism]]: one could hold ITP while denying that the reality behind the interface is conscious.
- Hoffman's 2015 paper "The Interface Theory of Perception" (with Manish Singh and Chetan Prakash, *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*) is the technical statement.
