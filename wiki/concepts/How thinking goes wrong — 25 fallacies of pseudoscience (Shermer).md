## Definition from Source

Shermer's taxonomy of twenty-five cognitive and rhetorical errors that lead otherwise normal people
to believe weird things — organized in four categories: Problems in Scientific Thinking (theory-laden
observation, observer effects, equipment artefacts), Problems in Pseudoscientific Thinking (anecdotes,
jargon, bold claims, heresy-as-proof, burden of proof, rumor, unexplained vs. inexplicable, failure
rationalization, post hoc reasoning, coincidence, representativeness), Logical Problems in Thinking
(emotive language, ad ignorantiam, ad hominem, hasty generalization, authority worship, either-or,
circular reasoning, slippery slope), and Psychological Problems in Thinking (effort inadequacies,
problem-solving biases, ideological immunity / the Planck Problem).

Shermer frames the list with **Hume's Maxim** at the opening — "A wise man proportions his belief
to the evidence" / "no testimony is sufficient to establish a miracle unless its falsehood would be
more miraculous than the fact" — and closes it with **Spinoza's Dictum**: "I have made a ceaseless
effort not to ridicule, not to bewail, not to scorn human actions, but to understand them."

## Description from Source

*Why People Believe Weird Things*, Chapter 3 ("How Thinking Goes Wrong"), presents all twenty-five
fallacies as a companion reference for the case-study chapters that follow:

### Category A — Problems in Scientific Thinking
1. **Theory influences observations** — The perceiver's framework shapes what is seen; science is
   "theory-laden." Example: Columbus saw "Asia" in the New World because his theory demanded it.
2. **The observer changes the observed** — Studying a phenomenon alters it; lack of double-blind
   controls is endemic in pseudoscience.
3. **Equipment constructs results** — The tools of measurement define the thing measured (Eddington's
   net: "what my net can't catch isn't fish").

### Category B — Problems in Pseudoscientific Thinking
4. **Anecdotes do not make a science** — Ten anecdotes are no better than one without corroborating
   physical evidence or controlled experiments.
5. **Scientific language does not make a science** — Jargon and technobabble convey no information
   without operational definitions and experimental tests.
6. **Bold statements do not make claims true** — Extraordinary claims ("comparable to Copernicus!")
   require extraordinarily well-tested evidence. Cold fusion is the canonical cautionary case.
7. **Heresy does not equal correctness** — Being laughed at or opposed does not validate a claim.
   The Schopenhauer three-stages-of-truth quote is a rationalization, not evidence.
8. **Burden of proof** — The person making an extraordinary claim bears the burden; evolutionists do
   not owe creationists a defense of evolution.
9. **Rumors do not equal reality** — "I read somewhere…" becomes "I know…"; the hundredth-monkey
   story is the paradigm case.
10. **Unexplained is not inexplicable** — Inability to explain something does not make it paranormal;
    firewalk physics is a canonical solved case.
11. **Failures are rationalized** — Pseudoscientists ignore or explain away disconfirmation; genuine
    scientists publish negative findings.
12. **After-the-fact reasoning (post hoc ergo propter hoc)** — Sequence is not causation; breast-
    feeding/IQ correlation confounded by maternal attention.
13. **Coincidence** — People have a poor intuitive grasp of probability; the phone-call "synchronicity"
    example shows selective memory of hits and neglect of misses.
14. **Representativeness** — Psychics publish hits, ignore misses; the Bermuda Triangle has a lower
    accident rate than surrounding waters once traffic density is controlled.

### Category C — Logical Problems in Thinking
15. **Emotive words and false analogies** — Rhetoric via emotional language or misleading metaphor;
    Al Gore's son/America analogy is the illustration.
16. **Ad ignorantiam** — "You cannot disprove ESP, therefore ESP exists" is no more valid than "You
    cannot disprove Santa Claus, therefore Santa exists."
17. **Ad hominem and tu quoque** — Attacking the person (neo-Nazi, atheist) does not refute the
    argument; the argument must be addressed directly.
18. **Hasty generalization** — Drawing sweeping conclusions from insufficient samples; the everyday
    mechanism of prejudice.
19. **Overreliance on authorities** — High IQ and credentials do not confer reliability outside
    one's domain; Mensa members believe in the paranormal.
20. **Either-or (false dilemma)** — Creationists spend most effort discrediting evolution rather
    than establishing positive evidence for creationism.
21. **Circular reasoning (tautology)** — "God is because the Bible says so and the Bible is true
    because God inspired it."
22. **Reductio ad absurdum / slippery slope** — Pushing an argument to extremes does not refute its
    premises; Ben & Jerry's ice cream does not cause death.

### Category D — Psychological Problems in Thinking
23. **Effort inadequacies / need for certainty, control, and simplicity** — Critical thinking does
    not come naturally; it requires training. Paranormal beliefs flourish under social uncertainty
    (post-communist Russia example).
24. **Problem-solving inadequacies** — Psychologist Barry Singer's research: people immediately form
    a hypothesis and seek only confirmatory evidence, resist changing it even when wrong, and if
    given random feedback still invent causal patterns.
25. **Ideological immunity (the Planck Problem)** — Jay Stuart Snelson's "ideological immune
    system": the more knowledge and the stronger the prior beliefs, the harder it is to change them.
    Max Planck: paradigms change when opponents die, not when they are converted. Psychologist David
    Perkins found IQ positively correlates with defending a position but negatively with considering
    alternatives.
