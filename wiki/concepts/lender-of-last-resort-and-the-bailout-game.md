## Definition from Source

Griffin describes the Federal Reserve's role as "lender of last resort" — created so that, when banks face currency drains, bank runs, or bad loans, the Fed can create new money to rescue them. He frames the recurring rescues of large debtors and banks as a "game called Bailout" with fixed rules (Chs. 2–5): endangered loans are rolled over and enlarged; failing that, the federal government is convinced to guarantee the loan ("not to do so would result in great damage to the economy"); the burden then transfers from the bank's ledger to the taxpayer; and when funds run out, "the balance is provided by the Federal Reserve System in the form of freshly created new money," passing the final cost to the public as inflation.

## Characterization in the Source

Griffin's domestic examples (Ch. 3): Penn Central (1970, $125M loan guarantees), Lockheed (1970, $250M), New York City (1975, $2.3B), Chrysler (1978, $1.5B), Commonwealth Bank of Detroit (1972), First Pennsylvania Bank (1979), and Continental Illinois (1982/84, FDIC took 80% ownership — "the bank was nationalized, but no one called it that").

On the **FDIC**, Griffin argues it "is not insurance, because the presence of 'moral hazard' makes the thing it supposedly protects against more likely to happen," and that its funds ultimately come from depositors and from Fed-created money. The international version of the game runs "from the Federal Reserve through the IMF/World Bank" (Ch. 5).
