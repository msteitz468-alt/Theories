## Definition from Source

The Gospel of Thomas is a collection of 114 sayings attributed to "the living Jesus" and recorded by Didymos Judas Thomas. It opens: "These are the hidden words that the living Jesus spoke. And Didymos Judas Thomas wrote them down." Unlike the canonical gospels, it contains no narrative of miracles, crucifixion, or resurrection — only logia (sayings, parables, and short dialogues). The text is preserved in Codex II of the Nag Hammadi library and translated by Stephen J. Patterson and James M. Robinson from a Coptic version of a lost Greek original believed to date to the 1st–3rd centuries AD.

## Description from Source

The Gospel of Thomas opens with a declaration that frames the entire collection as a path to escaping death through understanding: "Whoever finds the meaning of these words will not taste death" (Logion 1). Salvation here is not ritual or creedal but hermeneutic — it consists in grasping the hidden sense of what the living Jesus says.

**The nature of the text.** The 114 logia are introduced by formulae such as "Jesus says:" or "He said:" They include parables (the sower, the merchant and the pearl, the lost sheep, the great dinner), aphorisms, dialogues with disciples, and enigmatic riddles. Many have parallels in the canonical synoptic gospels but with different emphases or endings. The collection is explicitly called "hidden" (secret) words — implying that their meaning is not on the surface and must be sought.

**The kingdom as an interior and present reality.** The Gospel of Thomas consistently locates the kingdom not in a future apocalyptic event but in the present, in and around the seeker. Logion 3 is programmatic: "If those who lead you say to you: 'Look, the kingdom is in the sky!' then the birds of the sky will precede you. If they say to you: 'It is in the sea,' then the fishes will precede you. Rather, the kingdom is inside of you, and outside of you." The same logion continues: "When you come to know yourselves, then you will be known, and you will realize that you are the children of the living Father." A parallel formulation appears in the two-column earlier version within the same codex, where Jesus says the kingdom "is spread out upon the earth, and men do not see it" (Logion 113).

**Self-knowledge as the path.** The Gospel of Thomas repeatedly ties salvation to self-knowledge. Logion 3 connects knowing oneself to recognizing one's identity as "children of the living Father." Logion 67 states: "Whoever knows the All but fails (to know) himself lacks everything." Logion 50 instructs disciples, when asked where they come from, to answer: "We have come from the light, the place where the light has come into being by itself… We are his children, and we are the elect of the living Father." Self-knowledge is thus simultaneously knowledge of divine origin.

**Seeking, finding, and being astonished.** Logion 2 sets out a four-stage path: "The one who seeks should not cease seeking until he finds. And when he finds, he will be dismayed. And when he is dismayed, he will be astonished. And he will be king over the All." The process moves from active seeking through crisis and awe to sovereignty — an inward transformation rather than an external event.

**Hidden light within persons.** Logion 24 states: "Light exists inside a person of light, and he shines on the whole world. If he does not shine, there is darkness." Logion 77 (in the columnar earlier version) gives a pantheistic-sounding formulation: "It is I who am the light which is above them all. It is I who am the all. From me did the all come forth, and unto me did the all extend. Split a piece of wood, and I am there. Lift up the stone, and you will find me there."

**The bridal chamber and the solitary ones.** The text hints at a mystical union: Logion 75 states "Many are standing at the door, but it is the solitary who will enter the bridal chamber." Logion 22 describes entering the kingdom by making "the two into one" — dissolving the distinction of inside and outside, above and below, male and female. This language anticipates the sacramental theology developed more fully in the Gospel of Philip (also Nag Hammadi).

**The world as corpse.** Logion 56 states: "Whoever has come to know the world has found a corpse. And whoever has found this corpse, of him the world is not worthy." Logion 27 counsels: "If you do not abstain from the world, you will not find the kingdom." The created world is treated as a domain of poverty and death for those who remain in it without self-knowledge.

**No fasting, prayer, or almsgiving as such.** Logion 14 reverses conventional piety: "If you fast, you will bring forth sin for yourselves. And if you pray, you will be condemned. And if you give alms, you will do harm to your spirits." What defiles is not what enters the mouth but what exits it (Logion 14:5). External ritual observance is relativized in favor of inner transformation.
