# Islamic demons and jinn (Bane)

## Definition from Source

Bane characterizes Islamic demonology as centered on the **djinn** (jinn) — a race of beings created by Allah out of smokeless fire and granted free will, which distinguishes them from many other demonic traditions where evil is inherent. Because they have free will, individual djinn can choose to be good or evil; evil djinn can even be redeemed by converting to Islam. Their homeland is called Jinnistan, with its capital the City of Jewels; outside their homeland they inhabit abandoned buildings, caves, graveyards, and dark places. When a djinn is mortally wounded it bleeds fire; the flames eventually consume the demon.

Bane notes two main classification systems within Islamic sources: one identifies five classes (Afreet, Ghilan, Jann, Marid, and Sheitan); the other recognizes three (Ghul, Ifrit, and Sila). Djinn are immortal unless slain, can shape-shift into virtually any form, can become invisible, cause insanity, foretell the future, and possess people. The seventy-two kings or "Suleymans" (Solomons) of the djinn governed the species before Iblis rose to supremacy. King Solomon is said in both Islamic and Jewish lore to have gained control over the djinn through magical spells given by an archangel.

The Koran mentions two genera of djinn, including the Ghilan (children of Iblis) and the Shaitans (fallen angels). The djinn appear in pre-Islamic Arab tradition as well — including the ghul, the qutrub, and the sila — and these were absorbed into the formal Islamic demonological framework.

## Notable Demons

**Ad Dajjal** — A demon from Islamic eschatology, the great Impostor and deceiver of the end times; believed to appear at Yawm al-Qiyamah (the Day of Resurrection), traveling between Syria and Iraq. He is described as having a ruddy complexion, thick hair, and being blind in his right eye. True Muslims alone will be able to see the word "infidel" written between his eyes.

**Dalhan** — An Islamic demon described as a man riding upon an ostrich; devours travelers and those washed ashore from shipwrecks, attacks ships near shore, and can unleash a cry that causes disorientation in all who hear it. He lives on various named and unnamed islands.

**Dasim** — A djinn from Islamic mythology, the demon of hatred between husband and wife; born one of the five Sons of Iblis.

**Eblis (Iblis)** — The supreme figure of evil in Islamic mythology; chief of all the djinn and a fallen angel. Two stories are given: in the first, Iblis was an angel near God's throne who refused to bow to Adam and was cast to Hell, transformed into a Shaitan condemned to haunt ruins. In the second, he was a djinn who was captured, re-educated into angelic status, became Treasurer of Heavenly Paradise, then broke free to lead his fellow djinn in rebellion. Created by God out of fire, Iblis is the father of five evil djinn sons, can lay eggs that hatch into demons, self-impregnate, shape-shift, and knows the three sacred words that grant immortality. He is powerless against Allah and his faithful followers.

**Ghaddar** — A demon from Islamic mythology, born a child of Iblis and a wife God created for him from the fire of the Samum; hideous in appearance, this demon preys on travelers by capturing them, torturing them with mutilation, and ultimately devouring their genitals. Lives in the desert near the Red Sea.

**Ghilan** — A class of djinn born the children of Iblis; expert shape-shifters who always retain hooves regardless of form. Fourth strongest class of djinn. They are the genus referenced in the Koran.

**Ghul** — From Muslim folklore and the Koran; a female vampiric demon that eats only the flesh of the dead, breaking into graves to feed on properly buried corpses. When no grave is available, she shape-shifts into a beautiful woman to lure male travelers into isolation and then kills them. Hooves always betray her true nature.

**Ifrit** — A species of djinn from Arabic mythology; subterranean spirits that appear as enormous, winged beings made of smoke. Cunning, immortal, and strong, with fire instead of blood in their veins. When mortally wounded, an ifrit combusts into flames. They live underground and in ruins, organized into a structured tribal society under a tribal leader. They fear lightning bolts. The female is called an ifritah.

**Jan-Ibn-Jan** — The last of the seventy-two Suleymans (kings) of the djinn; the ruler of Jinnistan. From Arabic mythology, his name means "Jan son of Jan."

**Marid** — The most powerful species of djinn in Islamic mythology and the favored troops of Iblis, though the least numerous. They have free will (unusual for a demon species), are arrogant and proud, physically powerful and knowledgeable, and very evil. They can grant wishes to mortals but only through battle, imprisonment, rituals, or copious flattery.

**Qutrub** — From pre-Islamic demonology; male demonic ghouls or djinn born from eggs as the children of Iblis and a wife created for him by God out of fire. They are dispatched by God to punish sinners but also attack without provocation; they hide near alleys and dark places.

**Shaitans (Sheitan)** — Created by the fire of Allah; a type of djinn who appear in their natural form as wisps of smoke, very ugly, with the feet of a rooster. Ruled by Iblis, the shaitans' purpose is to tempt mankind into sin through unclean thoughts and desires. Some shaitans have converted to Islam and become faithful servants of Allah. According to the Koran, the shaitans are fallen angels; the Hebrew word shaitan means "Satan" or "enemy."
