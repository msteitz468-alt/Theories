## Definition from Source

In the *Psychonaut* section of [[Liber Null & Psychonaut (Carroll)]], Carroll applies René Thom's
mathematical catastrophe theory to magical and mystical state changes. He describes catastrophe
theory as "a mathematical tool which allows sudden discontinuous changes to be represented by a
topological model" that "provides a qualitative but not a quantitative description of how a sudden
change is likely to occur." Carroll notes that most mathematicians still consider the theory "black
magic," which he finds fitting.

### The Cusp Catastrophe Surface

Carroll uses the cusp catastrophe model (a folded surface in three dimensions) to represent
situations — initiation, illumination, religious conversion, belief change — where a small push on
one control factor produces a sudden, discontinuous leap in the behavior or worldview of the
subject. The surface has a fold; when a point on the surface reaches the fold's edge, it "falls
over" catastrophically to a different region rather than changing smoothly.

### Four Applications

**1. Occult vs. Materialist Worldviews**
Control factors: Y = number of magical experiences recalled; X = degree of critical rigour applied.
- A = tough-minded occultist; B = tough-minded materialist; C = sloppy-minded occultist;
  D = sloppy-minded materialist.
- The key catastrophic transition is C → A: a sloppy occultist exposed to enough genuine magical
  experiences undergoes a sudden revision to a disciplined occult worldview. Carroll considers the
  gradual D → A path (increasing both objectivity and experience together) the most reliable route.

**2. Change of Belief**
Control factors: Y = emotional commitment; X = rationality/irrationality.
- A and C represent irrationally held beliefs of opposite types; B and D are rationally held
  versions.
- The A ↔ C catastrophe corresponds to religious conversion: an irrational change of emotional
  commitment tips the subject from one strong belief to its opposite.
- Carroll notes "bifurcation": descent into irrationality produces two possible strong beliefs
  that are highly unstable with respect to emotional commitment — "a technique that has its uses
  in magic."

**3. Mystical Initiation**
Control factors: Y = mystical knowledge; X = power / expertise with gnosis.
- Power acquired before knowledge (D → C path) "often produces heresy or madness" and actually
  erects a catastrophe barrier to further progress.
- The D → A path (balanced increase in both) is what better mystical systems follow.

**4. Magical Initiation**
Control factors are reversed: Y = power; X = knowledge.
- For magic, knowledge without power produces only a dilettante; power without knowledge is
  dangerous.
- Carroll observes that "mystic masters denigrate power for its own sake, and magical masters
  denigrate knowledge for its own sake" because each single-axis path removes the candidate
  further from the goal and requires a catastrophic correction to proceed.

The chapter closes with a combined "double catastrophe surface" integrating the mystical and
magical axes, noted to show "how perilously thin the path D → A becomes at one point."
