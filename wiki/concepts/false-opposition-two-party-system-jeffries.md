## Definition from Source

Per *Hidden History*, "false opposition" (or "controlled opposition" / "loyal opposition") is the
pattern in which ostensibly anti-establishment leaders, organizations, and media outlets are
secretly funded, staffed, or controlled by the very establishment they claim to oppose. Jeffries
argues this mechanism ensures that dissent is channelled, co-opted, and ultimately neutered,
leaving real power structures untouched regardless of which nominal "side" prevails.

## Description from Source

Jeffries traces the pattern across both the left and the right of the political spectrum and across
multiple decades:

**Sixties counterculture and radical left.** Researcher Sherman Skolnick, per Jeffries, alleged
that prominent counterculture leaders — Jerry Rubin, Abbie Hoffman, Tom Hayden, Rennie Davis —
were funded by establishment think tanks (Carnegie Foundation, Institute for Policy Studies) and
government agencies (Office of Economic Opportunity). Hayden, Skolnick reportedly claimed, carried
one of the highest security clearances in the country. Radical attorney William Kunstler, according
to Jeffries, formerly served as an officer of the OSS (CIA precursor). Gloria Steinem, per the
book, was placed in charge of an activist group by CIA officer Cord Meyer in 1958 and later
acknowledged the CIA's role in financing her network, publicly stating, "The CIA's big mistake was
not supplanting itself with private funds fast enough." Steinem's feminist magazine *Ms.* also
received substantial CIA funding. Timothy Leary admitted to author Walter Bowart that he had
"proceeded as an intelligence asset since 1962" and answered "yes" to the direct question "Did you
ever willingly work for the CIA?" Aldous Huxley, who laid cultural groundwork for the Sixties drug
culture, was linked by Jeffries to CIA mind-control networks and MK-Ultra-adjacent research funded
through the Ford Foundation and the Rand Corporation.

**Foundation-funded "left" media.** Jeffries alleges that the four major "progressive" news
outlets — *The Nation*, *The Progressive*, *In These Times*, and *Mother Jones* — received millions
in foundation grants from sources with establishment pedigrees; *The Nation*'s parent received
more than $2 million in 2010 and former LBJ aide Bill Moyers's Schumann Center (itself backed by
an IBM founder's daughter and a General Motors Acceptance Corporation president) funded several
of them. The far-left Pacifica Broadcasting, Jeffries notes, is funded by the Rockefeller
Foundation. "Leftist" billionaire George Soros, per the book, invested at least $100 million in
the arms-dealing Carlyle Group, owned substantial stock in Boeing and Lockheed-Martin, and was a
former director of the [[Council on Foreign Relations]].

**Establishment "right."** Richard Nixon, per Jeffries, issued Executive Order 11490 permitting
mass state control (takeover of media, industry, and transport; civilian work brigades; confiscation
of private property) in a "national emergency" — hardly the "small government" conservatism he
advertised. Nixon's article in the CFR's *Foreign Affairs* in 1967 called for "a new world order."
The "people's lobby" Common Cause, founded in the Seventies, was financed by the Rockefellers, the
Ford Motor Co., and the *New York Times* while publicly claiming to fight "special interests."
*Mother Jones* and similar outlets, per the book, faithfully toed the establishment party line on
genuinely important issues (JFK assassination, 9/11 Commission, 2000–04 vote fraud).

**Agent provocateurs and FBI/COINTELPRO infiltration.** Jeffries details how "extremist" groups
at both political poles were routinely infiltrated; in multiple cited cases the undercover agent
or informant actually planned or financed the alleged radical acts (1965 Black Liberation Front
bomb plot — the fourth member was a cop who drew the plans and purchased the dynamite; Ku Klux
Klan murder of civil rights worker Viola Liuzzo — one of the riders was FBI informant Gary Rowe;
the KKK's Imperial Wizard in Texas in the 1990s was simultaneously an FBI informant).

**Eugene McCarthy's campaign and CIA personnel.** Jeffries notes that CIA official Richard Helms
was an "occasional lunch partner" of McCarthy, that former CIA officials Thomas Finney and Thomas
McCoy resigned from the Agency to become top aides to the "peace candidate," and that James Woolsey
— later CIA director under Clinton — chaired Yale Citizens for Eugene McCarthy in 1967–68.

The overall thrust is a thesis Jeffries attributes to journalist Bob Feldman: "Are the interests of
the people being served by 'dissidents' who are being subsidized by the agencies of the ruling class
whom they should be exposing?" Jeffries argues the answer is no, and that the illusion of
left/right competition masks a singular establishment consensus on issues that matter most (trade,
war, financial control, political assassinations).
