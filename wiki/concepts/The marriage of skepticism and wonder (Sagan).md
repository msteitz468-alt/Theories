## Concept from Source

In Chapter 17 ("The Marriage of Scepticism and Wonder"), Sagan argues that science is grounded in a creative tension between two modes of thought that are often mistakenly seen as opposites: ruthless skeptical scrutiny and genuine openness to wonder. Sagan insists that both are necessary — that without wonder and openness, no new ideas can enter the mind, and without skepticism, there is no way to distinguish promising ideas from worthless ones. Neither alone is sufficient; their "harmonious marriage" is what makes science work.

Sagan is explicit that **pure skepticism is a failure mode**: a person who is only skeptical "never learns anything" and becomes "a crotchety misanthrope." Equally, a person who is entirely open without skepticism "cannot distinguish the promising ideas from the worthless ones." Good scientists do both — they churn up new ideas internally and then criticize them rigorously before allowing them into public discourse.

He also offers a pointed self-critique of the skeptical community, noting that the chief deficiency he sees in the skeptical movement is "its polarization: Us v. Them." Sagan argues that a compassionate approach, one that acknowledges the human roots of pseudoscience and superstition, would be more widely accepted than contemptuous debunking.

Both skepticism and wonder are described as **skills that require practice and cultivation**. Their harmonious marriage "within the mind of every schoolchild ought to be a principal goal of public education."
