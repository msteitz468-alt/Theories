## Definition from Source

**Conscious Realism** is the positive ontology Donald Hoffman proposes in chapter 10 of *[[The Case Against Reality (Why Evolution Hid the Truth from Our Eyes)]]*. It is a **monism** holding that **conscious agents — not objects in spacetime — are fundamental**, and that objective reality consists entirely of a network of interacting conscious agents.

> "We have met reality and it is like us. We are conscious agents, and so is objective reality."

On this view, spacetime, matter, neurobiology, physics, and Darwinian evolution are not foundational; they must *emerge* as the perceptual interface ([[Interface Theory of Perception (ITP)]]) of certain conscious agents, when the dynamics of agents is projected into the spacetime data format of Homo sapiens.

## Key Points

- Proposed as the simplest monism (by **Occam's Razor**) that can accommodate consciousness, after physicalism's repeated failure to derive consciousness from unconscious ingredients (see [[Physicalism cannot explain the origin of consciousness (the hard problem)]]).
- **Not panpsychism.** Conscious realism denies that physical objects exist unperceived and denies that they are conscious when perceived. A rock you see is your conscious experience (an icon), but the rock itself is not conscious; the icon is a low-information *portal* into a realm of conscious agents behind it. Panpsychism, by contrast, attributes consciousness to physical objects/particles, and (Hoffman argues) cannot avoid dualism.
- **Not solipsism.** There is an objective reality independent of any one observer; it is simply made of *other conscious agents*, not of spacetime objects.
- Independent of ITP: ITP says perception is an interface; conscious realism makes the further claim about *what* lies behind the interface.
- A **promissory note**: to earn its place it must ground quantum gravity, derive the spacetime interface, and recover Darwinian evolution and human psychology. Hoffman concedes the required agent dynamics is not yet known.

## Speculative Extensions in the Source

- Combining agents *ad infinitum* yields **infinite conscious agents**, which Hoffman likens to a mathematically describable notion of God (suspecting such an agent is *not* omniscient, omnipotent, omnipresent, or alone) — a "scientific theology."
- Reframes death (do we "slip out of the spacetime interface"?), AI ("can transistors be assembled to open a new portal into the realm of conscious agents?"), and the science/spirituality divide.
