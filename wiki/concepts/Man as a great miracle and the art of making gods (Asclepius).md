## Definition from Source

Two famous teachings of the Latin **Asclepius**. First, the dignity of humanity: *"a human being is a
great wonder/miracle"* (**magnum miraculum est homo**) — a living thing to be honored, who "changes his
nature into a god's," holds "middle status" between the gods and the lower world, and "is everything and
everywhere." Second, the **art of making gods**: the Egyptians, unable to make souls, learned to **call
down the souls of demons or angels and implant them in statues** ("statues ensouled and conscious,
filled with spirit"), so the idols could foreknow the future and heal or harm.

## Description from Source

The made-gods passage is the classic textual warrant for **theurgy** and statue-animation in the
Western magical tradition, and (with its monism — "all are one and one all," the sun as "a second god")
it gives the Asclepius its cosmological reach. The exaltation of man feeds directly into the Renaissance
"dignity of man" theme that [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]] traces, and it pairs with the
darker [[The Lament: the prophesied decline of Egyptian religion (Asclepius)|Lament]] that follows.
