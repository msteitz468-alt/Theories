## Definition from Source

The "Aldrich Plan" is the name under which the central-bank legislation drafted at [[Event: Jekyll Island drafting meeting (November 1910)]] was first presented to Congress, sponsored by Senator [[Nelson Aldrich]] and the [[National Monetary Commission]]. Mullins argues it was substantively the same plan later enacted as the Federal Reserve Act.

## Characterization in the Source

- Aldrich insisted the legislation bear his name over Paul Warburg's objection that "the use of Aldrich's name would certainly condemn the bill as representing the great Wall Street interests."
- It became the official platform of the Republican Party in 1911; the New York Chamber of Commerce adopted a near-identical "Central Reserve Bank" plan (Warburg as Special Currency Committee chairman) in March 1911.
- National banks were, per the source, forced to contribute to a $5 million propaganda "slush fund"; the National Citizens' League (J. Laurence Laughlin) promoted it through universities.
- Because Aldrich was "notorious" (tobacco/rubber trusts), the plan aroused nationwide opposition and failed — clearing the way for the identical plan to pass under Wilson and the Democrats as the Federal Reserve Act.
