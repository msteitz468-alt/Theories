## Definition from Source

Griffin (Ch. 24) presents the *Report from Iron Mountain* as a government-sponsored think-tank study released in 1966, whose purpose was "to analyze methods by which a government can perpetuate itself in power — ways to control its citizens and prevent them from rebelling." Per Griffin, the report concluded that war had historically been "the only reliable means" to that end, and — anticipating that "under world government ... war technically would be impossible" — sought "a suitable substitute for war."

## Characterization in the Source

- The required substitute, per the report as Griffin describes it, "would require a new enemy which posed a frightful threat to survival. Neither the threat nor the enemy had to be real. They merely had to be believable."
- Griffin states the model judged most promising was "the environmental-pollution model," chosen because it could be tied to observable conditions (smog, water pollution) and support "end-of-earth scenarios," where "accuracy in these predictions would not be important. Their purpose would be to frighten, not to inform."
- Griffin asserts that "while the followers of the current environmental movement are preoccupied with visions of planetary doom, the leaders have an entirely different agenda. It is world government."
- He cites John Kenneth Galbraith as confirming (against the government's "hoax" claim) that the report "was totally authentic."

**Extraction note:** The authenticity of the *Report from Iron Mountain* is widely disputed; it is commonly regarded as a 1967 political satire by Leonard Lewin. Recorded here as Griffin's assertion, without endorsement.
