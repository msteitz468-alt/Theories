## Definition from Source

Pure intention is desire and action without attribution of importance. It is the state in which a person acts toward a goal without creating [[Excess Potential]] through excessive attachment, anxiety, or elevated significance.

"Pure intention = desire and action without attribution of importance." (Ch. III)
"Pure intention never creates excess potential. Pure intention assumes that everything is already in the bag." (Ch. VII)

## Key Properties (from source)

- **Does not create excess potential**: because pure intention carries no excess importance, it does not trigger [[Law of Balance]] counter-forces
- **Assumes completion**: pure intention operates from the posture that the outcome is already secured — "everything is already in the bag" — which eliminates desire (as a concentrated focus on the unrealized goal) and replaces it with settled movement
- **Distinct from desire**: desire concentrates attention on the goal as something lacking and wanted; pure intention acts without that gap or deficiency
- **Distinct from belief**: "Intention neither believes nor desires; it simply does." (Ch. VII) — pure intention does not require the mind to believe the goal is achievable; it acts regardless
- **Three types of desire in source** (Ch. III):
  1. Pure intention → fulfilled
  2. Inactive tormenting desire → wastes energy
  3. Desire dependent on an object → creates violent excess potential

## Purification of Intention (from source)

Ch. VII ("Purification of Intention") provides the two conditions for achieving pure intention:
1. **Accept the possibility of failure at the outset** — this eliminates the excess potential of desire by removing the stakes
2. **Reduce the intensity of importance** — bring the goal within the comfort zone so that having it feels natural rather than extraordinary

"Once accepted possibility of failure: move in direction of goal without thinking about it." (Ch. VII)
