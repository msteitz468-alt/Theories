## Definition from Source

The **Man in the Car Paradox** is Morgan Housel's name (chapter 8 of *[[The Psychology of Money (Timeless Lessons on Wealth, Greed, and Happiness)]]*) for the gap between why people buy status symbols and what those symbols actually achieve: "people tend to want wealth to signal to others that they should be liked and admired. But in reality those other people often bypass admiring you... because they use your wealth as a benchmark for their own desire to be liked and admired."

## Key Points

- Drawn from Housel's years as a valet: "When you see someone driving a nice car, you rarely think, 'Wow, the guy driving that car is cool.' Instead, you think, 'Wow, if I had that car people would think I'm cool.'"
- He "rarely if ever looked at" the drivers — only the cars — so the admiration the buyer sought never reached them.
- Generalizes beyond cars to big homes, jewelry, and clothes.
- Housel's takeaway (from a letter to his son): what people actually want is "respect and admiration," and "Humility, kindness, and empathy will bring you more respect than horsepower ever will."
