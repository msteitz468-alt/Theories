## Concept from Source

Michael Barkun, in *A Culture of Conspiracy* (2003), Chapters 5 and 6, traces a two-stage historical process by which UFO belief communities and New World Order (NWO) conspiracism became fused into a unified superconspiracy belief system between the mid-1970s and the early 1990s.

### Background: Two Separate Stigmatized Subcultures

Barkun identifies two distinct milieus that developed largely independently through the late 1970s and early 1980s. On one side was ufology — a large, semi-mainstream subculture (tens of millions of Americans accepted UFO reality in polls) whose political program had not extended beyond demanding government disclosure. On the other was the radical right's NWO conspiracism — a deeply stigmatized belief system centered on Illuminati plots, imminent UN takeover, and concentration camps, which was effectively excluded from mainstream media and discourse.

### Phase One: UFO Conspiracism Takes Shape (1975–1986)

Barkun argues that between roughly 1975 and 1986, ufology began developing internally conspiracist themes — implants in abductees, cattle-mutilation cover-ups, Nazi-saucer legends (Ernst Zundel), underground bases, men in black — without yet connecting to radical-right NWO theory. The fictional British TV documentary *Alternative 3* (1977/1978) introduced the idea of an elite secret space-colonization plan, demonstrating the fact/fiction dissolution that would become structurally important: many viewers treated an admitted hoax as a disguised truth. The key preexisting elements in ufology that made it receptive to NWO fusion were the men-in-black legend and the underground-bases legend, both of which implied powerful, hidden, malevolent forces operating beneath visible society.

### Phase Two: Convergence (1986–1990)

The second phase, beginning in 1986, brought explicit fusion. George Andrews's *Extra-Terrestrials among Us* (1986) was the first published text placing extraterrestrials atop a recognizable NWO-style conspiracy (CIA as "government within the government," concentration camps, Constitution under threat). The 1987 MJ-12 papers — purported briefing documents for Eisenhower about a secret alien-liaison group — gave the fusion a founding mythology and a hidden-government structure. John Lear's 1987 statement and William Cooper's 1988–1989 documents elaborated MJ-12 into a full superconspiracy: government-alien treaties, abductions as agreed currency, mind-control implants, concentration camps, and the "Grand Deception" in which aliens violated their deal with the U.S. government. Val Valerian (John Grace) extended the structure into an all-encompassing system treating all mainstream knowledge as false by definition.

### The Key Structural Move: Reduction of Stigma by Association

Barkun identifies the core mechanism of the fusion as a mutual legitimation. NWO ideas from the radical right were deeply stigmatized and excluded from public discourse; UFO ideas, though stigmatized, retained access to cable television, mainstream bookstores, and mass public interest (46–57 percent of Americans accepted UFO reality across several decades). By attaching NWO ideas to UFO ideas, conspiracists gained a bridge to a "semirespectable" audience. The fusion moved in two directions: first, NWO ideas entered ufology; second, in the 1990s, NWO-UFO ideas returned to the right-wing milieu in two opposed forms.

### Two Opposed Syntheses: Cooper vs. Icke

Barkun emphasizes that the fusion produced two diametrically opposed end-points:

1. **The Cooper/Keith trajectory**: Aliens may be real, or may be manufactured. William Cooper ultimately concluded there were no genuine aliens — the appearance of an alien threat was fabricated by human conspirators to justify global dictatorship (the *Alternative 3* frame). Jim Keith arrived at a similar position, holding that alleged alien abductees were in fact CIA mind-control subjects.

2. **The Icke trajectory**: The human conspirators (Illuminati, Rothschilds, Trilateral Commission) are themselves the puppets of genuinely extraterrestrial — and later other-dimensional — reptilian beings from the Draco constellation. Icke imported NWO beliefs from Christian patriot literature but topped them with alien/dimensional masters.

Both trajectories exemplify what Barkun calls improvisational millennialism: eclectic, end-of-history scenarios assembled from incompatible sources (Babylonian mystery religions, *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion*, UFOs, Masonic magic) unified by the conspiracist logic that everything rejected by official culture must be true.

### The Fact/Fiction Boundary Dissolution

Barkun stresses that the fusion was enabled by a systematic erosion of the distinction between fact and fiction. *Alternative 3*, an explicit hoax, was widely treated as possibly true; its unavailability in the U.S. was taken as evidence of suppression. MJ-12 documents that many ufologists acknowledged as probable forgeries were nonetheless treated as pointing to real events. Once the logic is established that cover-up explains all absence of evidence, and rejection confirms truth, any text — fictional, forged, or anonymous — can feed the conspiracy system.

### Key Figures (per Barkun)

- **William Cooper** — the pivotal bridge between UFO conspiracism and militia/antigovernment circles; author of [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]
- **John Lear** — first to elaborate MJ-12 into a full government-alien treaty narrative
- **Val Valerian (John Grace)** — produced the most extreme all-encompassing superconspiracy synthesis
- **Jim Keith** — professional conspiracist who absorbed NWO ideas and eventually the Alternative 3 frame
- **David Icke** — New Age conspiracist who arrived at genuinely extraterrestrial (then other-dimensional) reptilian masters in [[The Biggest Secret (Icke, 1999)]] and [[Children of the Matrix (Icke, 2001)]]
