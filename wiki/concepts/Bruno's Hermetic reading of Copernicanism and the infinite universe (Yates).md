## Bruno's Hermetic Reading of Copernicanism and the Infinite Universe from Source

Frances Yates argues in [[Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (Yates)]] that [[Giordano Bruno]]'s celebrated Copernicanism and his conception of an infinite universe with innumerable worlds are not scientific advances in any modern sense but are rather expressions of an expanded Hermetic gnosis — a magical-animist vision of an infinite, divinely animated cosmos, rooted in Bruno's reading of the [[The Corpus Hermeticum]] and the Asclepius.

### Copernicus as a Hermetic Hieroglyph

George Abbot's account of Bruno's Oxford lectures records that Bruno expounded the Copernican theory "almost verbatim" out of Ficino's *De vita coelitus comparanda* — that is, within the framework of Ficino's astral magic and solar theurgy. Yates takes this as decisive confirmation of what Bruno's own published works already show: for him the Copernican diagram was not a mathematical hypothesis but a "hieroglyph", a Hermetic seal hiding potent divine mysteries. As Kepler would later say of Fludd, "Tu tractas Mathematica more Hermetico" — Bruno reads Copernicus "more Hermetico."

The clue was there in Copernicus himself, who quoted [[Hermes Trismegistus]] in the Asclepius on the sun as the visible god near his heliocentric diagram. For Bruno this quotation was the key: the Copernican sun heralded the full sunrise of the ancient Egyptian truth after its long burial in darkness, its suppression by the "false Mercuries" (Christians). The heliocentrism confirmed the Hermetic sun as the divine intellect of which the visible sun is an image — the sun that Hermes called the second god. Copernicus was the "dawn" precursor; the Nolan (Bruno) was the prophet who understood the full meaning.

### Earth-Movement as Hermetic Vitalism

Bruno welcomed earth-movement not on mathematical grounds but because *Corpus Hermeticum* XII (*De communi*) had stated that the earth is alive and in motion, since "all that is in the world, without exception, is in movement." Bruno slightly altered this Hermetic argument — extending it from the movement of the earth's parts to the movement of the earth as a whole — and reproduced its language in the *Cena de le ceneri* to argue that the earth moves in order to "renew itself and be born again," passing through all forms in a cosmic cycle of animate substance. For Bruno, Copernican earth movement confirmed Hermes Trismegistus and Cornelius Agrippa, or in other words confirmed the magical philosophy of universal animation.

### The Infinite Universe as Extended Hermetic Gnosis

Yates argues that Bruno's most celebrated idea — the infinite universe populated with innumerable animated worlds — is best understood as a vast extension of Hermetic gnosis. Bruno found the raw materials for infinite space and innumerable worlds in Lucretius's *De natura rerum*, but he absolutely transformed Lucretius's cold, godless universe by imparting to the innumerable worlds magical animation and to the infinite itself the function of being an image of the infinite divinity. The passage in the *De immenso* where this leap occurs shows Bruno starting explicitly from the Hermetic "man as great miracle" formula ("*Miraculum magnum a Trismegisto appellabitur homo...*"), extending it into a power to know an infinite God and an infinite universe.

Yates quotes the key formula from the *De l'infinito universo e mondi*: "Needs must indeed that there should be an infinite image of the inaccessible divine countenance and that there should be in this image as infinite members thereof, innumerable worlds." The innumerable worlds are divine centres; each is animated like a great animal moving freely through space. They are all One in the All — "a most solid foundation for the truths and secrets of nature" because the Magus can depend on the ladders of occult sympathies running through all nature when the All is One.

Bruno placed Copernicus explicitly in a genealogy of *prisca magia* — the Temple of Wisdom built first by Egyptians and Chaldeans, then by the Magi, Orphics, Greeks, and, in modern times, by Albertus Magnus, Nicholas of Cusa, Palingenius, and Copernicus, with Lucretius also in the line. The Lucretian infinite universe, for Bruno, was a kind of extended Egyptian wisdom — whence he adopted it into his Copernicanism as all part of an expanded Hermetic vision.

### The Animist Cosmology

The infinite universe and innumerable worlds are vitalistic and magical throughout. Bruno's planets and worlds are animated beings that move of their own accord, as Plato had said of the world-animal. Yates cites Alexandre Koyré's admission that "Bruno's world-view is vitalistic, magical; his planets are animated beings that move freely through space of their own accord... Bruno's is not a modern mind by any means." Yates's point is that this is not a deficiency but the essence of Bruno's view: the acceptance of Copernicanism was based on magical and vitalistic grounds, and the infinite universe expresses an animist, Hermetic cosmology in which God is not absent from the world but is the soul of the soul of the world.
