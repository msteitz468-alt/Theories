# Mesopotamian demons (Bane)

## Definition from Source

According to Bane's *Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures*, Mesopotamian demonology encompasses the overlapping demonic traditions of the Sumerians, Akkadians, Babylonians, Assyrians, and Chaldeans — among the oldest documented demonologies in the world, with Sumerian texts dating back as far as 2150 B.C.E. Bane characterizes Mesopotamian demons as agents of disease, madness, and death who inhabit deserts, abandoned places of worship, and graveyards, and who operate in named groups (often sets of seven), serving under the goddess Ereshkigal, queen of the underworld (IRKALLA). They cannot be bribed, are frequently described as being beyond ordinary human control, and can only be expelled by a skilled exorcist (ashipu). Many later Babylonian and Assyrian demons were originally gods who were gradually demonized as religious traditions shifted.

Notable features Bane identifies in this tradition: demons frequently work in synchronized groups of seven; the underworld realm (Kur/Aralu/Irkalla) is the organizing center of demonic power; exorcism texts such as the Magan Text name and bind specific spirits; and several Mesopotamian demons (notably Lilitu, Pazuzu, and Nergal) were absorbed into later Hebrew, Christian, and Islamic demonologies.

## Notable Demons

**Ala Demon** — A nocturnal, amorphous, cloudlike Mesopotamian demon that stalks streets, enters homes uninvited, preys upon sleeping men (causing nocturnal emissions), and inflicts insomnia on those it envelops; signs of attack include depression and loss of appetite.

**Arallu** — A class of extremely powerful, immortal demons from Assyro-Babylonian demonology, born from the bile of the god Ea (Enki) and the stagnant water beneath Kur; depicted as lion-headed, winged, and horned, they cause disease, corrupt families, inspire crime, and kill livestock. When they possess a person, only a very powerful exorcist can expel them.

**Gallu / Galla** — The seven messengers and attendants of the death goddess Ereshkigal in Sumerian mythology; incorruptible beings who travel freely between the living world and the underworld, they hunt the earth terrorizing mankind and dragging souls back below. The Gallu cannot be bribed, need neither food nor drink, and have no sexual desires. One Sumerian text reads: "Small galla are like the reeds in a low reed fence, large galla are like the reeds in a high reed fence."

**Gigim** — One of a group of seven demons working in unison in service to Ereshkigal, detailed in the Magan Text of ancient Sumerian demonology; an aerial devil that enters homes through no known means and attacks the bowels or possesses the occupant. The name in Sumerian translates to mean "evil."

**Kingu** — The second consort of the demonic goddess Tiamat; according to the Babylonian creation epic Enuma Elish (possibly dating to 1100 B.C.E.), he was gifted the Tablets of Destiny by Tiamat and commanded her armies. He was slain by the solar god Marduk, and his blood was used to create humanity, ensuring he could not be resurrected. His name in Akkadian means "unskilled laborer."

**Lamashtu (Lamaštu)** — An Akkadian demonness born the daughter of the sky god Anu; she lives in marshes and mountains, blights plants, causes disease, and is the most feared goddess of her era — a remorseless baby-killer who would reach into the womb of a pregnant woman and induce miscarriage, then consume the fetus, or steal newborns and nurse them on poisoned milk. She is depicted suckling dogs and pigs. Pazuzu, though himself a demon, was invoked specifically to protect pregnant women from Lamashtu.

**Namtar** — A Sumerian demon of plague and fate, born the offspring of the guardian of the fount of life; originally the messenger of the death goddess Ereshkigal, he was later demonized as a chief minister of the underworld. He grabs men by the hair and drags them to the underworld; he has the ability to spread sixty different types of disease and can enter a man through his heart.

**Nergal** — Originally a Sumerian and Babylonian god of the underworld (Aralu/Irkalla), born son of the god Enlil and ruler of the subterranean cave-world; married to Ereshkigal. Later demonized into the chief of Hell's Secret Police and honorary spy under Beelzebuth in Christian demonology. Titled the demon of destruction, fire, pestilence, and war; he appears as a grim warrior carrying a mace with a lion's head and is most powerful at the summer solstice.

**Pazuzu** — The Lord of the Wind Demons in ancient Assyrian, Babylonian, and Mesopotamian demonology; demon of diseases, recklessness, and the southeast storm winds, born son of Hanpa, king of the evil spirits of the air. He is described as having a deformed canine head, bulging eyes, a scaly body, a snake-headed penis, four eagle wings, lion claws, and a scorpion tail. His roar makes mountains tremble. Uniquely, he was also invoked as a protective demon against Lamashtu.

**Sebettu** — The "seven" from Akkadian and Babylonian demonology; appearing as two sets of seven demons each (one benign, one malevolent), born the seven children of the sky god Anu. The evil Sebettu are assistant to the plague god Erra and live in deserted places and graveyards.

**Tiamat** — The primordial demonic goddess of Babylonian cosmogony, the personification of chaos and the saltwater sea; consort of Apsu (the primordial abyss of freshwater). She gave birth to all bull-men, demons, fish-men, gods, and horned serpents. Slain by the solar god Marduk, who used her body to create the heavens and the earth, clouds, mountains, and rivers.

**Utuk / Utukku** — A group of seven demons from Babylonian, Chaldean, and Sumerian demonology working in unison in service under Ereshkigal; appearing as gigantic larvae, they enter homes without any means of prevention, attack a person's head, possess the body, and spread disease. They live in deserts and in abandoned places of worship. In Assyrian tradition the utuq demons are distinct — neither wholly good nor evil — and good and evil utuq are adversaries of one another.
