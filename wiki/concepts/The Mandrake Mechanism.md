## Definition from Source

The "Mandrake Mechanism" is Griffin's term (Ch. 10) for the process by which the Federal Reserve converts debt into money — named after the comic-strip magician Mandrake, who could create things out of nothing. Griffin argues that "all the money in the banking system has been created out of nothing through the process of making loans," and more precisely that American money "is based upon debt": "The entire money supply would vanish into bank vaults and computer chips if all debts were repaid."

## Characterization in the Source

- The American dollar "has no intrinsic value"; it is fiat money whose value rests on legal-tender laws and public acceptance.
- When the Fed buys government securities, it pays with money created out of nothing, expanding the money supply; selling securities extinguishes money. (Griffin's account parallels Mullins's quotation of [[Marriner Eccles]].)
- "Charging interest on pretended loans is usury, and that has become institutionalized under the Federal Reserve System."
- The mechanism's "end product" is artificial expansion of the money supply — "the root cause of the hidden tax called inflation" — which then leads to contraction, producing the boom-bust cycle.
- Griffin argues that because money is created as debt, leaders "cannot allow a serious reduction in either the national or consumer debt" — a "doomsday mechanism."
