## Definition / Characterization from Source

The class-domination theory of power is [[G. William Domhoff]]'s central explanatory framework for understanding political outcomes in the United States. It holds that the social upper class — specifically the corporate rich who own and control large income-producing properties — exercises sustained, institutionalized domination over government and public policy, not through secret conspiracy but through visible, documentable organizational networks.

Domhoff defines domination operationally: "domination means the main objectives of a group or class are carried out with relatively little resistance." This outcome is possible because the corporate rich have "been able to establish the organizational bases they need to maintain power," including the legal entities called corporations and "a variety of tax-free nonprofit organizations, which made it possible for them to put as much money as needed into perpetuating their power."

Domination is grounded in multiple, layered forms of power. "The overall distributive power of the dominant class is first of all based on its structural power, which derives from being the owners of, and high-level executives in, corporations." Structural power is augmented by the ability to generate policy through the policy-planning network, by social cohesion within the upper class, by control of the candidate-selection process through campaign finance, and by influence over opinion via the opinion-shaping network. "Domination, in other words, is the institutionalized outcome of great distributive power (power over)."

The theory measures power empirically by three indicators: who occupies seats of government power, who benefits most in terms of wealth and income, and who wins when alternative policy proposals are on the table. Domhoff reports that "the American power elite have a greater proportion of wealth and income than their counterparts in any other major industrialized democracy" and that "they are overrepresented in key government positions and decision-making groups, and they still win far more often than they lose on those issues that make it to the government for legislative consideration."

A crucial feature of the theory is that class domination and individual freedom are not mutually exclusive. Domhoff notes: "Thus, class domination actually increased from 1970 to 2020 in spite of increases in individual freedom. It is therefore possible to have class domination in a society based on individualistic liberal values."
