**Definition from Source**

The "underground stream" is a recurring metaphor throughout the source, originating in René d'Anjou's cultural output and the Arcadian imagery he introduced into post-Christian Western culture. The source argues that this image — used consistently across centuries by figures associated with the Prieuré de Sion's alleged Grand Masters — functions on multiple levels simultaneously:

1. **Literally**: The river Alpheus in geographical Arcadia (Greece), which flows underground and resurfaces at the Fountain of Arethusa in Sicily. Sacred since antiquity; the name derives from the same root as Greek "Alpha" (first/source).

2. **Esoterically**: The "underground" or hidden tradition of Pythagorean, Gnostic, Cabalistic, and Hermetic thought — transmitted covertly from generation to generation, surfacing only in disguised form.

3. **Genealogically**: The clandestine perpetuation of a specific bloodline (the Merovingian/Jesus bloodline) — hidden, like an underground stream, from official history.

**Arcadia as Cultural Motif**

René d'Anjou introduced the Arcadia motif into Western culture in his 1449 "Pas d'Armes of the Shepherdess" — an allegory fusing pastoral romance, the Grail mysteries, and the Round Table. From René, the motif passed through the Italian courts (Sforza, Medici, Este, Gonzaga) to Sannazaro's poem *Arcadia* (1502), to Philip Sidney's *Arcadia* (England), and ultimately to Torquato Tasso (*Jerusalem Delivered*, about Godfroi de Bouillon's conquest of Jerusalem) and Nicolas Poussin ("Les Bergers d'Arcadie").

The source argues that the "underground stream" image was deployed obsessively by specific political families — all of whom appear in the "Prieuré documents" genealogies — to transmit both esoteric knowledge and the secret of the bloodline. A fountain or a tombstone in Arcadian imagery typically denotes the underground stream.

**Key Figures Associated with This Tradition (per source)**

René d'Anjou → Medici academies (Botticelli, Leonardo da Vinci) → Rosicrucian manifestos (Robert Fludd, Johann Valentin Andréa) → Newton → Charles Nodier → Victor Hugo → Claude Debussy → Jean Cocteau — all alleged Grand Masters of the Prieuré de Sion.
