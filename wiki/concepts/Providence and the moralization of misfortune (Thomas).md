## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas's account of the doctrine of special providence that filled the explanatory vacuum left by the Reformation's rejection of ecclesiastical magic. Post-Reformation English theology, he argues, denied the very existence of chance or fortune: every event, however trivial, was held to be the direct or mediated act of God, and — crucially — was assumed to be morally meaningful, a reward or punishment keyed to the sin or virtue of the individual or community it befell. Thomas presents this as a psychologically "self-confirming" system (any outcome could be read as either just punishment or providential trial) that offered genuine consolation while also generating a vast literature of "judgements and providences," prodigies, portents, and the sacrilege-curse tradition attached to former monastic lands.

## Key Claims
- Post-Reformation theologians uniformly denied chance/fortune as a pagan concept; misfortune was "the assured providence of God," not luck.
- The doctrine offered real consolation (patience in adversity, hope of a compensating afterlife) but was logically unfalsifiable: prosperity of the godly confirmed God's favor, suffering of the godly confirmed a testing, prosperity of the wicked was suspicious, suffering of the wicked was just.
- National and local disasters (plague, fire, flood, storm) were routinely traced by clergy and laity to specific local sins, producing scapegoating (e.g., the Cranbrook plague blamed on drunkenness at named alehouses).
- Natural prodigies and portents (comets, monstrous births, apparitions in the sky) were widely read across the social and educational spectrum as divine warnings, a practice Thomas argues was compatible with, not opposed to, contemporary science.
- A large genre of published "Theatres" and "Mirrors" of God's judgements (Beard, Clarke, Turner) and organized data-collection projects (Poole's "Design for registring of Illustrious Providences," Mather's Essay) systematized the recording of providences almost in imitation of Royal Society empiricism.
- The specific tradition that a divine curse attached to purchasers of dissolved monastic lands (Spelman, Degge, Willis) exemplifies the broader belief that "ill-gotten goods" and sacrilege invariably brought corporate, multi-generational punishment.
