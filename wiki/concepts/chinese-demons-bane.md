## Definition from Source

According to [[Theresa Bane]]'s *Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures*, Chinese demonology is a layered tradition blending Taoist cosmology, Buddhist influence, and older folk belief. The principal framework, as Bane presents it, is one in which demons are bureaucratically organized beneath a divine ruler of Hell — Yan Luo Wang (also known as Yen Wang), the Chinese equivalent of Yama — who judges the souls of the dead brought to him by his demonic constables Horse Faces and Ox Heads. This bureaucratic ordering of Hell reflects broader Chinese cultural emphases on hierarchy and civic order; a demon may even be assigned its underworld post because it displayed incorruptible virtue in mortal life.

The Chinese term yaoguai translates simply as "demon," encompassing a wide range of supernatural and malevolent beings. A nocturnal class called kuei are ghostly demons that animate corpses. Bane notes the Taoist tradition contributed demonic figures drawn from cosmic conflict myths, such as the flood-causing god-demon Gong Gong, while Buddhist influence introduced Hell's bureaucracy and judgment processes. Chinese demonology, in Bane's account, is also notable for the prevalence of vampiric and blood-drinking creatures.

## Notable Demons

**Yan Luo Wang** — The ruler of Hell and judge of the dead in Chinese mythology; essentially Yama, the god of death, who commands numerous assistants. When a person dies their soul is brought to him for judgment by Bullheads and Horse Faces.

**Yen Wang** — Also the king of Hell and final judge of the underworld in Buddhist and Chinese demonology, very similar to Yan Luo Wang. He controls the manner and time of a person's death, enforces laws of retribution, and torments the most terrible souls. His power extends only to the lower realms of existence.

**Horse Faces** — Guardians of the underworld and messengers for Yan Luo Wang; along with Ox Heads, they escort the souls of the dead to judgment. Horse Faces holds the title Constable and First Torturer and stands on the king's right; depicted in art as a human figure with a white horse head in the "breathing defiance" pose, carrying a pronged fork or trident.

**Gong Gong** — A demonic god in Chinese demonology, working in tandem with Xiang Yao to cause widescale flooding. He looks like a dragon or gigantic baboon. After losing a war against Zhu-Rong for rulership of the heavens, he banged his head against one of the pillars holding up the sky, tilting the heavens and releasing a great flood upon the earth.

**Xiang Yao** — A Chinese demon with a snakelike body and nine human heads; works in tandem with Gong Gong to create floods. The excrement of Xiang Yao turns lakes and rivers into putrid swamps.

**Shiyu** — A demonic god in Chinese mythology and the inventor of weapons. Appearing as a cow-human hybrid, he was eventually defeated by Huang-Ti; according to lore he has either seventy-two or eighty-one siblings.

**Shui-Mu** ("Water Mother") — An aqueous devil in Chinese demonology, possibly with Buddhist origins; her personal adversary is the god Yo.

**T'an Mo** — In Chinese demonology, a demonic figure associated with desire and attachment.

**Madame White** — According to Taoist lore, a female demonic figure; details of her nature appear across Chinese literary traditions.

**Yamabushi Tengu** ("Mountain Priest Tengu") — A vampiric Tengu demon from Chinese demonology. Looking like a barefoot priest with a very long nose, these demons carry a staff or mallet and walk through the mountains using their magic to fight corruption. As they age, their feet become more wrinkled.

**Hsu Hao** — A demon from Chinese demonology recorded by Bane from primary Chinese sources.

**Kuei** — A nocturnal ghostly demon in Chinese demonology that animates the corpse of the recently deceased. It seeks out bodies that did not receive proper burial rites or ceremonies performed correctly. As it ages the kuei gains the ability to fly, but until then it is limited in how it can attack.
