## Definition / Characterization from Source

The policy-planning network is the system of nonprofit, non-partisan organizations — foundations, think tanks, and policy-discussion groups — through which the corporate community develops, refines, and conveys its policy preferences to government. Domhoff describes it as "a necessary addition to the corporate community," which "gradually developed" during the first 30–40 years of the twentieth century because "shared economic interests and social cohesion provide the starting point for the development of policy consensus, but they are not enough in and of themselves to lead to agreed-upon policies without research, consultation, and deliberation."

The network has three main institutional types. **Foundations** (e.g., Rockefeller, Carnegie, Ford) provide the primary financial support and can sponsor entirely new organizations. **Think tanks** (e.g., Brookings Institution, Urban Institute, Resources for the Future) employ full-time expert researchers to develop policy alternatives. **Policy-discussion groups** (e.g., the Council on Foreign Relations, the Business Council, the Committee for Economic Development, the Business Roundtable) are "the linchpins in the policy-planning network" — they allow corporate leaders to hear expert findings, debate competing policy alternatives, and informally identify future government appointees.

Domhoff also identifies a parallel "policy-obstruction network" of ultraconservative foundations and think tanks (Heritage Foundation, American Enterprise Institute, Federalist Society) that challenge the mainstream network's conclusions rather than contributing independent expertise.
