## Giordano Bruno as a Hermetic Magus and Religious Reformer from Source

Frances Yates argues in [[Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (Yates)]] that the conventional portrait of [[Giordano Bruno]] as an early martyr for modern science fundamentally misrepresents him. For Yates, Bruno is first and foremost a Hermetic magus of the most extreme type — a practitioner and prophet of the magical religion described in the Asclepius, who sought to replace Christianity with a return to what he called the ancient Egyptian religion.

### Bruno's Hermetic Identity

Yates shows that Bruno's earliest published works — the *De umbris idearum* (Paris, 1582) and the *Cantus Circaeus* (1582) — are already works of solar magic, drawing heavily on Cornelius Agrippa's *De occulta philosophia* and on [[Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499)]]'s *De vita coelitus comparanda*. The instructor figure in these works is [[Hermes Trismegistus]] himself; Bruno explicitly presents his art of memory not as a mnemotechnical device but as a way of imprinting celestial and demonic images on the imagination so as to conform the Magus to the archetypal powers of the cosmos. By inscribing the decan demons and planetary images on the wheels of his memory system, Bruno aimed at becoming the gnostic Aion — possessing the divine Powers within him — in the manner described in the [[The Corpus Hermeticum]].

Where Ficino had practised natural astral magic with Christian inhibitions, Bruno abandoned all such restraints. He embraced the "god-making" passage of the Asclepius as genuine and admired, rejecting Ficino's Neoplatonised compromises and Agrippa's Trinitarian scaffolding alike. His magic was frankly demonic: the De magia and De vinculis in genere describe drawing spirits through "links" — incantations, images, seals, and above all through the magically conditioned imagination, "the sole gate to all internal affections and the link of links."

### The Egyptian Religion

Yates argues that the core of Bruno's message, most fully expressed in his Italian dialogues written in England (especially *Spaccio della bestia trionfante*, 1584, and *Cena de le ceneri*, 1584), is the glorification of the magical religion of the Egyptians and a prophecy of its imminent return. Bruno translated the full Lament from the Asclepius — which mourns the coming overthrow of Egypt's "religion of the mind" — into Italian and presented its prophesied restoration as already beginning. The "false Mercuries" who had suppressed the Egyptian solar truth were the Christians; the return of the Copernican sun was its sign.

In the *Spaccio*, Jupiter convokes the gods to reform the forty-eight constellations — driving out vices and enthroning virtues — an operation Yates reads as genuine celestial magic: by rearranging the heavenly images on which all earthly things depend, the reforming magician makes the reform come. The work is simultaneously a moral philosophy, a political manifesto (anti-Spanish, pro-Henri III and Queen Elizabeth), and a Hermetic liturgy.

Bruno's version of *prisca theologia* is radically anti-Christian: Egyptians are earliest and best; Jews derived their wisdom from Egyptians and corrupted it; Christians are the worst of all and represent the darkness prophesied in the Lament. "Mercurio Egizio sapientissimo" is for Bruno the name of divine wisdom itself.

### The "Giordanisti"

Yates uses the term "Giordanisti" to point to Bruno's self-understanding as the founder or herald of a new/renewed religious dispensation. He addressed himself to a potential following: the sympathetically dissatisfied in Elizabethan England, "sub-Catholics, discontented intelligentsia" who might find in his magical Egyptian Hermetism relief from both Calvinist austerity and Spanish Catholicism. His own definition of his mission encapsulates this: the reform of the heavens is a reform of the personality, and of the world through the personality of the Magus.

Yates also notes his curious anticipation of Freemasonry in the combination of religious toleration, nostalgia for the mediaeval past, emphasis on good works, and Egyptian symbolism.
