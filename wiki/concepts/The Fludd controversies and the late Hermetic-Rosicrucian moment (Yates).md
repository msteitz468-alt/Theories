## The Concept from Source

[[Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (Yates)]] (Chapters XXI–XXII) treats the cluster of controversies surrounding [[Robert Fludd (1574–1637)]] in the 1620s–1630s as the defining moment when the Renaissance Hermetic-magical worldview met its decisive public defeat at the hands of the new mathematical science and the new Catholic orthodoxy — and simultaneously went underground into Rosicrucianism and related secret societies.

### Robert Fludd: the last great Renaissance Magus

Yates presents Fludd as a deliberately anachronistic figure: an Englishman writing in the early seventeenth century who entirely reconstructs the Renaissance Hermetic-Cabalist outlook as though it were still the height of Ficino's Florence. Three years after Casaubon's demolition of Hermetic antiquity, Fludd dedicated his *Utriusque cosmi … historia* (Vol. I, 1617) to the same monarch, James I, quoting Hermes Trismegistus as "the most divine of all philosophers and near to Moses" and treating Ficino's Latin *Pimander* as of practically canonical authority.

Yates observes that "on nearly every page of Fludd's works there will be found a quotation from Ficino's Latin translation of the Corpus Hermeticum," often quoted from memory. Fludd adds Cabala to Ficino's Hermetism in the manner of Pico della Mirandola — mapping the Sephiroth onto the Pseudo-Dionysian angelic hierarchies — and Yates believes he was almost certainly a practising magus, given his heavy reliance on Agrippa's *De occulta philosophia*. Marin Mersenne accused him of being a magician; Yates regards the accusation as just.

Unlike Bruno and Campanella, who worked within an Italian naturalist Hermetic tradition and avoided Trinitarianism and heavy Cabalism, Fludd is a "reactionary towards the origins, towards Ficino and Pico." He is not heliocentric, unlike Bruno. His system is the full Hermetic-Cabalist Renaissance synthesis at a moment when that synthesis was about to collapse.

Fludd announced himself a disciple of the Rosicrucians, placing him within what Yates describes as "the tendency of Renaissance Hermetism and other occultisms to go underground in the seventeenth century, transforming what was once an outlook associated with dominant philosophies into a preoccupation of secret societies and minority groups."

### The Rosicrucians

Yates reads the Rosicrucian manifestoes — the *Fama Fraternitatis* (Cassel, 1614), the *Confessio Fraternitatis* (1615), and the *Chymische Hochzeit* (1616, probably by Johann Valentin Andreae) — as representing the Hermetic-Cabalist tradition of the Renaissance going underground in a Lutheran milieu. Their Magia and Cabala were heavily alchemical and eirenist; they claimed allegiance to no one denomination and hoped for a universal religious-magical reform of the world.

Yates raises the possibility of Brunian influence on the Rosicrucian movement, noting the sequence: Bruno preaching magical reform in Germany in the 1580s, his likely founding of "Giordanisti" groups; Henri IV's assassination in 1610 ending liberal hopes; Boccalini's *Ragguagli di Parnaso* (1612–13) lamenting the same causes; and the German Rosicrucians prefixing their *Fama* with Boccalini's chapter on a general reformation of the world (1614). She also identifies a certain link through Campanella: his German disciple Tobias Adami brought manuscripts to Tübingen around 1611–13, where Andreae was based, and Andreae's ideal republic was heavily influenced by Campanella's *Civitas Solis*.

Yates further traces the Hermetic underground beyond Rosicrucianism — through Fludd and Thomas Vaughan to Elias Ashmole and early Freemasonry. She notes that Ashmole was made a Freemason in 1646 and was a fervent royalist with strong interests in chivalry, and that Bruno had earlier attached his Hermetism to Elizabethan royalism. She even suggests, very tentatively, that Mozart's *Magic Flute* carries a Hermetic-Egyptian theme that reflects this underground transmission.

### The Fludd–Mersenne controversy

The Franciscan friar Marin Mersenne — friend of Descartes and Gassendi, admirer of Galileo — was, in Yates's account, Fludd's chief adversary and the most important anti-magical polemicist of early seventeenth-century France. For Mersenne, the chief enemy of both orthodox Christianity and genuine science was Renaissance naturalism with all its associated magics.

Mersenne's *Quaestiones in Genesim* (1623) is Yates's prime text. She reads it as a summa directed against the entire Hermetic-Cabalist tradition — Ficino's magic images and talismans, the anima mundi, Cabalist angelology, the Paracelsians, Agrippa, Giorgi, Bruno, Campanella — but with Fludd as the living chief target. Fludd's *Utriusque cosmi historia* was, Yates argues, "the immediate irritant which precipitated the whole vast counter-flood" of the *Quaestiones*: Mersenne's orthodox Catholic Genesis commentary was conceived as a systematic reply to Fludd's Hermetic-Cabalist Genesis commentary, with Moses (orthodox, anti-magical) against Hermes-Moses (Fludd's syncretic, magical figure).

After Mersenne discovered Casaubon, he deployed the redating directly against Fludd: in a letter prefacing Gassendi's 1630 reply to Fludd, Mersenne accused Fludd of giving to "pseudo-Trismegistus" — note the "pseudo" — an authority equal to Scripture, but argued these writings now have no such authority "since what Casaubon has written about them." Yates quotes E. Garin's judgment that this great polemic around Fludd and the Rosicrucians "closes a period of the fortunes of Renaissance Hermetism."

Fludd replied angrily in his *Sophiae cum moria certamen* (preface dated Oxford, 1626), presenting himself as deep wisdom against Mersenne's shallow folly. The Summum Bonum (1629), partly by Fludd, defined its highest good as "the Magia, Cabala and Alchymia of the Brothers of the Rosy Cross" and endorsed Ficino's apologia for magic as its own position. Fludd also quoted at length from the *Pimander* and the Asclepius, advancing "under the banner of Hermes Trismegistus to defend wisdom against falsehood and malice."

### The Fludd–Kepler controversy

Yates regards the Fludd–Kepler controversy as the most famous and philosophically important of the disputes. The exchange began in 1619 when Kepler attacked Fludd in an appendix to his *Harmonice Mundi Libri V*; Fludd replied in his second volume of the *Utriusque cosmi historia* (1621); Kepler answered with an *Apologia* (1622) and Fludd with his *Monochordum Mundi* (1622).

Kepler, Yates notes, was himself not entirely free of Renaissance influences — his heliocentricity had a mystical background and he welcomed the discovery of elliptical orbits as confirmation of the music of the spheres. Nevertheless, Kepler "had an absolutely clear perception of the basic difference between genuine mathematics, based on quantitative measurement, and the 'Pythagorean' or 'Hermetic' mystical approach to number."

Kepler had studied the *Corpus Hermeticum* carefully — identifying Hermetic number mysticism with Pythagorean harmony ("either Pythagoras is Hermetising, or Hermes is Pythagorising") — and knew the Christian Hermetic tradition connecting the Hermetica with Genesis and St John's Gospel. But he drew a sharp line between this numerological tradition and genuine mathematical science. His replies to Fludd made this distinction explicit and public for the first time: Fludd's geometry and harmony are "mathesis" and "vana geometria" confused with alchemy and "Hermes"; Kepler's own work is mathematical and quantitative, applied only to the celestial world and only to the movement of planets.

Yates quotes Kepler directly: "rem mathematicam ego tradam Mathematice, tu Hermetice" — "I will handle the mathematical matter mathematically; you do so Hermetice."

She uses this exchange to frame the parting of the ways: "Kepler's masterly analyses of this difference … brought this matter out into the clear light of day for the first time and performed a great service in finally releasing genuine mathematics from the agelong accretions of numerology."

### The historical significance Yates draws

Yates closes Chapter XXII — and the book — by placing the Fludd controversies at "the crucial moment when the new turn begins to be made, when the mechanical philosophy of nature provided the hypothesis and the development of mathematics provided the tool for the first decisive victory of man over nature." This is also the moment when the Hermetic tradition definitively loses its central position and retreats underground.

Her broader argument is that "Hermes Trismegistus" — encompassing the whole Ficinian-Cabalist-magical complex — played a positive role in preparing the will toward nature that eventually became modern science: the very intensity of the Hermetic engagement with the world, its concentration on number, its operative ambition, may have helped turn attention toward the world in ways that, when the magical framework was stripped away, left genuine mathematical science. The Fludd controversies are the moment the stripping begins in earnest.

Yates also reflects that the seventeenth century's strong reaction against the magical internalisation of the world (the Hermetic mens reflecting the cosmos) may explain why Descartes and his successors "parked" the problem of mind so completely outside the mechanical universe — the Cartesian dualism, she suggests, was in part a reaction against the dangers of Hermetic animism.
