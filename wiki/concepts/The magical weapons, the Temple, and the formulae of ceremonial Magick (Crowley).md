## Definition from Source

In *Magick in Theory and Practice* Crowley teaches that the Temple and its furnishings are symbolic representations of the magician's own faculties — not merely physical objects but exteriorizations of inner forces. The instruments include: the Wand (the Will), the Cup (the Understanding or passive receptive faculty), the Sword or Dagger (the analytical mind, used chiefly for banishing), the Pantacle or Disk (the physical world or body), and the Circle (the protected sphere of the magician's consecrated consciousness). The Temple itself represents the Universe as the magician comprehends it.

## Description from Source

**The Temple and its principle of Equilibrium**

Crowley insists that "the object of any magick ceremony is to unite the Microcosm with the Macrocosm." The Temple embodies this aim physically. Everything in the Temple must be balanced: "Nothing must be lop-sided. If you have anything in the North, you must put something equal and opposite to it in the South." The weapons, altar, Circle, and the Magus himself "are all carefully proportioned one with another." Any unbalanced arrangement violates the fundamental magical principle that Equilibrium is the condition of manifested existence.

**The Four Weapons and their symbolic meanings**

Crowley draws on the Qabalistic attribution of the four weapons to the four elements and to the formula of Tetragrammaton (Yod-He-Vau-He):

- **The Wand** (Fire / Yod): represents the creative Will, the active projective force. The formula of the Wand is the principal formula of evocation — rising through a hierarchy from God to Archangel to Angel to Spirit, commanding each in turn. The Wand must be cut from a branch in a single stroke; hesitation or clumsiness negates its virtue. It symbolizes the Phallus, the creative impulse.
- **The Cup** (Water / He): the passive, receptive faculty; Understanding. "The formula of the Cup is not so well suited for Evocations." Its principal use is prayer, and "that prayer the 'prayer of silence'." It receives the divine influx.
- **The Sword / Dagger** (Air / He-final): the analytical and critical intellect. "The nature of the dagger is to criticise, to destroy, to disperse." It is used primarily in banishing — clearing the working space — before constructive operations begin.
- **The Pantacle / Disk** (Earth / Vau): the physical world, the body, inert matter. "The pantacle is inert." It is used to ground and materialize operations in the physical sphere.

Each weapon must be made by the magician with his own hands from virgin materials, treated as a candidate for initiation, and passed through the threefold process of Purification, Consecration, and Initiation before use.

**The Circle**

The Circle defines and protects the magician's sphere of operation. Through habitual magical work, Crowley teaches, "the Circle of the Magician will have been perfected" until he "never steps outside the Circle during his whole life." It corresponds to Nuit — the totality of possible ways in which the magician may manifest.

**The principal formulae of operative Magick**

Crowley expounds several operative formulae:

- **Banishing / The Banishing Ritual of the Pentagram:** The first act of every ceremony. It removes "all things extraneous or antagonistic to the one thought." The Sword/Dagger is the primary instrument. Without effective banishing, any energy raised will be "entirely absorbed" by residual spirits or forces left in the space.
- **Invocation vs. Evocation:** Crowley draws a sharp distinction. "To invoke is to call in, just as to evoke is to call forth." In invocation, the macrocosm floods the magician's consciousness — he identifies with the God. In evocation, the magician (having become the macrocosm) creates a microcosm: "You invoke a God into the Circle. You evoke a Spirit into the Triangle." Invocation is accomplished by three methods: (1) devotion and surrender; (2) ceremonial identification through positive attention to the desired quality; (3) dramatic sympathy — performing the legend of the God as an actor.
- **The Formula of IAO:** The central initiatory formula. I = Isis (the beginning, growth, Nature); A = Apophis (the Destroyer, the Dark Night of the Soul); O = Osiris (resurrection, a new and superior state). This formula underlies the structure of most major initiations, the 5°=6° ceremony, and ceremonial self-initiation: "robing yourself as a king, then stripping and slaying yourself, and rising from that death to the Knowledge and Conversation of the Holy Guardian Angel."
- **The Formula of Tetragrammaton:** The most universal formula, corresponding to the four letters of the divine name and the four elements. The Yod is the swift creative force; He is the calm powerful flow of Will; Vau is expansion of consciousness through all space; He-final is the crystallization in matter. "The first step of this is the attainment of the Knowledge and Conversation of the Holy Guardian Angel."

**The Enochian connection**

Crowley notes that the conjurations given by Dr. Dee "are in a language called Angelic, or Enochian" which "it works" even for beginners — linking his system to [[John Dee (1527–1608-9)]] and the earlier tradition examined more fully in his Enochian writings.
