## Definition from Source

The Guardian is the inner witness or observer who monitors one's own levels of importance and degree of immersion in situations. Zeland describes it as the ideal role in the Transurfing model: "The ideal role in Transurfing = the Guardian." (Ch. VII)

The Guardian is not a separate entity but a mode of self-relation — the capacity to observe one's own reactions, values, and levels of importance from a detached position, without being drawn into the game of reactions.

## Key Properties (from source)

- **Inner witness**: observes rather than controls; "Conscious awareness lies in observation, not in the ability to control" (Ch. VII)
- **Monitors importance**: specifically tasked with monitoring inner values and levels of importance (Ch. IV)
- **Awareness inversely proportional to susceptibility**: "Awareness inversely proportional to susceptibility to pendulums" — the more aware, the less the pendulum can entrain
- **Distinguishes projections**: "The position of the inner Guardian in role plays will enable you to quickly tell whether someone is trying to hang a projection on you." (Ch. VIII, Distorting Reality)
- **Not a controller**: the Guardian does not try to force outcomes but maintains detached observation; "You only have the right to choose, not to change" (Ch. VII)
- **Detachment with full participation**: "Any game must be played with detachment like a participating observer" — the Guardian role allows full engagement in life without losing awareness

## The Guardian and Awareness (from source)

The source explicitly distinguishes the Guardian from the three dysfunctional roles (Needy, Indignant, Fighter). The Guardian does not beg, does not demand, and does not battle — it observes and chooses. Awareness is described as what distinguishes humans from animals: "Man differs from animals in level of awareness not intellect." (Ch. VII)

"To gain control both in dreaming and in real life it is essential to switch from the role of immersed participant to the role of witness." (Ch. VII)
