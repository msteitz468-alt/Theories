## Definition and Characterization from Source

According to [[Robert Monroe (1915–1995)|Robert A. Monroe]] in *[[Journeys Out of the Body]]*, "Locales" are distinct regions, environments, or realms within the non-physical reality. Each Locale has its own characteristics, inhabitants, architecture, and relationship to physical existence. Monroe implies a hierarchical or nested structure with varying degrees of proximity to physical reality.

Monroe references multiple Locales throughout his account but provides most systematic treatment in Chapters 2-12 and integrated commentary in analytical chapters. The exact classification and nature of Locales remains somewhat implicit in Monroe's presentation; he emphasizes experiential discovery over theoretical framework.

## Locale I: Adjacent to Physical Reality

### Characteristics
Locale I appears to be the non-physical environment most closely associated with and structured by physical reality. Monroe states: "All of the experiments in Chapter 3 were made in Locale I."

Monroe's early experiments involved visiting the physical residences of acquaintances in Locale I while in Second Body form. For example:
- Visits to colleagues' homes in Virginia and California
- Observations of familiar people and places
- Descriptions that could be verified against physical reality

### Structural Features
Locale I appears to contain:
- Non-physical architecture corresponding to physical structures
- Recognizable locations corresponding to physical geography
- Ordinary human beings (described as living persons)
- Physical settings and objects transformed into non-physical equivalents

### Relationship to Physical World
Monroe suggests that Locale I serves as a kind of bridge or threshold realm. The non-physical objects and structures in Locale I correspond sufficiently to physical objects that Second Body perception can identify them with reasonable accuracy—though subject to the mind's tendency to rationalize unfamiliar elements.

## Locale II: Intermediate Reality

### Characteristics
Locale II appears less tethered to physical-world correspondence. Monroe's accounts of Locale II experiences often involve:
- More unfamiliar architecture and environments
- Abstract or symbolic configurations
- Encounters with non-physical entities of various types
- Experiences less readily translatable to physical-world terms

Monroe indicates these locations have been visited repeatedly but provides less specific detail than for Locale I experiences, perhaps due to the difficulty in translating such experiences into physical-world language.

### Inhabitants and Activity
Monroe alludes to organized activity, societies, and hierarchies of beings in Locale II, though specifics remain vague in *Journeys Out of the Body*. He references "Intelligent Animals" as a chapter title (Chapter 10) and discusses encounters with various forms of non-physical entities.

## Locale III: Most Distant Realms

### Characteristics
Locale III represents realms or dimensions even more remote from physical existence. Monroe provides the least specific description of Locale III, suggesting extreme difficulty in translating such experiences into physical-world language and concepts.

### Access and Experience
Monroe notes that certain "memories" or experiences recalled during separation seem unrelated to Locale I, II, or III visitation: "Nor do they seem to have any bearing on the visits to Locales II or III. The source of these memories still remains a mystery."

This suggests Locale III (and perhaps II) contain experiences or dimensions that resist even Monroe's attempts at systematic description.

## Structural Principles

### Thought-Responsive Environment
Throughout his accounts, Monroe suggests that the non-physical Locales are responsive to thought and consciousness in ways the physical world is not. The structure and nature of experienced environment appears correlated with the mental state, expectations, and consciousness of the observer.

Monroe notes: "the mind seems to act strongly in response to an unformed thought command to 'Identify!'" This suggests that Locales themselves may be mental or consciousness-dependent in their manifestation.

### Hierarchical Organization
Monroe implies but does not explicitly state a hierarchical relationship among Locales:
- Locale I: closest to physical consciousness
- Locale II: intermediate distance/development
- Locale III: most remote/advanced

This structure may reflect degrees of consciousness development, spiritual advancement, or distance from physical-world anchoring.

### Mental State Correlation
Monroe hints that the Locales one experiences correlate with one's mental state, consciousness development, and perhaps one's "focus" level. Early in his work, he remained largely in Locale I; as his practice developed, access to other regions became possible.

## Inhabitants and Societies

### Locale I Inhabitants
Primarily living human beings in their non-physical aspect during sleep or meditation. Monroe conducted experiments visiting known individuals in Locale I.

### Higher Locale Inhabitants
Monroe encounters various types of entities described as:
- Intelligent beings of advanced development
- Robed figures (possibly guides or teachers)
- Groups organized in hierarchical structures
- Entities communicating through thought rather than vocalization

Monroe suggests gradations of being, with different entities at different "levels" or states of development.

## Perceptual Differences Across Locales

Monroe notes that perception operates differently in more distant Locales. The mind's tendency to rationalize perception becomes more pronounced:

"If it proves this complex in familiar environments, one can well imagine what takes place when that which is perceived has no relationship whatsoever to previous experience. Only by laborious trial and error have a few facts been assembled, and these may not all be common with the interpretation of other minds with other experience backgrounds."

This suggests Locale II and III experiences are inherently more difficult to interpret reliably.

## Verification and Evidence

For Locale I experiences, Monroe pursued verification against physical reality:
- Detailed descriptions of visited locations
- Noting objects and details observed
- Later physical comparison with described locations
- Testing correspondence between non-physical observation and physical reality

For higher Locales, verification becomes problematic. Monroe acknowledges the difficulty and argues for multiple independent observers to develop consensus understanding.
