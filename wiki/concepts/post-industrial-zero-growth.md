**Definition (from the source)**

"Post-Industrial Zero-Growth" is, in Coleman's account, the core economic policy of the [[Club of Rome (Coleman account)]] and the [[Committee of 300 (Coleman)]]: the deliberate halting and reversal of industrial and technological development in order to shrink populations and concentrate resources in the hands of the rulers. Coleman ties it to the economic doctrines of **Malthus and Adam Smith** (whom he casts as "servants" of the Committee of 300).

**Use in the source**

Coleman presents the policy as the operational engine of depopulation:
- "The COR's policy is fewer and fewer people, consuming less and less and requiring less services, by whatever means."
- He links it to **Willis Harmon**'s call to "change the industrial technological image of man — fast" for "the postindustrial era," and to Mitterand's alleged COR statement that "industrial capitalist development is the enemy and the opposite of freedom."
- The logic Coleman ascribes: industrial development gives people better lives, leading to larger families and greater resource consumption; therefore industry itself must be dismantled (a "return to the Dark Ages").
- He connects it to the *Global 2000 Report*'s premise: "keep consumption as low as possible by cutting off technology and keeping food in short supply."
