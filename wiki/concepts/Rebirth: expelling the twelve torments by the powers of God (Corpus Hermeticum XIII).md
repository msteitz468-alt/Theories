## Definition from Source

The doctrine of the thirteenth treatise, "On Being Born Again." Hermes leads his son Tat through a
**spiritual rebirth (palingenesia)**: the **twelve torments** of the body (ignorance, grief,
incontinence, lust, injustice, greed, deceit, envy, treachery, anger, recklessness, malice) are driven
out as **ten powers of God** (knowledge, joy, self-control, perseverance, justice, liberality, truth,
the good, life, light) arrive — "the decad sets in order a birth of mind that expels the twelve; we have
been divinized by this birth." The reborn no longer pictures himself "in three bodily dimensions" and
knows himself as constituted of the intelligibles.

## Description from Source

Rebirth is presented as a secret transmitted only to the worthy, sealed with a "hymn of rebirth" spoken
in the open air toward the sun. The treatise is the Hermetica's clearest statement of
[[God is Mind and knowledge (gnosis) divinizes the human (Hermetica)|gnosis as divinizing]], and it
parallels the soul's [[The descent of the heavenly Man and the soul's ascent to divinization (Corpus Hermeticum I)|ascent]]
(the rebirth achieves in life what the ascent achieves at death).
