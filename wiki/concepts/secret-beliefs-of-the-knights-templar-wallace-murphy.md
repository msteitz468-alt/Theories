## Definition from Source

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the Knights Templar were outwardly a devout Catholic military order, but their initiated inner leadership held a secret, heterodox belief system derived from the [[Rex Deus]] gnostic tradition. The authors argue that the vast majority of rank-and-file knights were sincere Christians, while the inner circle — drawn exclusively from Rex Deus families — were initiates into "the great mysteries."

### The Two-Tier Structure

Wallace-Murphy and Hopkins cite French scholars Georges Caggar and Jean Robin to argue that the order was structured as seven "exterior" circles dedicated to minor mysteries and three "interior" circles corresponding to initiation into the great mysteries, with a nucleus of some seventy Templars. Leadership was restricted to proven Rex Deus members.

### Core Doctrinal Positions

According to the book, the Templar inner leadership held the following heterodox views:

1. **Jesus came to reveal, not to redeem.** The Templars rejected the Church's central dogma of the atoning death of Christ. As evidence, the authors point out that Chartres Cathedral — built and financed by the Templars — contains no carving of the crucifixion from the 12th century.

2. **Veneration of the Black Madonna / Mary Magdalene.** The authors argue that the great Notre Dame cathedrals were dedicated not to the Virgin Mary but to Mary Magdalene, who represented divine wisdom (Sophia) and the dynastic line of Jesus. At multiple levels of symbolic reading, the Black Madonna figure simultaneously represents the Egyptian goddess Isis, the Nazorean priestess, and the mother of Jesus' royal offspring.

3. **Veneration of John the Baptist.** The Templars held John the Baptist in higher regard than orthodox Christianity permitted. Amiens Cathedral, described as a "true Baphometic head" reliquary, was built specifically to house the severed head of John the Baptist. Churches in Templar territories throughout Languedoc and Provence consistently feature John the Baptist carvings while omitting the crucifixion.

4. **The Baphomet / The Head.** The most credible of the formal charges against the Templars at their suppression, per the authors, was the adoration of a bearded head. Wallace-Murphy and Hopkins connect this to the reliquary of John the Baptist at Amiens, the Templecombe painting, and possibly the Shroud of Turin (which the inner circle may have used as a ritual object). The "Mandylion" — a bearded head on cloth — was a known Templar veneration object.

5. **Sacred geometry and gnosis from the Temple Mount.** The Templars returned from their nine-year excavation beneath the Temple Mount with documents and knowledge that fuelled the explosion of Gothic cathedral building. Working with the craftmason fraternity known as the Children of Solomon, they encoded their gnostic initiatory teaching into the architecture of Chartres, Amiens, and other Notre Dame cathedrals as a "three-dimensional code" in la langue verte, the language of initiation.

6. **The Holy Grail as coded initiatory allegory.** The Grail romances originating in Troyes (home of Count Hughes of Champagne, a Rex Deus leader) were deliberately composed to preserve Rex Deus teachings in disguised form. The Grail quest is an allegory for alchemical and initiatory enlightenment, not a search for a physical relic.

7. **Initiatory cord / cable-tow.** The charge that the Templars wore "a cord of heretical significance" has the ring of truth, according to the authors, as it corresponds to the cable-tow used in modern Freemasonry's initiation ceremonies, suggesting direct transmission.

### Dissembling and Outward Conformity

Like the Rex Deus families from which they sprang, the Templar leaders were "masters of dissembling." They publicly affirmed loyalty to the pope, outwardly observed Catholic liturgy, and claimed to fight for Christ — all while preserving an initiatory spiritual tradition that contradicted Church doctrine at its foundations.
