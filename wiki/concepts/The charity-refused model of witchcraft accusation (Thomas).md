## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas's central sociological explanation for how individual witchcraft accusations arose: an old, poor, usually widowed woman comes to a neighbour's door to beg or borrow food, drink, or the loan of some household item; the neighbour, under increasing economic and ideological pressure to prefer individualist self-interest to the traditional ethic of village charity, turns her away, often to the accompaniment of a muttered curse or visible malice on her part. Soon afterward some misfortune — illness, death of a child, failure of the brewing or the butter — strikes the refuser's household. Because the refuser's own conscience already told him he had behaved uncharitably, he readily concludes that the beggar-woman's malice, empowered by occult means, caused his misfortune. The witch is thus typically the injured party in the relationship, morally in the right even though legally condemned.

## Key Claims
- Thomas treats this pattern (documented at length through the case of Margery Stanton of Wimbish, tried at Chelmsford in 1579, and Margaret Harkett of Stanmore, executed 1585) as the modal structure underlying the majority of fully-documented English witchcraft accusations.
- A witchcraft accusation required a pre-existing personal relationship of presumed hostility between accuser and accused; strangers were essentially never accused, and large-scale impersonal disasters (plague, citywide fires, storms on land) were almost never blamed on witches because no individual could plausibly be seen as hostile to an entire community.
- The pattern reflects a real historical tension between the decaying medieval ethic of neighbourly mutual aid (embodied in manorial customs like the widow's freebench and communal gleaning rights) and the rising individualism of Tudor-Stuart commercial society, compounded by the ambiguous moral status created by the new Poor Law.
- Witch-beliefs of this kind functioned as a conservative social force: fear of being thought to have provoked a witch's revenge deterred people from refusing charity, so that "witches" could in practice extract better treatment from richer neighbours than they might otherwise have received.
- The decline of this pattern in the later seventeenth century — as the Poor Law converted charity from a moral obligation into a legal entitlement administered by parish overseers — is one of Thomas's proposed social (as opposed to purely intellectual) explanations for the decline of witch-prosecution.
