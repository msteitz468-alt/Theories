## Definition from Source

Feder uses two classic frauds as teaching cases in the **anatomy of a hoax**: the **Cardiff Giant**
(an 1869 carved-gypsum "petrified man" buried and "discovered" in New York, which drew paying crowds
eager to believe in biblical giants) and **Piltdown Man** (a forged "missing link," a human cranium
paired with an orangutan jaw, accepted for decades until modern testing exposed it in 1953).

## Description from Source

The Cardiff Giant illustrates the public's *eagerness to be fooled* and the commercial incentive
behind fakes; Piltdown illustrates how a fraud can exploit researchers' expectations — yet, crucially,
that **science's self-correcting machinery eventually caught it** (fluorine dating and microscopic
examination), which Feder contrasts with pseudoscience's inability to police itself. Both feed the
umbrella [[Pseudo-archaeology and how to detect it (Feder)]] and its
[[Epistemology — how we know what we know about the past (Feder)|epistemology]].
