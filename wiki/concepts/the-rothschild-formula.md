## Definition from Source

"The Rothschild Formula" is Griffin's term (Ch. 11) for a theoretical strategy by which international financiers are motivated "to propel governments into war for the profits they yield." Griffin presents it as a behavioral pattern arising from "certain character traits" he attributes to "that special breed of international financiers": "cold objectivity, immunity to patriotism, and indifference to the human condition." He states the formula "most likely has never been consciously phrased as it appears here," but operates through subconscious motivation: "As long as the mechanism of central banking exists, it will be to such men an irresistible temptation to convert debt into perpetual war and war into perpetual debt."

## Characterization in the Source

- Griffin traces the formula to the House of Rothschild — "the world's first international financial network" — as "pioneers in the practice of lending money to governments," profiting "from both sides" of conflicts.
- He recounts the legend of Nathan Rothschild using advance news of Waterloo to trigger a London bond selling panic and acquire "the dominant holding of England's entire debt at but a tiny fraction of its worth."
- The formula is the interpretive thread Griffin follows through WWI (the [[Event: Sinking of the Lusitania (1915)|Lusitania]]), the [[Wall Street financiers financed the Bolshevik Revolution|Bolshevik Revolution]], and the American Civil War ("the boot print of the Rothschild formula is unmistakable").
