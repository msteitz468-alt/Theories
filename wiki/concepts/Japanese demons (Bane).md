## Definition from Source

According to [[Theresa Bane]]'s *Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures*, Japanese demonology is rooted in Shinto belief and Buddhist adaptation, with a rich folklore tradition of supernatural beings that resist simple moral classification. Bane documents that many Japanese demons were originally invisible spirits that evolved into physical forms over time, and that Buddhist influence brought both new demonic categories and systematic attempts to combat existing ones. The sage Nichiren created an entire school of Buddhism specifically to combat the oni demons and reform his people.

The broadest category in Japanese demonology is the yokai — a class of demonic creature with both animal and human features, shape-shifting ability, and varied supernatural powers. The term means "apparitions," "demons," or "spirits" and encompasses virtually all fantastical creatures and supernatural beings. Within this broad class, the oni are the most iconic: originally invisible spirits, they became gigantic humanoids with horns, wild hair, and skin in shades of red or blue. Tengu demons, originating in eighth-century chronicles, serve as omens of catastrophe and war; medieval belief held that haughty, insincere Buddhist monks were reborn as tengu. The tengu tradition itself split into two sub-types: the karasu tengu (a crow-headed, never-human immortal) and the yamabushi tengu (a humanlike mountain-priest figure).

## Notable Demons

**Oni** — The most iconic class of Japanese demon, originally invisible spirits who evolved into gigantic humanoids with horns, sharp nails, and wild hair. Their skin is commonly red or blue; they wear tiger-skin loincloths and carry a heavy oak wood club with iron spikes (kanabo). Oni cause earthquakes, eclipses, and enable enemy invasions. Seasonal ceremonies and monkey statues are used to drive them away. They prey on the souls of evil people; only the ceremony called oni-yarabi can save such a soul.

**Yokai** — A broad class of demonic creature in Japanese folklore with both animal and human features, shape-shifting ability, and an array of supernatural powers. The term encompasses virtually all fantastical supernatural beings.

**Tengu** — Originating in eighth-century Japanese chronicles, tengu ("celestial dogs") are omens of catastrophes and war. Medieval belief held that haughty, insincere Buddhist monks were reborn as tengu. They are described as looking like a crow, a crow with a long beak and claws, or a man with a crow's beak and red face. They communicate through telepathy, cause rock slides, fell trees, set forest fires, and are renowned martial artists. They can be slain only by a power greater than their own or by a superior martial combatant.

**Karasu Tengu** — The original, purely demonic form of the tengu; never a human but always an immortal being with a green face, crow beak, claws, and wings. Malicious and fiercely territorial, especially fond of the flesh of children. Can shape-shift into the forms of a man, woman, or child; known for an unusual sense of humor; can be appeased with bean paste and rice.

**Hannya** — A vampiric demon that feeds exclusively on truly beautiful women and infants. Described as having a large chin, long fangs and horns, green scales, a snakelike forked tongue, and eyes that burn like twin flames. It lives near the sea or wells, slips unseen into houses, possesses sleeping women, and slowly drives them insane while physically transforming them into hideous monsters. Featured in Japanese No theater.

**Emma-O** — The ruler and chief judge of Hell in Japanese mythology.

**Ika-Zuchi-No-Kami** — One of the seven Shinto demons; a subterranean spirit living in the underworld whose rumblings are heard through volcanic earthquakes and eruptions.

**Fujin** — A demonic figure in Japanese demonology; the demon of wind and the eldest of the Shinto gods.

**Raiju** ("Thunder animal" / "Thunder beast") — A demonic creature in Japanese demonology and companion of Raijin, the Shinto god of lightning. The demon of lightning, the raiju is described as looking like a cat, badger, ball of fire, ball of lightning, monkey, tanuki, weasel, white and blue wolf, or a wolf wrapped in lightning. Normally calm and harmless, it becomes extremely agitated during thunderstorms; lightning strikes on trees and houses are said to be the claw marks of the raiju. If it falls asleep inside a person's navel, Raiden will shoot bolts at it to wake it, usually killing the person.

**Shikigami** — A class of demonic familiar servant in Japanese mythology in service to a sorcerer. Neither good nor evil, they perform whatever the sorcerer commands — common household tasks, placing curses, or killing. Normally invisible.

**Rokuro-kubi** — Demonic creatures in Japanese demonology; thought to have once been human.

**Oyasi** — Shape-shifting "demon" beings from Sakhalin and Japan that specialize in transforming themselves into various forms.
