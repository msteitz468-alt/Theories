# Magical intentions and powers (Kynes)

## Definition from Source

Chapter One of [[Llewellyn's Complete Book of Correspondences (Kynes)]] by [[Sandra Kynes]] is organized around what she calls "issues, intentions, and powers" — a catch-all phrase for the magical goals or purposes a practitioner might hold. Entries range from clearly directional intentions (Love, Banish, Prosperity) to qualities one might cultivate (Beauty, Courage, Wisdom) to life conditions one might address (Grief, Anxiety, Healing). All three types function the same way: they serve as the anchor point from which a web of associated correspondences radiates outward.

Kynes's governing metaphor throughout is the **web**, not the table. She argues explicitly against treating correspondences as rote lists: "I wanted to move away from the idea of rote tables and into the concept of a web where the correspondences we use are not only associated with an intention but also with each other." Her introductory figures illustrate how, for the intention "Love," element, color, direction, goddess, and bird are each linked not only *to Love* but *to one another* — fire links to red, red to roses, roses to Aphrodite, Aphrodite to the swan. Adding water amplifies the web because water corresponds to love, to Aphrodite, and to the swan simultaneously. This layered cross-linking is what she means by a web of correspondences.

This framework operates on a cognitive as well as a magical logic. Kynes cites Isaac Bonewits's principle that "the power of one thing can be used through something else connected or associated with it because our brains function through association," and Aristotle's law of association by contiguity. In this sense the sympathetic foundation of the work — that like attracts like, that the part can stand for the whole — is close kin to the Hermetic axiom explored in [[The Principle of Correspondence (Kybalion)]]: "as above, so below." What Kybalion frames cosmologically, Kynes renders as practical spellwork: the correspondence between an intention and its associated correspondences is both a mental/symbolic link and, in Pagan belief, an energetic one.

Each intention entry in Chapter One draws from every domain Kynes covers elsewhere in the book: planets, zodiac signs, moon phases, seasonal celebrations, Ogham letters, runes, Tarot cards, elements, directions, chakras, numbers, colors, trees, herbs, gemstones and minerals, metals, sea items, angels, deities (goddesses and gods separately), magical beings, animals, birds, marine life, insects, and reptiles. The full taxonomy of correspondences described in [[Magical correspondences (Kynes)]] is thus always "live" within each intention entry.

## Notes on Organization

Each intention in Chapter One is formatted with a consistent sequence of correspondence types: zodiac sign, planet, moon phase, full moon name, season, day of the week, time of day, celebration (Sabbat or other feast), Ogham letter, rune, element, direction, chakra, number, Tarot card, color, trees, herbs and garden plants, miscellaneous plants, gemstones and minerals, metals, items from the sea, angels, goddesses, gods, magical beings, animals, marine life, birds, insects, and reptiles. Not every field appears under every intention — a sparse entry like Enlightenment has far fewer categories than a sprawling one like Protection or Healing.

Kynes explicitly limits the scope to what is "most frequently used in ritual and spellwork" for general Pagan and Wiccan practitioners. Ceremonial, High Magic, or specifically destructive applications are out of scope.
