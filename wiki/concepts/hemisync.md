## Definition from Source

Hemi-Sync (Hemispheric Synchronization) is a proprietary audio technology developed by Robert Monroe and the Monroe Institute, patented in 1975. It uses the Frequency-Following Response (FFR) — the brain's tendency to generate electrical activity at the frequency of an incoming audio signal — combined with binaural beat delivery (separate frequencies to each ear via headphones) to create a "third signal" equal to the difference between the two input frequencies. This third signal is generated electrically by both brain hemispheres working in unison and cannot be heard as an actual sound; it creates synchronized hemispheric brain-wave activity at the target frequency.

Monroe describes the specific innovation: "Each ear sends its dominant nerve signal to the opposite brain hemisphere, following the X pattern. When separate sound pulses are sent to each ear… the halves of the brain must act in unison to 'hear' a third signal, which is the difference between the two signals in each ear." The resulting synchronized signal is "narrow-band in frequency and often twice the amplitude or strength of a typical EEG brain-wave form."

## Key Uses and Applications in Source

- Inducing and maintaining specific states of consciousness (Focus 10, Focus 12, and higher Focus levels)
- Surgical pain control (Emergency Treatment series)
- Terminal care and end-of-life comfort
- Stress and tension reduction in psychiatric settings
- Temporary symptom reduction in schizophrenia (single case reported)
- Learning enhancement (government training programs, Morse code learning, primary school education)
- Gateway Program training in altered states of consciousness
- Explorer Team laboratory research on OBE states

## Source Context

Monroe developed Hemi-Sync at the Monroe Institute of Applied Sciences (later Monroe Institute), Faber, Virginia. The foundational patent (1975) covers the method and technique of using FFR to control brain-wave states. Monroe notes that the technology does not work on himself: his deep involvement in producing the recordings caused him to habitually analyze and suppress the effect.
