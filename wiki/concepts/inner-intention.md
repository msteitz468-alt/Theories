## Definition from Source

Inner intention is the decision to act — to achieve a goal through one's own direct effort on the external world. It is one of three forms of directed thought energy aimed at goals, contrasted with [[Outer Intention]] as the more powerful alternative.

"Inner intention = doing something by own efforts." (Ch. VII)
"Inner intention is resoluteness in the decision to act." (Ch. VII Summary)
"Inner intention: I insist that..." (Ch. VII — the characteristic mental posture)

## Key Properties (from source)

- **Works through personal effort**: inner intention achieves goals by the person's own exertion on external conditions; it is directed at the goal via action
- **Concentration on process**: "Inner intention = concentration of attention on process of moving toward goal" (Ch. VII) — unlike desire (which focuses on the goal itself), inner intention focuses on the steps
- **Effective but effortful**: "works but demands effort" (Ch. VII)
- **Achieves, does not choose**: "Inner intention achieves the goal, outer intention chooses it" (Ch. VII) — the distinction is between attaining a goal through action versus selecting it from the alternatives space
- **Cannot access distant sectors**: inner intention "strives to impact outside world directly" but cannot shift life lines in the alternatives space; it is confined to the current life line
- **The decision to act**: "Inner intention is the decision to act, outer intention is closer to the decision to have" (Ch. VII)

## Comparison with Outer Intention (from source)

| | Inner Intention | Outer Intention |
|---|---|---|
| Mental posture | "I insist that..." | "Circumstances are such that..." / "It turns out that..." |
| Concentration | On process of moving toward goal | On how goal realizes itself |
| Mechanism | Personal effort | Sector selection in alternatives space |
| Power | Works but demands effort | Most powerful; arises from heart–mind unity |
| Definition | Decision to act | Decision to have |
