## Definition from Source

**"Tails drive everything"** is Morgan Housel's term (chapter 6 of *[[The Psychology of Money (Timeless Lessons on Wealth, Greed, and Happiness)]]*) for the principle that, in business, investing, and finance, **a small number of outlying events account for the majority of outcomes**. "Long tails — the farthest ends of a distribution of outcomes — have tremendous influence in finance, where a small number of events can account for the majority of outcomes."

## Key Points

- Anything "huge, profitable, famous, or influential is the result of a tail event," so when most of what we notice is itself a tail, "it's easy to underestimate how rare and powerful they are."
- Worked examples: art dealer **Heinz Berggruen** (1% of acquisitions were Picassos); **Disney**'s *Snow White* offsetting hundreds of money-losing cartoons; venture capital (per Correlation Ventures, ~65% of 2004–2014 financings lost money; ~0.5% returned 50x+ and drove most returns); public stocks (per J.P. Morgan, 40% of Russell 3000 components were "effectively failures" while ~7% drove the whole index's 73-fold rise).
- Tails operate *within* winners too: Amazon (Prime/AWS) and Apple (iPhone) drove most of their own returns.
- Applies to behavior: success as an investor is mostly determined by how you act during the rare days "when everyone else around you is going crazy." Napoleon's "genius" = "the man who can do the average thing when all those around him are going crazy."
- Consequence: you can be **wrong half the time and still make a fortune** (George Soros; Peter Lynch's "right six times out of 10"); judge results by the whole portfolio, not individual picks.
