## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas treats the "ancient prophecy" as a distinct genre of divination from astrology or cunning-folk magic: an elusively vague or ambiguous piece of verse or prose, usually attributed to a venerable historical or mythical figure (Merlin, Bede, Thomas Becket, Thomas of Erceldoune, Mother Shipton), and typically claimed to have been rediscovered in an old manuscript, wall, or abbey ruin. Its deliberate obscurity — animal symbolism (the Mouldwarp, the White Dragon, the Dun Cow), painted scrolls, or letter/number ciphers ("Hempe," "K.L.M.") — made it endlessly adaptable to new political circumstances, since interpreters could retrospectively fit the imagery to whichever crisis was at hand.

## Key Claims
- From the Wars of the Roses through the Civil War, ancient prophecies were invoked in almost every English, Welsh and Irish rebellion or popular rising as a source of legitimating "foreknowledge" for resistance to authority.
- Both incumbent governments and rebels manipulated the same prophetic vocabulary — Tudor monarchs commissioned counter-prophecies (e.g. against the Mouldwarp legend) while opponents used it to prophesy their fall.
- Successive governments (Henry VIII, Edward VI, Mary, Elizabeth I) treated the circulation of prophecies as seditious enough to warrant statutory felony charges, proclamations, and interrogations, testifying to their perceived political potency.
- The genre reached its greatest volume of publication during the Civil War and Interregnum, when it fused with astrological prognostication (chiefly through [[William Lilly (1602–1681)]]) to interpret the fate of Charles I and the Commonwealth.
- Prophecies were characteristically ambiguous enough to be reinterpreted after the fact to fit whatever outcome occurred (the Restoration, the Fire of London), which Thomas presents as the key to their durability despite constant apparent "failure."
- The myth of the "sleeping hero" or returning dynasty (Arthur, Cadwalader) provided a recurring template by which competing claimants to the English throne sought retrospective sanction for their rule.
