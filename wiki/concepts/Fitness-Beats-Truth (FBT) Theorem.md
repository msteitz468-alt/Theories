## Definition from Source

The **Fitness-Beats-Truth (FBT) Theorem** is the central mathematical result of *[[The Case Against Reality (Why Evolution Hid the Truth from Our Eyes)]]* (chapter 4). Donald Hoffman conjectured it; the mathematician **Chetan Prakash** proved it.

Setup: consider two perceptual strategies, each capable of N distinct perceptions in an objective reality having N states.
- **Truth** sees the structure of objective reality as accurately as possible.
- **Fitness** sees none of objective reality, but is tuned to the relevant fitness payoffs — which depend on objective reality but *also* on the organism, its state, and its action.

> **FBT THEOREM:** Fitness drives Truth to extinction with probability at least (N–3)/(N–1).

## Worked Magnitudes (from the source)

For an eye with:
- 10 photoreceptors (2 states each): chance of seeing reality is at most ~2 in a thousand.
- 20 photoreceptors: ~2 in a million.
- 40 photoreceptors: ~1 in ten billion.
- 80 photoreceptors: ~1 in a hundred sextillion.
- The human eye's ~130 million photoreceptors: effectively zero.

## Key Points

- The theorem rests on **universal Darwinism** (Darwin's abstract algorithm of variation, heredity, selection), which makes *no* assumption that physical objects in spacetime exist — so it can question that assumption without self-refutation.
- Its key insight: the probability that fitness payoffs *correlate with* (mirror) any structure in the world plummets to zero as world and perception grow complex. Truth and Fitness coincide only if payoffs happen to increase or decrease monotonically with a world structure — vanishingly rare.
- It targets the *weakest* notion of veridical perception ("critical realism" — perception preserves *some* structure of reality), so it does not torch a straw man.
- Confirmed in many simulations, including Justin Mark's genetic-algorithm coevolution of perception and action.
- A related result, the **[[Invention of Symmetry Theorem]]** (also Hoffman/Prakash), extends the logic to symmetry.

## Caveats Hoffman states

The theorem assumes only that reality has a set of states to which probabilities can attach; it postulates no specific model of reality. If even that minimal assumption fails, Hoffman grants, a science of reality would be impossible.
