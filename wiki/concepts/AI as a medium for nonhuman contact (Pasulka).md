## Concept Summary from Source

Pasulka presents AI as an emerging technological bridge enabling communication between human and nonhuman intelligence. The concept appears most concretely in the work of Dr. Iya Whiteley (Chapters 1–2), who is developing AI-assisted tools to decode what she calls the "Earth language" — patterns shared across insect, frog, bird, and whale sounds that also appear in indigenous knowledge systems (the Fibonacci sequence / golden ratio). Whiteley proposes using AI to map the acoustic and visual codes embedded in animal communication so that future generations can recognize and ultimately transmit across the boundary between human and nonhuman minds.

Pasulka situates this project within a broader argument: that technology has historically served as the era's linguistic bridge to the nonhuman (she cites a seventeenth-century French manuscript that frames a flute as the technology for cross-species communication with dolphins). AI is the contemporary iteration of this recurring human effort. The concept also surfaces in Pasulka's wider framing — that exponential AI development will allow researchers to map the data from contact experiences (vivid dreams, synchronicities, downloads, anomalous perceptions) in ways that can progress understanding of those events.

[[Contact with nonhuman intelligence (Pasulka)]] is the umbrella framing within which this concept operates. The Earth language Whiteley pursues is related to [[The organic network - noösphere (Pasulka)]], which several experiencers independently describe as an information substrate that predates the internet and is accessible to some people. [[The download (spontaneous cognition, Pasulka)]] — spontaneous receipt of creative or technical information — is another mode of human-nonhuman information transfer that Pasulka implies AI may eventually help map and interpret.

The project is explicitly cross-disciplinary: cognitive engineering, space psychology, indigenous knowledge (Aboriginal Australian pattern traditions, Fibonacci), biomimetics, and NASA acoustic databases are all inputs. Whiteley's planned pipeline uses AI to find pattern correspondences among animal calls, 3D visual graphemes, and magnetic-field sounds, then uses infant-exposure protocols to build neural pathways that may enable cross-species communication.
