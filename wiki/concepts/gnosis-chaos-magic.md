## Definition from Source

Carroll defines gnosis as the particular altered state of consciousness required for magic to
work. It is an extension of the magical trance into a "no-mind" condition — the cessation of
the internal dialogue and the arrival at what various traditions call samadhi, ain, or
"one-pointedness." Carroll writes: "Altered states of consciousness are the key to magical
powers. The particular state of mind required has a name in every tradition: No-mind. Stopping
the internal dialogue, passing through the eye of the needle, ain or nothing, samadhi, or
one-pointedness. In this book, it will be known as gnosis."

Methods of achieving gnosis divide into two modes:

**Inhibitory gnosis** — the mind is progressively silenced until only the object of concentration
remains. Techniques include: the death posture, magical trance, concentration (object, sound,
image), sleeplessness, fasting, exhaustion, gazing, sensory deprivation, and hypnotic or
trance-inducing drugs.

**Excitatory gnosis** — the mind is raised to a high pitch of excitement while maintaining
concentration. Techniques include: sexual excitation, emotional arousal (fear, anger, horror),
pain and flagellation, dancing, drumming, chanting, over-breathing, sensory overload, and
excitatory or disinhibitory drugs. Carroll notes that "strong stimulation eventually elicits a
reflex inhibition and paralyzes all but the most central function — the object of concentration,"
so both modes ultimately converge on the same one-pointed state.

Gnosis is the operative moment in which "beliefs can be implanted for magic, and the life force
induced to manifest." Carroll quotes an unnamed master: "There are two methods of becoming god,
the upright or the averse. Let the mind become as a flame or a pool of still water."

The inhibitory and excitatory techniques can be employed sequentially but not simultaneously in
the same operation.
