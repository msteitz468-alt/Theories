## Definition / Characterization from Source

The **Twelve-Wind System** is an ancient mathematical method of dividing a circle into twelve equal arcs of 30 degrees (or six arcs of 60 degrees), which was utilized in ancient navigation and cartography. 

According to Charles Hapgood:
* **Pre-Babylonian Origin:** Although history credits the Babylonians with inventing the 360-degree circle, the sexagesimal (base-60) numbering system, and the 12 signs of the zodiac (each occupying 30 degrees), Hapgood argues that the twelve-wind system actually predates both Babylonia and Phoenicia.
* **Ancient Cartographic Use:** In ancient navigation charts, this system was used to orient maps. Hapgood's students identified the twelve-wind system in the Venetian Chart of 1484, proving that some medieval portolanos preserved this older ancient system alongside the more common medieval "eight-wind system" (based on 16 or 32 points).
* **Evidence of Lost Science:** The presence of the 360-degree division in maps depicting Antarctica (such as the 80th parallel circle mistaken for the Antarctic Circle on the Oronteus Finaeus Map) implies that this mathematical system was established long before the historic cultures of the Near East arose, serving as a legacy inherited from an advanced prehistoric global civilization.
