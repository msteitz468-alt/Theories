## Definition from Source

The Book of Lies (full title: "The Book of Lies which is also falsely called Breaks — The Wanderings or Falsifications of the One Thought of Frater Perdurabo, Which Thought is Itself Untrue") is a collection of 93 short chapters — each with a title and a brief prose or verse passage — published in 1913. Its declared purpose is to translate "Beyond-Reason into the words of Reason" ("The Hawk and the Blindworm"), and it announces itself as lying even in its title: the falsehoods recorded are themselves admitted to be only approximations of a truth that exceeds language. The number 93 is the numerical value of the Greek word Thelema (will) and Agape (love), making the count itself a Thelemic cipher.

## Description from Source

### Method and Purpose

The introductory chapter (unnumbered, titled "O!") establishes the cosmological framework via a Kabbalistic sequence descending from "Nothing" through the triads of divinity to "The World." This structure announces that the chapters proceed from absolute non-being to manifest existence, with paradox as the vehicle. "The Looby" (chapter 32) explains the method directly: "by forcing the brain to accept propositions of which one set is absurdity, the other truism, a new function of brain is established… Unreason becomes Experience. This lifts the leaden-footed soul to the Experience of THAT of which Reason is the blasphemy."

### Non-Duality and the Abyss

Multiple chapters treat the collapse of subject-object, being-non-being, and truth-falsehood distinctions. "The Battle of the Ants" (chapter 5) asserts: "That is not which is. The only Word is Silence. The only Meaning of that Word is not." "Windlestraws" (chapter 10) names the realm of ordinary consciousness "The Abyss of Hallucinations" and declares "there is no bond between the Toys of the Gods." "The Garrote" (chapter 25) pushes the reader toward rational collapse as a deliberate initiatory technique: "Strain forth thine Intelligence, O man… thy reason shall at last break down, as the fetter is struck from a slave's throat."

### Thelema Embedded in the Text

"The Cry of the Hawk" (chapter 2) encodes the Law of Thelema within the figure of Hoor: "Hoor hath a secret fourfold name: it is Do What Thou Wilt." The Mass of the Phoenix (chapter 44) is a complete ritual text culminating in the declaration "This is the Law: DO WHAT THOU WILT!" — framing Thelema as liturgical practice, not mere philosophy.

### The Three Deities above The Abyss

"The Glow-Worm" (chapter 11) provides the Book of Lies' clearest doctrinal statement on the Thelemic triad: "Nuit, Hadit, Ra-Hoor-Khuit, are only to be understood by the Master of the Temple. They are above The Abyss, and contain all contradiction in themselves." This situates the deities of Liber AL at the summit of an initiatory hierarchy.

### Ritual Chapters

Several chapters — "The Star Ruby" (chapter 20) and "The Star Sapphire" (chapter 36) — are ritual texts (pentagrams and hexagrams workings) embedded within the prose sequence, demonstrating that the Book of Lies functions partly as a grimoire embedded in a philosophical text.
