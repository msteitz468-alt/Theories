## Definition from Source

"The Money Trust" is the term Mullins (following Senator LaFollette and Congressman Lindbergh) uses for the small group of New York and international bankers alleged to control the money and credit of the United States through interlocking directorates. The source quotes Senator LaFollette charging that "a money trust of fifty men controlled the nation," and George F. Baker (a Morgan partner) replying that "not more than eight men ran this country."

## Characterization in the Source

- The Money Trust is presented as operating through interlocking directorates, control of newspapers, and the ability to "elect Congressmen, appoint Judges, and buy and sell newspapers."
- The source names the dominant family dynasties (citing Ferdinand Lundberg's *America's Sixty Families*): "The Duponts, Morgans, Rockefellers, Warburgs, Baruchs, Lehmans, and their satellites."
- Mullins argues the Money Trust precipitated the money panics of 1873, 1893, and 1907 to force monetary "reform" on its own terms, and that the [[Event: Pujo Committee Money Trust hearings (1912)]] were staged to convince the public a monopoly existed so the Federal Reserve Act could be sold as the remedy.
