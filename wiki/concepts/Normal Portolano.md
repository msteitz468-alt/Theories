## Definition / Characterization from Source

The **Normal Portolano** is a term coined by Swedish geographer A.E. Nordenskiöld (and detailed by Charles Hapgood in *Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings*) to describe a highly accurate, mathematical prehistoric master chart from which all medieval and Renaissance portolan sea charts of the Mediterranean and Black Seas were copied.

Key aspects of the concept include:
* **Unprecedented Accuracy:** Medieval portolan charts (such as the Dulcert Portolano of 1339) are far superior to the classical maps of Claudius Ptolemy (2nd Century AD). They depict relative distances, coastlines, and longitudes with an accuracy that exceeded the mathematical and navigational capabilities of the medieval and classical worlds.
* **Lack of Evolution:** Nordenskiöld noted that successive portolan charts showed no signs of gradual development. The earliest charts from the beginning of the 14th Century are just as accurate as those from the 16th Century, suggesting that mapmakers were copying from a pre-existing perfect model rather than surveying the coasts themselves.
* **Prehistoric Origin:** Because neither medieval sailors nor classical Greek astronomers possessed the instruments necessary to accurately measure longitude or calculate the size of the Earth to this degree, Nordenskiöld and Hapgood concluded that the original "normal portolano" must have been drawn by a lost civilization with a high level of mathematical, astronomical, and cartographic technology, which was subsequently preserved and compiled in ancient repositories like the Library of Alexandria.
