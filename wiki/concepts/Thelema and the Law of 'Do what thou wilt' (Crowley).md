## Definition from Source

Thelema (from the Greek word for "will") is the spiritual law and philosophical system proclaimed in [[The Book of the Law (Crowley)]]. Its central axiom, voiced by Nuit in Chapter I, verse 40, is: **"Do what thou wilt shall be the whole of the Law."** The complementary formula, from verse 57, is: **"Love is the law, love under will."** Liber AL declares in verse 39 that "the word of the Law is THELEMA," and in Chapter III verse 60 that "There is no law beyond Do what thou wilt."

## Description from Source

Liber AL presents Thelema not as licence for arbitrary desire but as the injunction to discover and enact one's **True Will** — the deepest, authentic purpose of each individual. Nuit declares in I:41 that "the word of Sin is Restriction," implying that the failure to pursue one's True Will is itself the fundamental transgression. In I:42–44 the text elaborates: "thou hast no right but to do thy will," and "for pure will, unassuaged of purpose, delivered from the lust of result, is every way perfect."

The formula "Love is the law, love under will" (I:57) qualifies Thelema: love is the operative force of the Law, but it is subordinated to and directed by Will rather than sentimental impulse. The text distinguishes between "the dove" and "the serpent" as two kinds of love (I:57).

In the Book of Lies, the chapter "The Cry of the Hawk" identifies Hoor's secret fourfold name as "Do What Thou Wilt," embedding the Thelemic formula within the cosmology of Ra-Hoor-Khuit and linking it to the Kabbalistic formula Naught–One–Many–All.

Crowley in the Book of Lies further restates the Law in ritual context: the Mass of the Phoenix concludes with the declaration "This is the Law: DO WHAT THOU WILT!" (chapter "Twig?").
