# Platonic Orientalism (Hanegraaff)

## Definition from Source
Hanegraaff uses "Platonic Orientalism" for the widespread ancient belief that Plato had taught not just philosophy but an ancient religious wisdom, rooted not in his own Greek culture but ultimately derived from the most ancient and venerable traditions of the Orient. Egyptians pointed to Hermes Trismegistus as the fountain of this "ancient theology," Persians to Zoroaster, Greeks to Orpheus or Pythagoras. Christian apologists (Justin Martyr, Tatian, Clement of Alexandria, Origen, Eusebius of Caesarea) adapted this belief, arguing that all those gentile philosophers ultimately depended on the ancient wisdom of the Hebrews codified by Moses at Sinai, which reached its full splendour in the teachings of Jesus Christ. Hanegraaff traces a Renaissance revival of Platonic Orientalism (via Gemistos Plethon, Marsilio Ficino, Giovanni Pico della Mirandola) known as prisca theologia and philosophia perennis, and also a reverse or negative mirror version — "Platonic Orientalism-in-reverse" — used by anti-heretical and anti-witchcraft polemicists, in which Plato and the Platonists are seen as the channel through which pagan demonic error (traced back to Zoroaster rather than Moses) infiltrated Christianity as heresy.

Hanegraaff's Chapter Two ("A very short history") already introduces the term for the Hellenistic-era belief that Plato's philosophy was grounded in the ancient religious wisdom of "Oriental peoples," notably the Persians, Egyptians, and Hebrews — in contrast to "Platonism understood as a tradition of Greek philosophical rationalism grounded in Socratic dialogue." He identifies George Gemistos Plethon as "a living embodiment" of Platonic Orientalism at the Renaissance court of Florence, and credits Marsilio Ficino's translation program (Plato, the Corpus Hermeticum) with founding "a large-scale Renaissance revival of Platonism – or more precisely, Platonic Orientalism," continued on "Platonic Orientalist foundations" by Agostino Steuco and Francesco Patrizi.
