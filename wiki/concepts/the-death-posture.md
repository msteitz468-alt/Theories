## Definition from Source

Carroll describes the death posture as an inhibitory method of achieving [[Gnosis (chaos magic)]]
by simulating or approaching death, thereby producing an "utter negation of thought." It is listed
alongside magical trance and sexual excitation in the table of gnostic methods in Liber LUX.

Carroll writes: "The 'death posture' is a feint at death to achieve an utter negation of thought.
It can take many forms, ranging from the simple not-thinking exercise up to complex rituals."

Two specific versions are described:

1. **Breath-jamming method.** The practitioner blocks the ears, nose, and mouth and covers the
   eyes with the hands. "The breath and thoughts are forcefully jammed back until near
   unconsciousness involuntarily breaks the posture." This produces rapid inhibitory gnosis by
   pushing the organism to the edge of physiological shutdown.

2. **Mirror gazing method.** The practitioner sits before a mirror at roughly two feet's distance
   and stares with an "unblinking, corpse-like gaze" at the reflection of one's own eyes. "The
   effort required to keep an absolutely unwavering image will of itself silence the mind after a
   while."

The death posture is placed in the inhibitory column of Carroll's gnosis taxonomy (alongside
magical trance, fasting, sleeplessness, exhaustion, gazing, sensory deprivation, and hypnotic
drugs). Its connection to the broader "not-thinking" exercise of Liber MMM (where the practitioner
learns to halt internal dialogue) situates it as an intensification of the basic trance training
rather than a wholly distinct technique.

The technique's heritage is associated with [[Austin Osman Spare]], whose system Carroll draws
upon, though Carroll does not attribute it to Spare by name within the lines covered here.
