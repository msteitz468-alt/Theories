## Definition and Characterization from the Source
The "Nuctemeron" (Lévi glosses the Greek as "the day of the night or the night illumined by day," also "The Light of Occultism") is a genre of text, presented twice as a Supplement to *Transcendental Magic*, that allegorizes a full day-and-night cycle of twelve "Hours" as stages of cosmic and initiatory progress, each governed by named tutelary genii understood as "moral forces or personified virtues" rather than literal angels or demons.

**The Nuctemeron of Apollonius of Tyana** is presented by Lévi as a Greek text of "transcendent Assyrian Magic," first published by Gilbert Gautrimis and reproduced by Laurentius Moshemius (1721), which Lévi claims to translate and interpret "for the first time." Its twelve Hours move from unity (demons chanting God's praise) through the duad, triad, and quaternary of Nature, to hours of magnetic direction, causal calculation, hierarchical silence (the Ninth Hour, explicitly withheld — "the number which must not be divulged"), astronomy, universal life, and finally the "works of the light eternal... fulfilled by fire." Each Hour's seven genii govern specific faculties and vices to be mastered — e.g., the genius of necromancy, of scandal, of fornication, of Goetic Magic, of the Schamir (Solomon's wonder-working diamond, said to have passed from Adam to Enoch to Zoroaster to Solomon).

**The Nuctemeron According to the Hebrews**, "extracted from the ancient Talmud termed Mishna," is presented as an independent confirmation of the same numerical philosophy, allegorizing the single day of Adam's creation as twelve Hours: from the kneading of scattered earth into unity, through duality, the triad of the generative organ, the perfect tetrad of ensoulment, the quintal soul, man's naming of the animals, the creation of Eve, the nuptial doubling, the imposed law of the "unrevealed" ninth number, Adam's fall into the materialism of Malkuth at the tenth hour, labor as trial at the eleventh, and finally expiation with "the Liberator... promised" at the twelfth.

Lévi explicitly links the two versions: "The NUCTEMERON of Apollonius, borrowed from Greek theurgy, completed and explained by the Assyrian hierarchy of genii, corresponds perfectly to the philosophy of numbers as we find it expounded in the most curious pages of the Talmud."
