## Definition / Characterization from Source

Carroll Quigley's thesis that the dominant **weapons system** of an era is the single most decisive factor shaping its political organization. Two axes:

- **Cost/accessibility:** "Amateur" weapons (cheap, widely obtainable, easy to use) disperse power and favor democracy and majority rule; "specialist" weapons (expensive, complex, controllable only by a few) concentrate power and favor authoritarian rule.
- **Offense/defense balance:** When offensive weapons dominate, obedience can be compelled over larger areas, so political units grow larger and fewer (feudal units → national states); when defensive weapons dominate, power fragments and decentralizes (the "Dark Ages"; potentially a future of guerrilla-enabled "small-group freedoms").

Quigley uses this to read both the past and the future: the "amateur" weapons of ~1880 made the mass citizen armies and democracies of the modern era possible, while the nuclear-age "specialist" weapons portend professionalized forces and a decline of democracy.
