## Definition / From Source

Per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], [[Giordano Bruno]]'s cosmological vision went far beyond Copernicus's heliocentric model and constituted a directly Hermetic extrapolation. The source presents two interlocking claims:

**1. The infinite universe.** In *On the Infinite Universe and Worlds* (1584), Bruno argued that creation was not enclosed within any "sphere of the fixed stars" but was infinite in extent, and that the stars were not small lights fixed on a celestial shell but were other suns — each at a different distance — scattered through that infinite space. In Bruno's own words:

> "For there is a single general space, a single vast immensity which we may freely call Void; in it are innumerable and infinite globes like this on which we live and grow … In it are an infinity of worlds of the same kind as our own."

The book notes that the final sentence of this passage — "Beyond the imaginary convex circumference of the universe is Time" — is "strangely prescient of the curvature of space-time that is regarded as one of Einstein's greatest insights."

**2. Inhabited other worlds.** Bruno further reasoned that if the stars are suns with their own planets, those planets are inhabited. The book quotes his character Fracastoro: "If not exactly as our own, and if not more nobly, at least no less inhabited and no less nobly."

**The Hermetic root.** The source traces the infinite-universe idea to a passage in *Asclepius* where Hermes refers to a region "beyond heaven," implying the heavens are not bounded by any fixed sphere. Bruno's extrapolation — that this infinite void is filled with other suns — was, the authors argue, his own intellectual extension of that Hermetic seed. His Elizabethan contemporary Thomas Digges had arrived at a similar position, but the book notes Digges's version also "comes straight from Asclepius."

**Why it mattered theologically.** Copernicus had merely shifted the centre of a finite cosmos from Earth to sun, leaving mankind's special status intact. Bruno's infinite universe had no centre at all, and its countless inhabited worlds fundamentally challenged the Christian doctrine that God created a single Earth for humanity. This, the source explains, is why "one of the heretical ideas for which Bruno was executed was that of an infinite, inhabited universe."

**Scientific legacy.** The book attributes to Bruno's Hermetic cosmology a chain of influence reaching into the Scientific Revolution:
- William Gilbert's theory of Earth as a magnet drew on Bruno's cosmology.
- William Harvey's discovery of the circulation of the blood was inspired by Robert Fludd, whose own idea was traced to Bruno, who in turn derived it from Corpus Hermeticum Treatise X: "the spirit, passing through veins and arteries and blood, moves the living thing."
- Bruno also anticipated modern atomic theory and evolutionary thinking, all derived, per the authors, from Hermetic principles.

The book concludes: "although Bruno's ideas were in many respects far closer to the modern scientific mindset than the works of Copernicus and Galileo, they sprung from his immersion in the ancient philosophy of Hermeticism."
