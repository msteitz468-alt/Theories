## Definition from Source

The Apocryphon of John teaches that the physical human body was assembled by Yaldabaoth and his 365 archons as a counterfeit imitation of the divine image, designed to trap and exploit the power of light originally belonging to Sophia. The trick by which that divine spark entered humanity — and the archons' subsequent efforts to extinguish or suppress awareness of it — form the core drama of Gnostic anthropology. Gnosis (knowledge) is the awakening of the divine spark to its own nature and origin.

## Description from Source

**The deliberate deception of Yaldabaoth.** When a divine voice announces "The Man exists and the Son of Man," Yaldabaoth and his archons see the reflected image of the true heavenly Man in the waters below and resolve to copy it: "He said to the authorities which attend him, 'Come, let us create a man according to the image of God and according to our likeness, that his image may become a light for us.'" Each of the 365 archons contributes a specific element — bones, sinews, flesh, marrow, blood, skin, hair — producing a detailed psychic and material body. But the result is inert: "their product was completely inactive and motionless for a long time."

**The stolen light.** To animate Adam, the higher powers devise a stratagem. They advise Yaldabaoth to "blow into his face something of your spirit and his body will arise." Yaldabaoth complies, not knowing that his "spirit" is actually his mother Sophia's power: "he blew into his face the spirit which is the power of his mother; he did not know (this), for he exists in ignorance. And the power of the mother went out of Yaldabaoth into the natural body… The body moved and gained strength, and it was luminous."

**The archons' panic.** Adam's animated intelligence immediately surpasses his makers: "in that moment the rest of the powers became jealous, because he had come into being through all of them and they had given their power to the man, and his intelligence was greater than that of those who had made him, and greater than that of the chief archon… they took him and threw him into the lowest region of all matter."

**The counterfeit body as prison.** The material world and the physical body are characterised as a deliberate dungeon: "they brought him (Adam) into the shadow of death, in order that they might form (him) again from earth and water and fire and the spirit which originates in matter, which is the ignorance of darkness and desire, and their counterfeit spirit. This is the tomb of the newly-formed body with which the robbers had clothed the man, the bond of forgetfulness; and he became a mortal man."

**The luminous Epinoia as hidden helper.** The Mother-Father sends a corrective principle into Adam: "a helper to Adam, luminous Epinoia which comes out of him, who is called Life. And she assists the whole creature, by toiling with him and by restoring him to his fullness and by teaching him about the descent of his seed (and) by teaching him about the way of ascent." This Epinoia is deliberately hidden inside Adam so the archons cannot detect and remove it.

**The counterfeit spirit versus the divine spirit.** The tractate distinguishes two competing forces in humanity: the divine spirit (light of the Pleroma) and the counterfeit spirit installed by the archons. Those in whom the divine spirit predominates are saved; those in whom the counterfeit spirit dominates "are drawn by him and they go astray." Souls ignorant of their origin are handed to archons after death: "it is handed over to the authorities… they bind it with chains and cast it into prison… until it is liberated from the forgetfulness and acquires knowledge."

**Gnosis as awakening.** The tractate closes with the Pronoia (divine forethought) descending three times into the realm of darkness — "the prison of the body" — to awaken the sleeping divine spark: "I said, 'He who hears, let him get up from the deep sleep.'… 'I am the Pronoia of the pure light; I am the thinking of the virginal Spirit, who raised you up to the honored place. Arise and remember that it is you who hearkened, and follow your root, which is I, the merciful one.'"

The Gospel of Truth, in the same collection, frames the same dynamic through the metaphor of ignorance as a nightmare: those who "cast ignorance from them as sheep do not consider it to be anything… but they renounce them like a dream in the night and they consider the knowledge of the Father to be the dawn."
