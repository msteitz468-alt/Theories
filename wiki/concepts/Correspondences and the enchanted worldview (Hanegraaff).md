# Correspondences and the enchanted worldview (Hanegraaff)

## Definition from Source
In Chapter 4 ("Worldviews"), Hanegraaff describes an "enchanted" cosmos in which spirit and matter, or divinity and world, are linked by structured correspondences rather than sealed off from one another. He develops this mainly through the "Platonic" model of mediation: the universe is a spatial "great chain of being" stretching from the most exalted angelic intelligences down to the lowest matter, all "divinely appointed" to an ordered hierarchy (p. 2825-2829 area). Human beings occupy the "middle" of this hierarchy (following Pico della Mirandola) and can move up toward the divine or down toward matter. Crucially, the hierarchy is not just contemplative but operative: influences are believed to flow continuously downward from higher (celestial/divine) levels to lower ones, and can be deliberately "channelled" — this is the basis of astral magic as practiced by Marsilio Ficino and systematized in Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa's *De occulta philosophia libri tres* (1533), and of talismanic practices reaching back to the Hermetic *Asclepius*. After the Copernican revolution destabilized the spatial "vertical" cosmos, Hanegraaff traces a shift toward panentheism, in which God is no longer located "above the stars" but is immanent as a living, animating power permeating the whole of an infinite universe — a "cosmic religiosity" already implicit in Hermetic literature and later echoed in Romantic and New Age thought. He also introduces a second, non-spatial paradigm of mediation, the "Alchemical" model (rooted in Protestant sensibilities, from Paracelsus to Jacob Böhme), which understands the connection between spirit and matter as a temporal process of transmutation rather than a static structural correspondence.
