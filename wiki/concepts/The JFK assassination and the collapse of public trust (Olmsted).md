## Definition from Source

Olmsted's central argument about the Kennedy assassination: the durable JFK conspiracy industry was
not generated primarily by the strength of conspiracy evidence, but by the *decision of government
officials to hide embarrassing and politically dangerous truths* — the CIA's Castro murder plots, the
FBI's surveillance failure, and the Kennedy family's personal secrets. Those concealments, combined
with the Cold War context that made honest inquiry seem existentially dangerous, shattered public
trust and left a permanent vacuum that conspiracy theories flooded in to fill.

## Description from Source

Olmsted traces a precise causal chain. When Kennedy was killed in November 1963, senior officials
immediately feared that an honest investigation might reveal: (1) the CIA's years of assassination
plots against Castro — which could have provoked a retaliatory killing; (2) the FBI's gross
incompetence in monitoring Oswald despite knowing he was violent, politically extreme, and employed
on the motorcade route; (3) Kennedy's secret personal health issues; and (4) the broader Cold War
context that linked U.S. Cuba policy to Oswald's likely motive. Because any of these disclosures
might trigger demands for war with Cuba or the Soviet Union — or simply destroy the reputations of
the institutions involved — President Johnson, J. Edgar Hoover, and Earl Warren chose to narrow the
investigation and suppress what they knew.

The Warren Commission, in Olmsted's account, was designed from the outset not to find the truth but
to validate the FBI's pre-formed conclusion that Oswald acted alone. Johnson told its members that
the FBI already had "the story" and that their job was to endorse it. Allen Dulles — the former CIA
director whose agency was concealing the Castro plots — was placed on the commission. Six of seven
members, including Senator Richard Russell, privately doubted the "single-bullet theory" that was
essential to the lone-gunman finding; both Russell and Johnson admitted as much to each other in a
candid phone call after the report was released.

The resulting report was, Robert Kennedy later told an aide, "a public relations exercise." Its
forensic gaps — a rushed autopsy, contradictory bullet-path accounts, the suppression of the Tague
bystander wound — were not evidence of a murder conspiracy but of institutional self-protection.
Yet those gaps gave the grassroots Warren Report critics — many of them direct victims of
McCarthyism who had every reason to distrust the FBI — the raw material they needed to build an
alternative narrative. The critics were, as Olmsted writes, "partly right": the government *was*
hiding things, even if not the things the critics imagined.

Olmsted follows the conspiracy culture through the 1960s: the grassroots Warrenologist network
(Sylvia Meagher, Shirley Martin, Harold Weisberg), the publication of *Rush to Judgment* and
*Inquest*, and the opportunistic crusade of New Orleans D.A. Jim Garrison — whom Olmsted compares
directly to McCarthy as a demagogue who exploited legitimate public distrust to advance his own
career. Garrison's central theory — CIA/military-industrial complex killed Kennedy to prevent
withdrawal from Vietnam — was, Olmsted shows, partly inspired by a Soviet KGB disinformation
campaign. Its long reach to Oliver Stone's *JFK* (1991) cemented it for mainstream America.

The chapter ends with Olmsted's thesis on consequences: the government's conspiracy (to hide its
Cuba policy and institutional failures) undermined the credibility of the very state its architects
were trying to protect. The assassination researchers' use of the Freedom of Information Act and
their challenge to "official expertise" inaugurated a permanent, populist suspicion of government
that the next administration — Nixon's — would dramatically deepen.
