## Definition from Source

Olmsted documents four major government conspiracies — Watergate, COINTELPRO, MKULTRA, and Iran-Contra — that were proven by congressional investigations, documentary evidence, and insider testimony between the 1970s and 1980s. Her central argument is that these real conspiracies were not merely historical curiosities: they vindicated earlier "paranoid" skeptics, permanently damaged public trust in government, and created the cultural and epistemic soil in which all subsequent antigovernment conspiracy theories (including superconspiracy theories) took root. The exposure of these programs was the turning point: after the Church Committee revelations, Americans knew enough about their government's secret actions to permanently distrust official narratives.

## Description from Source

**Watergate (Chapter 5):** Nixon's administration engaged simultaneously in paranoid thinking about enemies, real criminal conspiracies (the Plumbers, the Enemies List, illegal wiretaps, the Watergate cover-up), and cynical conspiracy-theory promotion (blaming the CIA, spreading fabricated documents). Nixon's mania for secrecy permeated his entire administration — even the Joint Chiefs secretly spied on the president. The cover-up ultimately failed because too many conspirators placed self-interest above loyalty; the Supreme Court ordered release of the tapes and Nixon resigned August 9, 1974.

**COINTELPRO (Chapters 5–6):** The FBI's secret counterintelligence program was first revealed when activists broke into the Media, Pennsylvania FBI office in March 1971 and leaked the files. The Church Committee confirmed its scope: the bureau had infiltrated, sabotaged, and harassed hundreds of American groups. The FBI's campaign against Martin Luther King Jr. — wiretaps, hotel-room bugs, and a letter urging him to kill himself just before he accepted the Nobel Peace Prize — shocked Americans who had known Hoover only through a heroic popular-culture image.

**MKULTRA (Chapter 6):** The CIA's mind-control program began in the 1950s as a response to Cold War fears about Soviet brainwashing. CIA officers dosed unsuspecting citizens with LSD in Greenwich Village and San Francisco ("Operation Midnight Climax"), paid Dr. Ewen Cameron to use "depatterning" experiments on Canadian psychiatric patients, and caused at least one death (Frank Olson). Five thousand documents discovered in 1977 confirmed the program. The Senate hearings found that forty-four universities and research institutions participated.

**Iran-Contra (Chapter 6):** Reagan administration officials secretly sold arms to Iran (in violation of stated policy) and diverted the proceeds to fund the Nicaraguan contras (in violation of the Boland Amendment). Oliver North ran "the Enterprise" — Senator Daniel Inouye's "shadowy government with its own air force, its own navy, its own fund-raising mechanism." CIA Director William Casey reportedly envisioned an even more permanent secret "off-the-shelf" covert agency. The congressional majority report called it the work of a "cabal of zealots." Iran-Contra validated fears about a government-within-a-government, and almost no one served prison time.
