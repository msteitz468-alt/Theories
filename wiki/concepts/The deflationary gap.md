## Definition / Characterization from Source

Carroll Quigley's term for the shortfall, at the heart of his economic analysis in *Tragedy and Hope*, between the purchasing power available in a community and the aggregate price of the goods it offers for sale. Since purchasing power = income − savings, any savings not returned to the market as **investment** leave goods unsellable by exactly that amount. When investment chronically lags savings — driven up by industrialism, the concentration of economic control, and a widening disparity in incomes (the rich save disproportionately) — the result is falling prices, declining activity, and depression. Quigley calls the deflationary gap "the key to the twentieth-century economic crisis and one of the three central cores of the whole tragedy of the century."

He argues the gap can be closed only two ways: by **destroying or not producing goods** (the down-phase of a normal business cycle; crop destruction; war), or by **producing goods that do not enter the market** — i.e. public spending, of which **rearmament** became the most widely adopted form (the economic root of Fascism).
