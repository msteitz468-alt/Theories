## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas treats belief in ghosts and fairies as a coherent complex of popular supernatural belief, parallel in social function to witchcraft and astrology. Ghosts were the spirits of the dead (or, after the Reformation, disputed apparitions no longer identifiable as such) who returned for a specific purpose — usually to enforce moral or property norms, denounce crime, or warn the living. Fairies were an older, heterogeneous body of spirit-belief (trooping fairies, hobgoblins, water-spirits, giants) reduced by the seventeenth century to a fairly standardized image of small, capricious beings who rewarded cleanliness and industry and punished neglect. Both beliefs declined not simply through intellectual disproof but because the social relationships and traditional community structures which had made them meaningful (deference to ancestors, close-knit village supervision of behaviour) themselves eroded.
