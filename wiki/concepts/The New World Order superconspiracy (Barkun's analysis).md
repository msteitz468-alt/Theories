# The New World Order Superconspiracy (Barkun's Analysis)

## Concept from Source

Michael Barkun's *A Culture of Conspiracy* (2003) traces the New World Order (NWO) belief system as the central, unifying superconspiracy of contemporary American millenarianism. Barkun argues that despite an unprecedented plurality of millenarian visions in late-twentieth-century America, they have been integrated — across religious and secular lines — by a single overarching narrative: that a secret, immensely powerful group is in the process of seizing total control of the world, commonly called the New World Order.

### Two Converging Streams

Barkun identifies two historically distinct sources for the NWO idea that eventually merged:

1. **Millenarian Christianity (the Antichrist tradition):** Protestant dispensationalism, particularly as elaborated by John Nelson Darby, posited a diabolical world dictator (the Antichrist) who would arise in the end-times and impose global control. Organizations like the League of Nations and later the United Nations were identified as his apparatus. Modern technology — computers, bar codes, microchips — was interpreted as the mechanism of the Antichrist's "mark of the beast" surveillance economy.

2. **Secular secret-society conspiracism (the Illuminati tradition):** Beginning with John Robison's *Proofs of a Conspiracy* (1798) and Abbé Barruel's *Memoirs* (1803), a literature developed blaming the French Revolution and subsequent upheavals on the Bavarian Illuminati. This tradition was vastly expanded in the interwar period by British writers Nesta Webster and Lady Queenborough, who added Jews as the driving force behind the conspiracy and extended the Illuminati's reach across all of modern history.

### From Simple Plots to Superconspiracies

Barkun traces a developmental logic within the secular stream: what began as accusations against a single secret society (the Illuminati) evolved, from the 1970s onward, into "superconspiracy" structures — Russian-nested-doll architectures in which multiple organizations (CFR, Trilateral Commission, Bilderbergers, UN, Jesuits, Vatican, Masons, etc.) are all subsumed as front groups or tools of a single hidden hierarchy. Key figures in this evolution include Des Griffin, John Todd, Stan Deyo, and most influentially, Milton William Cooper, whose [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] Barkun regards as the most elaborate and influential superconspiracy text.

### The Protocols as Illuminati Blueprint

A structurally important move in the NWO superconspiracy, traced by Barkun through Griffin and then Cooper, is the rebranding of *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion* as an "Illuminati document." Cooper reprinted the entire *Protocols* with the instruction to substitute "Illuminati" for "Zion/Jews." This maneuver allowed an overtly antisemitic forgery to be recycled into a broader, nominally non-antisemitic superconspiracy framework while preserving its conspiratorial content.

### The Post-Cold War Crystallization

Barkun argues that the NWO belief system rose to central prominence around 1990 partly because the collapse of the Soviet Union eliminated the prior organizing enemy in American millenarian and right-wing thought. The NWO filled the resulting moral vacuum. It had three structural advantages: deep historical roots lending it legitimacy; inherent invisibility (it can never be falsified because nothing is what it seems); and the ability to refocus millenarian anxieties from international affairs to domestic politics (federal tyranny, gun confiscation, FEMA camps).

### The Militia/Patriot Milieu and Specific Claims

By the 1990s, elaborated NWO theory — particularly in militia and Patriot circles — had added specific operational claims: black helicopters as visible evidence of NWO forces; a network of FEMA concentration camps to incarcerate patriots; and CIA mind-control programs (MK-ULTRA expanded into Project Monarch) as tools of population management. Barkun traces each of these back to kernels of real government activity that were then vastly inflated.

### "Ecumenical" Appeal

Barkun emphasizes that the NWO superconspiracy achieved a rare cross-sectoral appeal — simultaneously attractive to Protestant fundamentalists (via the Antichrist tradition), the secular far right (via Illuminati/elite-domination tradition), UFO believers (who merged alien cover-ups with NWO governance), and, to a lesser extent, New Age circles. This breadth is central to Barkun's explanation of why the NWO became the dominant conspiracy framework of the 1990s–2000s.
