## Concept from Source

Michael Barkun, in *[[A Culture of Conspiracy (Barkun)]]* (Ch. 1), proposes a three-level taxonomy of conspiracy belief organized by ascending scope. All three types share the same underlying logic — a universe governed by intentionality in which nothing happens by accident, nothing is as it seems, and everything is connected — but they differ fundamentally in the scale of the evil they posit.

### Event conspiracies

The narrowest category. The conspiracy is held responsible for a single, limited, discrete event or set of events. Barkun's primary examples include the Kennedy assassination literature, the TWA Flight 800 crash, the spread of AIDS in the Black community, and the burning of Black churches in the 1990s. The conspiratorial forces are alleged to have focused their energies on a well-defined objective. Event conspiracy beliefs need not imply a world-spanning hidden power; the plot remains local.

### Systemic conspiracies

At this level the conspiracy has broad goals — typically seizing control of a country, a region, or the entire world — but the conspiratorial machinery is conceptually simple: a single evil organization infiltrates and subverts existing institutions. Barkun cites theories centred on alleged Jewish, Masonic, or Catholic cabals, as well as theories about communism or international capitalism, as paradigmatic examples of systemic conspiracy belief. The scope is sweeping but the explanatory structure remains relatively flat.

### Superconspiracies

The most encompassing and structurally complex type. Superconspiracies are constructs in which multiple conspiracies are linked together hierarchically: event and systemic conspiracies are nested within one another, with a distant but all-powerful evil force — invisible, operating in total secrecy (Type I in Barkun's secrecy matrix) — sitting at the summit manipulating all lesser conspiratorial actors. Barkun notes that superconspiracies enjoyed particular growth since the 1980s, citing David Icke, Valdamar Valerian, and Milton William Cooper ([[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]) as exemplars.

### The escalation dynamic

Barkun argues that the logic of conspiracism itself drives escalation from event to systemic to superconspiracy. The more inclusive a conspiracy theory becomes, the less falsifiable it is: skeptics and counter-evidence are increasingly identified as instruments of the conspiracy. At each stage the domain of evil expands, new putative evildoers are absorbed, and the theory becomes more impervious to disproof. This dynamic is central to [[The culture of conspiracy: stigmatized knowledge and superconspiracy (Barkun)]].
