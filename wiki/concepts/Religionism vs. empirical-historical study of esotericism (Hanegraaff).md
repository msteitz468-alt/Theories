# Religionism vs. empirical-historical study of esotericism (Hanegraaff)

## Definition from Source
In Chapter One, Hanegraaff names "religionism" as the technical term in the modern science of religion for the search for a universal "inner" spiritual dimension underlying all religious traditions, independent of social, historical, or cultural circumstance. On this model, true esoteric spirituality is one, and "Western" esotericism is merely the occidental slice of a larger, universal domain also found in Hinduism, Buddhism, Shamanism, etc. He traces its influence, especially in American religious studies after World War II under Mircea Eliade and his school, and names Henry Corbin, Antoine Faivre (in his earlier work), Arthur Versluis, and Nicholas Goodrick-Clarke as scholars shaped by religionist agendas — while noting mainstream scholarship has been moving away from religionism since the 1990s.

Hanegraaff's critique is twofold. First, methodologically: religionism assumes a hidden, absolute reality that scholarly method — "exoteric" by definition — has no instruments to verify or falsify; the Absolute or Divine "is simply not a possible object of research." He advocates instead "methodological agnosticism," which neither affirms nor denies that other means (spiritual technique, mystical contemplation) might access such truth, while restricting scholarship to studying the beliefs and theories people have held about it. Second, historically: religionism's emphasis on one universal truth downplays historical specificity, individual creativity, and change, flattening the real and often mutually exclusive differences between currents like Hermetic texts, Böhme/Swedenborg theosophy, Crowleyan occultism, and contemporary New Age thought. He also rejects, on similar grounds, the idea that "Western esotericism" implies a parallel "Oriental Esotericism," arguing comparative religionist studies of inner experience are a separate, already well-established field that does not need — and does not coincide with — the "esotericism" label.
