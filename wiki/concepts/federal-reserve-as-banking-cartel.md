## Definition from Source

Griffin's central thesis is that the Federal Reserve is a **banking cartel with a government facade** — "a cartel which has been legalized." A cartel, in his definition, is "a group of independent businesses which join together to coordinate the production, pricing, or marketing of their members" to reduce competition and increase profitability. The Jekyll Island meeting, he argues, was "a classic example of cartel structure," bringing together the otherwise-competing Morgan, Rockefeller, Rothschild, Warburg, and Kuhn-Loeb interests against their common enemy: competition.

## Characterization in the Source

Griffin lists the cartel's five objectives (see [[The Federal Reserve is a banking cartel with five objectives]]):
1. Stop growing competition from newer banks (esp. in the South and West)
2. Make the money supply "elastic" to recapture the industrial loan market
3. Pool reserves so all banks follow the same loan-to-deposit ratios (protection from currency drains/bank runs)
4. Shift the cartel's inevitable losses to the taxpayer
5. Convince Congress the scheme protects the public

He quotes insiders as confirmation: Aldrich ("not a bank, but a cooperative union of all the banks of the country"), A. Barton Hepburn of Chase ("it will make all incorporated banks together joint owners of a central dominating power"), and Aldrich's 1914 boast that "now we are able to dominate the bank reserves of the entire country." Griffin argues the Fed "failed" at its stated stabilization goals because "those were never its true objectives."
