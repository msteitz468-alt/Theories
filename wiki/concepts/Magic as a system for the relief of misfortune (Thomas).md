## Definition / Characterization from Source
Keith Thomas frames the whole apparatus of village magic — charming, healing by touch, thief-detection by sieve-and-shears or crystal-gazing, girdle-measuring, weapon-salves, and the diagnosis of witchcraft — as a coherent practical response to the "helplessness of the poorer classes in the face of disease and the physical hazards of their environment." Where orthodox medicine, law, and the Church left gaps (inadequate doctors, no police force, an abolished confessional), cunning men and wise women stepped in to supply comfort, diagnosis, and a sense of remedy. Thomas treats magic less as a false belief-system to be debunked than as a set of practical techniques that functioned socially and psychologically even where they had no physical efficacy.

## Key Claims
- Cunning folk offered healing, thief-detection, fortune-telling and protective charms as a single interlocking "repertoire," not a coherent theology.
- Much of this magic was a debased residue of the medieval Church's own magical repertoire (Latin prayers, invocation of saints), surviving among the laity after the Reformation stripped the clergy of ecclesiastical magic.
- Divinatory techniques such as the sieve and shears or key and book worked less by supernatural power than by confirming suspicions clients already held — Thomas draws the explicit parallel with modern African diviners.
- Thief-magic functioned as a social ordeal: it intimidated the guilty into confessing or restoring goods, much like the medieval judicial ordeal.
- The system persisted because it filled real practical needs — detection of theft, diagnosis of unexplained illness, protection in childbirth — that neither orthodox religion nor formal medicine adequately met.
