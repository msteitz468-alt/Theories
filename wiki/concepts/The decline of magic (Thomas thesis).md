## Definition / Characterization from Source

The organizing thesis of **[[Keith Thomas (b. 1933)|Keith Thomas]]'s** *[[Religion and the Decline of Magic (Thomas)]]*: that the many overlapping systems of belief current in sixteenth- and seventeenth-century England — the magic of the medieval Church, astrology, witchcraft, magical healing, divination, ancient prophecies, ghosts and fairies — shared a common social function: they offered explanations for misfortune and means of redress at times of adversity, discharging a role close to that of organized religion. Thomas argues these beliefs were held by intelligent people because they possessed real practical utility in a world of endemic insecurity, disease, fire, and premature death.

Over the period between the Reformation and the dawn of the Enlightenment these beliefs decayed in prestige. Thomas emphasizes that the decline of magic **preceded**, rather than followed, the technological and scientific advances that might have made it redundant — a change in the intellectual atmosphere striking in the years approaching 1700, whose full explanation he regards as one of the outstanding problems of the period.
