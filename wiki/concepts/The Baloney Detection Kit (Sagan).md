## Definition from Source

Sagan's term (Ch. 12, *The Demon-Haunted World*) for the suite of critical-thinking tools that
scientists deploy when evaluating new claims. He describes it as "a tried-and-true, consumer-tested
method" available to anyone — not just trained scientists — for distinguishing sound arguments from
"baloney": deception, self-deception, wishful thinking, and pseudoscience.

## Description from Source

### Positive tools — what to do

- **Independent confirmation.** Wherever possible, "facts" must be confirmed by observers who have
  no prior stake in the outcome and who did not collaborate on the original claim.
- **Encourage substantive debate** among knowledgeable proponents of *all* competing views, rather
  than simply deferring to whichever side has the most passionate advocates.
- **Arguments from authority carry little weight.** In science there are no authorities, only
  experts who may themselves err. "Perhaps a better way to say it is that in science there are no
  authorities; at most, there are experts."
- **Spin more than one hypothesis.** Generate all the ways the data could be explained, then devise
  tests to *disprove* each alternative. What survives this "Darwinian selection among multiple
  working hypotheses" is much likelier to be correct than the first explanation that came to mind.
- **Avoid over-attachment to your own hypothesis.** Ask yourself why you like it. Compare it fairly
  with alternatives. "If you don't, others will."
- **Quantify.** Vague, qualitative claims permit many explanations; numerical predictions
  discriminate sharply among them.
- **Every link in a chain of argument must work**, including the premises. A mostly-valid chain of
  reasoning that includes one false step fails.
- **Occam's Razor.** Faced with two hypotheses that explain the data equally well, prefer the
  simpler one.
- **Falsifiability.** "Always ask whether the hypothesis can be, at least in principle, falsified.
  Propositions that are untestable, unfalsifiable are not worth much." (See also
  [[The Dragon in My Garage — the unfalsifiability test (Sagan)]].)
- **Controlled experiments and variable separation** are essential; and where results depend on
  subjective assessment, experiments should be **double-blind**.

### Logical and rhetorical fallacies to recognize — what not to do

Sagan lists these explicitly as the "negative toolkit" — the common moves that make bad arguments
sound plausible:

| Fallacy | Brief description |
|---|---|
| **Ad hominem** | Attacking the person, not the argument |
| **Argument from authority** | "Believe it because an important person said so" |
| **Argument from adverse consequences** | "It must be true because the alternative would be terrible" |
| **Appeal to ignorance** | "It hasn't been disproved, so it must be true" (or vice versa) |
| **Special pleading** | Invoking unstatable exceptions to rescue a claim in trouble |
| **Begging the question** | Assuming the very conclusion at issue |
| **Observational selection** | Counting the hits and forgetting the misses |
| **Statistics of small numbers** | Drawing large conclusions from too few cases |
| **Misunderstanding of statistics** | e.g., surprised that half of all people are below average |
| **Inconsistency** | Applying one evidentiary standard to favoured claims and another to disfavoured ones |
| **Non sequitur** | The conclusion doesn't follow from the premises |
| **Post hoc, ergo propter hoc** | "It happened after, so it was caused by" |
| **Meaningless question** | Asking something that contains a logical contradiction |
| **Excluded middle / false dichotomy** | Presenting only the two extremes when a continuum exists |
| **Slippery slope** | Assuming one step inevitably leads to an extreme |
| **Confusion of correlation and causation** | Treating co-occurrence as proof of mechanism |
| **Straw man** | Caricaturing a position to make it easier to attack |
| **Suppressed evidence / half-truths** | Omitting the part of the evidence that would alter the verdict |
| **Weasel words** | Euphemisms designed to obscure what is actually being claimed |

### Application

Sagan uses the Kit to evaluate: channelling and spiritualism, New Age claims, psychic surgery, faith
healing, crop circles, the Carlos hoax, astrology, recovered-memory therapy, and alien abduction
testimony. In every case he argues that the claim either fails the falsifiability test or relies on
one or more fallacies from the list above.

The Kit is also Sagan's proposed defense against politically dangerous credulity: "When governments
and societies lose the capacity for critical thinking, the results can be catastrophic."
