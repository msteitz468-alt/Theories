## Definition from Source

According to [[Theresa Bane]]'s *Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures*, Slavic demonology is characterized above all by the widespread demonization of pre-Christian gods during the Christianization of Eastern Europe. Bane documents this pattern repeatedly: Czarnobog, Dabog, Boruta, Chernobog, and others were originally gods of winter, the sun, the woods, or the forces of nature who were recast as demons of evil and darkness when Christian missionaries arrived. This overlap between the demonic and the divine is a defining structural feature of Slavic tradition as Bane presents it.

Slavic demons are closely tied to natural phenomena and liminal spaces: the forest, crossroads, water, midday sun, and the night. Several are personifications of natural forces or misfortunes — sunstroke, storms, pestilence, the power of the dead — and some inhabit or haunt specific landscapes. The Rusalki are created when women die violent or unnatural deaths; they are demons of the dualistic quality of nature itself. A running thread in Slavic demonology is the use of specific dances, sacrifices, and naming rituals to interact with demons: knowing a demon's true name renders it a slave; dancing and drumming can exorcise or placate possessing spirits. Bane notes that some Slavic demonic traditions overlap with Baltic and, occasionally, Germanic traditions.

## Notable Demons

**Czarnobog** / **Chernobog** ("Black god") — Originally a god of winter in Baltic and Slavic mythologies, demonized during the introduction of Christianity; named as the demon of evil, darkness, and night, comparable to the Persian Ahriman. His personal adversary is Bylebog, the White God of the waxing year. In later traditions he was born the son of the god of the underworld and the goddess Mara.

**Dabog** — Originally a sun god and ancestral deity in Slavic lore; the phrase "grandchildren of Dazhbog" referred to the people of Russia. After Christianization he was ranked as a demonic Lord of the underworld, described as a lame and ugly shepherd of wolves with a silver beard.

**Boruta** ("Pine tree") — Originally a god of hunting and the woods in ancient Slavic mythology; after Christianization he was given antlers, surrounded by bears and wolves, and recast as a terrestrial devil. Tricked into trying to build a church (thinking it was a tavern), he still left clawed handmarks on a tower at Tum in modern Poland. Modern folklore places him in the ruins of Lenezyca Castle.

**Bies** — In Slavic mythology, originally an evil spirit; later demonized further and associated with the forces of darkness during Christianization.

**Chort** — In Slavic demonology, a demon born the son of a god of winter, closely associated with the Devil.

**Pscipolnitsa** ("Lady Midday") — The personification of sunstroke in Slavic demonology; appears as a young woman dressed in white during the hottest part of summer days, causing confusion, illness, and death in those she approaches.

**Psoglav** ("Doghead") — A demonic creature from Slavic lore described as looking like a man with horse legs, a dog's head, a mouth filled with iron teeth, and one eye on its forehead. An opportunist, it excavates graves and consumes the dead. Said to live in caves with gemstones throughout Bosnia and Montenegro.

**Rusalki** — Demonic nature spirits from Slavic lore; the name loosely translates to mean "mermaid." They are seen as demons of the dualistic quality of nature, created when a woman dies an unnatural death — by suicide, drowning, or murder. The Slavic and East European Folklore Association Journal records multiple regional variants of this tradition.

**Koschei the Deathless** — In Russian folklore, a greedy demonic being described as having a skull for a head and a fleshless body with a pulsing intensity. His name in Russian means "immortal." A complex figure, he preys on everything he sees.

**Nari** — A species of demonic being from Slavic tradition recorded by Bane.

**Skrzak** — In Polish and Wendish mythology, a type of demonic familiar.
