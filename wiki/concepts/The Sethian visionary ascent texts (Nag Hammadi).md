## Definition from Source

Four closely related Sethian tractates — the **Three Steles of Seth** (Codex VII), **Zostrianos** (Codex VIII), **Marsanes** (Codex X), and **Allogenes** (Codex XI) — share a common pattern: a visionary (Seth, Zostrianos, Marsanes, or Allogenes "the Stranger") is carried upward through successive levels of the divine world, receives baptisms or revelations at each station, and returns to record the experience for the "living elect." All four texts are heavily Platonizing (scholars call them the "Platonizing Sethian" corpus) and many are severely damaged by lacunae.

## Description from Source

**Shared cosmological map.** All four texts navigate the same aeon-hierarchy, ascending from the lowest divine levels upward:

- **Autogenes** ("Self-Begotten One") — the fourth / lowest of the great divine aeons; the realm of the four Lights (Armozel, Oroiael, Daveithe, Eleleth) and of Seth / Adamas
- **Protophanes** ("First-Appearing") — the noetic realm of the male perfect Mind; the second major aeon
- **Kalyptos** ("Hidden One") — the "unborn" or "concealed" aeon; higher than Protophanes, itself derived from Barbelo
- **Barbelo** — the First Thought, the aeon that encompasses and precedes all the above (also the subject of [[Trimorphic Protennoia: the threefold descent of the First Thought (Nag Hammadi)]])
- **The Triple-Powered Invisible Spirit / the Unknowable One** — the absolute summit; utterly beyond predication

The direction of travel is always upward; the direction of grace is always downward; and — as the *Three Steles* puts it — "the way of ascent is the way of descent."

### The Three Steles of Seth (Codex VII)

The simplest of the four: three liturgical **hymns of praise** sung by Seth (and through him by his "immovable race") addressed successively to Geradamas / Pigeradamas (the first perfect man, the "eye of the Autogenes"), to Barbelo, and to the utterly unknowable "pre-existent One." The text is not a narrative of ascent but a script for it — a liturgy that enacts the soul's return through blessing each divine level in turn. The Third Stele addresses the summit:

> "We bless thee, non-being, existence which is before existences, first being which is before beings, Father of divinity and life, creator of mind, giver of good, giver of blessedness!"

The colophon instructs: "After the silence, they descend from the third. They bless the second; after these the first. The way of ascent is the way of descent." The stele-form signals that these were living liturgical texts, not merely speculative theology.

### Zostrianos (Codex VIII)

The longest and most damaged of the four (the Neoplatonist Porphyry mentions that "Zostrianos" was read in Plotinus's seminar circle c. 265 CE). The prophet Zostrianos, on the verge of suicide in the desert, is rescued by "the angel of the knowledge of eternal light" and carried upward on a "great light-cloud," escaping the "thirteen aeons" of the material world. He is then **baptized repeatedly** — four times in living water corresponding to the four levels (Exile, Repentance, Autogenes, Protophanes / Kalyptos), and again at the fifth aeon — each baptism transforming him into a progressively more divine being:

> "I was baptized in the [name of] the divine Autogenes by those powers which are [upon] living waters, Michar and Micheus... I became a root-seeing angel and stood upon the first aeon which is the fourth."

The text is punctuated by dense philosophical inquiry into the structure of the aeons, the nature of soul-types, and the ontological distinctions among Existence, Blessedness, and Life. Three corresponding "waters" — the water of Life (Autogenes), the water of Blessedness (Protophanes), and the water of Existence (Kalyptos) — serve as the ritual medium of transformation. The text concludes with Zostrianos descending to write three tablets of revelation for the living elect and preaching to the "holy seed of Seth" in the world. Heavy lacunae afflict many pages.

### Marsanes (Codex X)

Severely damaged; many pages are almost entirely unrecoverable. Marsanes describes thirteen seals corresponding to levels of reality from the material to the "Silent One" beyond the Triple-Powered. He records having ascended through all of them and returned:

> "I have deliberated, and have attained to the boundary of the sense-perceptible world. I have come to know part by part the entire place of the incorporeal being, and I have come to know the intelligible world."

The text contains unusual material on the structure of the soul, the shapes of letters and vowels (a kind of Gnostic phonology), and the role of the zodiac, suggesting a wider synthesis of Platonism, Pythagoreanism, and Sethian theology. The Third Power of the Three-Powered speaks directly to Marsanes and conducts him to Barbelo. Despite the lacunae, the "thirteen seals" structure and the identification of the summit as a "Silent One" who "worked from silence" are clear.

---

### Allogenes (Codex XI)

"Allogenes" means "the Stranger" or "of another race." The text is addressed to his son Messos and describes a two-stage revelation: first, over a hundred years, from the "all-glorious One, Youel" (a feminine revealer figure), and then from "holy powers" / "Luminaries" of the Barbelo aeon. Youel reveals the nature of the Triple-Powered Invisible Spirit and teaches Allogenes how to approach the Unknowable One:

> "O Allogenes, in an unknowing knowledge you know that the Triple-Powered One exists before the glories. They do not exist among those who exist. They do not exist together with those who exist nor those who truly exist. Rather, all these exist as divinity and blessedness and existence, and as nonsubstantiality and non-being existence."

The method of approach is apophatic (negative theology) pushed to its extreme: "Do not know him, for it is impossible; but if by means of an enlightened thought you should know him, be ignorant of him." After the hundred years, Allogenes is "taken by the eternal Light out of the garment that was upon me, and taken up to a holy place" where he sees Autogenes, Protophanes-Harmedon, Kalyptos, the Aeon of Barbelo, and the "spiritual, invisible Triple-Powered One, the Universal One that is higher than perfect." He then writes the book and commands Messos to seal it on a mountain.

---

### Common themes

All four texts share: the **identification of gnosis with ascent**; **serial baptism or initiation** as the mechanism of transformation; the **unknowability of the summit** (the One cannot be predicated, grasped, or even correctly named); the **authority of Seth's race** as the elect who alone can travel these paths; and a **return-and-record** obligation — the visionary must come back and preserve the revelation for "those who will come after."

These texts represent the most philosophically sophisticated stratum of Sethian Gnosticism, heavily influenced by Middle Platonism and anticipating Neoplatonic thought. Their closeness to the Plotinian circle (Porphyry explicitly names "Zostrianos" and "Allogenes" as texts his group debated and refuted) makes them a rare bridge between Gnostic religion and Greek philosophy.
