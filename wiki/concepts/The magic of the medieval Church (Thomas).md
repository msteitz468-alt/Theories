## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas's term for the pre-Reformation English Church's dual character as both a devotional institution and, in popular practice, a reservoir of supernatural power to be tapped for practical, earthly ends. He argues that the medieval Church encouraged and profited from beliefs that its sacraments, relics, saints, blessings, and prayers worked automatically (or near-automatically) to cure disease, protect against fire and storm, secure fertility, detect thieves, and ward off misfortune — beliefs which theologians only ambivalently distinguished from "superstition." Thomas frames this as a general feature of "primitive religion," of which early Christianity was no exception, and draws explicit comparisons to modern African converts to Christianity who likewise expect the new religion to deliver supernatural power.

## Key Claims
- The Church's own doctrine of the sacraments working *ex opere operato* (automatically, regardless of the priest's moral worth) gave medieval Christianity "an apparently magical character."
- Saints were venerated as specialist wonder-workers, each with an assigned affliction or occupation, and their shrines, relics, and images were credited with power to cure and to curse.
- Holy water, holy bread, the agnus dei, rosaries, and the sign of the cross functioned in popular use as protective talismans against fire, lightning, plague, and evil spirits.
- The Mass and consecrated Host were widely believed to carry a detachable magical potency usable for healing, love-charms, fertility, and even maleficence.
- The Church's own missionary and pastoral practice — assimilating pagan wells, festivals, and fertility rites into a Christian idiom — helped keep alive expectations of automatic supernatural benefit.
- The concept is Thomas's diagnostic baseline: the Reformation's subsequent assault (see [[The Reformation's assault on ecclesiastical magic (Thomas)]]) is defined against precisely this magical inheritance.
