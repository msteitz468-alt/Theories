## Definition from Source

The Round Table Group is the name Quigley gives to the network that grew out of the secret society organized by Cecil Rhodes and W. T. Stead on February 5, 1891, and later developed by Alfred Milner. Quigley describes a concentric structure: Rhodes as leader; an executive committee (Stead, Brett/Lord Esher, Milner); a "Circle of Initiates" (including Lord Rothschild, Lord Balfour, Lord Grey, and others); and an outer "Association of Helpers" — "later organized by Milner as the Round Table organization."

## Characterization in the Source

- Funded after Rhodes's death (1902) by his fortune and by supporters such as Alfred Beit and Sir Abe Bailey.
- Milner recruited young Oxford men ("Milner's Kindergarten") who won "influential posts in government and international finance" and "became the dominant influence in British imperial and foreign affairs up to 1939."
- In 1909–1913, the group "organized semisecret groups, known as Round Table Groups, in the chief British dependencies and the United States," coordinated by the journal *The Round Table* (1910).
- After WWI it founded the [[Royal Institute of International Affairs]] (1919) and, in the US, the [[Council on Foreign Relations and the Round Table network|Council on Foreign Relations]].
- Quigley: "The power and influence of this Rhodes-Milner group in British imperial affairs and in foreign policy since 1889, although not widely recognized, can hardly be exaggerated."

Quigley writes of this as an informed, broadly sympathetic insider — which is why hostile authors ([[Tragedy and Hope (Quigley)|Quigley]] aside, Griffin and Mullins) cite him as confirmation.
