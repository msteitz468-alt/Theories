# 2 Enoch: the ascent through the ten heavens and the secrets of creation

## Overview from Source

**2 Enoch** (also called *Slavonic Enoch* or *The Book of the Secrets of Enoch*) is a pseudepigraphical text preserved only in Old Slavonic manuscripts dating from the 13th century AD onward, translated from a Greek original that itself likely derived from a Hebrew or Aramaic source. The scholarly consensus places the core composition in the latter half of the first century AD, making it significantly later than [[The Watchers, their fall, and the forbidden teachings (Book of Enoch)|1 Enoch]] (c. 300 BC). The longer version contains evident Christian and Gnostic interpolations; the shorter version is regarded as closer to the original Jewish text.

The governing narrative device of 2 Enoch is the **progressive ascent of Enoch through ten heavens**, in contrast to 1 Enoch's simpler heavenly journey. Each heaven discloses a different order of angels, cosmic phenomena, or moral landscape. At the apex (the tenth heaven, called *Aravoth*), Enoch encounters God directly, is transformed into an angelic being, and receives a first-person dictation of the secrets of creation. He is then returned to earth for thirty days to instruct his sons before his permanent ascension.

*(The 8th–10th heavens are considered later additions to the original text by the book's own scholarly notes.)*

## Enoch's Transfiguration and the Heavenly Books

At the 10th heaven, God commands Michael to remove Enoch's earthly garments and anoint him with divine ointment, transforming him into one of the glorious angelic ones. The archangel **Pravuil** (whose knowledge surpasses all other archangels) delivers to Enoch the heavenly record books and a reed for quick-writing. Enoch writes 366 books in 30 days and 30 nights, recording all the works of heaven and earth, the souls of all humans (said to be prepared before the world's formation), and every detail of creation. This parallels the Enoch of 1 Enoch as heavenly scribe but with more explicit cosmological content.

## God's First-Person Account of Creation (Chapters 24–33)

The most theologically dense section is a direct monologue by God to Enoch, dictating the secrets of creation. Key doctrines:

- **Creation from nothing / from invisible to visible**: "I created all things from non-being. I created the visible, physical things from the invisible, spiritual world." (Ch. 24)
- **The emanation of Adoil**: A being named *Adoil* (carrying "a belly of great light") is commanded to "become undone," releasing the primordial light — a passage showing strong Gnostic influence, paralleling emanationist cosmologies.
- **The fall of a leading angel**: Before the creation of Adam, an angel conceived the thought of placing his throne above the clouds to equal God's power; God cast him out into the abyss. (Ch. 29 — an explicit parallel to the Isaiah 14 / Lucifer tradition.)
- **Creation of Adam from seven components** (Ch. 30): flesh from the earth; blood from dew; eyes from the sun; bones from stone; intelligence from the swiftness of angels and clouds; veins and hair from the grass of the earth; soul from God's breath and the wind. Adam is also assigned seven natures corresponding to his seven components.
- **Adam's name from four cardinal directions**: The four letters of Adam's name derive from East, West, South, and North — a cosmological etymology.
- **The fall of Eve and Adam**: The devil (formerly an angel cast from heaven, renamed Satan) entered and seduced Eve. God cursed man's evil results, not man himself or the earth. Adam spent "five and a half hours in paradise" — a figure tied elsewhere to 5,500 years of punishment.
- **The Sabbath and the eighth-day prophecy** (Ch. 33): The seventh thousand years is a Sabbath rest; at the beginning of the eighth thousand, time ends and an endless age begins — a millennial/eschatological schema.

## Enoch's Ethical Teaching (the "Thirty-Day Return")

Given thirty days on earth before his final ascent, Enoch delivers extensive moral instruction to his sons (Chs. 39–66). Key themes: no intercessor can pray away another's sins; predestination of souls before creation; judgment by precise moral weight; care for the poor and naked; prohibition on oaths; a notable absence of redemption or mercy — a feature scholars use to identify the core Jewish (pre-Christian) stratum of the text.

## Relationship to 1 Enoch

2 Enoch expands on 1 Enoch's Watchers/Grigori tradition (locating them in the 5th heaven, still mourning) but shifts emphasis from prophetic/apocalyptic drama to cosmological revelation and ethical instruction. The ten-heaven schema, the first-person creation account, and the Melchizedek appendix are unique to 2 Enoch. The Gnostic creation passages (Adoil, Archas) have no parallel in 1 Enoch. 2 Enoch uses the same 365-year lifespan for Enoch as the Genesis source text and shares the solar-calendar interest of 1 Enoch's Astronomical Book, but casts this in a different frame.
