## Pico della Mirandola and the birth of Christian Cabala from Source

Yates argues in Chapters V and VI of Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (1964) that [[Pico della Mirandola (1463–1494)]] was the pivotal figure who created Christian Cabala by fusing [[Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499)]]'s Hermetic magia naturalis with Jewish Kabbalistic magic, producing the first systematic Hermetic-Cabalist synthesis in Western thought.

### The Double Magic of Pico

Yates shows that Pico inherited from Ficino an enthusiasm for natural magic (magia naturalis) — the use of sympathetic links between earth and heaven — but found it fundamentally weak. In his fifteen magical Conclusiones (1486), Pico declared that "No magical operation can have any efficacy unless the work of Cabala, explicit or implicit, is annexed to it." Ficino's natural magic, reaching only as far as the planets through spiritus, was insufficient. What Pico added was practical Cabala: magic operating through the Hebrew sacred language, the divine names, the seventy-two angelic names, the ten Sephiroth, and letter-combinatory techniques — a spiritual magic ascending beyond the stars into the supercelestial world of divine and angelic powers.

### The Hermetism-Cabalism Marriage

Yates calls Pico "the instigator and founder" of the Hermetic-Cabalist union, "of most far-reaching importance." He perceived structural parallels between the two systems: the Hermetic Asclepius (on man the great miracle) matched the Cabalist mystical scheme; the ten Powers of Corpus Hermeticum XIII that drive out the twelve Punishments corresponded to the ten Sephiroth driving out their evil opposites. Both were gnostic in origin, and Pico saw them as corroborating the same deep truths — particularly about the creative Word, the Logos, spoken in both Hermetic and Cabalist creation accounts.

### The Oration on the Dignity of Man

The Oration — intended to open the great debate on the Conclusiones — is described by Yates as "the great charter of Renaissance Magic." It opens with the Hermetic text "Magnum, o Asclepi, miraculum est homo" (from the Asclepius), not from any patristic authority. Pico thereby grounded the Dignity of Man not on orthodox theology but on gnostic-Hermetic teaching: man is a divine being who, through his intellect, can become the operator, the magus who "marries earth to heaven." This Dignity rests on man as Magus, operator, not merely contemplator.

### Christian Cabala as Apologetics

Pico also used Cabala for Christian apologetics. His seventy-two Cabalist conclusions were introduced as "confirming the Christian religion from the foundations of Hebrew wisdom." He argued that no Hebrew Cabalist can deny that the name IESU, interpreted by Cabalistic methods, signifies the Son of God. His most controversial magical conclusion — condemned by Pope Innocent VIII — was that "there is no science which gives us more assurance of Christ's divinity than magic and the Cabala." Despite his condemnation and eventual submission, the tradition he founded flowed forward through Reuchlin's De arte cabalistica (1517) directly to the angel magic of Cornelius Agrippa.

### Pseudo-Dionysius and the Three Worlds

In Chapter VI, Yates shows how Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite provided the Christian theological bridge for the Ficino-Pico synthesis. Pico fused the Cabalist three worlds (elemental, celestial, supercelestial) with the Pseudo-Dionysian nine angelic hierarchies, "astrologising" the hierarchies by assigning the first triad to the supercelestial world, the second to the celestial world, and the third to the elemental. Practical Cabala then offered Pico a means to reach and operate with these angelic forces — denied to Ficino — through Hebrew divine names and angelic invocations.
