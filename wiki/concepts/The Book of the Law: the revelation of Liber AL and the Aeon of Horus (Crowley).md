## Definition from Source

Liber AL vel Legis, also called The Book of the Law, presents itself as a scripture dictated to [[Aleister Crowley]] by a praeternatural intelligence named **Aiwass** in Cairo in April 1904. The text frames this event as the inauguration of a new aeon — the **Aeon of Horus**, the Crowned and Conquering Child — superseding the preceding Aeons of Isis (matriarchal nature-religion) and Osiris (the dying-and-rising-god era, encompassing Judaism, Christianity, and Islam). The book consists of three chapters, each spoken by one of the three deities of the Thelemic cosmos.

## Description from Source

### The Reception in Cairo (1904)

Chapter I, verse 7 identifies the revelatory source: "Behold! it is revealed by Aiwass the minister of Hoor-paar-kraat." The text is addressed to the prophet — identified as Ankh-af-na-khonsu and as "the Beast" — through a three-day dictation (the feast for "the three days of the writing of the Book of the Law" is mentioned in II:38). The scribe is explicitly forbidden from altering the text: "My scribe Ankh-af-na-khonsu, the priest of the princes, shall not in one letter change this book" (I:36), and "Change not as much as the style of a letter" (I:54).

### Proclamation of the Aeon of Horus

Chapter I, verse 49 announces the transition of aeons: "Ra-Hoor-Khuit hath taken his seat in the East at the Equinox of the Gods; and let Asar be with Isa, who also are one. But they are not of me." The names Asar (Osiris) and Isa (Isis) are explicitly set aside; the enthroned power is Ra-Hoor-Khuit, the hawk-headed war-god who is the governing deity of the new era. Chapter III, verse 46 has Ra-Hoor-Khuit declare himself "the warrior Lord of the Forties."

Chapter III, verse 34 also acknowledges the aeon is bounded: it shall last "until the fall of the Great Equinox; when Hrumachis shall arise and the double-wanded one assume my throne and place." This places the Aeon of Horus within a larger cyclical scheme.

### The Threefold Book

Each chapter is voiced by one member of the Thelemic triad: Chapter I by Nuit, Chapter II by Hadit, Chapter III by Ra-Hoor-Khuit. This structure is explicitly named in I:35: "This that thou writest is the threefold book of Law."

### The Law and its promulgation

The text commands universal distribution: "to each man and woman that thou meetest, were it but to dine or to drink at them, it is the Law to give" (III:39). It also insists: "the Law is for all" (I:34), making no restriction of audience.
