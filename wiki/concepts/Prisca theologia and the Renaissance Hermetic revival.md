## Definition / From Source

Per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], the *prisca theologia* ("ancient theology") — also called *prisca philosophia* or *philosophia perennis* — was a Renaissance conviction that a single, primordial religion lay behind all later faiths. The idea held that this original wisdom could be found preserved, above all, in Egypt; since Hermes Trismegistus was regarded as the supreme sage of ancient Egypt, his collected writings (the Corpus Hermeticum) were taken as the living record of that lost ur-religion.

The concept crystallised when Marsilio Ficino translated the Corpus Hermeticum into Latin in 1463, on the urgent commission of Cosimo de' Medici. The translation sent "seismic shockwaves throughout the academic community in Florence and beyond," and Ficino himself concluded that a single, magical current had always flowed beneath humanity's otherwise irreconcilable belief systems — linking Zoroaster, Hermes Trismegistus, Orpheus, Pythagoras, Plato, St Paul, and St Augustine in one unbroken sacerdotal line. This was the *prisca theologia* as Ficino articulated it, and it supplied the spiritual framework for the entire Renaissance Hermetic revival.

As [[Frances Yates]] demonstrated across her studies of the 1960s–70s, the Renaissance was "largely motivated and driven by" this "occult philosophy" — a blend of Neoplatonism, Christianised Cabala, and above all Hermeticism. The book argues that Hermeticism was "by far the most important strand" of the three, and that it "effectively created the Renaissance," whose defining motto was Hermes' adage *Magnum miraculum est homo* ("Man is a great miracle").

Key features of the *prisca theologia* as presented in the source:

- **Antiquity as authority.** The Hermetica were believed to pre-date Moses and Plato. Their rediscovery excited Renaissance scholars precisely because they were thought to preserve pristine divine wisdom, uncorrupted by later religious institutions.
- **Compatibility with Christianity (contested).** Many Catholic thinkers — including, astonishingly, Pope Alexander VI — held that Hermeticism could be reconciled with Christianity, since passages in the Hermetica seemed to foreshadow Christ. The Borgia pope commissioned Hermetic frescoes for his Vatican apartments and wrote a personal fan letter to the Hermeticist Giovanni Pico della Mirandola.
- **Giordano Bruno's radicalism.** [[Giordano Bruno]] rejected the softer synthesis and argued that the Egyptian Hermetic religion was *the* true religion, of which both Judaism and Christianity were later corruptions. This transformed the *prisca theologia* from a scholarly curiosity into a revolutionary programme.
- **Isaac Casaubon's blow (for Agent B).** The source notes that the Renaissance belief in the Hermetica's extreme antiquity was later devastated by Isaac Casaubon's 1614 re-dating of the texts to the early Christian era — described by historian Garth Fowden as "the watershed between Renaissance occultism and the scientific rationalism of the new age."
