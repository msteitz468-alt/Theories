## Definition / Characterization from Source

Richard Dolan's term for the post-WWII American structure of power — "the military and intelligence communities, as well as the worlds that feed upon them, such as defense contractors and other underground, nebulous entities." He argues the U.S. is "nominally a republic and free society, but in reality an empire and oligarchy," and that its security apparatus has five fundamental traits:

1. **Secrecy** — nearly everything significant (the Manhattan Project, biological weapons, Nazi-scientist importation, "terminal mind-control experiments," mail interception, coups, assassinations) was done outside public and even congressional knowledge.
2. **Wealth** — vast official and undocumented funds (CIA proprietary airlines, ties to organized crime/narcotics, old-money backing).
3. **Independence** — compartmentalized secrecy defeats civilian oversight; e.g. Eisenhower could not get a straight answer from, or control over, Strategic Air Command's nuclear war plan.
4. **Power** — secrecy + wealth + independence yield control over technology, sealed land, surveillance, wars, and elected officials.
5. **Duplicity** — deception as routine (the 1948 "war crisis," the fabricated "missile gap," the Gulf of Tonkin, and the UFO cover-up).

Dolan uses this framework to argue the UFO cover-up is "one secret among many" handled the same way — "secretly, with great deception, and with significant resources."
