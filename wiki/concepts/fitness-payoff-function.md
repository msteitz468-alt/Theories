## Definition from Source

A **fitness payoff function** is the central quantity of evolutionary game theory as Donald Hoffman deploys it in *[[The Case Against Reality (Why Evolution Hid the Truth from Our Eyes)]]*. It specifies the fitness consequences of a state of the world — but crucially, fitness payoffs depend not only on the true state of the world but **also on the organism, its state, its action, and its competition**.

This dependence is the linchpin of Hoffman's whole argument: because the *same* world state can yield wildly different payoffs for different organisms (feces are a payoff bonanza for a fly, none for a human; a hydrothermal vent nourishes the Pompeii worm but kills almost everything else), **seeing truth and seeing fitness are two distinct perceptual strategies, not one strategy seen in different lights**. They can compete, and one can drive the other extinct.

## Key Points

- Payoffs vary with the organism's **state**: a famished teen delights in the smell of pizza (high payoff); an hour and six slices later the same smell signals a dearth of fitness.
- A canonical fitness function is **non-monotonic** (bell-shaped): too little or too much of a resource (like oxygen) is fatal; an intermediate amount is best. This is exactly the case in which a fitness-tracking perception *hides* the truth about the resource's quantity (the "kritter/stuf" example).
- If payoffs happened to increase or decrease *monotonically* with a world structure, fitness-tracking perception would coincidentally also track truth — but such functions are vanishingly rare, which is why the [[Fitness-Beats-Truth (FBT) Theorem]] drives truth to extinction.
- Perception is shaped to report *differences* in payoffs rather than absolute payoffs (perceptual adaptation: rose-colored glasses, the waterfall illusion, Brian Marion's color-discrimination reward experiment).
- The "cloud of fitness payoffs" shrouds the thing-in-itself: "the sun-in-itself affects the cloud and, in consequence, my perceptual experience as of the sun, but my perceptual experience as of the sun does not describe the sun-in-itself."
