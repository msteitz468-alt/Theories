## Definition / Characterization from Source

The corporate community is Domhoff's term for the network of large corporations, banks, and financial firms that are interconnected through shared ownership, overlapping boards of directors, common use of the same legal, accounting, advertising, and public relations firms, and membership in the same overarching business associations. Domhoff defines it precisely: "the corporate community, which is defined as those people and corporations interconnected in a duality of persons and groups." This duality — persons sitting on multiple boards, and those boards linking corporations to one another — is what gives the network its coherence.

Empirically, the corporate community's backbone is the Fortune 500. A 2010 study found that "87.5% of the Fortune 500 were connected to each other" through shared directors. Adding the major overarching business associations — the National Association of Manufacturers, the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, the Conference Board, the Committee for Economic Development, the Business Council, and the Business Roundtable — brought "453 of the 506 organizations (89.5%) … part of one large network." The Business Roundtable and Business Council sit at the center of this network; together, their 271 members can reach two-fifths of the Fortune 500 directly or indirectly.

The corporate community's origins lie in colonial-era jointly owned textile and banking ventures in New England and New York, expanded into a national network during the great merger movement of 1895–1904, and has remained "relatively stable in its contours and membership ever since." It includes high-tech firms (Amazon, Apple, Google, Microsoft), defense contractors (Lockheed Martin, Boeing, Northrop Grumman), and agribusiness — all integrated into the same interlock structure despite occasional claims of independence.
