## Definition from Source

Per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], the **Casaubon redating** refers to Isaac Casaubon's 1614 philological argument — published in *De rebus sacris et ecclesiasticis* (*Of Things Holy and Ecclesiastical*) — that the *Corpus Hermeticum* was not the primordial Egyptian wisdom everyone had assumed since Marsilio Ficino's 1463 translation, but was rather composed in the early Christian era (second or third century CE). The authors describe this as one of two major blows dealt to Hermeticism in 1614 (the other being Giordano Bruno's execution in 1600, fourteen years earlier), and argue it ultimately did far more damage to the Hermetic movement than Casaubon intended — since he was actually trying to demolish the *Catholic* tradition of honouring Hermes as a pagan prophet of Christ, not to attack Hermeticism per se.

## Description from Source

### Isaac Casaubon (1559–1614)

Casaubon was born in Geneva to Huguenot refugee parents and became "the most learned man in Europe," specialising in classical languages. After a distinguished career in Switzerland and France under the patronage of Henri IV, the assassination of Henri in May 1610 forced him out of France. James I invited him to England to write a rebuttal of Cardinal Caesar Baronius's Catholic *Ecclesiastical Annals*, which had defended the view that Hermes Trismegistus was a genuine pagan prophet of Christ. Casaubon died in July 1614, completing only the first volume of his projected multi-volume reply.

### The Argument

Casaubon began by observing that no ancient author — prior to early Christian writers such as Lactantius and Augustine — even mentioned Hermes Trismegistus. He then compared the Hermetic texts with earlier works and concluded that, far from pre-dating Plato and the New Testament, the *Hermetica* had drawn upon them — for instance, that passages in the *Pimander* which Hermeticists celebrated as prefiguring John's Gospel were actually derived from it. Returning to the original Greek (using a 1554 printed edition, his heavily annotated copy now in the British Museum), he demonstrated that the language belongs to the early centuries CE, not to classical antiquity. He therefore concluded that the texts were later pious forgeries, composed to build a bridge from paganism to Christianity — though he accepted that a real person once known as Thrice-Great Hermes had existed in ancient Egypt.

### Immediate Consequences

- For Garth Fowden, cited by the authors, Casaubon's critique is "the watershed between Renaissance occultism and the scientific rationalism of the new age."
- [[Frances Yates]] called the Renaissance belief in the antiquity of the *Corpus Hermeticum* "the great Egyptian illusion."
- The text's impact was slow: buried in an abstruse scholarly work, it took ten years before it was used against Hermeticists (first by the Jesuit Marin Mersenne in 1623), and fifty years to become widely known and accepted.
- Defenders such as Robert Fludd and Michael Maier, both of whom moved in the same intellectual circles as Casaubon, simply ignored it.

### The Authors' Counter-Assessment

Picknett & Prince argue that Casaubon was substantially wrong in the ways that mattered most to Hermeticists:

1. The Greek dating proves composition in Greek, not the origin of the ideas. The Cambridge Platonists had already observed that translated or preserved Egyptian wisdom could have been committed to Greek writing at any point during Hellenic rule.
2. Modern scholarship has roundly rejected Casaubon's central claim that the *Hermetica* derived from the New Testament. Any overlap reflects shared sources in the Hellenic-Jewish-Egyptian intellectual milieu of Alexandria, not Christian dependence.
3. Evidence assembled in the twentieth century — especially Garth Fowden's *The Egyptian Hermes* (1986) and Karl Luckert's *Egyptian Light and Hebrew Fire* (1991) — demonstrates that the cosmology and theology of the *Hermetica* are authentically Egyptian, traceable to Heliopolitan theology expressed in the Pyramid Texts (c.2500–2200 BCE). An inscription from c.160 BCE names "Thoth, the three times great," providing the earliest-known prototype of "Thrice-Great Hermes."
4. The authors conclude: "Isaac Casaubon — whose work is still trotted out to trash the value of the Hermetica — was just plain wrong."

### Long-Term Cultural Effect

The most damaging consequence of the redating was not intellectual but political: it robbed Hermeticists of the claim — whipped up especially by [[Giordano Bruno]] — that their movement represented a return to the most ancient religion of all, the *prisca theologia*. Combined with the military disaster of the Thirty Years War and the philosophical rise of Cartesian mechanism (itself partly a Catholic reaction against Rosicrucianism), Casaubon's critique helped drive Hermeticism underground, where it persisted in secret societies and esoteric lodges rather than mainstream intellectual life. The authors argue this suppression is why the Hermetic roots of the Scientific Revolution have been systematically underacknowledged.
