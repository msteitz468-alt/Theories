## Definition from Source

Shermer's "Easy Answer to the Hard Question": **smart people believe weird things because they are
skilled at defending beliefs they arrived at for non-smart reasons.** Intelligence does not protect
against weird beliefs — it enables their more sophisticated defence.

## Description from Source

### The Hard Question and the Easy Answer

In Chapter 18, Shermer poses what he calls the Hard Question: why do intelligent, educated people
hold beliefs that most people in the relevant field of expertise regard as fringe, implausible, or
evidentially unsupported? The standard skeptical assumption — that critical thinking, education,
and high IQ insulate against pseudoscience — turns out to be empirically weak and sociologically
naive.

Shermer's Easy Answer (stated twice for emphasis): "Smart people believe weird things because they
are skilled at defending beliefs they arrived at for non-smart reasons."

The mechanism: most people most of the time come to their beliefs for reasons that have little to
do with "empirical evidence and logical reasoning" — genetic predispositions, parental influences,
sibling and peer pressures, educational experiences, emotional inclinations, and cultural context.
Facts then arrive "through the colored filters of the theories, hypotheses, hunches, biases, and
prejudices we have accumulated through our lifetime." We sort the data for what confirms what we
already believe, and rationalize away what contradicts it. Smart people, with greater verbal and
analytical facility, do this *better* — they construct more airtight, more persuasive-sounding
defences of positions they reached for non-smart reasons.

### Intelligence is orthogonal to belief

The research literature shows that the relationship between intelligence (IQ, education,
credentials) and belief is mixed and often negligible:

- Some studies find modest negative correlations between IQ and superstition (Georgia high
  schoolers, 1974; Alcock & Otis, 1980); but the New Age movement has made paranormal belief
  popular precisely among "those with higher intelligence, higher socioeconomic status, and higher
  educational levels" (Vyse, 1997).
- Gender is related to *what* people believe, not *whether* they believe: UFO/creationism
  conferences are overwhelmingly male; psychic-medium events are overwhelmingly female. The power
  of belief itself is gender-neutral.
- Education correlates negatively with some superstitions but positively with others (belief in
  mind-body healing increases with education). Science education in particular shows almost no
  relationship to paranormal scepticism (Walker, Hoekstra & Vogl, 2001): "Students were taught
  what to think but not how to think."
- Intelligence is, Shermer argues, **orthogonal** — statistically independent — from the content
  of one's beliefs. Once you are above a threshold IQ (~125) in any professional domain, other
  variables (openness to experience, locus of control, personality, cultural context) determine
  belief, not raw intelligence.

Shermer's analogy: magicians report that rooms full of scientists and Mensa members are *easier*
to fool, not harder, because their confidence in their own analytical ability makes them fall more
completely for misdirection.

### The two central cognitive biases smart people exploit

**Intellectual Attribution Bias.** In a survey conducted with Frank Sulloway, subjects were asked
why *they* believe in God, and why *other people* believe in God. They attributed their own belief
to rational reasons (design argument, personal experience of God) nearly nine times more often than
they attributed *others'* belief to rational reasons — for others, they selected emotional reasons
(comfort, upbringing). This "intellectual attribution bias" — "we consider our own actions as
being rationally motivated, whereas we see those of others as more emotionally driven" — is
disproportionately available to smart people, who have more elaborate intellectual justifications
ready to hand.

**Confirmation Bias.** Shermer cites Raymond Nickerson's (1998) comprehensive review: if a single
cognitive flaw accounts for "a significant fraction of the disputes, altercations, and
misunderstandings that occur among individuals, groups, and nations," it is the tendency to seek
or interpret evidence favouring already existing beliefs and to ignore or reinterpret contradictory
evidence. Smart people use confirmation bias not merely to accept friendly evidence but to generate
more sophisticated reasons to dismiss hostile evidence — a skill built by the same education and
analytical practice that produces academic achievement. David Perkins' research (1981) found a
*positive* relationship between intelligence and the ability to *justify* beliefs, and a *negative*
relationship between intelligence and openness to considering alternative positions.

### Case studies: the academy endorses weird beliefs

Shermer's examples of smart people with weird beliefs are deliberately drawn from major research
universities and elite careers:

- **Frank Tipler** (Tulane, global general relativist, peer of Hawking and Penrose): argues in
  *The Physics of Immortality* that God and bodily resurrection are provable from quantum mechanics
  and general relativity; Kip Thorne found each step sound but the leaps between steps unfounded.
- **Jodi Dean** (Columbia Ph.D., Cornell University Press): refuses in radio interview to say
  whether she believes alien abduction accounts are real — lending credibility without accountability.
- **David Jacobs** (Temple University history professor, Ph.D. Wisconsin): argues aliens are
  genuinely abducting humans; admits the evidence is "anecdotal and thus nonfalsifiable" but
  believes anyway.
- **Courtney Brown** (Emory University political science): grounds alien belief in "Scientific
  Remote Viewing" and publishes bestselling books through mainstream trade publishers; Emory
  required him to suppress the university affiliation when discussing aliens publicly.
- **John Mack** (Harvard Medical School, Pulitzer Prize winner): interviewed 100+ "abductees"
  and concluded that the experiences constitute evidence of genuine alien intelligence; Harvard
  investigated but he retained tenure under academic freedom.

The pattern: PhD credentials are prominently displayed precisely because the claims are weird.
Shermer's formula — "smart man endorses weird belief" — is the explicit marketing strategy of
fringe literature seeking mainstream legitimacy.

### Domain specificity

Another mechanism: smart people may be smart in *one* domain and naive in another. Biologist Barry
Fell's move into pre-Columbian archaeology produced claims (Egyptians, Phoenicians, Romans in
ancient America) that archaeologists had already considered and rejected. He was not *less*
intelligent than the archaeologists — he was simply unaware of the knowledge infrastructure that
makes their field a community of corrective peers. Similarly, Tipler's theology lacks the communal
correction that his physics has.

### Why this thesis matters beyond weird beliefs

Shermer emphasises that this mechanism is not limited to the fringe. The confirmation bias and
intellectual attribution bias "operate to confirm and justify" any belief — political, moral,
scientific, conspiratorial — by making the holder construct more and more sophisticated defences
of positions reached for emotional, cultural, or circumstantial reasons. "And the more intelligent
the better."

## What this counters in the wiki

- **[[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]** and **[[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]]**: The "smart people" thesis directly explains why elaborate, internally consistent conspiracy architectures attract educated adherents — the very sophistication of the system (internal coherence, pseudo-documentation, grand explanatory sweep) mimics the markers of intelligence that the intellectual attribution bias rewards. Readers who adopt the framework then use it with the confirmation bias to reinforce and extend it.
- **[[Communion (Strieber)]]**: Whitley Strieber is an intelligent, published literary author. Shermer's framework does not imply Strieber is *stupid*; it implies that his intelligence was available to defend and elaborate an experience reached via non-rational channels (hypnagogia, traumatic memory, cultural priming), which is exactly the pattern Shermer identifies.
- **[[The Demon-Haunted World (Sagan)]]**: Companion text. Sagan's "Baloney Detection Kit" is the corrective toolkit that Shermer's diagnosis calls for — the formalised, teachable habits that counteract the intellectual attribution and confirmation biases. Where Shermer diagnoses, Sagan prescribes.
- **[[What techniques of mass social control do the sources allege?]]**: The mechanism Shermer describes — whereby intelligent actors construct persuasive intellectual frameworks that justify emotionally-pre-formed conclusions — is exactly how Shermer would account for the persuasive force of ideological systems, including those alleged by conspiracy sources to function as social control.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer explicitly notes that intelligence correlates with slightly *lower* levels of some
superstitions (possession by the devil, astrology, haunted houses) even if it does not protect
against paranormal belief at large. He also acknowledges that some smart-people weird beliefs turn
out to be correct — the history of science includes many initially fringe ideas that became
orthodox. His thesis is not that smart people's weird beliefs are always wrong, but that their
intelligence is doing the rationalisation work *after the fact*, not the epistemic grounding work
*before the fact*.
