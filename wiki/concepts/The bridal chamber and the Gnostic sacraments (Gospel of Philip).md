## Definition from Source

The **Gospel of Philip** (Codex II of [[The Nag Hammadi Library]]) presents a five-fold sacramental system. The highest and most secret of the five is the **bridal chamber** — a mystery of spiritual union that transcends all others. The text treats sacraments not merely as external rites but as participatory encounters with truth through "types and images," culminating in a union that renders the initiate invisible to the archons and incapable of being detained.

## Description from Source

### The Five Sacraments

The Gospel of Philip states plainly:

> "The Lord did everything in a mystery, a baptism and a chrism and a eucharist and a redemption and a bridal chamber."
> — Gospel of Philip, Codex II

These five are arranged in ascending order of depth and hiddenness. The text maps them onto the three sacred buildings of Jerusalem: "Baptism is 'the Holy' building. Redemption is the 'Holy of the Holy'. 'The Holy of the Holies' is the bridal chamber."

### The Chrism — Superior to Baptism

Philip places special emphasis on **chrism** (anointing with oil), arguing it is actually superior to baptism:

> "The chrism is superior to baptism, for it is from the word 'Chrism' that we have been called 'Christians,' certainly not because of the word 'baptism' … For the Father anointed the Son, and the Son anointed the apostles, and the apostles anointed us. He who has been anointed possesses everything. He possesses the resurrection, the light, the cross, the Holy Spirit. The Father gave him this in the bridal chamber; he merely accepted (the gift)."

The text adds: "It is from water and fire and light that the son of the bridal chamber came into being. The fire is the chrism, the light is the fire." The chrism mediates the light that is also the fire — a chain linking the Father through Christ to the initiate.

### The Bridal Chamber — The Hidden Holy of Holies

The bridal chamber is the most guarded mystery:

> "The bridal chamber, however, remains hidden. It is the Holy in the Holy … the mysteries of truth are revealed, though in type and image."

Admission is exclusive: "A bridal chamber is not for the animals, nor is it for the slaves, nor for defiled women; but it is for free men and virgins."

The union accomplished there is cosmic in scope. The text ties Adam and Eve's original unity — and their fatal separation — directly to the bridal chamber's purpose:

> "If the woman had not separated from the man, she should not die with the man. His separation became the beginning of death. Because of this, Christ came to repair the separation, which was from the beginning, and again unite the two, and to give life to those who died as a result of the separation, and unite them. But the woman is united to her husband in the bridal chamber."

And: "those who have united in the bridal chamber will no longer be separated."

### Protection from the Archons

Entry into the bridal chamber confers a kind of invisibility to the powers:

> "The powers do not see those who are clothed in the perfect light, and consequently are not able to detain them. One will clothe himself in this light sacramentally in the union."

And: "He who will receive that light will not be seen, nor can he be detained. And none shall be able to torment a person like this, even while he dwells in the world."

In the **Dialogue of the Saviour** (Codex III), the Lord tells Judas: "It is you who will rule over them! But when you rid yourselves of jealousy, then you will clothe yourselves in light and enter the bridal chamber."

### Marriage as Cosmic Mystery

Philip insists the bridal chamber union is not carnal but spiritual:

> "marriage in the world is a mystery for those who have taken a wife. If there is a hidden quality to the marriage of defilement, how much more is the undefiled marriage a true mystery! It is not fleshly, but pure. It belongs not to desire, but to the will. It belongs not to the darkness or the night, but to the day and the light."

The **Exegesis on the Soul** (Codex II), included in the same codex, offers a parallel allegory: the wandering soul is a "bride" who has prostituted herself among the archons of matter; her redemption comes only when "the bridegroom came down to the bride" and she is united with her true consort in a spiritual bridal chamber.

### Permanence of the Light

Philip concludes with an eschatological statement: "If anyone becomes a son of the bridal chamber, he will receive the light. If anyone does not receive it while he is here, he will not be able to receive it in the other place." The initiation must occur in life, in the body, through the sacraments. The world then "becomes the Aeon (eternal realm), for the Aeon is fullness for him."
