## Definition from Source

The central cosmological argument of Part Two of *The Forbidden Universe*: that the accumulated evidence from physics — cosmic fine-tuning, the anthropic principle, quantum mechanics and the observer effect — points to a universe deliberately designed to produce intelligent life, and that this conclusion aligns with the ancient Hermetic worldview far better than with either scientific materialism or conventional theism.

Picknett & Prince trace the argument through two interrelated lines of evidence:

1. **The anthropic principle and fine-tuning.** The laws of physics are calibrated with such extraordinary precision that even tiny deviations would make life impossible — from the triple-alpha nuclear resonance that produces carbon (Fred Hoyle's "put-up job") to the vacuum energy balanced to 120 decimal places. The authors contend that the only scientifically credible alternatives are (a) intelligent design or (b) the multiverse, and that the multiverse hypothesis is untestable and therefore no more scientific than design itself.

2. **The participatory universe.** Drawing on John Archibald Wheeler's interpretation of quantum mechanics, the authors argue that conscious observers are not merely products of the universe but are necessary for the universe to exist — collapsing wave functions reaching back to the big bang itself. This "participatory anthropic principle" makes mind fundamental to the cosmos.

The authors explicitly connect both conclusions to the Hermetic tradition: the cosmos as an emanation of divine Mind, teeming with purposeful life, and human consciousness as a necessary participant in creation. They argue Paul Davies' identification of the "designer universe" as fitting the Hermetic Demiurge — a constrained creative force rather than the omnipotent biblical God — confirms this alignment.
