**Definition from Source**

*Rex Mundi* ("King of the World") is the Cathar term for the evil god or demiurge who created the material universe. In Cathar cosmology, there are two gods of roughly equal status: a "good" God of pure spirit (the principle of love) who is entirely disincarnate, and *Rex Mundi*, the usurper creator god who fashioned the material world and therefore rules it. Because material creation is intrinsically evil, all matter is tainted by Rex Mundi's sovereignty.

**Key Characteristics from Source**

- The Cathar cosmology is described as "cosmological dualism" — evil pervades the whole of reality, not just human action (as in Catholic "ethical dualism")
- The Roman Church was identified by Cathars with Rex Mundi: AMOR (love) inverted becomes ROMA; the Church's opulence, power, and the cross (emblem of Rex Mundi) all marked it as a manifestation of the "usurper god's" sovereignty
- The word "REX MUNDI" appears encoded in the parchments found by Saunière — eight small letters deliberately different from the surrounding text, spelling out two words immediately recognizable to anyone familiar with Cathar thought
- The Cathars regarded Jesus as either purely spiritual (incapable of crucifixion) or as a mortal prophet, not the divine Son of God
