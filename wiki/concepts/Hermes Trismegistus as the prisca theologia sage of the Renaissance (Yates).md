## The Concept from Source

[[Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (Yates)]] (Chapters I–II) sets out what Yates regards as the foundational error that animated the Renaissance Hermetic revival: the conviction that [[Hermes Trismegistus]] was a real Egyptian sage of supreme antiquity — older than Moses, far older than Plato — who had personally written the Corpus Hermeticum and the Asclepius.

### Origin and anatomy of the legend

Yates traces the figure back to the Greco-Egyptian god Thoth, scribe of the gods, whom the Greeks identified with Hermes and gave the epithet "Thrice Great" (Trismegistus). The Latin tradition, following Cicero's *De natura deorum*, held that the fifth Mercury had "given the Egyptians their laws and letters" and taken the Egyptian name of Theuth or Thoth. Under this name a vast Greek literature accumulated — astrological, alchemical, and magical treatises alongside the philosophical dialogues of the Corpus Hermeticum — all attributed to this revered name.

The actual Corpus Hermeticum and Asclepius, Yates stresses, were composed between roughly A.D. 100 and 300 by various unknown Greek authors. They breathe Platonic and Stoic philosophy mixed with Jewish and Persian influences, cast in a pseudo-Egyptian frame. Yet because they contain the language of deep piety, prophesy about God the Father and a "Son of God," and parallel Genesis in striking ways, they were wholly convincing to Renaissance readers as authentic Egyptian revelation.

### The Church Fathers as guarantors

Yates emphasises that the Renaissance did not adopt the Hermes legend through credulity alone. The Fathers of the Church had established it as authoritative fact. Lactantius (third century) placed Trismegistus among the Sibyls as a Gentile prophet of Christ, calling him "of great antiquity" and "most fully imbued with every kind of learning," noting that he spoke of God as Father and of a "Son of God." In *De ira Dei*, Lactantius stated that Trismegistus was "much more ancient than Plato and Pythagoras."

Augustine in *De Civitate Dei* gave Hermes a specific genealogy — three generations after a contemporary of Moses, yet "long before the sages and philosophers of Greece" — while condemning his praise of Egyptian idol-making. Yates shows how this dual legacy (Lactantius's praise, Augustine's condemnation of the magic) shaped Renaissance strategies for handling Hermes: most followers embraced Lactantius and side-stepped Augustine; Ficino adopted this approach, while Bruno would go further and reject Augustine's condemnation outright.

### The prisca theologia chain

The key doctrinal move, Yates argues, was Ficino's genealogy of wisdom articulated in the preface (*argumentum*) to his Latin *Pimander* (1463). Ficino wrote:

> He is called the first author of theology: he was succeeded by Orpheus, who came second amongst ancient theologians: Aglaophemus … was succeeded in theology by Pythagoras, whose disciple was Philolaus, the teacher of our Divine Plato. Hence there is one ancient theology (*prisca theologia*) … taking its origin in Mercurius and culminating in the Divine Plato.

Hermes Trismegistus thus stood at the fountainhead of all philosophy, with Plato as his ultimate heir. Yates notes that Ficino sometimes bracketed Zoroaster with Hermes as co-founder, following Gemistus Pletho. Regardless of variant, the logic was invariant: Plato's greatest insights were derived, through Pythagoras and Orpheus, from the still more ancient Egyptian Hermes.

### Importance for Renaissance magic

Yates argues that Hermes Trismegistus was probably the single most important figure in the Renaissance revival of magic. Egypt had always been associated with the strongest, most archaic magic; now the Corpus Hermeticum demonstrated that the Egyptian priest-sage of supreme authority was also a man of extraordinary piety, recognized by the Fathers. The discovery of the Corpus, Yates contends, rehabilitated the Asclepius — which described actual magical god-making — and with it rehabilitated learned magic as a respectable adjunct to Neoplatonic philosophy.

The historical error, Yates notes, "was to have amazing results." What the Renaissance believed to be primordial Egyptian revelation was in fact the religious philosophy of a weary Hellenistic world, seeking gnosis. But this misdating gave the Hermetica an authority that no correctly dated late-antique text could have commanded, and on that authority an entire tradition of magic, philosophy, and eventually science was built.
