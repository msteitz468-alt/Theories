## Definition (from the source)

The umbrella thesis of [[Christopher I. Lehrich]]'s *The Occult Mind: Magic in Theory and Practice*
(2007). Lehrich argues that there is no recognized discipline devoted to the analytical study of
occult, magical, or esoteric traditions, and that existing work is "hampered" by the disciplinary
conventions of intellectual history, history of science, sociology, and anthropology. He proposes
instead a **theoretical, comparative method** for reading magical texts — one that treats occult
thought as a serious intellectual system in its own right, an "alien world" to be understood on its
own terms rather than debunked, defended, or historicized away.

The method draws primarily on the structuralism of [[Claude Lévi-Strauss]] and the deconstruction of
[[Jacques Derrida]], with [[Marcel Mauss]]'s theory of magic and the comparative study of religion.
Lehrich's move is to place magic, comparison, and writing in a single analytical frame: the very
operations of the magician (comparison, correspondence, the manipulation of signs) mirror the
operations of the theorist who studies them. The book's title deliberately echoes
[[Aleister Crowley]]'s *Magick in Theory and Practice*.

## Key claims and figures associated with this concept

Extracted across the six chapters of [[The Occult Mind: Magic in Theory and Practice (Lehrich)]]:
Ægypt and the nostalgic *prisca magia* ([[Frances Yates]], [[Mircea Eliade]]); the epistemology of
occult history (ley lines); [[John Dee (1527–1608-9)]]'s hieroglyphic monad and the problem of
writing; [[Athanasius Kircher]]'s museum and [[Giordano Bruno]]'s semiotics; tarot and Lévi-Strauss's
mythographic/musical method; and a "deconstructive" theory of magic as differential classification.
