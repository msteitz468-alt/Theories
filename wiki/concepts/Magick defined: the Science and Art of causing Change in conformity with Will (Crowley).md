## Definition from Source

Crowley offers the following formal definition at the opening of *Magick in Theory and Practice*:

> "MAGICK is the Science and Art of causing Change to occur in conformity with Will."

He immediately follows this with a Postulate: "ANY required Change may be effected by the application of the proper kind and degree of Force in the proper manner through the proper medium to the proper object." The definition is deliberately universal — Crowley illustrates it by noting that even writing a book and distributing it through publishers and booksellers is an act of Magick, since it causes changes in conformity with the author's Will.

## Description from Source

Crowley presents twenty-eight numbered Theorems that develop the definition into a systematic philosophy. The most important for understanding his system are:

- **Theorem 1:** Every intentional act is a Magical Act (because every willed act causes change).
- **Theorems 6–9:** Every person is "a star" with an individual course. Anyone forced from their own course "comes into conflict with the order of the Universe, and suffers accordingly." A man whose conscious will is at odds with his True Will wastes his strength; a man doing his True Will "has the inertia of the Universe to assist him."
- **Theorem 12:** Man is ignorant of his own nature and powers; there is therefore no reason to assign theoretical limits to what he may be or do.
- **Theorem 23:** "Magick is the Science of understanding oneself and one's conditions. It is the Art of applying that understanding in action."
- **Theorem 25:** Every man must do Magick each time he acts or even thinks.
- **Theorem 27:** Every man should make Magick the keynote of his life.

Crowley insists the system is universal — intended not for occult specialists but for "the Banker, the Pugilist, the Biologist, the Poet, the Navvy, the Grocer." He distinguishes his rehabilitated "MAGICK" (spelled with a K to distinguish it from stage conjuring) as "essentially the most sublime" of all available terms. The essence, he writes, is simple: "to be and to do" — to discover one's True Will and to enact it with the full force of one's nature.

The Theorems also stress epistemological humility: Nature is continuous; forces exist that science has not yet detected; every force in the Universe is capable of being transformed into any other kind of force. These principles frame Magick as a science continuous with, not opposed to, empirical investigation.
