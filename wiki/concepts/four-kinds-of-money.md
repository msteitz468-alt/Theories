## Definition from Source

In his "crash course on money" (Section II), Griffin defines money as "anything which is accepted as a medium of exchange" and identifies **four kinds**:

1. **Commodity money** — intrinsically valuable goods (precious metals first appeared in history and, per Griffin, "have been proven by actual experience to be the only reliable base for an honest monetary system").
2. **Receipt money** — fully backed paper receipts redeemable for the commodity (e.g., gold).
3. **Fiat money** — "paper money without precious-metal backing which people are required by law to accept."
4. **Fractional money** — "paper money with precious-metal backing for part, not all, of its stated value."

## Characterization in the Source

- Griffin argues "fractional money always degenerates into pure fiat money," and that fiat money historically produces "massive inflation, unemployment, loss of property, and political unrest."
- He dates the first fiat money to thirteenth-century China, with its first major use in colonial America (disastrous, per the source), and again during the Revolutionary War.
- He argues the recurring error is "the false theory that the quantity of money is important, specifically that more money is better than less," leading to coin clipping, debasement, and over-issuance of receipts — "in every case ... economic and political disaster."
- Conversely, when the money supply is "determined by free-market production of the gold supply, the result has been prosperity and tranquility."
