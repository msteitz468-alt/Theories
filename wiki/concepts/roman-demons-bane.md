# Roman demons (Bane)

## Definition from Source

According to Bane's *Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures*, Roman demonology is closely intertwined with Greek demonology — the two traditions are frequently treated together as "Greco-Roman" in the encyclopedia — but also drew on Etruscan sources and produced distinctly Roman demonic figures. Rome absorbed much of the Greek daemon/daimon tradition while adding its own underworld gods (Dis Pater, Orcus), spirits of the dead (Lemures, Larvae, Manes), and demonic creatures (Striges/Strixes). As Christianity spread across the Empire, many Roman demonic or underworld figures were absorbed into Christian demonological hierarchies as princes or demons of Hell.

Bane notes that the Roman philosopher Plutarch (46–120 A.D.) described demons as entities born with the ability to inspire moral reasoning and act as intermediaries between gods and humans, while the Roman physician Quintus Serenus Sammonicus recorded the earliest known use of the word "abracadabra" in a second-century Latin medical poem. A defining feature of Roman demonology is its civic dimension: black sheep were sacrificed to Dis, the Roman Senate decreed a three-day festival to honor him every hundred years, and the Strix was catalogued by Pliny in his *Natural History* as a possibly imaginary owl-creature.

## Notable Demons

**Dis (Dis Pater)** — In ancient Greek, Roman, and Slavic mythology, Dis ("Wealthy") was born one of the three sons of Saturn and Ops; his brothers are Jupiter and Neptune, and his wife is Proserpina. The demon of death and ruler of the Underworld, with three faces — one black, one red, one white. Only oaths and curses sworn while striking the ground can reach him. Black sheep were sacrificed to him; the Roman Senate declared a special three-day festival every hundred years in his honor. He commands the demons Arioch, Baalzephon, Biffant, Bitru, Furcas (Forcas), Merodach, and Titivillus. Also the name of one of the cities in Hell. Later absorbed into Christian demonology as an alternative name for the prince of the underworld.

**Orcus** — Possibly originating in Etruscan mythology; the god of the underworld who appeared as a hairy, bearded giant that acted as a compassionate reaper, gently taking those who were suffering to the afterlife. He later became the Roman god of the underworld and a punisher of broken oaths. When absorbed into Christian demonology, he was ranked as the Prince of the Undead and demon of death who commands the underworld.

**Egestes** — In Roman mythology, Egestes is the demon of poverty. Sources vary as to gender; this demon resides in the Underworld.

**Furies (Dirae)** — Absorbed from Greek mythology into Roman religion as the Dirae ("the terrible"), also called Furor, Maniae, and Praxidikae. The three demonic goddesses of vengeance: Alecto, Megaera, and Tisiphone. In Roman identification they were linked with the goddess Diana (as Greek Artemis) and Faunus (as Greek Pan). Bane notes that in both the Greek and Roman framework they evolved from the ghosts of murdered people into personifications of curses and retributive justice.

**Striges (Strix)** — A demonic creature catalogued by Pliny the Elder in his *Natural History*, who suggested they might be imaginary since the only "bird" that nurses its young is the bat. The strix mythology originated in ancient Rome but was not linked to Satan until medieval times; the word translates as "owl" in Greek but came to mean "witch" in Italian. In medieval Christian demonology, the strixes were described as demonic subterranean creatures under the command of Satan, created when a person renounces their humanity and is transformed.

**Ephélés** — Conceived in ancient Greece and later adopted by ancient Rome; the ephélés ("one who leaps upon") was a vampiric demon with hooked talons, created when a person died before their time or was murdered. A bringer of nightmares, it would sit on a person's chest at night, grabbing hold tightly with its hooks and sending forth bad dreams. In Roman times it was identified with the gods Diana and Faunus, as well as satyrs, sirens, and silvani.

**Lemures and Larvae** — Spirits of the dead in Roman religion; Larvae were specifically the malevolent spirits of those who died violently or lived evil lives, condemned to wander the earth tormenting the living. Distinguished from the Manes (spirits of the honored dead) and the Lares (tutelary household spirits).
