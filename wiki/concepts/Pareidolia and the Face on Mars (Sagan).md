## Pareidolia and the Face on Mars (Sagan) — from Source

Sagan defines **pareidolia** — the brain's tendency to extract meaningful patterns, especially faces,
from random visual data — as the core explanatory framework for a wide range of pseudoscientific
"discoveries," from the Man in the Moon to the alleged Face on Mars at Cydonia.

### The Neurological Basis

Sagan argues (Ch. 3) that human infants are hard-wired from birth to recognise faces; those
ancestors who failed to do so were less likely to bond with parents and less likely to survive. As an
"inadvertent side effect," the pattern-recognition machinery in our brains is so efficient that it
**sometimes sees faces where there are none**. Examples he gives: the Man in the Moon; geological
formations named after faces (Old Man of the Mountain, New Hampshire; the Devil's Head; Sphinx Rock);
faces seen in clouds, wood grain, vegetables, and tortillas.

### The Martian Canal Precedent

Sagan uses the nineteenth-century "canals of Mars" as a cautionary tale. Trained professional
astronomers, looking through large telescopes, mapped hundreds of geometrically regular canals that
were later shown by spacecraft to be entirely non-existent — a malfunction of the hand-eye-brain
system at the limit of optical resolution through a turbulent atmosphere. The implication: **even
expert pattern recognition can be systematically wrong**, especially when the observed phenomenon
seems to carry profound implications.

### The Cydonia "Face"

Sagan examines the kilometre-wide hill in the Cydonia region of Mars photographed by Viking in 1976.
He notes:

- Mars has a surface area of ~150 million km²; it is not surprising that one small patch resembles a
  human face given the brain's face-detection bias.
- A strategically placed "nostril" that enhances the face impression is actually missing data from
  radio transmission, not a real surface feature.
- Enhanced analysis shows only one side lit by the Sun; the shadow side does not complete the face.
- Similar tabloid claims — faces on Venus, Neptune, and the Moon — are routinely attributed to
  fictional spacecraft and imaginary scientists.
- The "city" and "fort" near the Face, and the claim about astronomical alignment half a million
  years ago, are speculative extrapolations from a single ambiguous image.

Sagan explicitly does not rule out a closer look: he calls for high-resolution orbital photography
precisely because the claim is *falsifiable* — a property he commends. But he judges the face
hypothesis "most unlikely."

### Pyramid Parallels

Sagan discusses Martian "pyramids" in the Elysium plateau, noting that identical small-scale forms
(**dreikanters**) are produced by wind erosion in Antarctica. Natural processes can generate
striking bilateral symmetry and geometric regularity without any intelligent agency.

### Relationship to Ancient-Astronaut Claims

Sagan notes that Face enthusiasts went further than cautious speculation: they deduced a nearby city,
temples, fortifications, and a civilisation half a million years old — which (since anatomically
modern humans did not have spacecraft then) implied builders from elsewhere, recycling ancient
astronaut logic. He links this to a long tradition including the Martian canal myth of Percival Lowell
and Cleator's 1936 book *Rockets Through Space*.
