## Definition from Source

From the collection's Part I ("Studies in the American Right"): Hofstadter's account of the
**"pseudo-conservative revolt"** and **"status politics."** Where ordinary "interest politics"
bargains over material stakes, *status politics* is a clash of "projected rationalizations arising
from status aspirations and other personal motives," rooted in the "social rootlessness and
insecurity of American life." Pseudo-conservatives — who *say* they defend the Constitution and
freedom while displaying an "angrily authoritarian temperament" — are mobilized in times of acute
political anxiety.

## Description from Source

Hofstadter locates the 1950s revolt (his focus was McCarthyism) in two status-stressed groups:
declining old-family Anglo-Saxon Protestants losing prestige, and old-stock German/Irish Catholic
immigrants straining to sustain middle-class respectability — both mobilized against two "sinister
foes": the international communist conspiracy and the post–New Deal federal government. This
status-anxiety thesis is the social-psychological companion to
[[The paranoid style in politics (Hofstadter)|the paranoid style]]: it explains *when* and *why* the
paranoid disposition is mobilized into mass movements. (The term "paranoid" itself grew out of a 1958
confidential memorandum Hofstadter wrote for the Fund for the Republic on the contemporary right.)
