## Definition from Source

Tep Zepi — literally "the First Time" — is the concept found repeatedly in the Egyptian Pyramid Texts referring to a legendary primordial age when Egypt was supposedly ruled directly by the gods in human form (the Neteru). Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the Pyramid Texts make repeated reference to this era as the moment at which the gods gave humanity the gift of sacred knowledge or gnosis.

Wallace-Murphy and Hopkins describe Tep Zepi as the conceptual foundation of the entire Egyptian initiatory tradition:

> "The Pyramid Texts make repeated reference to Tep Zepi, the so-called First Time, the legendary time of Osiris when Egypt was believed to have been ruled directly by the gods in human form. These gods, according to legend, gave the Egyptians the wondrous gift of sacred knowledge."

The authors argue that the knowledge associated with the First Time — encompassing astronomy, architecture, medicine, mathematics, navigation, and metallurgy — appears fully formed in the earliest dynastic records with no detectable developmental period, suggesting it was inherited from an even earlier civilization or transmitted by an elite priestly lineage known as the Shemsu-Hor (Followers of Horus).

The Edfu Texts, according to the source, constantly reference the "wisdom of the Sages" and repeatedly emphasize that knowledge was their most prized gift — a direct continuation of the Tep Zepi tradition passed from master to pupil through successive generations of priestly initiates centered at Heliopolis-Giza.

The authors connect Tep Zepi's legacy to later religious traditions: the stellar and solar gnosis preserved by Egyptian priests was transmitted, in their view, through Atenism into early Judaism, and thus ultimately into Christianity and Islam. In this reading, the Rex Deus families are the most direct modern custodians of a sacred wisdom tradition whose roots lie in Tep Zepi.
