## Definition from Source

According to [[Theresa Bane]]'s *Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures*, Celtic and Norse demonology share the broader pattern she observes across many traditions: pre-Christian supernatural beings — gods, giants, spirits of nature — were progressively demonized after the arrival of Christianity, and their mythological attributes were reinterpreted as demonic evil. Bane draws on Irish manuscripts, Welsh mythological texts, Scandinavian sagas, and Germanic folklore to compile her entries.

The Celtic tradition is primarily documented through medieval Irish manuscripts, most notably the Book of the Dun Cow (Lebor Na H-Uidhri, c. 1100 CE), which preserves accounts of the Fomorians — the subaquatic demonic race that preceded the gods and was defeated by the Tuatha De Danaan in the conquest of Ireland. Celtic aqueous demons, liminal water-creatures, and the denizens of the underworld all feature prominently. Several Celtic words for the demonic — drouk, dus — translate simply to "evil" or "specter," reflecting the tradition's integration of demonhood into everyday landscape and language.

The Norse tradition in Bane's account is anchored in the giants (thursir) and the demonized pantheon of winter and darkness. Thursir are primordial frost giants, created at the beginning of the world from melting ice, who were demonized after Christianization. Germanic folklore contributed the imp tradition as a class of household demonic messenger, which eventually merged with broader European demonological categories.

## Notable Demons

**Fomorians** — The ancient subaquatic demonic race of Celtic lore, documented in the Book of the Dun Cow (c. 1100 CE). Their origin varies: some sources say they were born of Noah's son Ham; others say they predate the gods, born of Chaos and the Old Night. Usually described as having the body of a man and the head of a goat, or some other distinctive physical deformity (one eye, missing a limb). Historically the symbol of chaos and wild nature, they were defeated by the Tuatha De Danaan and have since resided under the sea on an island called Lochlan.

**Addanc** ("Beaver" / Afanc) — An aqueous demon from British, Celtic, and Welsh mythology; said to look like a beaver, crocodile, or dwarflike being and to prey upon those who enter the lakes it inhabits. The Book of the Dun Cow and related medieval Welsh manuscripts record various accounts of its destruction; one tradition holds that the addanc's thrashing in its lake once caused a catastrophic flood that drowned all original inhabitants of Britain except the founding pair Dwyfan and Dwyfach.

**Elathan** — A chief among the Fomorians recorded in the Book of the Dun Cow; considered by some sources to be a demon of the sea.

**Bile** — Originally the Celtic god of Hell; demonized after Christianization.

**Draca** — In Celtic mythology, the drake; a demonic class.

**Dus** — In Celtic lore, a species of hairy vampiric demons called "specters" that consume the flesh and blood of humans; believed to exist in liminal spaces.

**Thursir** ("Frost giants") — A demonic giant from Norse mythology, created at the beginning of the world from the melting ice. Described as having large ears and a body covered with long hair. They cause illness and steal man's ability to comprehend; it was after the introduction of Christianity that the Thursir were demonized as malicious and bringers of misfortune.

**Alfar** — Originally seen as a half-god, half-dwarf being in Scandinavian folklore, named in the Nibelungen Saga. Alfar later evolved into demonic or fairy-like beings associated with illness and nighttime afflictions.

**Andskoti** — An ancient Norse word for the adversary; used to name a demonic figure of opposition from Old Norse tradition.

**Imps** — Originating in Germanic folklore as messenger spirits; small demonic beings that serve as familiars to witches and sorcerers. The imp tradition entered broader European demonology primarily through its Germanic origins.

**Tunrida** — A devil from Scandinavian demonology.

**Lutins** — In Scandinavian demonology, a type of demonic trickster spirit.
