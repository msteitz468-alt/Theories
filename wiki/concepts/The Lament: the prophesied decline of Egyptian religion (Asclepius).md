## Definition from Source

The apocalyptic "Lament" of the Asclepius. Hermes prophesies to Asclepius that **a time will come when
the Egyptians' faithful worship will be in vain**: "divinity will return from earth to heaven, and Egypt
will be abandoned." Foreigners will occupy the land, reverence will be neglected and then **outlawed
under penalty of law**, and "this most holy land, seat of shrines and temples, will be filled completely
with tombs and corpses." The passage mourns the foreseen death of the old religion before a promised
eventual renewal.

## Description from Source

The Lament is the single most influential passage of the Asclepius in later esotericism. It is the
direct referent of [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)|Picknett & Prince's]]
[[Lament for Hermes: the scientific revolution was the Hermetic revolution, whose origins were then suppressed|"Lament for Hermes"]],
where the foretold suppression of Hermetic religion is read as a template for the later suppression of
the Hermetic origins of modern science. It pairs with the exaltation of
[[Man as a great miracle and the art of making gods (Asclepius)|man as a great miracle]] in the same treatise.
