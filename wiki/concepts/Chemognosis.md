## Definition from Source

Chemognosis is Carroll's term for the use of chemical and botanical substances to facilitate the
altered states ([[Gnosis (chaos magic)]]) required for magical work. The *Psychonaut* section of
[[Liber Null & Psychonaut (Carroll)]] opens the chapter with a prominent disclaimer: "To use drugs
of any kind is to poison the body… Neither the publisher nor the author wishes to incite any reader
to the irresponsible use of toxic substances and would advise against their use." Carroll frames the
chapter as a historical survey only.

### Four Governing Factors

Carroll holds that four factors control the outcome of chemognostic experiments:

1. **Physiological effects** of the drug itself.
2. **Training and abilities** of the user — quantities that produce only mild euphoria in untrained
   subjects may allow an adept to enter trance.
3. **Innate magical forces** in the substance — botanical drugs especially may carry the "spirit of
   the species," which "may yield its secrets to the user."
4. **Outside magical events** — an experienced initiate can guide the neophyte's perception during
   the experience.

### Three Drug Categories

Carroll groups magically useful drugs by their primary physiological action:

- **Hallucinogens** — enhance perception. Overdose or undirected use produces disordered trips.
  Carroll treats naturally occurring hallucinogens (Amanita muscaria, Psilocybe mushrooms) as
  richer than synthetics: LSD is said to "not possess any intrinsic magical quality" and to have
  "acquired an aura of paranoia and madness," whereas psilocybin mushrooms "are highly communicative
  if approached with respect."
- **Disinhibitory agents** — alcohol and hashish lower thresholds to the ecstatic gnostic states
  needed for excitatory rites.
- **Hypnotics / narcotics** — opium, tobacco, mandrake, ether, and chloroform induce trance and
  degrees of unconsciousness; "flying ointments" (solanaceae alkaloids) are discussed as producing
  astral travel during sleep, but Carroll warns they "carry a severe risk of fatal poisoning."

### Cautions and Limits

Carroll explicitly states: "All drugs are poisons and the previously mentioned substances are
capable of acting as lethal toxins. With many natural psychoactives, the difference between a fatal
and a merely psychoactive dosage is impossible to assay by amateur methods."

He also delimits the domain: "In general, chemical agents are only useful in receptive magic — such
as astral travel, divination, and invocation — and after a while, the adept should be able to obtain
these experiences without chemical assistance. Chemical agents find very little application in more
active forms of magic such as sigil casting and enchantment. In magical combat, their use may prove
disastrous."
