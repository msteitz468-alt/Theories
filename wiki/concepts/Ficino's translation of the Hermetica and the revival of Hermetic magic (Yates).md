## The Concept from Source

[[Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (Yates)]] (Chapters I–II) presents [[Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499)]]'s 1463 Latin translation of the Corpus Hermeticum as the pivotal event that launched the Renaissance Hermetic revival and, with it, the rehabilitation of learned magic in European intellectual life.

### The commission and the rush to translate

Around 1460 a Greek manuscript of the Corpus Hermeticum was brought to Florence from Macedonia by a monk employed by Cosimo de' Medici to collect manuscripts. It contained fourteen of the fifteen treatises that would come to make up the Corpus. Cosimo's library already held the assembled works of Plato awaiting translation. Yet Cosimo ordered Ficino to set Plato aside entirely and translate Hermes first.

Ficino himself recorded this in his dedication of his Plotinus commentaries to Lorenzo de' Medici, noting that in 1463 Cosimo commanded him to translate Hermes immediately, before going on to Plato: *"mihi Mercurium primo Termaximum, mox Platonem mandavit interpretandum."* Ficino completed the translation in a few months while Cosimo — who died in 1464 — was still alive. Yates calls this situation "extraordinary": the complete works of Plato wait while Ficino hurriedly translates Hermes, "probably because Cosimo wants to read him before he dies." The episode testifies, she argues, to the immense mystique of [[Hermes Trismegistus]] in the Renaissance imagination.

### The *Pimander* and its argumentum

Ficino entitled his translation the *Pimander* — extending the title of just the first treatise to cover the whole Corpus — and dedicated it to Cosimo. The preface (*argumentum*) reveals, Yates argues, "a state of profound awe and wonder" at what Ficino believed to be most ancient Egyptian wisdom. It opens with Ficino's genealogy of Hermes (slightly garbled from Augustine), placing him in extreme antiquity, at the time of Moses, and goes on to construct the famous *prisca theologia* chain: Hermes → Orpheus → Aglaophemus → Pythagoras → Plato.

Ficino's commentary on the first treatise (the Egyptian Genesis or *Pimander*) shows how compelling he found the parallels with Moses: Hermes seeing darkness, the Word of God warming the humid nature, creation by the divine Word, Man made in the image of God, the command to multiply. In the *Theologia Platonica* Ficino even wondered in writing whether Hermes Trismegistus and Moses were the same person.

### Impact and diffusion

Yates documents the extraordinary spread of Ficino's *Pimander*. More manuscripts of it survive than of any other Ficino work. It was first printed in 1471 and went through sixteen editions by the end of the sixteenth century, not counting appearances in collected editions. An Italian translation appeared in Florence in 1548. In 1505 Lefèvre d'Etaples combined Ficino's *Pimander* with the pseudo-Apuleian *Asclepius* into a single volume that became a standard reference work. The bibliography of Hermetic editions, translations, and commentaries in the sixteenth century is, Yates notes, "long and complicated," testifying to enthusiastic interest throughout the Renaissance.

### Rehabilitation of magic

Yates argues that Ficino's translation fundamentally changed the status of magic in European culture. The medieval church had driven magic into "dark holes and corners." The flood of late-antique literature arriving from Byzantium — Iamblichus, Porphyry, Plotinus, and above all the Hermetica — now associated magic with revered philosophers. More specifically, the *prisca theologia* genealogy meant that the ancient sages (*prisci theologi*) were also ancient magicians (*prisci magi*): Hermes himself, Orpheus, Zoroaster. The discovery of the Corpus Hermeticum demonstrated the extraordinary piety of Hermes, and this piety retroactively rehabilitated his Asclepius, which Augustine had condemned for its descriptions of Egyptian idol-animating magic. Hermes Trismegistus was, Yates concludes, "probably the most important figure in the Renaissance revival of magic."

### The content that moved Ficino

Yates gives particular attention to the *Asclepius* and its centrality to Ficino's programme. The *Asclepius* describes how Egyptian priests drew the powers of the cosmos into the statues of their gods by magic means; it also contains a "Lament" prophesying that Egypt's ancient religion will perish — passages both condemning (to Augustine) and deeply attractive (to Ficino) as revelations of sacred Egyptian practice. Ficino's strategy was to follow Lactantius in praising Hermes and to find a Neoplatonic interpretation that would make the god-making magic "natural" rather than demonic — a strategy fully developed in *De vita coelitus comparanda* (see [[Ficino's natural magic: talismans, spiritus, and astral music (Yates)]]).
