## Definition / Characterization from Source

Karl Pribram's model, as reported by Talbot: the brain stores memory and processes perception not in localized "engrams" but in a **distributed** fashion governed by holographic principles — encoding information as interference patterns across wide regions, so that (as in a hologram) "every portion contains the whole." This explains why Karl Lashley could not surgically remove a specific memory from a rat no matter which brain region he excised, and why memory degrades gracefully rather than vanishing.

In its strong form the model proposes the brain mathematically constructs "hard" reality "by interpreting frequencies from a dimension transcending time and space" — i.e., the brain is "a hologram interpreting a holographic universe." This dovetails with [[The implicate and explicate order|Bohm's implicate order]] to yield the book's central synthesis.
