## Definition from Source

Griffin (Chs. 5–6) argues that the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank, created at the [[Event: Bretton Woods Conference (1944)]], had announced goals (facilitating trade, stabilizing exchange rates) that masked unannounced goals: "the elimination of the gold-exchange standard as the basis of currency valuation and the establishment of world socialism." The IMF, "acting as a world central bank," would create a world currency "out of nothing"; the World Bank would transfer money "disguised as loans" to governments "in such a way as to insure the demise of free enterprise."

## Characterization in the Source

- Griffin names the conference's guiding theoreticians as **John Maynard Keynes** (a Fabian Socialist) and **Harry Dexter White** (Assistant Secretary of the U.S. Treasury and, per the source, "a Member of a Communist espionage ring") — "working together to bring about their mutual goal: world socialism."
- The Fabians, per Griffin, "agreed with Communists as to the goal of socialism but disagreed over tactics," favoring "gradualism and the transformation of society through legislation." (He cites the Fabian crest — a wolf in sheep's clothing — and the Beatrice Webb House window "REMOULD IT NEARER TO THE HEART'S DESIRE.")
- The mechanism: "loans that will never be paid back" drain the industrialized nations' economies while buying the loyalty of less-developed countries' rulers, drawing both toward surrender of sovereignty to "a true world government within the framework of the United Nations."
