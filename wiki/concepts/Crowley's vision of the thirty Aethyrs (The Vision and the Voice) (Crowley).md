## Definition from Source

*The Vision and the Voice* (also titled Liber XXX Aervm, or Liber CDXVIII) is Crowley's first-person visionary record of scrying all thirty Enochian Aethyrs, recorded in 1909 while walking across the North African Sahara (Algeria), primarily with Victor Neuburg as scribe. It is presented in *The Practice of Enochian Magick* as Liber XXX Aervm: Vel Saecvli. The visions are described as ascending in depth and intensity from the 30th (lowest) Aethyr — TEX — to the 1st (highest) — LIL — with the 10th Aethyr, ZAX (the Abyss), forming the critical threshold that must be crossed to reach the higher planes.

## Description from Source

**The structure of the scrying sessions**

Each vision is called a "Cry" — e.g., "The Cry of the 30th Aethyr which is called TEX." Crowley employed the single Call of the Thirty Aethyrs, inserting the name of the target Aethyr, and then scried using a shew-stone (crystal). The text is primarily Crowley's first-person visionary narrative, with interspersed notes by the Scribe (Neuburg). The visions were conducted at various locations across Algeria during late November and December 1909.

**Character of the visions**

The lower Aethyrs (30th down to approximately 15th) present visions of increasing elaboration — angelic figures, symbolic tableaux, allegorical encounters. Each Aethyr corresponds to a spiritual level; the text states that from the 1st to the 15th Aethyr, "there is no vision and no voice save for him that is called NEMO" — suggesting those higher planes are accessible only to adepts who have crossed the Abyss. The 13th Aethyr (ZIM) introduces the figure of NEMO — the magician who has "beheld the face of my Father" and gone beyond all name — who tends a garden and whose understanding supersedes knowledge. The 12th Aethyr (LOE) presents the mystery of Babylon the Beautiful, the Scarlet Woman who rides the Beast and whose "blood is mingled in the cup" as the wine of compassion and understanding.

**The 10th Aethyr — ZAX and the encounter with Choronzon**

The 10th Aethyr (ZAX) is described as "accursed." Crowley enters it from within a triangle inscribed with the name of Choronzon and protective divine names, while Neuburg guarded a circle. The demon Choronzon — whose name numerates to 333 and is called "Dispersion" and "the mighty devil that inhabiteth the outermost Abyss" — manifests and speaks. The text presents Choronzon as the principle of formlessness and fragmentation: "I am the Master of Form, and from me all forms proceed ... I am I." The Scribe's record documents Choronzon's attempts to seduce, deceive, and physically break through the protective circle by trickling sand into it; he takes on diverse forms — a beautiful woman, the voice of the Frater. Crowley teaches that Choronzon's fundamental nature is Dispersion: "there is no centre, nay, nothing but Dispersion," and that he "feareth of all things concentration and silence." The encounter ends when Crowley writes the name BABALON, which is "victory over Choronzon."

The 11th Aethyr (IKH), which guards the approach, warns that "whoso passeth into the outermost Abyss, except he be of them that understand, holdeth out his hands, and boweth his neck, unto the chains of Choronzon."

**The higher Aethyrs and the City of the Pyramids**

Above the Abyss, the visions shift in character. The 9th Aethyr (ZIP) onward are described as harder to express in ordinary terms, with the seer increasingly dissolving into the vision. The 8th Aethyr presents the City of the Pyramids — hooded adepts like pyramids seated upon the "Sea" — described as masters of the Temple who have renounced all lower nature. The 6th Aethyr (MAZ) presents the god Thoth-Mercury as the inmost guardian before the shrine, described as the "maker of Illusions" and bound by the "curse of Magus" — a condition affecting all great prophets (Moses, Buddha, Jesus, Muhammad) who attained the grade of Magus and were therefore "bound to teach nothing but falsehood, except unto such as understood." The 5th Aethyr (LIT) presents the divine Arrow as a supreme symbol: its crown the Father of all Light, its shaft the Father of all Life, its barb the Father of all Love, described as the source of infinite motion yet itself unmoved. The 1st Aethyr (LIL) culminates in a complete dissolution of the Seer's individual consciousness into the infinite, with repeated invocations of Nuit from the Egyptian stele hymn.

**Cosmological significance**

The text connects the Aethyrs explicitly to the Thelemic cosmology: the 26th Aethyr (DES) presents a vision resolving into the image of Nuit, the winged disk, and Ra-Hoor-Khuit "even as it is upon the Stélé of Revealing" — described as "the dawn of the aeon." The 12th Aethyr includes reference to Choronzon as "the mighty devil ... whose name is the Second Death," acting against "the Dark Brothers" who seal themselves from compassion. The higher Aethyrs are stated to interpenetrate and surround the universe of the Sephiroth but not to follow the same order as the Tree of Life.
