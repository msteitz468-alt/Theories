## Concept from Source

Barkun develops the concept of **stigmatized knowledge** in Ch. 2 of *[[A Culture of Conspiracy (Barkun)]]* to describe claims to truth that their holders regard as verified despite the marginalization of those claims by the institutions that conventionally distinguish between knowledge and error — universities, communities of scientific researchers, and the like.

### Five varieties of stigmatized knowledge

Barkun identifies five sub-types within the domain of stigmatized knowledge:

1. **Forgotten knowledge** — knowledge once allegedly known but lost through cataclysm or faulty memory (e.g., ancient wisdom of Atlantis).
2. **Superseded knowledge** — claims once authoritatively recognized but later regarded as false or inferior (e.g., astrology, alchemy).
3. **Ignored knowledge** — claims that persist in low-prestige social groups but are not taken seriously by mainstream institutions (e.g., folk medicine).
4. **Rejected knowledge** — claims explicitly rejected as false from the outset (e.g., UFO abductions).
5. **Suppressed knowledge** — claims allegedly known to be valid by authoritative institutions but deliberately hidden because those institutions fear the consequences of public knowledge or have evil motives (e.g., the alien origins of UFOs, suppressed cancer cures).

Barkun observes that the suppressed-knowledge category tends to absorb all the others: once believers conclude that their ideas conflict with orthodoxy, they assume the forces of orthodoxy perpetuate error out of self-interest or malice, attributing all forms of stigmatization to a conspiracy.

### The proof-by-rejection inversion

The central and most consequential feature of the stigmatized knowledge domain, in Barkun's analysis, is what can be called the **proof-by-rejection inversion**: stigmatization itself is taken as evidence of truth. Rather than making a truth claim appear problematic, rejection by mainstream institutions is seen to give it credibility — proof that some malign force conspired to prevent the truth from becoming known. A presumption of validity therefore attaches to any stigmatized claim, greatly facilitating the flow of such claims through the cultic milieu.

This inversion generates a closed epistemic circle: because the conspiracy controls the channels of information (universities, media), any evidence that appears to refute a conspiracy theory must itself have been planted by the conspirators. The theory becomes effectively nonfalsifiable, and every attempt at falsification is dismissed as a ruse.

### Reciprocal citation as pseudoconfirmation

Barkun notes that the literature of stigmatized knowledge mimics mainstream scholarship through elaborate citations and bibliographies, but these consist largely of reciprocal citation in which authors obligingly cite one another. The same sources repeat, producing pseudoconfirmation: if a source is cited many times, it must be true. Because the claims are nonfalsifiable, multiplication of citations leaves the impression of validation without subjecting any proposition to an actual test of evidence. Barkun traces this observation back to [[The paranoid style in politics (Hofstadter)]], who noted "heroic strivings for evidence" proportional to the sweepingness of the claims.

### The cultic milieu as reservoir

Barkun draws on Colin Campbell's concept of the **cultic milieu** — the cultural underground of deviant belief systems, alternative medicine, occultism, sectarian religion, and radical politics — and James Webb's earlier concept of **rejected knowledge** to explain how stigmatized claims circulate. The cultic milieu is intrinsically hostile to authority and receptive to all forms of revisionism. Ideas migrate easily across its subcultures, facilitated by publications, meetings, and interlinked websites. An individual who enters the milieu at any one point quickly travels through a variety of movements and belief systems. Barkun argues that the cultic milieu forms the reservoir from which improvisational millennialism draws its eclectic materials.

### Connection to conspiracism

Because stigmatized knowledge believers assume that widely accepted beliefs are necessarily false — the product of indoctrination or suppression — they are structurally predisposed to conspiracism. Conspiracy theories function both as an instance of suppressed knowledge and as a meta-explanation for why all other stigmatized claims have been marginalized. This two-level operation makes the conspiracy framework nearly irresistible within the stigmatized-knowledge domain.
