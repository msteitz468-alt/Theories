## Definition from Source

The Apocryphon of John teaches that at the summit of all reality stands the Monad — the invisible, virginal Spirit who is the unknowable Father of everything. He is not a god among gods but something far beyond any category: "The Monad is a monarchy with nothing above it. It is he who exists as God and Father of everything, the invisible One who is above everything, who exists as incorruption, which is in the pure light into which no eye can look" (Apocryphon of John, Codex II). He is "illimitable, since there is no one prior to him to set limits to him. He is unsearchable… He is immeasurable… He is invisible… He is ineffable, since no one was able to comprehend him to speak about him."

The Pleroma — the "Fullness" — is the totality of divine aeons (eternal principles or realms) that emanate from this Father in a series of paired, consenting acts of self-disclosure.

## Description from Source

The Apocryphon of John presents a detailed cosmology of the invisible realm. The Father gazes upon his own light "like the spring of the water of life" and his thought performs a deed: Barbelo comes forth — "the first power which was before all of them… the forethought of the All… the Mother-Father, the first man, the holy Spirit, the thrice-male, the thrice-powerful, the thrice-named androgynous one, and the eternal aeon among the invisible ones." Barbelo is the first emanation, the womb of everything.

Barbelo then petitions the invisible Spirit for successive gifts: foreknowledge, indestructibility, eternal life, and truth — each of which comes forth as a distinct aeon. These form "the pentad of the aeons of the Father, which is the first man, the image of the invisible Spirit." From this originating dyad of Father and Barbelo, the Only-Begotten (Autogenes, identified with Christ) is begotten as "a spark of light." The invisible Spirit anoints him with goodness until he is perfect.

From Autogenes proceed the four luminaries (Armozel, Oriel, Daveithai, Eleleth), each attended by three aeons, producing the twelve aeons of the Pleroma. The perfect Man (Adamas / Pigera-Adamas) and his son Seth are placed within these luminaries. All these divine hypostases constitute the Pleroma — the complete, undeficient divine realm existing in harmonious silence.

The Gospel of Truth, treating the same domain, describes the Father as the one in whom "all the spaces are his emanations" and whose name — the Son — is "invisible, for it alone is the mystery of the invisible about to come to ears completely filled with it."

The defining characteristic of this entire structure is consent and harmony: every new aeon comes forth only when the invisible Spirit consents, and all things move in praise of the Father and Barbelo. Sophia's subsequent solitary act — creating without consent — is therefore a catastrophic departure from this structural logic.
