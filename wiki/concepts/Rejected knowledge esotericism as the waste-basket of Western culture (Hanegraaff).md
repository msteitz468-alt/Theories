# Rejected knowledge: esotericism as the "waste-basket" of Western culture (Hanegraaff)

## Definition from Source
Hanegraaff's core organizing thesis in Chapter One is that Western esotericism should be understood as the "chief casualty of academic specialization after the eighteenth century" — its modern status is that of "rejected knowledge." It comprises "precisely everything that has been consigned to the dustbin of history by Enlightenment ideologues and their intellectual heirs," because it was judged incompatible with normative post-Enlightenment concepts of religion, rationality, and science. Mainstream intellectuals after the eighteenth century treated the domain as something to be avoided and ignored rather than studied.

He stresses this exclusion was not accidental or sudden but "the final outcome of a long history of apologetic and polemical battles and negotiations, beginning in late antiquity," through which religious and intellectual elites defined their own identity against a rejected "other" (detailed further in his Chapter Three, outside this range). Crucially, being a dustbin of rejected knowledge does not mean the field is "a random collection of discarded materials without any further connection" — a broad consensus emerged around the eighteenth century about what, specifically, was being rejected, and those characteristics correspond to recognizable worldviews and approaches to knowledge (elaborated in his later chapters on worldviews and epistemology).

This is offered as "the closest we will get to a definition of Western esotericism": (1) a domain set apart by mainstream religious and intellectual culture as constitutive "other," and (2) marked by worldviews and epistemologies at odds with normative post-Enlightenment culture.
