## Definition from Source

Across several tractates of [[The Nag Hammadi Library]], "Yaldabaoth" (also called Sakla, Samael, or the Arch-Begetter) is the ignorant, arrogant deity who mistakes himself for the supreme God and rules the material world through a hierarchy of subordinate powers called **archons** ("rulers"). He is the offspring of Sophia's error — not the unknowable Father — and his creation of the material cosmos is consequently a defective imitation of the Pleroma above.

## Description from Source

### Origin of Yaldabaoth

The **Hypostasis of the Archons** (Codex II) — "The Reality of the Rulers" — narrates that Sophia (Pistis) willed to create something alone, without her consort. A veil fell between the higher world and the realms below; shadow condensed into matter, and from that shadow emerged Yaldabaoth: "a product in the matter, like an aborted fetus … an arrogant beast resembling a lion. It was androgynous, as I have already said, because it was from matter that it derived."

Opening his eyes upon limitless matter, the demiurge immediately committed his defining sin of arrogance:

> "It is I who am God, and there is none other apart from me." When he said this, he sinned against the entirety. And a voice came forth from above the realm of absolute power, saying, "You are mistaken, Samael" — which is, 'god of the blind'.
> — *Hypostasis of the Archons*, Codex II

Zoe (Life), the daughter of Pistis Sophia, then rebuked him a second time ("You are mistaken, Sakla!"), breathed into his face, and her breath became a fiery angel that bound Yaldabaoth and cast him into Tartaros below the abyss.

### The Archons

From the **Hypostasis of the Archons**: Yaldabaoth "created for himself seven offspring, androgynous just like their parent," telling them "It is I who am god of the entirety." The archons are described as partly beast-faced: the text notes Adam's modellers had "the face of a beast." They govern the lower heavens and are characterized throughout by **blindness**, **ignorance**, and **arrogance** — they are the "authorities of darkness" and "rulers of unrighteousness."

The **Gospel of the Egyptians** (Codex III) lists twelve named archons over Hades and chaos, headed by Sakla (Yaldabaoth), each given charge of a world. After founding this realm, Sakla declares: "I, I am a jealous god, and apart from me nothing has come into being."

The **Sophia of Jesus Christ** (Codex III) identifies Yaldabaoth as "Arch-Begetter" whose "bond of forgetfulness" keeps humanity ignorant of the higher Father: "I came to remove them from their blindness, that I might tell everyone about the God who is above the universe."

### Sabaoth's Repentance

A nuanced episode appears within the *Hypostasis of the Archons*: Yaldabaoth's offspring **Sabaoth**, seeing the power of the angel who bound his father, "repented and condemned his father and his mother, matter … and sang songs of praise up to Sophia and her daughter Zoe." Sophia rewarded him with the seventh heaven, placing Zoe at his right to teach him — so not all archonic beings are irredeemably hostile. His elevation generates Yaldabaoth's **envy**, from which Death itself is said to originate.

### Humanity Trapped Below

The central function of the archons and their creation is to keep the divine sparks imprisoned in matter and ignorance: "they threw mankind into great distraction and into a life of toil, so that their mankind might be occupied by worldly affairs, and might not have the opportunity of being devoted to the holy spirit."
