## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas argues that the theoretical line between magic and religion in early modern England was doctrinally coherent but practically blurred. The medieval and post-Reformation Church held that any supernatural effect had to derive from one of two legitimate sources — God (through sanctioned rituals such as the Mass or holy water) or an admitted miracle — while any other claimed supernatural effect, not reducible to known natural causes, was by definition diabolical. Under this logic, the Church could license its own "magic" (holy water, consecrated bells against storms) while condemning identical-looking practices when performed by unauthorized cunning folk. Thomas stresses that in practice magic and religion were "travelling companions" rather than opposites: charms recycled Christian prayers, conjurers summoned what they took to be Christian angels, and the search for occult knowledge (alchemy, theurgy, Dee's angelic conversations) was pursued by its practitioners as a religious quest requiring purity of life, fasting and prayer.

## Key Claims
- The Church's definition of "superstition" depended entirely on its own prior, shifting judgment about what counted as "natural" — Neoplatonist clergy (e.g. supporters of Robert Fludd) drew the line very differently from Aristotelian conservatives.
- Protestant reformers narrowed the category of legitimate ecclesiastical magic (rejecting holy water, exorcism, relics) while keeping hostility to lay magic exactly as strong as before — arguably widening, not narrowing, the gap between sanctioned and condemned supernaturalism.
- Cunning folk who healed using ordinary Protestant prayers posed a genuine dogmatic problem: theologians such as Richard Greenham could only say prayer must accompany, not substitute for, natural means.
- Learned magicians such as John Dee and Robert Fludd regarded their own conjuring and alchemy as devout activity, believing they summoned angels and pursued gnosis through prayer and asceticism, and considered themselves within the bounds of Christianity.
- Clergy themselves were frequently implicated in practical magic (treasure-seeking, charming, thief-detection), showing that the line between priest and magician was blurred in the popular mind even as official doctrine insisted on a sharp distinction.
- Thomas concludes that religion could only durably displace magic not by out-competing it as counter-magic, but by teaching that practical problems should be met through self-help and hard work rather than any magical shortcut, sacred or profane.
