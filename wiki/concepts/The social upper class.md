## Definition / Characterization from Source

Domhoff defines the social upper class as the network of wealthy families and corporate leaders — fewer than 0.5% of the American population — who are bound together through a distinctive set of social institutions: exclusive private boarding schools, elite social clubs, debutante seasons, resort communities, and telephone directories such as the Social Register. As he puts it, the upper class "at any given historical moment consists of a complex network of overlapping social circles linked by the members they have in common and by the numerous bonds of trust created by common cultural styles and values, which emerge from a similar upbringing, education, and lifestyle."

The class is not merely a wealth stratum but a *socially cohesive* group. Research in social psychology, Domhoff notes, shows "the most socially cohesive groups more often succeed in arriving at consensus when asked to deal with a problem." The institutions of the upper class — prep schools, social clubs, retreats, resorts — provide "settings and occasions" that maximise exactly this kind of cohesion. A classic 1930s study of the New York plutocracy that Domhoff quotes concluded: "The elaborate private life of the plutocracy serves in considerable measure to separate them out in their own consciousness as a superior, more refined element."

The class is reproduced across generations through assimilation: "newer members [are] assimilated into the lifestyle of the class through participation in the schools, clubs, and other social institutions." Upwardly mobile corporate executives are drawn into upper-class networks through charitable boards, social clubs, their children's prep schools, and stock-option wealth. The institutions "transcend the presence or absence of any given person or family. Families can rise and fall in the class structure, but the institutions of the upper class persist."
