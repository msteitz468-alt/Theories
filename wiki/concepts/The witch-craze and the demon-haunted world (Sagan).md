## The Witch-Craze and the Demon-Haunted World (Sagan) — from Source

Sagan's seventh chapter argues that the Christian witch-craze of the fifteenth through seventeenth
centuries and the modern alien-abduction phenomenon share a single underlying psychology: vivid,
culturally shaped hallucinations of sexual violation by aerial, wall-penetrating non-human beings,
interpreted through the dominant cosmology of the age.

### Demons in Classical and Early Christian Thought

Sagan traces belief in demons from Hesiod, Plato ("demons are superior beings placed over us by God
to give peace and order"), and Aristotle (dreams scripted by demons) through Augustine's influential
demonisation — all demons are evil, "aerial animals… subtle in deceit," able to assume any form. By
the Middle Ages this framework was unquestioned across Western Europe: demons inhabited the "upper
air," passed through walls, abducted sleepers, and committed sexual violations.

### Incubi, Succubi, and Sleep Paralysis

The medieval categories are particularly important to Sagan's argument:

- **Incubi** (demons who sexually assault sleeping women) and **succubi** (who assault men) were
  perceived as a weight bearing down on the chest — the word *mare* in "nightmare" originally meant
  the demon that sat on the sleeper's chest.
- Nuns reported that incubi resembled their confessor or bishop; they woke "polluted as if they had
  commingled with a man." Similar accounts appear in ancient Chinese harems.
- St Augustine, St Thomas Aquinas, and St Bonaventura all theorised about demonic collection and
  redistribution of human semen — a multi-generational, trans-species reproductive programme.
- Athanasius (c.360) and later Sinistrari (1700) both noted that demons pass through locked doors
  and walls.

Sagan draws the explicit parallel: **all the central elements of the modern alien abduction account
were present** in the medieval demon tradition — sexually obsessive non-human aerial beings, wall
penetration, paralysis, reproductive interference, telepathic communication — with no spaceship.

### The Witch-Craze as Institutional Catastrophe

Pope Innocent VIII's Bull of 1484 initiated systematic accusation, torture, and execution. The
*Malleus Maleficarum* (Kramer and Sprenger) codified the procedures:

- Accusation was sufficient for guilt; torture was the means of proof.
- All costs were borne by the accused; inquisitors received bonuses per witch burned and confiscated
  her property.
- "Witch-finders" (prickers) were paid per conviction, with no incentive to caution.
- One pricker confessed to the deaths of 220 women.
- Each accused person, under torture, was made to implicate others, producing exponential growth in
  charges.

The Chronicle of Wurzburg (1598) lists a single city's immolations in one year, including girls of
nine and ten, boys of twelve, and "the most beautiful girl in Wurzburg." Sagan quotes the aggregate:
possibly hundreds of thousands to millions killed across Europe.

### Continuity from Demons to Aliens

Sagan argues the cultural sequence is: gods → demons → fairies/incubi → spirits (Victorian
spiritualism) → aliens. In each era, the same underlying neurological experiences (temporal lobe
hallucinations, sleep paralysis, hypnagogic imagery) are refracted through the dominant cosmology:

- Anne Jefferies (1645, Cornwall): attacked by "half a dozen little men," carried paralysed to "a
  castle in the air," sexually molested, returned home. If she had grown up with UFOs rather than
  fairies, her story would be indistinguishable from a modern abduction account.
- Folkloric analogs to incubi exist across cultures: Arabian djinn, Greek satyrs, Hindu bhuts,
  Samoan hotua poro, Celtic dusii.
- In *Communion*, Whitley Strieber himself described his visitors as "demons" on first encounter.

Jacques Vallée's argument — that aliens have always visited us and were called gods or demons in
earlier eras — is noted and rejected by Sagan: if so, why no flying saucers before 1947? Why no
saucer iconography in any world religion?

### The Microbiology/Meteorology Point

Sagan returns to Thomas Ady's 1656 book *A Candle in the Dark*, which attacked witch-hunting as a
scam — "microbiology and meteorology now explain what only a few centuries ago was considered
sufficient cause to burn women to death." The expansion of naturalistic explanation contracts the
space for supernatural attribution: what was once a demon causing disease is now a pathogen; what was
once demonic storm-raising is now meteorology.
