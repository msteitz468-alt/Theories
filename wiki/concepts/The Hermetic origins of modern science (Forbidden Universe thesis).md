## Definition from Source

The central thesis of *The Forbidden Universe*: that the **Hermetic tradition** — the *Corpus
Hermeticum* attributed to [[Hermes Trismegistus]], revived in the Renaissance through Marsilio
Ficino's 1463 translation — was "the greatest inspiration" of the founders of modern science, and that
its later eclipse was a deliberate suppression rather than a natural outgrowth of scientific maturity.
Picknett & Prince build on the "Yates thesis" of [[Frances Yates]] (*Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic
Tradition*) and extend it: the occult, mystical, and "magical" worldview was not the enemy of the
Scientific Revolution but its engine, and (in Part Two) remains relevant to modern cosmology's "search
for the Mind of God." This is the umbrella concept of the source.

## Description from Source

The argument unfolds historically (Part One): the Hermetic revival inspired Copernican heliocentrism
(the sun as a "Second God"), [[Giordano Bruno]]'s infinite universe, Galileo, the Rosicrucian
movement, and [[Isaac Newton (1643–1727)]]'s alchemy and theology; then (Part Two) it is carried into
the modern debate over cosmic fine-tuning, consciousness, and design, set against the "scientism" of
Dawkins and Hitchens. The wiki's other Hermetic source, *[[The Kybalion (Three Initiates, 1908)]]*,
expounds the doctrinal side of the same tradition.
