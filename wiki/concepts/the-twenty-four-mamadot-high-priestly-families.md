## Definition from Source

The ma'amadot (also rendered ma'-madot) were the twenty-four hereditary high-priestly teams who rotated service in the Temple of Jerusalem. Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the term literally refers to the priestly courses or divisions who "took turns in serving in the Temple in Jerusalem," an institution the authors trace back as an anachronistic retrojection by post-exilic scribes onto the time of David and Solomon.

### Temple Boarding Schools (per the Rex Deus informant "Michael")

The most distinctive claim in the source concerns two boarding schools attached to the Jerusalem Temple — one for boys, one for girls — staffed and administered by the twenty-four ma'amadot. All pupils were drawn from the tribe of Levi, the hereditary priesthood. Male graduates were destined to become high priests, rabbis, or leaders of Israel.

The high priests were ritually named according to rank — titles including Melchizedek, Michael, and Gabriel. Per the Rex Deus account relayed through "Michael" (the informant who approached [[Tim Wallace-Murphy]] in 1994), the high priests were also responsible for impregnating the female pupils when they reached puberty, after which the girls were married into leading families of Israel. Children born of these ritual unions were returned to the temple school at age seven, further preserving the hereditary nature of the high priesthood and keeping the sacred bloodlines "pure and unsullied."

This tradition is presented as the institutional context for the conception of Jesus: the high priest Gabriel impregnated Mary (Miriam), a pupil at the girls' school and daughter of a previous pupil named Anne.

### Post-Exilic Codification

The authors note that the historian Sanmell acknowledges the ma'amadot as a post-exilic institution projected backward in scriptural accounts:

> "The ordinary view of modern scholars is that in Chronicles the ecclesiastical organisation which arose in the latter part of the post-exilic period was anachronistically read back into the times of David and Solomon, thereby giving the sanction of antiquity to the ecclesiastical system of the post-exilic period. This ecclesiastical organisation provided for twenty-four ma'-madot, priestly teams who took turns in serving in the Temple in Jerusalem."

The authors treat the ma'amadot not merely as a liturgical rota but as the living custodians of the Egyptian-derived gnosis transmitted from the time of the Exodus. The Zadokite priests of the ma'amadot who refused to serve under the Maccabean-appointed non-Zadokite high priests withdrew to Qumran and formed the Essene sect. James the Just is described as "a hereditary member of the ma'madot" who retained the right to enter the Holy of Holies.

### Dispersal and Continuity as Rex Deus

After the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 C.E., the surviving members of the ma'amadot dispersed across Europe and the Middle East. Per the Rex Deus hypothesis, these families — bound by oath to maintain accurate genealogies and to marry within the group — became the Rex Deus families, secretly preserving the initiatory gnosis of the Temple while outwardly conforming to the dominant religion of their host culture.
