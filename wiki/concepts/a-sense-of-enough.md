## Definition from Source

**"Enough"** is Morgan Housel's term (chapter 3 of *[[The Psychology of Money (Timeless Lessons on Wealth, Greed, and Happiness)]]*) for the realization that an insatiable appetite for more will push you "to the point of regret." The chapter opens with John Bogle's story of Kurt Vonnegut telling Joseph Heller that a hedge-fund host made more in a day than *Catch-22* ever earned, and Heller's reply: "Yes, but I have something he will never have… enough."

## Key Points

- "The hardest financial skill is getting the goalpost to stop moving." When expectations rise faster than results, "one step forward pushes the goalpost two steps ahead." (Happiness "is just results minus expectations.")
- **Social comparison** is the engine of the problem and is unwinnable: the ceiling is so high "that virtually no one will ever hit it" (the rookie ballplayer → Mike Trout → top hedge-fund managers → Buffett → Bezos chain). "The only way to win is to not fight to begin with."
- "Enough" is **not** conservatism or leaving potential on the table; it is avoiding the regret of overreaching.
- The cost of having no "enough": Rajat Gupta, Bernie Madoff, and LTCM "risked what they had and needed for what they didn't have and didn't need." Some things — reputation, freedom, family, happiness — are "invaluable" and never worth risking.
