## Definition from Source

The cosmogony of the first treatise (Poimandres). **Poimandres** — "the mind of sovereignty" — appears
to Hermes in a vision and reveals the origin of all things: the supreme God is **Mind (Nous)**, who is
**Light and Life**; from him issues the holy **Word (Logos)**; Mind brings forth a second creator-Mind
(the demiurge) who, with the Word, fashions the seven **governors** (the planetary spheres) whose
revolution governs the sensible cosmos.

## Description from Source

In the vision, primal Light gives way to a fearful watery Darkness; a holy Word descends upon the
watery nature; the creator-Mind and the Word together set the elements and the governors in motion, and
nature brings forth the living kinds. The account deliberately parallels (and reinterprets) Genesis,
which Copenhaver's introduction discusses as part of the Hermetica's Jewish-Egyptian-Greek matrix. The
cosmogony sets up the anthropogony that follows — see
[[The descent of the heavenly Man and the soul's ascent to divinization (Corpus Hermeticum I)]].
