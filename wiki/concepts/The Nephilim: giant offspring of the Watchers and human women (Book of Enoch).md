## Definition and Characterisation from Source

The Book of Enoch narrates that when the two hundred Watchers took human women as wives (ch. 6–7), the women bore offspring called **giants** — the Nephilim. The text states their height was "three thousand cubits" (ch. 7:3), a number so enormous it is generally read as symbolic of their terrifying scale rather than a literal measurement.

### Devastation of the Earth (ch. 7)

The giants are depicted as engines of destruction in three escalating stages:

1. They "consumed all the work and toil of men" — they devoured human agricultural and material production.
2. "When men could no longer sustain them, the giants turned against them and devoured mankind."
3. They then "began to sin against birds, and beasts, and reptiles, and fish, and to devour one another's flesh, and drank the blood."

The earth itself "laid accusation against the lawless ones" (ch. 7:6), an image of creation crying out in protest at the violence.

### Divine Response to the Nephilim

The Lord's decree through Gabriel was to "proceed against the bastards and the reprobates, and against the children of fornication and destroy the children of fornication and the children of the Watchers. Cause them to go against one another that they may destroy each other in battle: Shorten their days" (ch. 10:9). The archangels saw "much blood being shed on the earth" (ch. 9:1) and the heavenly petition cites the whole earth being "filled with blood and unrighteousness" (ch. 9:9).

### The Nephilim's Spirits as Demons (ch. 15–16)

One of the most distinctive doctrines of the Book of Enoch is that the Nephilim do not simply die — their disembodied spirits persist as **evil spirits on earth**. The text explains the logic: they are "born from men and from the holy Watchers," so they are neither fully angelic (which would give them a heavenly dwelling) nor fully human. "On earth shall be their dwelling" (ch. 15:10).

These spirits "afflict, oppress, destroy, attack, war, destroy, and cause trouble on the earth. They take no food, but do not hunger or thirst. They cause offences but are not observed" (ch. 15, between v. 10 and 11). They shall "rise up against the children of men and against the women" until the "day of the consummation, the great judgment" (ch. 16:1).

This is the text's explicit explanation for the origin of demonic spirits: they are the ghosts of the Nephilim, condemned to wander the earth causing mischief until the Final Judgment.

### Connection to the Flood

The devastation wrought by the Nephilim forms the proximate moral cause of the Flood. The Lord's decree to Raphael to warn Noah of the coming flood is issued alongside — and in direct response to — the Nephilim's violence (ch. 10:1–3). The giants' destruction was to precede and necessitate the cleansing of the earth.
