## Definition from Source

Carroll describes sigil magic as a technique for causing effects by encoding desire into an
abstract glyph, then implanting it into the subconscious during a gnostic state. The rationale
is psychological: "The mere act of wanting is rarely effective, as the will becomes involved in a
dialogue with the mind. This dilutes magical ability in many ways. The desire becomes part of the
ego complex; the mind becomes anxious of failure. The will not to fulfill desire arises to reduce
fear of failure. Soon the original desire is a mass of conflicting ideas. Often the wished-for
result arises only when it has been forgotten. This last fact is the key to sigils and most forms
of magic spell. Sigils work because they stimulate the will to work subconsciously, bypassing
the mind."

Carroll traces the technique to [[Austin Osman Spare]] (though he does not name Spare explicitly
in this section) and notes the three-stage operation:

**1. Construction.** A glyph of desire is created, "stylized so as not to immediately suggest
the desire." Three methods are given:
- *Word method* — letters of a statement of intent are combined/overlapped into a single symbol.
- *Pictorial method* — a picture of the desired outcome is abstracted into a simplified image.
- *Mantrical method* — a word or phrase is similarly encoded as a sound-sigil.

**2. Losing (forgetting).** "Both the sigil form and the associated desire must be banished from
normal waking consciousness." The sigil may be burnt, buried, or cast away. Repetition of a word
spell can empty the mind of its associated desire.

**3. Charging.** The sigil is concentrated on during a state of [[Gnosis (chaos magic)]] — during
magical trance, at orgasm, during intense fear, anger, embarrassment, frustration, or high
elation. "After holding the sigil in the mind for as long as possible, it is wise to banish it by
evoking laughter."

Sigils also appear in evocation (creating elemental beings from combined attribute-symbols),
invocation (the sigil of the Augoeides), divination (sigilizing the object of divination), and
enchantment (where desire is represented "by some pictorial glyph, by a wax image, by the
characters of a magical alphabet, or by some image in the mind's eye"). Image concentration —
holding a simple shape in the mind's eye — is identified as "the basis of casting sigils and
creating independent thought forms."
