## Concept Summary from Source

A central argument in Kathryn S. Olmsted's *Real Enemies* is that the U.S. government has not merely been the *target* of conspiracy theories — it has also been one of their most prolific *producers*. Government officials, including presidents, have regularly advanced official conspiracy theories about enemies foreign and domestic. This practice dates at least to Woodrow Wilson's World War I propaganda apparatus but persists through the Bush administration's post-9/11 claims about Saddam Hussein and Al-Qaeda.

Olmsted argues that this official conspiracism performs a specific political function: it expands state power by designating domestic dissenters as agents of foreign enemies, thus criminalizing opposition and justifying surveillance, repression, and secrecy. Wilson proclaimed that Germany had "filled our unsuspecting communities … with spies" and that disloyal citizens had poured "the poison of disloyalty into the nation's arteries." Roosevelt warned of "evil forces … already within our own gates." Bush administration officials implied that critics of the Iraq War were enablers of terrorism.

The key dynamic Olmsted identifies is asymmetric: government officials want to *produce* official conspiracy theories while *suppressing* alternative ones. "Let us never tolerate outrageous conspiracy theories concerning the attacks of September the 11th," Bush said on November 10, 2001 — shortly before his administration spread its own theories about Saddam Hussein's role in 9/11. Officials arrogate to themselves the exclusive right to name conspirators.

This official conspiracism has a recursive effect: when the government promotes a culture of suspicion about hidden enemies, it teaches the public to think in conspiratorial terms. Citizens then turn that same conspiratorial logic back on the government itself. Alternative conspiracy theorists develop counter-narratives — bin Laden plotted with Dick Cheney, not Saddam — that mirror the structure of the official theory while reversing its target.

Olmsted also argues that government officials' own conspiracy theories were sometimes factually correct (the Soviet espionage penetration of U.S. institutions was real), which added credibility to the general mode of conspiratorial thinking and made it harder to dismiss antigovernment theories by contrast.

The concept illuminates why antigovernment conspiracy theories track so closely with wartime periods and moments of national crisis: these are precisely the moments when government officials most aggressively promote their own conspiracy theories about enemies within.
