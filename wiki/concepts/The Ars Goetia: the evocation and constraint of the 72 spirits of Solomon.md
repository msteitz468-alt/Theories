## Overview
The Ars Goetia ("Liber Malorum Spirituum seu Goetia," per the Harley 6483 heading — "This Booke contains all the names, Orders, and Offices of all the spirits Salomon ever conversed with. The seales and characters belonging to each spirit, and the manner of calling them forth to visible appearance") is Book One of the five-book Lemegeton. It is the wiki's umbrella concept for the whole operative system presented in Part I: a catalog of 72 named infernal kings, dukes, princes, marquises, earls, and presidents that King Solomon is said to have bound into a brass vessel and then invoked individually, plus the full ritual apparatus (circle, triangle, hexagram/pentagram, magical ring, secret seal, conjurations, curse, and licence to depart) by which the magician "calls forth" and dismisses each spirit.

Peterson's introduction frames Goetia as "a Greek term more or less synonymous with magic, but with negative connotations, as distinguished from the more elevated Theurgia ('working of a god')." He traces the Goetia's textual history to Johann Weyer's *Pseudomonarchia daemonum* (published 1563 as an appendix to *De Praestigiis Daemonum*), noting close but imperfect correspondence: the order of spirits differs "almost as if a stack of cards got scrambled," four spirits in the Goetia (Vassago, Seere, Dantalion, Andromalius) are absent from Weyer, and Weyer's Pruflas/Bufas is missing from the Goetia. Peterson also identifies Agrippa's *Three Books of Occult Philosophy* (the "Scale of the Number Seven" and "Scale of the Number Ten") and Peter de Abano's *Heptameron* as sources for the vessel's Hebrew lettering, the magic circle, and the ritual structure respectively.

The concept unifies two claims owned on this page's cluster: the catalog of the 72 spirits themselves (their names, ranks, legions, and offices), and the ritual machinery of constraint used to compel and dismiss them.
