**Definition from Source**

The source's central linguistic argument: the medieval term "Sangraal" or "Sangreal" (the Holy Grail as found in many manuscripts including Malory) was "subsequently broken in the wrong place." Rather than *San Graal* (Holy Cup/Grail), it should be read as *Sang Real* or *Sang Raal* — in modern French, *Sang Royal*: "Royal Blood."

The Grail, therefore, is not a physical object (a cup) but a bloodline — specifically, the divine bloodline descended from Jesus, transmitted through Mary Magdalene to Gaul and eventually to the Merovingian dynasty. This is the "Holy Blood" of the title.

**Key Evidence Cited in Source**

1. In many early manuscripts, the term appears as a single word: "Sangraal" or "Sangreal."
2. The Grail romances are suffused with genealogy, lineage, pedigree, heritage, and inheritance — more consistent with a bloodline than a cup.
3. The traditional associations of the Grail with blood (a receptacle for Jesus's blood at the cross, or the cup of the Last Supper) reinforce the blood/bloodline reading.
4. Medieval traditions hold that the Magdalene brought the Grail (i.e., the bloodline/offspring) to France — not to Britain.
5. The Grail romances are set in Merovingian times and refer to a "Grail family" culminating in Godfroi de Bouillon — identified by the source as a Merovingian scion.
