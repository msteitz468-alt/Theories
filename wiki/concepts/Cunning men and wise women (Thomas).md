## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas documents the "cunning man" or "wise woman" — also called charmer, blesser, conjurer, sorcerer, or white witch — as the standard village practitioner of practical magic in Tudor and Stuart England, present "in every village" according to Robert Burton. Their services ranged across healing (of both people and livestock), finding lost or stolen goods, fortune-telling, love magic, diagnosing bewitchment, and protective charms for childbirth. They typically charged fees, sometimes specialized in particular ailments, and worked with a mixture of herbal remedies, debased Catholic prayers, written charms worn on the body, and simple divinatory apparatus (sieve and shears, key and book, crystal or mirror).

## Key Claims
- The term "cunning" originally meant simply knowing more than other people, not necessarily implying supernatural power; many practitioners denied using "charms" and claimed only herbal medicine.
- Named practitioners Thomas records in detail include Margaret Hunt (1528), Joan Tyrry of Taunton (who claimed the fairies told her who was a witch), Elizabeth Page of Somerset, Elizabeth Mortlock and Agnes Hancock (girdle-measurers), and the London poulterer Grig, revered "as if he had been a god."
- Clergy regarded the cunning folk as their most dangerous rivals for the laity's loyalty: "Hath not the blesser more proselytes and patients than the physician... yea than the conscionable preacher?"
- Ecclesiastical courts pursued cunning folk as routinely as other minor moral offenders, but punishment was usually light (public penance, excommunication for non-appearance) — nowhere near the severity reserved for maleficent witches.
- Ordinary people frequently saw no contradiction between orthodox piety and consulting a cunning man; churchwardens themselves sometimes hired wizards to recover stolen church property.
- The most famous later exemplar of magical healing by touch, Valentine Greatrakes, stood in the same tradition of personally-gifted, non-institutional healers.
