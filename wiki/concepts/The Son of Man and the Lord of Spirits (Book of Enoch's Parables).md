## Definition and Characterisation from Source

The **Book of Parables** (1 Enoch ch. 37–71), composed c. late 1st century BC, introduces two of the most theologically significant figures in the Enochic corpus: the **Lord of Spirits** and the **Son of Man / Elect One**.

### The Lord of Spirits

Throughout the Parables, God is consistently addressed as the "**Lord of Spirits**" and the "**Head of Days**" — an ancient, white-haired figure seated on a throne of glory (ch. 47:3, 71:10). The title "Lord of Spirits" emphasises divine sovereignty over all angelic and spiritual beings, over kings and mighty men, and over cosmic forces (sun, moon, lightning, winds, rain). The Lord of Spirits has foreknowledge of all things, maintains cosmic order through oath and covenant, and is the ultimate judge before whom all deeds are weighed.

### The Son of Man / Elect One

The central visionary figure encountered by Enoch in the Parables is a pre-existent heavenly being described across two modes:

**As "the Son of Man"**: Enoch sees "One whose face looked ancient" (the Head of Days) accompanied by "another being whose countenance had the appearance of a man, and his face was full of graciousness, like one of the holy angels" (ch. 46:1). When Enoch asks who this is, the accompanying angel replies:

> "This is the Son of Man who hath righteousness, with whom dwells righteousness, and who reveals all the treasures of that which is hidden, because the Lord of spirits hath chosen him, and whose lot has preeminence before the Lord of spirits in righteousness and is for ever." (ch. 46:3)

**As "the Elect One"**: Enoch sees the Elect One's dwelling-place "under the wings of the Lord of spirits" (ch. 39:7) and observes him seated on the throne of glory to execute judgment (ch. 45:3, 51:3, 61:8, 62:2).

### Pre-existence and Cosmic Status

The Parables make a strong claim about the Son of Man's pre-cosmic origin: "Even before the sun and the signs were created, before the stars of heaven were made, His name was named before the Lord of spirits" (ch. 48:3) and "from the beginning the Son of Man was hidden, and the Most High preserved Him in the presence of His might, and revealed Him to the elect" (ch. 62:7).

He is also described in unmistakably universal terms: "He shall be a staff to the righteous… the light of the Gentiles, and the hope of those who are troubled of heart. All who dwell on earth shall fall down and worship before him" (ch. 48:4–5).

### The Judgment of Kings and the Mighty

The dominant function of the Son of Man / Elect One in the Parables is judicial and eschatological:

- "He shall raise up the kings and the mighty from their seats, and the strong from their thrones and shall loosen the reins of the strong, and break the teeth of the sinners" (ch. 46:4).
- "He shall put down the kings from their thrones and kingdoms because they do not exalt and praise Him" (ch. 46:5).
- On the great day, kings and the mighty who ruled by the power of their wealth and denied the Lord of Spirits shall be delivered to angels of punishment, filled with shame, and cast into darkness (ch. 62–63).
- The Elect One sits on the throne of glory; "the spirit of righteousness was poured out on Him, and the word of His mouth slays all the sinners" (ch. 62:2).

The kings are shown to have constructed power on riches and "gods which they have made with their hands" (ch. 46:7). Their confession at the end is that they failed to acknowledge who gave them their kingdoms (ch. 63:4).

### Enoch's Identification with the Son of Man (ch. 70–71)

In a climactic and theologically controversial passage, Enoch's own spirit is "raised aloft on the chariots of the spirit" and he is brought before the Head of Days. An angel addresses him: "This is the Son of Man who is born to righteousness, and righteousness abides over him" (ch. 71:14). The text appears to identify Enoch himself with the Son of Man — a doctrine with implications for later Metatron traditions (developed explicitly in 3 Enoch).

## Significance in the Enochic Corpus

The Parables provide the only pre-Christian Jewish text (outside Daniel) that unambiguously speaks of a pre-existent, heavenly "Son of Man" figure who will sit in judgment. Scholars note that the New Testament's use of the title "Son of Man" in apocalyptic contexts likely draws on this Enochic tradition.
