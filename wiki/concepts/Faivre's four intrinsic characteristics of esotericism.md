# Faivre's four intrinsic characteristics of esotericism

## Definition from Source
In Chapters 6 and 7, Hanegraaff refers to Antoine Faivre's influential typological definition of Western esotericism in terms of a set of "intrinsic characteristics" without restating the full list of four in this range; the two he names here are:

1. **Correspondences** — described as "the first intrinsic characteristic of Western esotericism": the notion that even the remotest parts of the universe correspond to one another through secret sympathy grounded in inherent similarity (similitudo), classically expressed in Plotinus's image of the universe as "one living creature." Hanegraaff notes this idea overlaps with "analogical thinking," "correlative thinking," "ressemblance," "signatures," "participation," and "synchronicity."
2. **Imagination/mediation** — the idea that "true imagination" (as opposed to mere fantasy) is a faculty of perception granting access to ontologically real worlds. Hanegraaff links this Faivrean characteristic to Henry Corbin's concept of the *mundus imaginalis*, and illustrates it with practices of trained visualization such as Golden Dawn ritual magic.

Hanegraaff does not enumerate Faivre's other two characteristics (commonly given elsewhere in Faivre scholarship as "living nature" and "the experience of transmutation," alongside secondary characteristics of concordance, transmission, and practices of the concordance) within the Chapter 6–7 text; only correspondences and imagination/mediation appear explicitly in this range.
