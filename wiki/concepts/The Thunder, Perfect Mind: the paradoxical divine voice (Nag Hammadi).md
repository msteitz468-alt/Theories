## Definition from Source

The Thunder, Perfect Mind (Codex VI, tractate 2; CG VI.2:13,1–21,32) is a Nag Hammadi text in which a divine female voice — sent forth "from the Power" — delivers an extended self-revelation in paradoxical antithetical pairs. The voice refuses every single category that human language offers: she is simultaneously each pole of every opposition. The name "Thunder" (Bronte) echoes a divine epithet known from Hellenistic-Egyptian religion; "Perfect Mind" (Nous Teleios) identifies her as a complete divine intellect.

## Description from Source

The tractate opens with the speaker announcing her origin and her summons:

> "I was sent forth from the power, / and I have come to those who reflect upon me, / and I was found among those who seek after me. / Look upon me, you who reflect upon me, / and you hearers, hear me!" (CG VI.2:13,2–10; trans. George W. MacRae)

She then unleashes a torrent of antithetical self-predications — what scholars sometimes call the "I am" or *ego eimi* style — in which every statement is immediately countered by its opposite. The voice is at once:

- **First and last**: "For I am the first and the last."
- **Holy and disgraced**: "I am the honored one and the scorned one. / I am the whore and the holy one."
- **Wife and virgin, mother and daughter**: "I am the wife and the virgin. / I am \<the mother\> and the daughter."
- **Knowledge and ignorance**: "For I am knowledge and ignorance. / I am shame and boldness."
- **Silence and word**: "I am the incomprehensible silence and the much-remembered thought. / I am the voice of many sounds and the utterance (logos) of many forms."
- **War and peace**: "I am war and peace."
- **Life and Death, Law and Lawlessness**: "I am she who is called Life and you have called Death. / I am she who is called Law and you have called Lawlessness."

A recurring motif is the speaker's presence among the despised and discarded — she is found on the garbage-heap, among those slain, among prostitutes — and she commands her hearers not to look away from her in those places. She claims to be wisdom for Greeks and gnosis for non-Greeks; to have multiple images in Egypt; to dwell everywhere yet be known by neither the close nor the far. Near the text's close, the voice identifies herself as the "perfect mind":

> "But I am the \[perfect\] mind and the repose of the \[...\] / I am the gnosis of my seeking, and the finding of those who seek after me." (CG VI.2:18,15–20)

The rhetorical force of the text is its refusal to let any stable identity be pinned on the divine: the sacred is radically beyond binary categories, and awakening lies in holding the tension of both poles simultaneously.

### Literary and religious parallels

The "I am" style echoes Isis aretalogies (hymns in which Isis lists her divine attributes and deeds) known from Greek-Egyptian religion, and anticipates later Wisdom (Sophia) literature in which divine Wisdom calls out from the streets to those who would hear her. The multiple identities claimed also resonate with the Gnostic Sophia figure and with the figure of Barbelo (the divine Mother-Father of Sethian Gnosticism).
