## Definition from Source

"The London Connection" is Mullins's thesis (Chs. 5–6) that the Federal Reserve — and through it, the United States — is ultimately controlled from the City of London, by a small group of London merchant banks tied to the House of Rothschild. Per the source: "in 1910, the United States was for all practical purposes being ruled from England ... centered in the London Branch of the House of Rothschild ... and so it is today."

## Characterization in the Source

- The chain of control: the [[Federal Reserve System (Mullins account)|Federal Reserve Bank of New York]] is controlled by its principal stockholder banks (in 1914: National City Bank, First National Bank, National Bank of Commerce), whose owners (Stillman/Rockefeller, Baker/Morgan, Schiff/Warburg) "all have a 'London Connection.'"
- Mullins names five London merchant banks that "actually control the New York banks which own the controlling interest in the Federal Reserve Bank of New York": Schroder (#2), Morgan Grenfell (#6, "the London branch of the House of Morgan and actually its dominant branch"), Lazard Brothers (#8), N.M. Rothschild (#9), and Brown Shipley (#14, London branch of Brown Brothers Harriman).
- **Gold-price control**: "Each day, representatives of four other London banking firms meet in the offices of N.M. Rothschild Company in London to fix the price of gold for that day." Because all credit must ultimately rest on gold, "the London merchant bankers, with their power to set the price of gold each day, become the final arbiters of the volume of money and the price of money."
- Mullins quotes a Morgan Grenfell head that London's pre-eminence rests on the fact that "only the American banks have a lender of last resort. The Federal Reserve Board ... can, and does, create dollars when necessary."
