## Definition from Source

Outer importance is the attribution of excessive meaning or significance to external objects, events, people, or goals. It is one of the two forms of [[Excess Potential]] identified in Ch. IV, the other being [[Inner Importance]].

"Outer importance: attributing huge meaning to external objects/events." (Ch. IV)

## Key Properties (from source)

- **Directed outward**: attaches excessive significance to things outside the self — a desired outcome, another person's opinion, money, status, a relationship
- **Creates excess potential**: by making external things excessively important, the person creates an energetic imbalance that [[Law of Balance]] forces act to correct — typically by frustrating the very thing held as important
- **The obstacle function**: "The only obstacle on the path to fulfilling your desires is the projection of artificial importance." (Ch. IV) — outer importance is the primary self-sabotage mechanism for goal achievement
- **Awe as obstacle**: in Ch. VIII (Widening Your Comfort Zone), awe toward the world of one's dream — treating a desired goal as something extraordinary or unavailable — is identified as outer importance that prevents the [[Comfort Zone (Transurfing)]] from expanding to include the goal

## Outer Importance and Goals (from source)

The source dedicates significant space to the problem of outer importance in relation to goals:
- Treating money as the goal rather than an accompanying attribute creates outer importance around money
- Treating wealth, fame, or achievement as belonging to "chosen others" creates outer importance that prevents the person from giving themselves permission to have
- Feeling awe or anxiety when entering expensive shops is identified as outer importance that signals the comfort zone does not yet include wealth
