## Definition from Source

According to [[Theresa Bane]]'s *Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures*, African and Mesoamerican demonologies are distinct traditions that Bane covers primarily through the Hausa (West African), Yoruba-adjacent, and East African sources for Africa, and through Aztec and Mayan sources for Mesoamerica. Bane groups these in her encyclopedia as separate entries but they share the characteristic, found across many traditions she surveys, of demons that were originally worshipped as gods before being reclassified as demonic beings — a process she documents occurring through colonial and missionary contact.

**Africa:** The African demonological material in Bane's encyclopedia centers heavily on the Hausa (northern Nigeria/West Africa) Bori tradition, in which demons (bori) are possessing spirits of disease, misfortune, and bad luck. These spirits are invoked through specific possession dances, rhythms, and animal sacrifices, and the possessed person (the Mai-Bori) can divine the future while inhabited. Each Bori demon has its own particular dance rhythm and may be appeased rather than exorcised. In East African traditions hyenas serve as mounts and familiars for evil sorcerers. The umbrella deity Abezethibou is identified by Bane as "the demon of African spirits."

**Mesoamerica:** In Aztec demonology the overarching ruler is Mictantecutli — the great Lord of Hell — beneath whom all other demons operate. He captures, tortures, and imprisons evil souls in the darkest recesses of his realm and is most powerful at midnight, when he opens the door of Hell and lets malign spirits wander the world until sunrise. The Tzitzimimeh (singular: tzitzimitl) are skeletal celestial demons who descend to devour humans during solar eclipses; they were worshipped as fertility goddesses by midwives before Aztec conquest. In Mayan demonology, the dead descend through nine levels of the underworld (Xibalba) presided over by demonic lords: Ahpuch is the chief demon of death, while Hunhau commands the lowest level, Mitnal.

## Notable Demons

### African

**Bori** — In the demonology of the Hausa people of West Africa and Nigeria, the bori are possessing spirits of bad luck and disease. When they manifest they appear as humans with hoofed feet who act oddly and have a distant, dreamy look. They terrify people by appearing as the man of the house or as headless figures. Primarily possessor demons who prefer women; the possessed person is called a Mai-Bori. Each bori has a particular rhythm it responds to; specific dances are performed to identify and placate the demon. They are easily repelled by iron.

**Nana Ayesha** — In North and West African demonology, the demon of eye sores and smallpox; born the daughter of Zanzanna. She can be persuaded not to spread her diseases by the sacrificial offering of speckled fowls.

**Mahalbiya** ("Markswoman") — From the demonology of the Hausa people; the demon of skin diseases who also causes fevers, sores, and topical ulcers. Born the daughter of the demon Jigo. To cure someone afflicted by her, a specific magical dance is performed, followed by animal sacrifice.

**Mai-Gangaddi** — In West African demonology, a demon that causes sleeping sickness. To save victims, a shaman performs a specific dance, falling to the floor as if asleep, then rising and dancing repeatedly. Animal sacrifice is typically required.

**Mai-Ja-Chikki** — From the Hausa people's demonology; appears as a serpent and causes the evil eye and stomach ulcers. Can be prevented with regular offerings of eggs and milk.

**Hyenas (East Africa)** — In East African demonology, hyenas are considered allies of evil sorcerers and witches; nocturnal animals used as mounts and sent to hunt and kill enemies. Their eyes glow yellow when possessed by a spirit. The substance secreted from their anal gland, "hyena butter," is collected and used in dark magic.

### Mesoamerican (Aztec)

**Mictantecutli** — The great Lord of Hell in the Aztec pantheon, beneath whom all other demons operate. He captures evil souls, tortures them, and locks them up forever. Depicted in the Codex Vaticanus A as an animated skeleton enthroned among symbols of sorcery, corpses, dishes filled with human hearts, and owls. Most powerful at midnight.

**Tzitzimimeh** (singular: Tzitzimitl) — Celestial demons in Aztec demonology whose name translates to "monsters descending from above." They appear as skeletal females wearing skirts with a skull and crossbones pattern. During solar eclipses they descend to devour humans. Before Aztec conquest they were considered fertility goddesses worshipped by midwives.

**Itzcoliuhqui** — In the demonology of the ancient Aztec people, a demonic god and an aspect of Quetzalcoatl or Tezcatlipoca (sources vary). He presides over the West, uses maya (illusions), and is described as judgmental.

**Xipe Totec** — Originally worshipped by the Aztecs as a god of penitence; demonic ghost who appears wherever there is bloodshed, sucking the blood of those who fall asleep during penance. Renowned as a trickster who on one occasion tricked an entire village into falling off a cliff.

### Mesoamerican (Maya)

**Ahpuch** — A demonic god of death in Mayan mythology; ruler of Mictlan (the lowest and worst of the nine Hells). Described as looking like a skeleton seated upon a throne, holding a sacrificial knife. Dogs and owls are his sacred animals.

**Hunhau** — Possibly a demonic manifestation of Ahpuch in Mayan demonology; a chief and demon of death under the command of Ahpuch. He has dominion over Mitnal, the ninth level of the Mayan underworld.

**Balam** — Mayan for "jaguar"; the demon of avarice and greed in Mayan demonology, a fallen angel formerly of the Order of Dominions. He commands forty legions and has three heads: one of a bull, one of a man, one of a ram. He rides upon a bear carrying a goshawk.

**Shtabai** — Serpentine demons in Mayan demonology that can shape-shift into human appearance; the females are renowned seducers of men who can cause their ruin. They live in caves.
