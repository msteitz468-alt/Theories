## Definition / Characterization from Source

Graham Hancock's central thesis across *Fingerprints of the Gods* (1995) and its sequel *Magicians of the Gods* (2015): that a technologically and astronomically advanced human civilization flourished during the last Ice Age and was destroyed in a global cataclysm around the end of the Pleistocene, leaving only scattered survivors who seeded their knowledge — agriculture, monumental architecture, astronomy, and a precessional "code" — into the hunter-gatherer cultures that became the known ancient civilizations.

Hancock is explicit that his "gods" are **human survivors of a lost culture, not extraterrestrials** — distinguishing his view from von Däniken and Sitchin. In *Fingerprints* the cataclysm is framed through Charles Hapgood's [[Earth-crust displacement (Hapgood)]]; in *Magicians* it is re-grounded in the [[The Younger Dryas comet impact (Hancock)]]. The hypothesis is rejected by mainstream archaeology (see [[The lost-continent myth: Atlantis and Mu debunked (Feder)]]).
