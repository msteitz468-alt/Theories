## Concept from Source

Across Chapters 19–25 of [[The Demon-Haunted World (Sagan)]], Sagan develops the argument that scientific literacy and the habits of skeptical thought are not merely useful — they are **structurally necessary for a free democratic society to survive**. This is one of the book's central theses.

The argument operates on several levels:

**Epistemological**: Without a scientifically literate citizenry, people cannot evaluate the claims of their leaders, corporations, or media. They become, in Jefferson's phrase (which Sagan quotes), "putty in the hands of those in power."

**Historical**: Sagan traces the connection between the European Enlightenment, the rise of scientific thinking, and the founding principles of the US Constitution. He argues that the Founders — Jefferson, Franklin, Madison — were genuinely shaped by scientific method and that the Bill of Rights embodies error-correction machinery analogous to scientific self-criticism.

**Structural**: Democratic error-correction requires that citizens be able to question authority. Sagan argues that the same habits of mind — openness to new evidence, willingness to revise beliefs, demanding standards of proof, freedom of inquiry — underpin both good science and functional democracy. They rise and fall together.

**The witch-craze parallel**: Sagan uses the historical witch-persecution as an illustration of what happens when a society lacks these safeguards. The procedural protections of the Bill of Rights (trial by jury, no self-incrimination, due process, separation of church and state) are explicitly legible as responses to the abuses catalogued in the witch trials.

**Threat diagnosis**: As of the mid-1990s, Sagan sees declining science education, a media culture that rewards pseudoscience and sensation over critical thinking, and the concentration of information control in a small number of corporations as active dangers to democratic self-governance.

The concept connects to Sagan's argument about Frederick Douglass (Ch. 21): just as literacy was the path from slavery to freedom for Douglass, scientific literacy is the modern equivalent — the capacity to interrogate claims and govern oneself.
