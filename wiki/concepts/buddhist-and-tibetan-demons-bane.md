## Definition from Source

According to [[Theresa Bane]]'s *Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures*, Buddhist and Tibetan demonology forms a distinct but deeply intertwined tradition, with important regional variants across India, Sri Lanka (Singhalese Buddhism), Nepal, and Tibet proper (including the pre-Buddhist Bon religion). Bane notes that the Buddhist tradition inherits the Indian concept of the Asuras as fallen god-figures but frames demons primarily as obstacles to enlightenment and as forces that tempt, distract, or consume the spiritually vulnerable. The principal demonic antagonist in Buddhism is Mara (also known as Papiyan), the tempter who embodies triple thirst — desire for existence, pleasure, and power — and who commanded armies against the Buddha's awakening before ultimately being converted into a Dharma-protector.

Tibetan demonology, as Bane presents it, is especially rich. It draws on the indigenous Bon tradition, layers of Vajrayana Buddhism, and a cosmology populated by wrathful deities, tutelary spirits, and demonic classes organized into ranked hierarchies. Many Tibetan demons were originally gods that were demonized or converted through the introduction of Buddhism, a pattern Bane documents repeatedly. The "angry ones" of Tibetan demonology are flesh-eaters who serve fresh human brains in a skull chalice. The Khado (dakini) are elemental female beings who operate on instinct rather than intention.

## Notable Demons

**Mara / Papiyan** — The central demonic figure of Buddhism; his name translates as "death," "thirst," or "evil one." Originally the king of the Heaven of Sensual Delight before his conflict with the Buddha, he commanded an army of demons and his three daughters — Lust, Thirst, and Delight. With one hundred arms and a monstrous form, he captures the souls of the dying and causes darkness to surround a person. After his conversion he became a Dharma-protector of the Buddha.

**Varsavarti** — "He who fulfills desires"; the king of the Heaven of Sensual Delight in Buddhist demonology and the demon of death, sin, and temptation. He captures and collects the souls of the dying and enables the triple thirst (existence, pleasure, power). He occasionally shape-shifts into a vulture while wandering the earth homeless.

**Bhutadamara** ("Turmoil of the Spirits") — In Buddhist demonology, this demonic god is titled lord of the demons and keeps all other demons in check. He has four arms and three eyes, and is often depicted in the alidha pose in devotional art. He holds a thunderbolt-scepter in his upper right hand and a noose in his left.

**Marakayikas** — In Buddhist demonology, a class of demons born of Mara the Evil One, said to be under the command of Papiyan. They attend Papiyan and are depicted in Buddhist art alongside him.

**Angry Ones, The** — In Tibetan demonology, a class of demons that consume the flesh of man and serve fresh human brains in a skull chalice.

**Lhamo** — In Tibetan mythology, the principal protector of Tibet and protector of the Dalai Lama lineage; consort of Mahakala. Originally a demon and one of the Wrathful Deities, she was converted to Buddhism. Described as having dark blue skin, three eyes, and red hair; she rides upon a white mule whose rump bears an extra eye, and drinks from a chalice. The saddle she sits upon is made of the flesh of her own son.

**Pekar** — In Tibetan demonology, a prince and regent of the northerly quarter of Hell. Born the reincarnation of the demon Mudu Tankhar, he has three faces and six arms and rides upon a white tiger or lion. He has five aspects — Activity, Body, Knowledge, Mind, and Speech — each with a distinct appearance and sacred color.

**Dud** — In Tibetan mythology, a demonic figure associated with "smoke"; Bane records 12,000 servitors and tens of thousands of angels under his command.

**Khado** — Originating in Tibetan lore, the khado are a type of dakini: mindless, naked, elemental female beings with green or red eyes who occasionally appear as old women. Symbols of primordial spontaneity, they operate only on animal instinct and seek out holy men in meditation to disturb them with flashes of inspiration. They live in the highest mountain peaks.

**Lhamayin** — In Tibetan demonology, "not a god"; a class of demonic beings ranked below the gods.

**Sri** — Originating in the Bon religion of Tibet, the sri is a demonic subterranean vampiric being.

**Gnod-sbyin** — A Tibetan demonic class that preys exclusively on humans.
