## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas uses "judicial astrology" (as distinct from astronomy, with which "astrology" was often used synonymously) to denote the practical, predictive branch of astrological science as practised in Tudor and Stuart England. He identifies four constituent branches: general predictions (forecasting weather, crops, epidemics, politics and war from eclipses and planetary conjunctions), nativities (horoscopes cast at birth, followed by "annual revolutions"), elections (choosing propitious times for action), and horary questions (resolving a specific question by reading the heavens at the exact moment it was asked). Astrology rested on the Ptolemaic cosmology in which the immutable celestial world ruled the mutable "sublunary" sphere, and it was, in Thomas's words, "less a separate discipline than an aspect of a generally accepted world picture," bound up with medicine, botany, metallurgy, psychology and ethnography.

## Key Claims
- Astrology was not an esoteric or fringe belief but part of the educated Englishman's standard picture of the universe at the opening of the sixteenth century.
- The system was inherently flexible and irreducibly subjective in application, since the interpretation of the "welter of combinations and permutations" of planets, houses, signs and qualities depended on the practitioner's judgement rather than mechanical calculation.
- The crucial period of astrology's popular reach in England was the Interregnum and later seventeenth century, when vernacular treatises and almanacs (by [[William Lilly (1602–1681)]] and others) put the whole subject before a mass readership for the first time.
- Astrology functioned simultaneously as natural science, medical adjunct, business advice, and psychological counsel, giving it a comprehensiveness of application matched by no rival system except religion itself.
- The astronomical revolution (Copernicus to Newton), and especially the collapse of the Aristotelian distinction between sublunary and celestial bodies, ultimately destroyed astrology's intellectual credibility, even though heliocentrism as such was not incompatible with astrological calculation.
