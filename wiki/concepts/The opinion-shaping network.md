## Definition / Characterization from Source

Domhoff defines the opinion-shaping network as the constellation of corporate public-affairs departments, large public relations firms, and financially supported nonprofit organizations through which the corporate rich spend "hundreds of millions of dollars each year, or perhaps a few billions, attempting to shape public opinion on policy issues." The network operates on multiple levels simultaneously: at the benign end, it tries to "build upon and reinforce the underlying principles of the American belief system" — individualism, free enterprise, minimal government — by cultivating relationships with schools, churches, and voluntary associations. At its "darker side," it "attempts to create a sense of crisis or doubt on specific issues or to enforce limits on the range of acceptable opinion, and disrupt activist groups that are having an impact on specific industries."

The network's architecture places the major policy-discussion groups (e.g., the Business Roundtable, the Council on Foreign Relations) at its core, but they "usually do not enter into the opinion-shaping process directly." Execution and coordination are handled by "the public affairs departments of major corporations and large public relations firms," which feed into "a widespread dissemination network" of local advertising agencies, media organizations, and single-issue committees. The result is that the network is "extremely diverse and diffuse at its point of direct contact with the general public."

The network's origins trace to the Rockefellers' hiring of PR agents in the 1910s–1920s following the deadly suppression of a miner's strike at Ludlow, Colorado — a crisis that made corporate image management a permanent institutional priority.
