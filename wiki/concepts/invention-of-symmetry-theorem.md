## Definition from Source

The **Invention of Symmetry Theorem** is a result that Donald Hoffman conjectured and the mathematician **Chetan Prakash** proved, stated in chapter 7 of *[[The Case Against Reality (Why Evolution Hid the Truth from Our Eyes)]]*.

It states that **symmetries in our perceptions entail nothing about the structure of objective reality**. The proof is *constructive*: it shows precisely how perceptions and actions can enjoy a symmetry — such as translation, rotation, mirror, or Lorentz symmetry — in a world that lacks any symmetry at all.

## Context and Significance

- It rebuts a specific realist argument (made by the vision scientist **Zygmunt Pizlo**): that because we see animal bodies as mirror-symmetric, mirror symmetry must be a real feature of objective reality. The theorem shows this inference is invalid.
- It applies to *any* perceptual system, biological or machine: a robot built to see the symmetries we see grants no insight into the world's structure.
- Hoffman's positive explanation for why we perceive symmetry anyway: **data compression and error correction**. Their algorithms often use symmetries (e.g. predicting an apple's appearance after a small movement needs only a few numbers — a rotation and translation — rather than millions per view). Symmetries in perception reveal how we *encode* information, not the nature of reality.
- Companion result to the [[Fitness-Beats-Truth (FBT) Theorem]]: both use the formal structure of universal Darwinism / information theory to constrain any evolved perceptual system regardless of its contents.
