## Definition and Characterization from Source

The "Second State" in [[Robert Monroe (1915–1995)|Robert A. Monroe]]'s framework refers to a non-physical reality or mode of consciousness that exists parallel to and independent of physical embodiment. It is not dream, hallucination, or imagination but rather an ontologically real (in Monroe's view) dimension of existence accessible through systematic technique.

The Second State is characterized by:
1. Maintenance of full consciousness throughout experience
2. Existence of the [[Concept: The Second Body|Second Body]] 
3. Different sensory modalities and perception mechanisms
4. Thought-responsive environment
5. Presence of other conscious beings
6. Structured [[Concept: Locales in Non-Physical Reality|Locales]] with distinct characteristics

## Nature of Experience

### Continuous Consciousness
Monroe emphasizes repeatedly that experience in the Second State maintains unbroken conscious awareness. Unlike dreams, which involve loss of consciousness followed by recalled fragments, Second State experience involves:
- No loss of consciousness at transition
- No discontinuity from "lying down in bed" through separation to Second Body activity
- Full memory formation during activity
- Clear recall upon physical reawakening

Monroe documents: "there had been no loss of consciousness" and "I had the feeling that I had no break in consciousness, no pre-sleep. Almost immediately, I felt..."

### Reality of Experience
Monroe argues forcefully that Second State experiences are genuinely real events occurring in an actual non-physical dimension, not subjective mental phenomena:

"What does this mean? Does that great consciousness change take place while still alive physically? Or in another reality, later? Who are the instructors, the helpers?"

The experiences are real enough that Monroe built a research institute and trained others to replicate his findings. Consensus among multiple independent explorers strengthens the case for objective reality.

### Difference from Dreams and Imagination
Monroe distinguishes Second State experience from dreams through:
- Clarity and vividness of memory
- Continuous consciousness throughout
- Repeatability and control
- Correspondence (in Locale I) with verifiable physical reality
- Ability to test and verify details against physical world

## Structural Features of the Second State

### Physical Analogs
The Second State contains non-physical versions of physical structures:
- Buildings and architecture
- Landscapes and geography
- Objects and their properties
- Spatial relationships

However, these are not identical to physical structures but rather non-physical representations or equivalents responsive to thought and consciousness.

### Thought-Responsive Nature
The Second State environment responds to consciousness and thought in ways the physical world does not:
- Objects can be modified through deliberate thought
- Movement is controlled through mental intention
- One's appearance can be transformed through thought
- The mind's tendency to rationalize shapes perception of unknown phenomena

Monroe notes: "To put it another way, there seems to be nothing that thought cannot produce in this new-old other life."

This invites caution: "be absolutely sure of the results you desire, and constantly in control of the thoughts you engender."

### Sensory Modalities
The Second State employs different sensory systems than the physical body:

**Vision**: 360-degree, thought-modulated perception that allows seeing in all directions simultaneously, controlled by conscious intention. Initially appears dim but clarifies with use.

**Communication**: Occurs through direct thought transmission, not vocalization. The mind receives thoughts and translates them into language form.

**Personality Recognition**: Unique to Second State—direct perception of inner personality through "emanation patterns" that cannot be concealed.

**Touch**: Maintains strongest parallel to physical sensation.

**Smell and Taste**: Conspicuously absent in Monroe's experience.

## Inhabitants and Society

The Second State contains:

### Human Consciousness
People in sleep or meditative states, experienced as conscious entities in their non-physical aspect. Monroe conducted experiments visiting and observing living persons in the Second State.

### Non-Physical Entities
Beings apparently native to or permanent residents of the Second State:
- Advanced conscious entities
- "Robed figures" interpreted as guides or teachers
- Various classifications of beings at different development levels
- Entities capable of communication and purposeful activity

### Hierarchical Organization
Monroe suggests gradations of being and consciousness, with some entities more advanced, educated, or spiritually developed than others.

## Relationship to Physical Reality

### Causality and Influence
Monroe questions whether consciousness in the Second State can influence physical reality:
- Can Second State activity affect physical events?
- Do encounters with other beings in Second State represent genuine communication?
- Are there etheric or subtle energy bridges between realms?

These questions remain largely unanswered in *Journeys Out of the Body*, presented as open research questions.

### Connection to Afterlife
Monroe suggests the Second State may be related to or continuous with the afterlife experience:

"Post Mortem" (Chapter 7) is a chapter title addressing death and what consciousness might experience after physical death. Monroe implies the Second State represents a preview or continuation of existence beyond physical life.

This raises theological and philosophical implications:
- If consciousness survives separation from physical body during life, perhaps it survives physical death
- The Second State might represent the "heaven" or non-physical realm described in various religious traditions
- Understanding the Second State now might illuminate the nature of post-death existence

## Perceptual Challenges in the Second State

### Mind's Rationalization
The mind tends to rationalize unfamiliar perceptions by matching them to past memory and experience. This produces:
- Misidentification of novel objects
- False translations of unfamiliar phenomena
- Systematic errors in reporting non-physical experiences

Monroe demonstrates this with extended examples:
- Van de Graaff generator perceived as "child's automobile"
- Bus journey misreported as airplane flight
- Complex unfamiliar events reconstructed to match most similar past experience

### Consensus as Verification Method
Because individual perception in the Second State is subject to systematic bias and rationalization, Monroe argues for consensus among multiple independent observers:

"This is the reason for the need for others to experience the same conditions. The entire picture may become clear with the help of such additional reports."

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Monroe introduces but does not fully develop the concept of "Focus levels" as a way of categorizing different states of consciousness or different frequencies in the Second State. This appears to be foundational to his later work but receives limited explicit treatment in *Journeys Out of the Body*.
