## Definition from Source

Feder's foundational chapter on **how the past is actually known**: not by authority, revelation, or
how appealing a story is, but by **evidence tested through the scientific method** — proposing
explanations, deducing what they imply, and checking those implications against material data. He
distinguishes *believing* something from *knowing* it (his example of a stone monument on Bear
Mountain that for years wrongly marked Connecticut's "highest ground" — a confident marker that was
simply mistaken).

## Description from Source

This epistemology is Feder's yardstick for the rest of the book: pseudo-archaeology fails not because
its conclusions are merely unpopular but because they ignore, distort, or cannot survive testing
against the evidence. It is the archaeological complement to Sagan's
[[The Baloney Detection Kit (Sagan)|baloney detection]] and the demarcation arguments of
[[Why people believe weird things (Shermer's thesis)|Shermer]]. The full method underlies the
umbrella [[Pseudo-archaeology and how to detect it (Feder)]].
