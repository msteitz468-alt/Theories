# Gnosis, reason, and faith: the three ways of knowing (Hanegraaff)

## Definition from Source
In Chapter 5 ("Knowledge"), Hanegraaff sets out a threefold typology of claims to knowledge in order to locate what is distinctive about Western esotericism. "Reason" designates claims communicated in discursive language and independently testable (science, philosophy, scholarship). "Faith" designates claims communicated in discursive language but not independently testable (e.g. Christian dogma, accepted on authority and tradition). "Gnosis" designates a third, esoteric type of claim: knowledge that is neither independently testable nor even communicable through discursive language, yet is held with total conviction and presented as superior to the other two. Hanegraaff grounds this in the Corpus Hermeticum's distinction between reasoned discourse (logos), faith (pistis), and mind (nous) as the faculty of gnosis, and traces how, before the Enlightenment, gnosis was seen as the capstone of a hierarchy that still required and respected reason and faith (Ficino, Agrippa, Bruno, Swedenborg were all rigorous scholars and devout Christians), whereas after the Enlightenment gnosis was increasingly set in polemical opposition to both, becoming a self-sufficient "counterculture" claim (Blavatsky, New Age spirituality). He further argues that the concrete mechanism underlying claims to gnosis is "alterations of consciousness" — a range of well-documented psychophysiological states (trance, ecstasy, dissociation) — and that in post-Enlightenment esotericism these experiences come to be treated as empirical "proof" of spiritual realities rather than merely ineffable glimpses of the Absolute.
