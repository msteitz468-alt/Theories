## Concept from Source

Barkun's chapter "Armageddon Below" (Chapter 7 of *A Culture of Conspiracy*) traces how UFO conspiracism developed a distinctive subterranean mythology — a literalization of underworld evil — in which malevolent aliens inhabit deep underground military bases (DUMBs), tunnel networks, and caverns beneath the American West. Barkun presents this as one of ufology's "most idiosyncratic variations," distinct from the New World Order ideas it borrowed from Christian patriot circles and fusing together several independent traditions: hollow-earth and crank-science speculation, Theosophical and neo-Theosophical occultism, pulp science fiction, and post–Cold War suspicions about secret government bunkers.

### The Dulce Legend

The narrative's epicenter is the alleged underground base near Dulce, New Mexico. Barkun traces its origins to Paul Bennewitz, an Albuquerque businessman who from 1979 onward believed he was intercepting electronic signals from alien spacecraft and installations. By the early 1980s, Bennewitz had identified the Dulce site; the story entered wider circulation in 1987–1988 through public statements by John Lear and writings by William F. Hamilton III and Jason Bishop III. Barkun notes that the region's pre-existing cattle-mutilation stories and UFO sightings gave Dulce a pre-built conspiratorial aura. The base legend features a government–alien treaty (brokered with the CIA), treaty violations, and violent firefights between Delta Force and the aliens — details, Barkun observes, "well outside even the most far-fetched reports of secret underground bases" yet consistent with the wider domain of stigmatized knowledge.

### Occult Genealogy: Theosophy and Inner-Earth Lore

Barkun identifies Madame Helena Blavatsky and the Theosophical Society (founded 1875) as a key conduit. Drawing on Ignatius Donnelly's Atlantis revival, Blavatsky posited a worldwide "net-work of subterranean passages" left by Atlantean civilization. Her followers elaborated this; Guy W. Ballard (founder of the "I AM" Religious Activity) claimed meetings with Ascended Master Saint Germain in caverns beneath the Grand Teton Mountains. Maurice Doreal (Claude Doggins), founder of the Brotherhood of the White Temple (~1930), developed an explicit inner-earth cosmology featuring underground races communicated by Atlantean guides beneath Mount Shasta. Doreal's pamphlets introduced the crucial "Serpent Race" — beings with human bodies but serpentine heads, possessing hypnotic shape-shifting powers — which Barkun identifies as the direct ancestor of the 1990s reptilian alien.

### The Shaver Mystery and Pulp Fiction

Barkun traces a second genealogical thread through pulp science fiction. Between 1945 and 1948, *Amazing Stories* editor Raymond Palmer published Richard S. Shaver's mythos of "deros" (detrimental robots) — enslaved humans in underground caverns whose psychic powers wreak havoc above ground. Presented as true, the Shaver Mystery prefigured UFO conspiracism before the first publicized sighting in 1947, and Palmer himself later championed the view that flying saucers originated from inside the earth. Barkun also traces the shape-changing serpent-men concept to Robert E. Howard's 1929 *Weird Tales* story "The Shadow Kingdom," later picked up by H. P. Lovecraft and Clark Ashton Smith, and then appropriated as supposed historical fact by occultists like Robert Ernst Dickhoff (*Agharta*, 1951). This, Barkun notes, is a characteristic "fact-fiction reversal" of stigmatized knowledge.

### Agharti / Shambhala

A third strand concerns the Asian underground realm of Agharti (or Shambhala), popularized in English by Ferdinand Ossendowski's 1922 *Beasts, Men and Gods*. Palmer absorbed Ossendowski's "King of the World" into his editorial mythology, placing a Venusian ruler in Agharti. Barkun observes that the benevolent subterranean redeemer of the Agharti tradition ran simultaneously in *Amazing Stories* alongside Shaver's vision of underground evil — demonstrating how contradictory inner-earth materials were absorbed into a single stigmatized-knowledge pool.

### Branton and the Reptilian Conspiracy

By the 1990s, "Branton" (identified as Bruce Alan Walton of Utah) synthesized the accumulated inner-earth and reptilian material into a comprehensive superconspiracy: a cosmic multi-species war fought in a gigantic system of caverns beneath the western United States ("Dreamland"). Barkun quotes Branton's view that the United States is "the last obstacle standing in the way of the joint Reptilian-Bavarian Illuminati's new world order." David Icke absorbed Branton's writings as authoritative, amplifying the reptilian thesis to a global audience and linking the underground aliens with the Babylonian Brotherhood — a connection Barkun cross-references to Icke's [[The Biggest Secret (Icke, 1999)]] and [[Children of the Matrix (Icke, 2001)]].

### Symbolic Logic: Hell Below

Barkun identifies the deep symbolic logic underlying the subterranean alien thesis: underground realms are culturally coded as the domain of the dead, the location of hell, and the dwelling of Satan. The reptilians are identified with the serpent of Eden and with Fallen Angels. Barkun notes that reptoid conspiracists retain elements of Christian Identity's "two-seedline" theology (the serpent's seed vs. Eve's seed locked in apocalyptic war) while typically stopping short of its explicit anti-Semitism — though he flags figures like Commander X whose language about "high finance" slides toward coded anti-Jewish scapegoating.

### Dulce as Subterranean Armageddon

Barkun's chapter concludes that in reptoid conspiracism the Dulce base and its underground analogues have acquired millennial significance: they are the locus of the final conflict. The battles allegedly already in progress are "the prelude to a subterranean Armageddon." This literalization of eschatological geography — heaven above, hell and its reptilian servants below — is, Barkun argues, the inner-earth myth's most distinctive contribution to superconspiracy belief.
