**Definition from Source**

The "Dossiers secrets" (Secret Dossiers) or "Prieuré documents" refers to a collection of documents deposited in the Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris — primarily during the 1960s — associated with Pierre Plantard de Saint-Clair and his associates. The authors of the source used this corpus as a primary evidentiary base alongside external archival confirmation.

**Contents Described in Source**

The documents include:
- Lists of alleged Grand Masters of the Prieuré de Sion (the "Nautonnier" list)
- Genealogies of Merovingian descent tracing bloodlines to the present
- A list of Templar Grand Masters (more accurate, the source claims, than any other known list)
- Internal organizational documents and statutes
- Miscellaneous historical materials connecting the Sion story to Rennes-le-Château and other sites
- A work attributed to "Madeleine Blancassal" entitled *Les Descendants mérovingiens et l'énigme du Razès*

A key document is *Le Serpent Rouge* (The Red Serpent), dated January 17, 1967, containing a Merovingian genealogy, two maps of France, and thirteen prose poems in the form of an allegorical pilgrimage — one for each sign of the zodiac plus a thirteenth, Ophiuchus (the serpent-bearer). This text is described as containing "unmistakeable echoes of what we had encountered in the course of our own investigation."

**Reliability as Assessed by the Source**

The source treats the documents with careful skepticism while noting a striking track record of external confirmation:
- The Orleans municipal archives confirm the charters installing the Ordre de Sion there
- The Inquisition records confirm the "Caput LVIIIm" head and Guillaume de Gisors
- The Dossiers secrets list of Templar Grand Masters is assessed as more accurate than any published alternative
- Many minor details (specific abbots, land transactions, dates) are confirmed by independent archival sources

The source acknowledges the documents may have been fabricated — but argues that a fabrication of this consistency, accuracy on verifiable details, and inclusion of obscure nobles (rather than only famous names) would itself require access to privileged historical information.
