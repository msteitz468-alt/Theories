# Western esotericism as a scholarly category

## Definition from Source
Hanegraaff opens the Guide by insisting that "Western esotericism" names a modern, artificial, retrospective category rather than a natural tradition that "already existed out there and merely needed to be discovered by historians." The substantive term is recent: coined in German (*Esoterik*) in 1792, migrating to French (*l'ésotérisme*) by 1828 and into English in 1883. It was applied after the fact to a heterogeneous range of currents that, before the later seventeenth century, were not seen as belonging to one single field at all. Yet Hanegraaff insists this does not make the category unreal: it emerged because scholars noticed genuine "structural similarities" among the worldviews and ideas of a wide variety of thinkers and movements.

The field also does not fit any established discipline — it is neither religion, philosophy, nor science as commonly understood, though it touches all three and the arts besides. Before the eighteenth-century watershed that created the modern academic disciplines, it was a recognized, if controversial, focus of intellectual inquiry; afterward it became "academically homeless."

Hanegraaff surveys three competing scholarly models for defining the category by prototype rather than formal criteria: (1) an "enchanted" pre-Enlightenment worldview flourishing in the early modern period (Faivre, Yates), (2) a modern/postmodern "occult" phenomenon centered on post-Enlightenment social organizations (sociologists of religion), and (3) a universal "inner" spiritual dimension common to all religions (religionism). He declines to adopt any of these as normative, instead treating Western esotericism as "a radically pluralistic field of currents, ideas and practices" studied from late antiquity to the present without privileging any period or worldview as most truly "esoteric." His own working formulation is that the field is (1) set apart by mainstream culture as its defining "other," and (2) characterized by worldviews and epistemologies at odds with normative post-Enlightenment intellectual culture — i.e., the closest thing to a definition offered in the Introduction/Chapter One.
