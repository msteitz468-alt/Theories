## Concept Summary from Source

Kathryn S. Olmsted argues in *Real Enemies* that the modern American conspiracy theory — specifically the theory that the *federal government itself* is the conspirator — could not have existed before the twentieth century. The pre-modern state was simply too small and too weak to be feared as a conspiratorial actor. It was the explosive growth of federal power during and after World War I that created the conditions in which antigovernment conspiracy theories could flourish.

Olmsted identifies three interlocking mechanisms through which government secrecy and propaganda generated and sustained conspiracy theories:

**1. Real conspiracies by the state.** As the government grew, it gained the capacity to carry out actual secret operations against its own citizens — drafting men, commandeering industries, suppressing dissent, assassinating foreign leaders, conducting psychological experiments on unwitting subjects, and launching domestic covert-action programs (COINTELPRO). When these operations were eventually exposed, they validated the premise of antigovernment conspiracy theories and made even more extreme claims seem plausible.

**2. Official propaganda as conspiracy-theory incubation.** The Wilson administration's Committee on Public Information (CPI) set a precedent by promoting an elaborate official narrative — that Germany had conspired to conquer the world and that domestic dissenters were enemy agents — while simultaneously suppressing, imprisoning, and censoring those who offered alternative interpretations. This manipulation of public opinion taught Americans that their government could and would lie to them, and planted the seed of lasting distrust.

**3. Surveillance and harassment of dissenters.** The government's efforts to spy on and suppress those who challenged its narratives — from the Espionage and Sedition Acts of 1917–18 through J. Edgar Hoover's FBI index cards through COINTELPRO — paradoxically *confirmed* the fears of those being watched. The FBI explicitly used "enhancing paranoia" among dissident circles as a tactical objective. When dissidents later learned they had been surveilled, they felt vindicated.

Olmsted argues that this was a self-reinforcing cycle: official secrecy bred suspicion; exposure of real conspiracies validated the suspicion; the government's attempts to crush alternative narratives taught ordinary citizens that the government feared the truth. Each generation of conspiracy theorists inherited a larger documented record of actual government malfeasance on which to build.

The transformation Olmsted traces is fundamental: before World War I, American conspiracy theorists mainly feared *alien forces* (Catholics, Masons, Jews, foreign powers) plotting to *capture* the government. After World War I, they feared the government *itself*.
