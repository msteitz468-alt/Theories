## Definition from Source

Crowley teaches that "there is a single main definition of the object of all magical Ritual. It is the uniting of the Microcosm with the Macrocosm. The Supreme and Complete Ritual is therefore the Invocation of the Holy Guardian Angel; or, in the language of Mysticism, Union with God."

The Great Work is defined as "the raising of the whole man in perfect balance to the power of Infinity." Crowley connects it directly to the personal work he undertook at Cambridge: "the Work of becoming a Spiritual Being, free from the constraints, accidents, and deceptions of material existence."

## Description from Source

**The Central Aim**

Crowley is emphatic that the Knowledge and Conversation of the Holy Guardian Angel (abbreviated K&C HGA throughout Thelemic literature) is not merely one operation among others but the only truly legitimate goal. In Chapter XXI he writes: "As was said at the opening of the second chapter, the Single Supreme Ritual is the attainment of the Knowledge and Conversation of the Holy Guardian Angel. It is the raising of the complete man in a vertical straight line. Any deviation from this line tends to become black magic. Any other operation is black magic."

All other magical operations — healing, divination, invocation of planetary forces — are legitimate only insofar as they are "necessary preliminaries to That One Work."

**The Name and its deliberate conventionalism**

Crowley explains why he chose this particular framing. He could have called the goal "God," "the Higher Self," "the Augoeides," "Adi-Buddha," or sixty-one other names — and had discovered that all were one. He chose the phrase "the obtaining of the Knowledge and Conversation of the Holy Guardian Angel" precisely because "the theory implied in these words is so patently absurd that only simpletons would waste much time in analysing it. It would be accepted as a convention, and no one would incur the grave danger of building a philosophical system upon it." The phrase derives from the *Book of the Sacred Magic of Abramelin the Mage*, which prescribes a six-month retirement for its attainment.

The Holy Guardian Angel, Crowley notes elsewhere, is identified by him as Aiwaz — "the solar-phallic-hermetic 'Lucifer'" — described as his own Angel and as the dictating intelligence behind *The Book of the Law*. He insists this figure is not "the enemy of Man" but the initiator who bids mankind "Know Thyself."

**The Great Work in its stages**

The Great Work unfolds in stages corresponding to the grades of the A∴A∴ (Crowley's magical order):

1. The attainment of the K&C HGA constitutes the grade of Adept of the Inner Order (5°=6°, corresponding to Tiphareth on the Tree of Life).
2. Beyond this lies the "Adventure of the Abyss" — the crossing of Daath, the dissolution of the ego in the City of the Pyramids — which makes the Master of the Temple (8°=3°, Binah).
3. Beyond that, grades that cannot be described.

Crowley frames this progression through the Formula of IAO: the magician as king is stripped and slain (the crossing of the Abyss) and rises to the Knowledge and Conversation as a new and superior state. Similarly, the Formula of Tetragrammaton describes the Great Work in its cosmic aspect: "The first step of this is the attainment of the Knowledge and Conversation of the Holy Guardian Angel, which constitutes the Adept of the Inner Order."

**The HGA and True Will**

The Knowledge and Conversation of the Holy Guardian Angel is inseparable in Crowley's system from the discovery and execution of True Will. Until the K&C is attained, "he does not know himself; how can he explain his business to others?" Divination on matters directly relating to the Great Work is improper, for "in the Knowledge and Conversation of his Holy Guardian Angel, the adept is possessed of all he can possibly need." The HGA's will is constant and eternal: "It is His constant and eternal Will to become one with the Aspirant, and the moment the conditions of the latter make it possible, That Bridal is consummated."

**The Word and the Lost Name**

Crowley connects the Great Work to the recovery of the "Lost Word" of Masonic legend — the lost Tetragrammaton: "Let the Magician earnestly seek this Lost Word, for its pronunciation is synonymous with the accomplishment of the Great Work." Each magician's Great Work, and therefore his Word, is unique: "Each man has a different Great Work, just as no two points on the circumference of a circle are connected with the centre by the same radius."

**Microcosm and Macrocosm**

The Great Work is expressed structurally as the equilibration of the microcosm (the magician as pentagram, the grade 5°=6°) with the macrocosm (the hexagram). "The Great Work is to make the initial [Vau] of Assiah (the world of material illusion) into the final [Vau] of Atziluth, the world of pure reality." The word ABRAHADABRA — eleven letters, the number of Magick — "represents the Great Work complete, and it is therefore an archetype of all lesser magical operations."
