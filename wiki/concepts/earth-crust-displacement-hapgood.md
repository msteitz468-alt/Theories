## Definition / Characterization from Source

Charles Hapgood's hypothesis (in *Earth's Shifting Crust*, 1958, with a foreword by Albert Einstein) that the entire lithosphere — the rigid outer crust of the Earth — can periodically slide as a single unit over the more fluid layers beneath, repositioning whole continents relative to the poles in a geologically short time. Hancock adopts this as the mechanism behind his lost-civilization thesis: a landmass once at temperate latitudes (he argues, **Antarctica**) could have been carried to the South Pole at the end of the last Ice Age, burying an advanced civilization under miles of ice and triggering the floods and catastrophes recorded in global myth.

It is distinct from mainstream plate tectonics (slow movement of separate plates) and is not accepted by conventional geology; Hancock presents it as the best explanation for the anomalies he assembles.
