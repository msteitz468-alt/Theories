## Definition / Characterization from Source

**Earth-crust displacement** is a geological hypothesis formulated by Charles Hapgood (first detailed in *Earth's Shifting Crust*, 1958, with a foreword by Albert Einstein, and expanded in *Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings*, 1966). The theory proposes that the Earth's entire lithosphere—the rigid outer crust—behaves like the loose skin of an orange, periodically sliding as a single, solid unit over the weaker, semi-fluid mantle layer beneath it. This movement shifts entire continents into different latitudes relative to the poles in a geologically brief period of time (on the order of a few thousand or tens of thousand years).

According to Hapgood:
* **The Mechanism:** The displacement is driven by the polar ice caps. Because the ice sheets accumulate off-center relative to the Earth's axis of rotation, their rotation generates a massive centrifugal force. This force acts horizontally on the Earth's crust. Once the ice caps reach a critical mass, the centrifugal pressure overcomes the friction and structural strength of the lithosphere, causing it to fail and slide.
* **The Transition:** During the last displacement, the crust shifted approximately 2,000 miles along the 90th meridian of West Longitude. This shifted North America (whose pole was centered in Hudson Bay or Quebec) southward out of the polar zone, melting the great Laurentide ice sheet. Conversely, it shifted the previously temperate continent of Antarctica (which was located 2,000 miles further north, outside the Antarctic Circle) into the South Pole, initiating its glaciation.
* **Tectonic Contrast:** It is distinct from mainstream plate tectonics, which describes the slow, independent drift of individual plates over millions of years. Earth-crust displacement involves the rapid, simultaneous movement of the entire crust as one piece.

In alternative history, Graham Hancock adopts this mechanism as the explanation for his lost-civilization thesis, arguing that an advanced Ice Age civilization was established on a temperate Antarctica before the crustal shift carried it to the pole, burying its ruins under miles of ice and triggering global floods and climate cataclysms.
