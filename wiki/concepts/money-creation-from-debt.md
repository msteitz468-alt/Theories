## Definition from Source

A central theme of the later chapters is that the Federal Reserve creates money out of debt — by purchasing government securities with checks drawn on itself, creating reserves that did not previously exist, and conversely "extinguishing" reserves (and money) by selling securities. The source treats Marriner Eccles's congressional testimony as the clearest statement of the mechanism.

## Characterization in the Source

Mullins quotes [[Marriner Eccles]] repeatedly:
- "Money is created out of the right to issue credit-money."
- "When you sell bonds on the open market, you extinguish reserves."
- "If there were no debts in our money system, there would be no money."
- Asked where the money to buy $2 billion in securities (1933) came from: "We created it ... Out of the right to issue credit money ... there is nothing behind it ... except our Government's credit."
- "All money is created by debt — either private or public debt."

The source also quotes economist Benjamin Anderson explaining that when the Reserve Banks buy government securities they "create reserves ... and then money exists which did not exist before," and Henry Ford: "The one aim of these financiers is world control by the creation of inextinguishable debts." Mullins ties the mechanism to the gold standard and to the System's growth from $143 million (1914) to $45 billion (1949) in assets.
