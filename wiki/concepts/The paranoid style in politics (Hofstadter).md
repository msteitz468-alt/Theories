## Definition from Source

The umbrella concept of Hofstadter's essay: the **"paranoid style"** is a recurring mode of political
expression characterized by "heated exaggeration, suspiciousness, and conspiratorial fantasy."
Hofstadter borrows the clinical word *paranoid* only as a metaphor for a *style* — "the way in which
ideas are believed and advocated rather than … the truth or falsity of their content" — and stresses
it is used by "more or less normal people," not the certifiably ill. Unlike the clinical paranoiac,
the paranoid spokesman sees the conspiracy as directed not against himself but against "a nation, a
culture, a way of life."

## Description from Source

The style's defining features (each detailed below) are: a central image of a **vast, sinister
conspiracy as the motive force of history**
([[The central image: history as a vast demonic conspiracy (Hofstadter)]]); a pedantic
"scholarship" that accumulates evidence and then makes a **leap into fantasy**
([[The paranoid scholar and the leap into fantasy (Hofstadter)]]); apocalypticism and a demand for
total victory; and the **projection of and imitation of the enemy**. Hofstadter links its episodic
mobilization to **status anxiety and dispossession**
([[The pseudo-conservative revolt and status politics (Hofstadter)]]).

This is the wiki's key **meta-counterweight**: it characterizes the *rhetorical mode* of much of the
conspiracy corpus — [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]], [[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]],
[[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]], [[The Illuminati (Marrs)]], [[Conspirators' Hierarchy: The Story of the Committee of 300 (Coleman)]],
[[None Dare Call It Conspiracy (Allen & Abraham, 1971)]], [[Secret Societies and Subversive Movements (Webster)]] —
and complements the skeptics [[The Demon-Haunted World (Sagan)]] and [[Why People Believe Weird Things (Shermer)]].
