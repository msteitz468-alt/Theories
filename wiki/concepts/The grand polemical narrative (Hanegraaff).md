# The grand polemical narrative (Hanegraaff)

## Definition from Source
Hanegraaff's term for the multi-century, three-act discursive process — spanning Pre-Reformation Christianity, Protestantism and Modernity — by which the category now called "Western esotericism" was constructed as the rejected "Other" of mainstream Christian and secular culture. He frames apologetics and polemics as mutually implicated: denouncing another's doctrines as false simultaneously asserts one's own claim to orthodoxy, and defending one's own position means attacking that of others. What is at stake in this rhetoric is discursive power — the ability to decide what counts as valid and true, which can be translated into real political power to silence or discredit dissenting voices. Hanegraaff distinguishes "history" (what actually happened) from "mnemohistory" (how a culture remembers/imagines it happened), arguing that in the real world mnemohistorical narratives about esotericism have proven far more powerful than the careful historical record, "regardless of whether they have any basis in demonstrable fact." He organizes the narrative into three acts: (1) Early Christianity/Rome establishing identity against Judaism and paganism via the apologetic fathers and the anti-heretical polemicists; (2) Protestantism turning anti-paganism against Roman Catholicism itself (anti-apologeticism, the Pietist reaction); (3) Modernity, in which Enlightenment eclecticism dismissed the whole domain as "superstition" while a later Romantic/Jungian/Eranos religionist reaction rehabilitated it as an "enchanted" inner spirituality — both approaches, he argues, being "ultimately anti-historical."
