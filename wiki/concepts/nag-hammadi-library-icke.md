## Definition and Characterisation (from the Source)

In *Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told* (2018), David Icke describes the Nag Hammadi Library as his primary documentary evidence for the Gnostic cosmological framework he adopts. The library consists of "13 leather-bound papyrus codices (manuscripts) and more than 50 texts written in Coptic Egyptian" found "in a sealed jar in 1945 near the town of Nag Hammadi about 75-80 miles north of Luxor on the banks of the River Nile in Egypt."

The texts are described as the work of Gnostics — "not a racial group so much as a way of perceiving reality under the heading of Gnosticism." Gnosticism comes from the Greek word *gnosis* (secret knowledge); "gnostic means 'learned.'"

Key Gnostic concepts from the texts as Icke reads them:
- The Demiurge (Yaldabaoth): the evil false-creator of the material world, identified with the Old Testament Yahweh/Jehovah
- Archons: the Demiurge's servants, the most common manifestations of which are "reptilian or serpentine" or "an unborn baby or foetus with grey skin and dark, unmoving eyes"
- *Nous* (intellect/ego): the limited, programmed mode of human awareness that the Archontic system exploits
- *Pneuma* (Infinite Self): the expanded awareness beyond nous through which "salvation" or liberation is achieved
- The material world as a "bad copy" of the perfect Upper Aeon original

Historical suppression: "Gnostics were active in many locations and were targeted mercilessly by the Roman Church which felt severely threatened by the way the foundation of its own belief system was being turned on its head." The Library of Alexandria (nearly half a million scrolls) is described as "dominated by Gnostic thought" until its destruction.

Hypatia (c. 350–415 AD): identified as "an Athens-educated mathematician, astronomer and philosopher ... head of the Platonist school" in Alexandria who was targeted by the Roman Church, cited as an example of violent suppression of Gnostic knowledge.

Icke emphasises that he reached his conclusions about the Archontic distortion from other sources before encountering the Nag Hammadi texts, describing this as important: the texts confirmed his prior conclusions rather than forming their foundation.
