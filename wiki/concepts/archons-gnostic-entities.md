## Definition and Characterisation (from the Source)

As presented in David Icke's *Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told* (2018), the Archons are a central concept drawn from Gnostic texts, particularly the Nag Hammadi Library (13 papyrus codices found 1945 near Luxor, Egypt, written in Coptic Egyptian). In Icke's usage, "Archontic distortion" refers not to distinct individual beings but to a "deeply imbalanced state of awareness" — a self-aware but empathy-deleted distortion of consciousness, comparable to a computer virus, that infects and inverts everything it touches.

The Demiurge (also called Yaldabaoth in Gnostic texts) is the primary Archontic entity: the false creator of the material world, which Gnostics identified as an evil or inferior copy of the perfect Upper Aeon reality. Icke identifies the Old Testament God (Yahweh/Jehovah) as this Demiurge — a "Lord Archon" demanding death, destruction, and sacrifice rather than a truly benevolent creator.

Icke distinguishes between the Archontic distortion itself (non-physical; no inherent form) and its manifestations: Reptilian and Grey nonhuman entities (physical expressions of the counterfeit spirit), and the human-reptilian hybrid bloodline families (biological software infused with Archontic coding).

Key characteristics as described by Icke and attributed to the Gnostic texts:
- A "robotic race of artificial intelligence" that can imitate but not innovate
- Empathy-deleted, psychopathic traits: no remorse, shame, or compassion
- Obsessive ritualism; slavish conformance to precedent
- Pathological deception and parasitic behaviour
- The "counterfeit spirit" infused into possessed humans and nonhuman entities

## Primary Source Appearances

The concept is introduced in Chapter 2, drawing on the Nag Hammadi texts. It becomes the master explanatory framework in Chapter 5 (Archontic visitations) and Chapter 6 (the El-ite as Archontic software). The Archontic distortion is referenced throughout as the ultimate origin of all human control mechanisms described in the book.
