## Definition / Characterization from Source

**Corporate Socialism** is a term coined by Antony C. Sutton to describe the manipulation of state power and regulatory apparatus by private corporate interests and international financiers to eliminate competition, regulate markets, and establish centralized monopolies.

According to Sutton's work:
* **The Illusion of Opposition:** While the public is led to believe that "Big Business" and state socialism (or Communism) are diametrically opposed, they share a common goal: the centralization of planning and the elimination of competition. A free-market economy forces businesses to compete and accept risk, whereas a corporate-socialist state uses the coercive power of the government to guarantee profits and protect monopolies.
* **Capitalist-Collectivist Partnership:** Under a corporate-socialist framework, international financiers fund and support collectivist regimes (such as Bolshevik Russia and National Socialist Germany). By doing so, they can operate within state-directed economies as monopoly partners, partitioning global markets and excluding free-enterprise competitors.
* **The State as an Instrument:** Business leaders who align with corporate socialism do not serve the national interest. Instead, they occupy key positions in government and regulatory agencies to utilize the seal and authority of the state for their own profit-maximizing goals, leveraging taxpayer funds and government contracts to subsidize their ventures.
