**Definition/Characterization from the Source**
In *The Secrets of Solomon's Temple*, Kevin L. Gest presents the Vesica Piscis — the almond-shaped area formed where two circles of equal diameter overlap such that the circumference of each touches the center of the other — as the foundational geometric figure ("sacred geometry") from which ancient masons and later medieval builders derived the circle, the square, the equilateral triangle, 30°/60°/90° angles, the Gothic arch, and (per Gest's own claimed discovery) both the hexagram (Star of David) and the pentagram when each is enclosed within a circle to become "Solomon's Seal." Gest attributes Euclid (c. 300 BCE) with recording the geometric principle of bisecting a line using two overlapping circles, and traces Vesica Piscis-derived design into Peterborough Cathedral, Lincoln Cathedral's rose windows, the cathedral of St. Denis near Paris, a church window in Lewes, and the Sussex Masonic Temple itself.

Gest treats the Vesica Piscis as the geometric parent of other recurring symbols in his wider analysis:
the golden proportion (phi), the square-root constants (√2, √3, √5), the pentagram/Star of David, the
octagon, and the Masonic square and compasses emblem, all of which he claims can be derived from vesica
constructions. He applies it to the Brighton Masonic Temple's floor plan and pavement circles, to
Peterborough Cathedral's 78-foot circles and Chartres Cathedral's floor plan (both allegedly laid out
using three circles in vesica form), to the Knights Templar Round Church at the Temple (London), and to
octagonal Templar church geometry (Rosslyn Chapel, the Round Churches at Cambridge and Northampton),
which he links to the same 22.5-degree angular system he finds in the Brighton Temple's pavement
lozenges. He also states the vesica symbol appears prominently above the main door of Chartres
Cathedral, enclosing an image of Jesus.
