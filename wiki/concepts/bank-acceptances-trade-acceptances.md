## Definition from Source

Mullins treats the introduction of the acceptance system of credit into the United States as one of the most consequential and least-documented effects of the Federal Reserve Act — "the changeover from the open-book system of credit to the acceptance system, which has wrought such vast changes in our practice of commerce." A bank (or trade) acceptance is a time draft accepted (guaranteed) by a bank; the source presents the acceptance business as a European central-banking practice that Paul Warburg imported.

## Characterization in the Source

- The source says the only material it could find on the changeover was American Acceptance Council pamphlets (1915–1928).
- Quoting Goldenweiser, Mullins states that "the purchase and liquidation by Reserve Banks of acceptances was left outside the control of the Open Market Committee, and even of the Federal Reserve Board" — carried on as a separate activity by the Reserve Banks — and adds that this was "because the acceptance business was the private preserve of Paul Warburg."
- W. H. Allen (*Moody's*, 1916) is quoted that the acceptance channel gave Wall Street access, through sales of acceptances "virtually guaranteed by the United States Government," to the great central banks of Europe.
