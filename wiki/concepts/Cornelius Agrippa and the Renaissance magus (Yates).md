## Cornelius Agrippa and the Renaissance magus from Source

Yates devotes Chapter VII of Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (1964) to [[Cornelius Agrippa (1486–1535)]]'s De occulta philosophia as the first comprehensive survey of Renaissance magic and the work that carried the Ficino-Pico synthesis to its logical extreme: a fully systematized, three-tiered magical philosophy culminating in the Magus as priest and co-operator with God.

### The Three-World Framework

Agrippa's defining contribution, in Yates's reading, is his explicit tripartite division of the universe into three worlds — elemental, celestial, and intellectual — each with its corresponding magical practice:

- **Book I (Natural magic)**: Magic operating in the elemental world through occult virtues in natural objects, following Ficino's spiritus theory, but without Ficino's caution about demonic forces.
- **Book II (Celestial or mathematical magic)**: Magic operating through number, mathematical harmony, and celestial images (talismans), involving the Pythagorean conviction that number is the key to operations.
- **Book III (Ceremonial or religious magic)**: Magic operating in the intellectual world through angels, divine names, Sephiroth, religious ceremonies — drawing on practical Cabala derived ultimately from Pico.

Yates stresses that Agrippa does not follow Ficino in carefully avoiding the demonic side of astral magic. Where Ficino aimed only at stellar spiritus, Agrippa openly pursues the full demonic-angelic magic of the Asclepius: "such is the concordance of the world that celestial things draw supercelestial things, and natural things, supernatural things." The Agrippan Magus aspires to "mount higher than the heavens, elevating himself above the angels to the archetype itself, with whom he then becomes co-operator and can do all things."

### Extension of Pico's Cabalism

Agrippa's third book systematizes Pico's practical Cabala into operative religious magic. He correlates the Pseudo-Dionysian angelic hierarchies with the Hebrew Sephiroth and angelic orders, producing the full Hermetic-Cabalist synthesis in which natural, celestial, and religious magic form an organic continuum. He gives tables of the ten divine Hebrew names, angelic orders, and letter-number calculations for summoning angels — material which, Yates notes, John Dee and Edward Kelley later used directly in their séances.

The logic of Agrippa's conclusion, as Yates frames it, is "something which is really very like the ideal Egyptian, or pseudo-Egyptian, society as presented in the Hermetic Asclepius" — a theocracy of priests who know magical religion, governing through activated talismans and angelic invocations grounded in an intellectual understanding of the One beyond all.

### Mathematical Magic and the Bridge to Science

Yates emphasizes that Agrippa's second book includes within the apparatus of the Magus genuine mathematical sciences and their application. A magician versed in natural philosophy, mathematics, and mechanics can produce "without any natural virtue" — that is, by purely mechanical means — wonderful operations. Agrippa cites the flying wooden pigeon of Architas, the speaking statues of Mercurius (the Asclepius's animated statues reread as applied science), and similar marvels. Yates argues this inclusion of "real artificial magic" within the Magus's curriculum was not forgotten: Campanella echoes it and John Dee's preface to Billingsley's Euclid cites Pico's mathematical conclusions in a similar spirit. The will to operate stimulated by magical thinking could slide, in Yates's account, into the will to operate in genuine applied science.

### Agrippa and Giordano Bruno

Yates notes that Giordano Bruno "got much — indeed most — of his material for his solution" of the problem of religious magic from Agrippa. The tradition flows: Ficino's natural magic → Pico's Cabala → Agrippa's survey → Bruno's Hermetic philosophy.
