## Concept from Source

Barkun's Chapter 8 ("UFOs and the Search for Scapegoats I: Anti-Catholicism and Anti-Masonry") argues that UFO conspiracism has become an unexpected refuge for nineteenth-century American nativism and its three historic targets — Catholics, Freemasons, and Jews. When New World Order ideas migrated into the UFO subculture, they carried with them the old hatreds, which Barkun treats as a case of conspiracy scapegoating: the mechanism by which anxiety about cosmic-scale threats is deflected onto identifiable social groups (the "scapegoat"), typically those already encoded in existing stigmatized-knowledge traditions as alien, hidden, and duplicitous.

### Nativism Preserved in the UFO Subculture

Barkun notes a historical paradox: by the post–World War II decades, historian David Bennett argued that nativism was effectively dead in mainstream American society. Yet in "one strange byway" — the world of political conspiracy theory, especially UFO-linked conspiracy theory — "the old hatreds are not merely preserved, they prosper." UFO conspiracists draw freely on eighteenth-century writers (Abbé Barruel), nineteenth-century figures (Alexander Hislop's *The Two Babylons*), and early twentieth-century sources (Nesta Webster's interwar secret-society writings), as if "no idea has ever been lost or forgotten." Barkun observes that this temporal promiscuity — combining space-age alien speculation with pre-modern prejudices — is a defining feature of the superconspiracy genre.

### Three Nativist Targets

Barkun identifies three groups that nineteenth-century nativists targeted and that reappear in UFO conspiracism:

**Catholics / Jesuits / Vatican.** Contemporary anti-Catholic conspiracism focuses not on doctrine but on the church's hierarchical, internationally ramified organizational structure, which makes it the model secret society. Barkun traces three strands: (1) the "Babylonian Mystery Religion" thesis, derived from Alexander Hislop (1853), which Icke and Branton reproduce verbatim — David Icke's claim that "The Roman Church and the Babylonian Brotherhood are one and the same" is Barkun's key example; (2) Jesuit conspiracy, in which Jesuits are said to cooperate with (or to have created) Scottish Rite Masonry, the Illuminati, and even Nazism; and (3) Vatican secrets, especially the Third Secret of Fatima, which Cooper's [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] claims was found to concern the Antichrist and was engineered by the aliens themselves.

**Freemasons / Illuminati.** Barkun traces the myth of Masonic world domination from early anti-Illuminatist literature through Nesta Webster and on to contemporary UFO writing. Cooper calls Freemasonry "one of the most wicked and terrible organizations upon this earth"; Icke places Masons at the center of a "sort of central meeting place" for all conspiratorial players. A recurring motif Barkun highlights is the "two Masonries" argument — an innocent public face concealing a sinister inner core revealed only above the thirty-third degree — which parallels the "two governments" structure common to the whole superconspiracy genre. Barkun also documents the Masonic absorption of UFO-specific material: Masonic symbols in the Washington D.C. street plan (including Icke's three pentagrams), the Denver airport's alleged Masonic homing beacon for alien spaceships, and Masonic connections to the JFK assassination.

**Jews / The Protocols template.** Chapter 8 ends with a transition Barkun treats as analytically central: alien-conspiracy literature creates a "perfect site for anti-Semitism" because its fascination with secrecy and duplicity structurally parallels the anti-Semitic imaginary. He flags that overt anti-Semitism is often accompanied by public disavowals, producing what he calls a "refracted racism and anti-Semitism" that allows simultaneous use and repudiation. Chapter 9 (outside Agent D's range) addresses the *Protocols of the Elders of Zion* as the template for this anti-Semitic turn; Barkun's framing in Chapter 8 establishes that the same scapegoating mechanism that targets Catholics and Masons is structurally available for antisemitic application.

### The Protocols of the Elders of Zion as Template

Barkun establishes that *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion* is cited approvingly in UFO and NWO conspiracy literature, alongside diatribes against the ADL and "the Jewish banker." His broader argument is that the *Protocols* functions as a template — a readymade framework for portraying a hidden global elite whose secret plans doom the world — that can be recycled across groups. Conspiratorial authors often use the *Protocols*' structure without acknowledging its anti-Semitic provenance, or while explicitly denying any animus toward Jews. This is what Barkun means by the "Protocols template": the document's conspiratorial architecture (secret council, multi-generational plan, control of finance and media, staged conflicts between apparent enemies) has been detached from its original target and applied universally, including to aliens, reptilians, Jesuits, and Masons.

### The Manufactured-Opposition Motif

Barkun identifies a cross-cutting motif he considers structurally fundamental to superconspiracy: apparent enemies are actually covert allies. Jesuits and Masons publicly oppose each other but secretly cooperate; communists and capitalists are both arms of the same plot; even the United States and the Soviet Union allegedly colluded. This motif — which Barkun traces to early nineteenth-century anti-Masonry — forecloses all counter-evidence: any apparent refutation can be reframed as manufactured opposition staged by the conspirators. It is the logical complement to the proof-by-rejection inversion described in the umbrella source.

### Scapegoating and the "Nativist Renascence"

Barkun's overarching argument in Chapter 8 is that UFO conspiracism has enabled a "nativist renascence": groups whose demonization was largely banned from public discourse after World War II (Catholics, Masons, Jews) survive and even flourish in the UFO-conspiracy subculture, transmitted through tracts, fringe publications, oral communication, and websites. The mechanism is scapegoating in the precise sociological sense: cosmic-scale anxieties (alien invasion, NWO takeover, subterranean Armageddon) are given a human face by identifying a pre-existing pariah group as the responsible agent. Because UFO conspiracism operates in the register of "stigmatized knowledge," it is insulated from mainstream scrutiny and can sustain nativist content that mainstream discourse excludes.
