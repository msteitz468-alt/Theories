## Definition from Source

**"Wealth is what you don't see"** is Morgan Housel's reframing (chapter 9 of *[[The Psychology of Money (Timeless Lessons on Wealth, Greed, and Happiness)]]*) of what wealth actually is: "Wealth is financial assets that haven't yet been converted into the stuff you see." It is "the nice cars not purchased. The diamonds not bought. The watches not worn." Because it consists of money *not* spent, wealth is by definition hidden.

## Key Points

- Housel distinguishes **rich** from **wealthy**: "Rich is a current income" (someone driving a $100,000 car is rich); "wealth is hidden. It's income not spent... an option not yet taken." The two are routinely confused, a source of "countless poor money decisions."
- "The only way to be wealthy is to not spend the money that you do have. It's... the very definition of wealth." (Bill Mann: "the way to be rich is to spend money you have, and to not spend money you don't have.")
- Diet/exercise analogy: being rich is doing the workout and then eating a big meal; being wealthy is turning down the treat — a gap "between what you could do and what you choose to do."
- A learning problem: because wealth is invisible, it is hard to imitate. "Ronald Read became many people's financial role model" only after death, because while alive "every penny of his wealth was hidden."
