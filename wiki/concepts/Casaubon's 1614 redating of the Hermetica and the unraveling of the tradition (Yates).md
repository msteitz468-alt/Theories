## The Concept from Source

[[Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (Yates)]] (Chapter XXI, "After Hermes Trismegistus Was Dated") treats [[Isaac Casaubon (1559–1614)]]'s 1614 philological demonstration that the Hermetic writings are post-Christian compositions as a watershed separating the Renaissance world from the modern world — though Yates observes that remarkably few people at the time treated it as such.

### The argument Casaubon made

[[Isaac Casaubon (1559–1614)]] — born in Geneva in 1559, described by his friend Joseph Scaliger as the most learned man in Europe — was invited to England in 1610 by James I to rebut Cardinal Baronius's *Annales Ecclesiastici*, a Counter-Reformation church history that uncritically repeated Lactantius's claim that Hermes Trismegistus was a genuine Gentile prophet of Christ. Casaubon embedded his attack on the Hermetica within his *De rebus sacris et ecclesiasticis exercitationes XVI* (London, 1614), dedicating it to James I. He died the same year.

Casaubon's case rested on convergent lines of evidence:

- **Silence in the classical record**: Plato, Aristotle, and the main pagan authors make no mention of Hermes Trismegistus or the Sibylline oracles. Their sudden appearance in early Christian sources is suspicious.
- **Content derived from later sources**: The *Pimander* (Corpus Hermeticum I) echoes Plato's *Timaeus*, Genesis, and St John's Gospel. Corpus Hermeticum XIII recalls Paul's Epistle to the Romans. Hymns in the texts derive from liturgies of St John Damascene or the Psalms. The "regeneration" passages echo Justin Martyr, Cyril, and Gregory Nazianzenus.
- **References to post-archaic Greek culture**: The texts mention Phidias and the Pythian games, and quote many later Greek authors.
- **Late Greek style**: The vocabulary and style are those of late, not early, Greek prose.

Casaubon concluded that the texts were written by Christian or semi-Christian authors — "forgeries made for a good purpose, yet detestable because untrue." He allowed that a real ancient person called Hermes Trismegistus probably existed, but held that the surviving texts could not have been written by him.

The annotated copy of Ficino's Latin *Pimander* (the Turnebus 1554 edition) that Casaubon used when making these notes survives in the British Museum, with his signature and marginal annotations throughout. Yates remarks that holding it "one realises, with a certain awe, that it represents the death of the Hermes Trismegistus of the Renaissance."

### Why it took so long

Yates notes with astonishment that the textual-critical methods Casaubon applied — dating from content and from style — had been developed long before by Latin humanists. The entire sixteenth century had passed, witnessing extraordinary philosophical and magical developments built on the misdating of Hermes, before anyone applied these well-known tools to the Greek *Corpus Hermeticum*. The delay, she implies, reflects how completely the religious authority of "Hermes" had insulated him from ordinary scholarly scrutiny.

The *Asclepius*, which circulated in the Renaissance only in a Latin translation (no Greek text), was not directly touched by Casaubon's philological argument, but its authority fell with the *Corpus Hermeticum* because the two had always been read together as the work of the same ancient Egyptian author.

### The uneven impact

Casaubon's exposure was tucked within an enormous polemical commentary on Baronius and could be, and was, ignored. Yates identifies several categories of response:

**Those who ignored it entirely** — Robert Fludd and Athanasius Kircher both maintained the full Renaissance Hermetic-Cabalist synthesis in the post-1614 decades as though Casaubon had never existed. Fludd (1574–1637) quotes Ficino's *Pimander* on nearly every page, treats Hermes as "near to Moses" and of canonical authority, and builds his *Utriusque cosmi historia* (first volume, 1617, also dedicated to James I) on exactly the foundations Casaubon had demolished. Kircher dates Hermes to the time of Abraham (Oedipus Aegyptiacus, 1652).

**Those who also ignored it but for different reasons** — Bruno had been burned in 1600, fourteen years before the publication; Campanella was in prison and, Yates notes, "conducted all his subsequent campaigns in total ignorance of it." Both came from a tradition entirely innocent of humanist philological scholarship; Yates is confident that even had Bruno survived into the 1620s he would never have abandoned faith in the extreme antiquity of Hermes.

**Those who accepted it and adapted** — The Cambridge Platonists Henry More and Ralph Cudworth knew and accepted Casaubon's critique. The result was a transformed Platonism: More's *Conjectura Cabbalistica* is a Mosaic-Pythagorean-Platonic synthesis conspicuously without Hermes Trismegistus, whose absence Yates notes as enormously significant. Cudworth in his *True Intellectual System of the Universe* (1678) explicitly set aside the Sibylline Oracles and the Hermetic writings as tampered-with Christian forgeries, choosing to build his case for monotheism on less suspect pagan sources.

**Those who weaponised it** — Marin Mersenne used Casaubon as a tool in his campaign against Renaissance magic and against Fludd in particular, branding the Hermetic writings "pseudo-Trismegistus" and arguing that Fludd's equation of the *Pimander* with canonical Scripture was baseless once Casaubon's critique was read.

### Yates's assessment of the watershed

Yates argues that Casaubon's discovery must be reckoned among the factors releasing seventeenth-century thinkers from magic, even though it was not immediately effective and many ignored it. She places it as one component of a complex shift: "the reign of 'Hermes Trismegistus' can be exactly dated. It begins in the late fifteenth century when Ficino translates the newly discovered Corpus Hermeticum. It ends in the early seventeenth century when Casaubon exposes him."

She also notes — following E. Garin — that the great polemic centred on Fludd and the Rosicrucians, in which Casaubon's redating became a weapon, "closes a period of the fortunes of Renaissance Hermetism." After that controversy, Hermetism ceases to maintain a dominating position at the core of main intellectual movements and sinks into esoteric channels — Rosicrucian lodges, Freemasonry, and later occult societies.

Yates adds a modern corrective: the Hermetica are not invalidated as "profoundly important documents of religious experience" by being correctly dated. Modern scholars, she notes, do not regard them as Christian forgeries and see little or no Christian influence in them — Casaubon and Cudworth were themselves so steeped in the Christian-Hermetic tradition that genuine parallels appeared to them as evidence of fraud.
