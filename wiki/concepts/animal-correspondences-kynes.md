# Animal correspondences (Kynes)

Part of the [[Magical correspondences (Kynes)]] system compiled by [[Sandra Kynes]].

See also sibling pages: [[Plant correspondences (Kynes)]] · [[Mineral correspondences (Kynes)]] · [[Magical intentions and powers (Kynes)]] · [[Deity and spirit correspondences (Kynes)]] · [[Astrological and time correspondences (Kynes)]] · [[Elemental, color, and number correspondences (Kynes)]]

## Definition from Source

Chapter Four of [[Llewellyn's Complete Book of Correspondences (Kynes)]] covers the Animal Kingdom, organized into three sections: **Animals** (terrestrial mammals and reptiles), **Birds**, and **Mythical Creatures**. Kynes frames the chapter with a shamanic and animist premise: animals have been power symbols since prehistory because people believed working with an animal's spirit granted access to its qualities. Earthbound animals represent qualities to be expressed and controlled; birds represent the soul; water creatures symbolize intuition and creativity. Animals were also understood as guides to other realms, with shamanic traditions holding that animal communication gave access to nature's secrets.

Kynes also addresses a practical ethical question: she declines to endorse acquiring physical animal materials that require harm, but accepts naturally found feathers, fur, or shed materials as gifts to be honored. Crucially, she argues that **physical objects are not required** — images, pictures, and recordings of animals carry equivalent symbolic force, because magical work operates through symbols and intent. This positions the chapter firmly within a framework where correspondence is a matter of meaning and energy rather than material substance.

Parts of animals carry their own symbolism: **horns** represent deities, the sun/moon, fertility, and majesty; **teeth, fangs, and claws** encode protection, perseverance, wisdom, and strength; **bones** hold a creature's basic essence; **skulls** were thought in shamanic traditions to possess magical powers; **eggs** represent protection and primal creative forces; **feathers** represent the flight of spirit.

Each entry follows the same multi-axis schema: zodiac, planet, season, celebration, element, energy (yin/yang), direction, color, chakra, associated deities, mythical parallels, and "Issues, Intentions & Powers."

## Organizational Notes

The animal section is notably less structured by planetary and elemental axes than the plant and mineral chapters — many entries list only powers without zodiac, planet, or chakra data, reflecting that animal symbolism in Pagan practice is more often drawn from mythological and folkloric tradition than from classical astrological correspondence. The mythical creatures section is compact (seven entries) but disproportionately dense in associations, likely because these creatures aggregate symbolism from multiple cultural traditions simultaneously. The chapter as a whole is well-suited to totem work, shamanic visualization, and ritual invocation of animal qualities — consistent with the animist and shamanic paradigm Kynes describes in her framing.
