## Definition / Characterization from Source

Mircea Eliade's defining thesis: **shamanism is pre-eminently a "technique of ecstasy."** The shaman is distinguished not by spirit-possession or magic in general but by a specific ability — to enter, at will, an ecstatic trance in which the soul leaves the body to **ascend to the sky or descend to the underworld**, traveling among spirits on behalf of the community (as healer, seer, and **psychopomp** guiding the dead).

Eliade documents the recurring structure across cultures: the **election** of the shaman through hereditary call or **initiatory crisis** (sickness, symbolic death, **dismemberment**, and rebirth); the acquisition of **helping spirits** and a secret language; the cosmology of **three cosmic zones** (sky, earth, underworld) joined by an **axis mundi** (World Tree, Cosmic Pillar, or Mountain) up and down which the shaman travels; and ecstatic feats such as **magical flight** and the **mastery of fire**. He treats shamanism as a universal archaic phenomenon and reads its ascent symbolism as a nostalgia for the lost primordial paradise. It is the scholarly root behind the wiki's modern shamanism/entheogen material (Hancock's *Supernatural*).
