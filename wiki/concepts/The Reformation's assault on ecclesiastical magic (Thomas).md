## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas's account of how Protestant reformers, from the Lollards through the Elizabethan and Stuart periods, systematically denied that any ecclesiastical ritual could work automatically or coerce divine power. He argues this was not a peripheral doctrinal dispute but a deliberate, sustained campaign to redraw the boundary between "religion" (non-coercive supplication) and "magic" (coercive, automatic ritual) — a distinction Thomas says was first clearly articulated by sixteenth-century Protestant theologians, not by nineteenth-century anthropologists. Reformers labeled Catholic consecration, exorcism, blessed objects, and formal prayer as "conjuration," "witchcraft," and "sorcery," and stripped the English Church's ritual apparatus down accordingly, though Thomas stresses the process was gradual, contested, and incomplete.

## Key Claims
- Protestant polemicists (from the Lollards' Twelve Conclusions of 1395 through Reginald Scot, Calvin, and Hobbes) argued that consecration, exorcism, holy water, the agnus dei, and above all the Mass were indistinguishable in structure from magical spells.
- The reformers reduced the seven Catholic sacraments to two (baptism and the eucharist) and stripped even these of exorcism, chrism, and other "magical" accretions.
- Consecrated ground, church buildings, and the Rogation-week perambulation of parish bounds were targets of sustained Protestant suspicion, with radical sectaries (Barrow, the Root and Branch petitioners) denying any inherent holiness to place.
- Protestant theology relocated the sanction for truth-telling from supernatural fear of a broken oath toward internal conscience and, increasingly, secular self-interest.
- Despite the campaign's vehemence, Thomas documents extensive survivals — holy wells, saints' names, protective charms, and popular belief in the curative power of communion elements — persisting well into the seventeenth century and beyond, especially in less evangelized regions.
- The Reformation's success in discrediting ecclesiastical magic left unanswered, in Thomas's framing, the practical problem of relieving everyday misfortune — setting up the developments of the following chapters (magic-as-relief-system, providence).
