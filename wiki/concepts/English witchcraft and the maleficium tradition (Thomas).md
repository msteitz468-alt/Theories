## Definition / Characterization from Source
Thomas argues that English witchcraft, both in popular belief and in the great majority of prosecutions, was fundamentally about *maleficium* — the occult infliction of harm on other people, their bodies, or their goods — rather than about the continental theological concept of witchcraft as devil-worship (a heretical pact and covenant with Satan, sealed at a sabbath). England imported the continental doctrine of the diabolical compact only partially and belatedly, mainly through Elizabethan and Jacobean demonological treatises; the sabbath, the incubus/succubus, and the "witch-cult" of covens never took root as more than sporadic literary motifs. What did take root as distinctively English was the belief in the "witch's mark" and the animal familiar.

## Key Claims
- The three witchcraft statutes of 1542, 1563, and 1604 graded punishment by the degree of actual damage done, not by proof of a diabolical pact, revealing that the crime was conceived primarily as an anti-social act of harm.
- Formal trial records (assize indictments) and informal defamation suits alike show maleficium, not devil-worship, as the substance of almost all accusations; explicit compact allegations are not recorded before 1612 and remained rare even after 1604 made the pact a statutory felony.
- Matthew Hopkins's mid-1640s campaign is the major exception, producing the highest proportion of devil-worship confessions in English witch-trial history, but even then most of his victims were also charged with maleficium.
- Margaret Murray's "witch-cult"/"old religion" thesis, which held that accused witches were members of an organized pagan fertility religion, is rejected by Thomas as resting on highly selective and largely coerced continental and Scottish evidence, unsupported by the English trial records.
