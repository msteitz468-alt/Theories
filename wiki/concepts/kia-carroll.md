## Definition from Source

Kia is Carroll's term for the singular core of the human being — the unity that appears to the
mind as the twin functions of will and perception. "Man considers himself a center of will and a
center of perception. Will and perception are not separate but only appear so to the mind. The
unity which appears to the mind to exert the twin functions of will and perception is called Kia
by magicians. Sometimes it is called the spirit, or soul, or life force, instead."

Kia cannot be experienced directly because it is the very basis of consciousness: "Kia is the
consciousness; it is the elusive 'I' which confers self-awareness but does not seem to consist of
anything itself." The mind, being dualistic, cannot grasp Kia as a unified thing. It can
sometimes be felt as "ecstasy or inspiration," but it is "deeply buried in the dualistic mind"
and "mostly trapped in the aimless wanderings of thought and in identification with experience
and in that cluster of opinions about ourselves called ego."

Kia is "a fragment of the great life force of the universe" and contains two impulses:
- To **immerse** itself in duality (the basis of incarnation).
- To **escape** from duality (the root of the mystic quest).

Carroll holds that Kia "will continuously reincarnate until the first impulse is exhausted."
The second impulse is the drive toward union with Chaos: "To the extent that the Kia can become
one with chaos, it can extend its will and perception into the universe to accomplish magic."

The aether surrounds the central core of the life force, and it is through the aether that Kia
acts magically. "Thought gives it shape, and Kia gives it power." Magic therefore consists in
giving "the Kia more freedom and flexibility and with providing means by which it can manifest
its occult power."

In evocation, beings "consist of a portion of Kia, or the life force, attached to some aetheric
matter." In the context of enchantment, the magician's individual Kia must align with the Chaos
upholding a normal event to change it: "The magician's will becomes the will of the universe in
some particular aspect."

The phrase "Be ye ever virgin unto Kia" appears in the Gnosis chapter as an injunction to
recover one's original, fantasy-free sexuality in service of magical working.
