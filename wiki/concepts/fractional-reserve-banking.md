## Definition from Source

Fractional-reserve banking is the practice by which banks issue more "promises to pay on demand" (deposit balances and bank notes) than they hold in actual reserves. Griffin traces its origin (Ch. 9) to European goldsmiths who "began to issue receipts for gold which they did not have," so that "the receipts in circulation exceeded the gold held in reserve." He defines "fractional money" as "paper money with precious-metal backing for part, not all, of its stated value," and asserts "fractional money always degenerates into pure fiat money."

## Characterization in the Source

- Griffin states modern banks' promises-to-pay "often exceed savings deposits by a factor of ten-to-one," with only ~3% held as cash — so promises exceed ability to keep them "by a factor of over three hundred-to-one."
- This creates vulnerability to a **run on the bank** and to **currency drains** (when checks clear net against a bank that has loaned up). Per the source, banks that "loaned up" closest to the edge failed; historian John Klein is quoted tying the panics of 1873, 1884, 1893, and 1907 to "reserve pyramiding and excessive deposit creation."
- The Bank of England (1694) "was formed to institutionalize fractional-reserve banking" as the world's first central bank, introducing "a partnership between bankers and politicians."
- Griffin presents the cartel's reserve-pooling objective as a way to force all banks to "walk the same distance from the edge" so individual banks could not be blamed for failures.
