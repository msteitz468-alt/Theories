## The Concept from Source

[[Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition (Yates)]] (Chapter XX) presents Tommaso Campanella as the last of the line of Italian Renaissance philosopher-magi descending from Ficino — a figure whose career and thought mirror Bruno's with significant variations, and whose *Città del Sole* (City of the Sun) is read by Yates as fundamentally a Hermetic-magical utopia derived ultimately from the *Asclepius* and the magical city of Adocentyn described in *Picatrix*.

### Campanella as Bruno's heir

Yates opens by placing Campanella squarely in the same lineage as [[Giordano Bruno]]: "Campanella was the last of the line of Italian Renaissance philosophers, of whom Giordano Bruno was the last but one. Like Bruno, Campanella was a magician-philosopher, in the line of the Renaissance Magi descending from Ficino." Both were Dominican friars from the Kingdom of Naples; both launched magico-religious reform missions rooted in Hermetic philosophy; both collided with the Inquisition. The twenty-year age gap means that Campanella's career echoes Bruno's at later dates. Yates notes that letters by Campanella contain "many notions, and even phrases, which are strangely reminiscent of passages in Bruno's Italian dialogues, particularly the *Cena de le ceneri*," suggesting Campanella had read at least some of Bruno's works.

Unlike Bruno, however, Campanella possessed a degree of "savoir-faire, or, perhaps, cunning" — including a willingness to dissimulate and, during his trial, to simulate madness — that allowed him to survive. He avoided the stake and eventually achieved in Paris an apotheosis as prophet of the French Monarchy.

### Three periods of Campanella's career

Yates divides Campanella's life into three periods:

1. **The revolutionary period**, culminating in the Calabrian revolt of 1599 — an armed insurrection against Spanish rule in Calabria, aimed at establishing a magical utopian republic with Campanella as head priest and prophet. The revolt was easily crushed, its timing heralded by Campanella's astrological portents (notably the "descent of the sun"). He was imprisoned in Naples for twenty-seven years.

2. **The prison period**, during which Campanella wrote vast philosophical and theological works and repositioned his programme — switching from revolutionary insurrection to appealing to the Spanish Monarchy, or the Papacy, as the framework for the universal magical reform. He was eventually released in 1626.

3. **The French period**, in which Campanella transferred the mantle of universal reform to the French Monarchy — cultivating Richelieu's patronage, hailing the birth of the future Louis XIV as the coming Sun King, and dying in Paris in 1639. Yates: "History again seems to be repeating itself, with Campanella as a more successful Bruno."

### The City of the Sun as Hermetic utopia

The *Città del Sole* (probably written c. 1602, published in a Latin version in Frankfurt 1623, revised Paris edition 1637) describes an ideal city on a hill, divided into seven circular zones (*giri*) named after the seven planets, with a great circular temple at the summit. The temple altar bore two globes — one of the heavens, one of the earth — its dome was covered with star-maps recording the powers of each star over terrestrial things, and seven lamps (named for the seven planets) burned perpetually. The city was governed by a Sun priest (whose name was represented by the solar symbol ☉) assisted by three collaborators: Power, Wisdom, and Love. Its walls served as encyclopaedic pictorial displays of all the sciences, arts, and peoples of the world.

Yates argues that the City is comprehensively misread if treated as a blueprint for rational governance. "The work is entirely misunderstood if it is viewed as a blue-print for a well-governed state in any modern sense. The City is arranged so as to be right with the stars, and thence flows all its happiness, health, and virtue." The priesthood of the City were Magi who knew how to "draw down the life of heaven" (in Ficino's phrase) for the benefit of mankind.

### Source in Picatrix and the Asclepius

The ultimate model for the City, Yates contends, is the magical city of Adocentyn built by [[Hermes Trismegistus]] as described in *Picatrix*: "To find the ultimate source one must dig deeper down and uncover those hidden magical springs from which the Renaissance was fed. For, to my mind, the closest parallel to Campanella's City is none other than the City of Adocentyn in Picatrix." The parallels Yates lists are striking — four gates and roads; a lighthouse flashing the colours of the seven planets (compared with the seven planetary lamps); celestial images that maintained the inhabitants in virtue; and control of generation.

In the *Asclepius*, moreover, Hermes prophesies that "the gods who exercise their dominion over the earth will be restored one day and installed in a City at the extreme limit of Egypt … into which will hasten, by land and sea, the whole race of mortal men." Yates: "Here, surely, in that foundation text for Renaissance magic, the Asclepius, is a prophecy of Campanella's universal City of the Sun." The Solarians, in this reading, "are really Egyptians, that is to say, Hermetic pseudo-Egyptians" — and the Sun priest is Hermes Trismegistus himself, in his triple role of priest, philosopher, and king.

### Anti-eclipse magic with Pope Urban VIII

Yates cites Walker's account of the most extraordinary practical episode in Campanella's career: in 1628, Pope Urban VIII — afraid that an eclipse prophesied by his enemies would cause his death — invited Campanella to perform protective magic in Rome. They sealed a room against outside air, hung it with white cloths, burned aromatic herbs, lit two lamps and five torches representing the planets, imitated the signs of the zodiac, played Jovial and Venereal music, used stones, plants, and colours belonging to the good planets, and drank astrologically distilled liquors. Campanella described this procedure in an appendix to his *Astrologica* (Lyons, 1629). The magic aimed, as Walker explained, at "artificially creating favourable heavens as a substitute for the real heavens which are going wrong in the eclipse." Campanella also performed the same ritual for his own benefit shortly before his death in 1639, fearing an eclipse would kill him.

Yates sees this episode as the direct practical implementation of the City of the Sun ideal: "if there were an organised state in which the priesthood knew how to do this magic and did it constantly, that state would be permanently preserved, free from all evil celestial influences, both as regards health and as regards morals. This was what the pseudo-Hermetic Egyptians knew how to do in their natural religion, as described in the Asclepius. … And such, it is quite clear, was Campanella's ideal City of the Sun."

### Hermetic animism and theology

Yates shows that both Campanella's animism and his magic are "ultimately Hermetic, like Bruno's." In the *Theologia* Campanella states that the world as a living animal was "first taught by Trismegistus." In his *Metaphysica*, he presents a comprehensive account of Ficino's magic derived from [[Hermes Trismegistus]], explicitly quoting the *Asclepius* on the Egyptian art of ensouling statues with celestial demons. He also developed a revised Thomist theology in prison — a Dominican *Summa* updated in the light of Platonists and Trismegistus — with the aim of providing a theological framework for Renaissance magic as a Counter-Reformation force. Yates: "Campanella's Theologia was intended to be a new Dominican Summa, formulated in the hope of providing a theological basis for Renaissance magic as a Counter Reformation force."

### Waning of the tradition

Yates ends Chapter XX with Campanella's final period in Paris (from 1634), where the new scientific philosophers — Mersenne and Descartes — saw him as irrelevant. Mersenne told Peiresc he could learn nothing from Campanella in the sciences; Descartes declined to meet him. The Hermetic tradition had been fatally undermined by Casaubon's redating of the Hermetica (1614), and Cartesian mechanism had made Renaissance animism obsolete. "Science replaced magic in the great seventeenth-century advance." Campanella was "a mammoth survivor of a nearly extinct race, the race of the Renaissance Magi."
