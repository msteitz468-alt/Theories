## Definition / Characterization from Source

David Bohm's account of reality, as reported by Talbot. Beneath "the tangible reality of our everyday lives" — which Bohm holds "is really a kind of illusion, like a holographic image" — lies "a deeper order of existence," the **implicate (enfolded) order**, which gives birth to all objects and appearances much as a piece of holographic film generates a hologram. Our familiar world is the **explicate (unfolded) order**. All forms arise through "countless enfoldings and unfoldings between these two orders": a particle such as an electron is not a thing but "a totality or ensemble enfolded throughout the whole of space," momentarily unfolding at a location (Bohm's image of an ink drop dispersed in glycerine and drawn back). When a particle is "destroyed" it has merely "enfolded back into the deeper order from which it sprang."

The order is fundamentally one of **undivided wholeness** and **nonlocality**: at the subquantum level "location ceased to exist… it was meaningless to speak of anything as being separate from anything else." Talbot cites the EPR paradox, Bell's theorem, the Aharonov–Bohm effect, and Alain Aspect's 1982 experiment as evidence that the subatomic web possesses a "holographic" interconnectedness.
