## Biographical Details from Source

The source refers to Louis XIV chiefly in the context of his pursuit of the secret apparently discovered by — or communicated to — Nicolas Fouquet and Nicolas Poussin.

**The Poussin affair and Fouquet's arrest**

In 1656, the Abbé Louis Fouquet wrote to his brother Nicolas Fouquet (Superintendent of Finances to Louis XIV) about a meeting with Poussin in Rome, alluding to advantages "which even kings would have great pains to draw from him." Shortly thereafter, "Nicolas Fouquet was arrested and imprisoned for the duration of his life." The source states: "the whole of his correspondence was confiscated by Louis XIV, who inspected all of it personally." The source also states that "in the years that followed the king went determinedly out of his way to obtain the original of Poussin's painting, 'Les Bergers d'Arcadie.'" When he at last succeeded, "it was sequestered in his private apartments at Versailles."

**The Fronde and Mazarin**

The source explains that when Louis XIII and Cardinal Richelieu both died in 1642, "a series of concerted attempts was launched to oust Mazarin and keep the young Louis XIV from the throne." These attempts — the Fronde — were instigated by figures whose genealogies appear in the "Prieuré documents" (the Tour d'Auvergne family, the Bouillons, the Longuevilles). The source states that "Louis XIV did mount the throne of France and Mazarin, though briefly removed, was quickly reinstated." The "Prieuré documents" assert that the Prieuré de Sion "dedicated itself to deposing Mazarin" during this period.

**Victor Hugo's condemnation**

The source notes, in its discussion of Victor Hugo, that despite being a staunch royalist, Hugo "was particularly fierce in his condemnation of Louis XIV."

**Fatio de Duillier and espionage**

The source identifies Nicolas Fatio de Duillier — Newton's closest friend — as appearing to have "worked as a spy, usually against Louis XIV of France."
