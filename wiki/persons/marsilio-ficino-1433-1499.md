## Biographical from Source

Per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499) was the Florentine scholar employed by Cosimo de' Medici as tutor to his grandson Lorenzo and as the intended head of a revived Platonic Academy in Florence. His commission was initially to produce the first complete Latin translation of Plato from the Greek — the *lingua franca* of scholarly Europe.

In 1463, however, Cosimo ordered Ficino to abandon Plato and concentrate immediately on translating a Greek manuscript of the first fourteen treatises of the Corpus Hermeticum, brought back from Macedonia by the monk Leonardo de Pistoia. The urgency arose from Cosimo's grave illness; he wanted to read the Hermetic books before he died and got his wish with a year to spare. Ficino's resulting Latin translation, first printed in 1471, was "by far his most popular work" and sent "seismic shockwaves throughout the academic community in Florence and beyond."

The translation lured Giovanni Pico della Mirandola to Florence, where he studied under Ficino from 1484 to 1486. Ficino himself discerned "strangely recurring themes" across wisdom traditions and concluded — in a statement the source describes as momentous — that the whole world "had always followed a single faith whose ancient priests included Zoroaster, Hermes Trismegistus, Orpheus, Pythagoras, Plato, St Paul, St Augustine." This was the [[Prisca theologia and the Renaissance Hermetic revival]] as Ficino articulated it.

Ficino also developed the concept of "natural magic," a form that worked with the forces of nature rather than by conjuring demons. His masterwork *Three Books on Life* (*De vita libri tres*, 1489) was "extremely influential on arcane philosophers such as Agrippa," with Hermeticism at its heart. The source further notes that Ficino "regarded sexual desire as a current of energy responsible for the cohesion of the entire universe" and even recommended the revels of Bacchus as a route to divine ecstasy.

[[Giordano Bruno]] was "heavily influenced by the works of Ficino and Agrippa" and explicitly invoked Ficino's work on the sun in his Oxford debate. Tommaso Campanella also counted Ficino among his major intellectual influences.
