## Biographical Details from Source

Per [[Count Michael Maier (Craven)]], Michael Maier was born at Rendsburg, in Holstein, about 1568, the date taken from the inscription on his portrait prefixed to the "Symbola," "Atalanta fugiens," and "Septimana Philosophica" (painted 1617, when he was forty-nine). His family was "well known... to all the nobility of Holstein." After graduating in medicine, apparently at Rostock, he came under the notice of Emperor Rudolf II, was appointed a body physician to the Emperor, was summoned to Prague, ennobled as Pfalzgraf (Count Palatine), and made the Emperor's private secretary — though he does not appear to have been in constant attendance (he was in Amsterdam in 1611).

After Rudolf II's death (1612), Maier visited England, befriending Robert Fludd, Sir William Paddy, Sir Thomas Smith, and Francis Anthony. His first publication, the "Arcana Arcanissima" (c. 1614), was dedicated to Paddy. It is said, though "buried in obscurity, if not in contradiction," that Maier admitted Fludd to the Rosicrucian order while in England and instigated Fludd's 1616/1617 defenses of the Brotherhood. In 1619 Maier became physician to the Landgrave Moritz of Hesse; he later settled at Magdeburg, where he died in the summer of 1622, described by a friend as having died "piously," a regular churchgoer and practitioner of Christian charity. He was, per Craven, "a devout Lutheran" who in his "Munera" attacked the Roman Church and Court, and who in the "Symbola" questioned the Church of England's rejection of images and statues of saints.

Craven contrasts Maier's classical and "profane" erudition — especially in Egyptian and Greek mythology — with Robert Fludd's theological, cabalistic, and anatomical learning, and with Henry Khunrath, who united both types. Maier's works, particularly the "Symbola Aureæ Mensæ" and "Atalanta Fugiens," are described as "mines" of alchemical biography from which later writers drew. Craven judges that Maier's conception of the Rosicrucian Society "is not a high one, nor a mysterious one," portraying the brethren in "Themis Aurea" as merely "hard-working students of Nature, physicians and chemists."

## Primary Source Appearances

- **Arcana Arcanissima** (c. 1614): argues Egyptian and Greek myths encode a single ancient "golden medicine," with extended allegorical readings of the Labours of Hercules and the Trojan War/Achilles.
- **De Circulo Physico, Quadrato** (1616): treats gold as bearing medicinal virtue paralleling the sun's rule over the human heart.
- **Lusus Serius** (1616/1619; English trans. 1654): a mock parliament in which Mercury is judged King of all worldly things.
- **Examen Fucorum Pseudo-Chymicorum** (1617): warns against four kinds of false ("pseudo") chemists.
- **Jocus Severus** (1617): a tribunal of birds crowning the Owl queen for wisdom, presided over by the Phoenix; written while travelling from England to Bohemia.
- **Silentium Post Clamores** (1617; 2nd ed. 1622): a formal apologetic defending the Rosicrucian Fraternity's silence toward petitioners, affirming the truth of the Fama and Confessio Fraternitatis, and tracing the Brotherhood's lineage to ancient secret philosophical colleges (Egyptian priests, Orphic/Eleusinian mysteries, Magi, Brahmans, Pythagoreans).
