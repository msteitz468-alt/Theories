**Biographical Details from Source**  
According to Chaldean and Babylonian accounts preserved by the Hellenistic priest Berossus (compiled in temple archives and recorded in Greek around 280 BC), **Oannes** was a legendary amphibious being who emerged from the Erythraean Sea (Persian Gulf) to instruct the early inhabitants of Sumer.

Key descriptions of Oannes in *The Sirius Mystery* (derived from Berossus's fragments):
* **Physical Description:** Oannes was an amphibious creature with the body of a fish but with another head beneath the fish's head, and feet like those of a man subjoined to the fish's tail. He possessed a human voice, and a representation of him was preserved in Babylonian art.
* **Daily Schedule:** Oannes spent his days on dry land instructing humanity, but was unable to eat human food. At sunset, he returned to the Persian Gulf, swimming out to sea to spend the night in the deep waters, because he was amphibious.
* **Civilizing Mission:** Oannes taught humans the art of writing, sciences, various crafts, agriculture, laws, and geometry. According to Berossus, before Oannes, the people of Mesopotamia lived like beasts without order or law, and Oannes brought complete civilization, so that nothing new had to be discovered since his time.
* **Equivalence to [[Nommos]]:** Robert Temple equates Oannes to the West African Dogon tribe's **[[Concept: Nommos]]**—amphibious extraterrestrial visitors from the Sirius system who also returned to a watery environment after teaching humans.

**Primary Source Appearances**  
* **Berossus's Chaldean History (Babyloniaca):** The primary source of the Oannes story. Although the original work is lost, fragments were preserved by classical authors such as Alexander Polyhistor, Apollodorus, Abydenus, and eventually Eusebius and Syncellus (reprinted in Cory's *Ancient Fragments*, which Temple includes in his Appendix II).
