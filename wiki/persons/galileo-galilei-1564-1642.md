## Biographical from Source

Per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], Galileo Galilei (1564–1642) is celebrated as "the father of modern science," praised by both Einstein and Hawking; the source describes him as "the world's first celebrity scientist — the Einstein of his day." He obtained the Chair of Mathematics at the University of Padua in 1592 — a position vacated because [[Giordano Bruno]]'s arrest prevented Bruno from taking it up.

Galileo's major scientific contributions were in physics (motion, optics, acoustics) rather than astronomy per se. His improvements to the telescope from around 1610 yielded astronomical observations — the Moon's rugged surface, the moons of Jupiter, the phases of Venus — that strongly supported Copernicus. He published these in *Starry Messenger* (*Sidereus nuncius*, 1610), cannily dedicating it to Cosimo II de' Medici and naming Jupiter's moons the "Medicean stars," winning the position of court mathematician to the Grand Duke of Tuscany.

**The Hermetic connection.** The book argues that Galileo's trial by the Inquisition in 1633 is conventionally misread as a clash between science and religion, or between two obstinate egos. In the authors' account, the real reason the Church — particularly the Jesuits — was terrified of Galileo's work was its Hermetic implication: ever since Bruno, Hermetic reformers had declared that *proving* heliocentrism would trigger a new Hermetic age, and Galileo was known to be connected to Hermetic circles.

Key evidence the book presents:
- Galileo obtained his Padua chair after Bruno's arrest and immediately met Tommaso Campanella (Bruno's spiritual heir) and Gian Vincenzo Pinelli (described as Galileo's mentor and an eminence grise of Hermetic networks).
- He maintained a lifelong correspondence and friendship with Campanella, who wrote *Defence of Galileo* from his prison cell in 1622 and urged Galileo "to stand firm because of the spiritual importance of his work."
- The cardinal who warned Galileo off in 1616 was Roberto Bellarmino — the same man who had interrogated Bruno and sent him to the stake — recalled to Rome specifically to deal with Galileo, and who interviewed Galileo "in the same room as he had interviewed Bruno."
- Galileo's *Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems* (1632) is structured closely on Bruno's *Ash Wednesday Supper*, including the proto-relativistic "moving ship" thought experiment that Bruno had articulated over forty years earlier.
- After his 1616 warning, Galileo immediately sought permission to visit Campanella in his Neapolitan prison cell — a request his patron Duke Cosimo refused.

The book describes Galileo's actual recantation formula — that he had "come to regard the Ptolemaic view as true and indisputable" — as neither the speech of "a noble defender of intellectual freedom" nor of "a willing would-be martyr." It suggests that "at the very least Bruno's work influenced Galileo's, which yet again places Hermeticism at the centre of the scientific revolution."

Galileo died at his villa outside Florence in 1642 under house arrest, having been "sentenced to life imprisonment" but spared on grounds of age (over seventy).
