## Biographical Details from Source

Per Griffin, J. P. Morgan Sr. "was brought into banking by his father, Junius Morgan, in England." Griffin characterizes the Morgans as "friendly competitors with the Rothschilds" who "became socially close to them," noting that Morgan's London-based firm "was saved from financial ruin in 1857 by the Bank of England over which the Rothschilds held great influence," after which "Morgan appears to have served as a Rothschild financial agent and went to great length to appear totally American." Griffin owned the Jekyll Island resort where the Fed was conceived, and his partners (Davison, Norton, Strong) were among the attendees.

In Mullins's account, Morgan's firm became "the principal international agency" of the Rothschilds in 1869; Morgan testified at the Pujo hearings ("Money is a commodity") and died shortly after. Griffin adds the WWI dimension: Morgan as Allied bond sales agent and purchasing agent profiting "from both ends of the cash flow."
