## Definition / Characterization from Source
As Thomas presents him, William Lilly was the most prominent and commercially successful English astrologer of the seventeenth century. Born a poor Leicestershire yeoman's son, he came to London as a servant, married his master's widow, learned astrology "in seven or eight weeks" in 1632, and began serious practice in 1641. His annual almanac and prognostication, *Merlinus Anglicus*, reportedly sold nearly 30,000 copies a year by 1649. His surviving case-books (in the Ashmolean collection) record a practice approaching 2,000 consultations a year at its peak, covering lost goods, missing persons and ships, business decisions, marriage and legal questions, and political prognostication.

## Key Claims
- Lilly built a large personal following of pupils and admirers across England and claimed in 1647 to have "made more scholars in this profession than all that profess this art in England."
- He was closely allied with the Parliamentary/Independent cause during the Civil War, advising figures such as Bulstrode Whitelocke, and was consulted by Levellers and Army radicals (e.g. Richard Overton) as well as by Charles I's supporters seeking his escape from Hampton Court — illustrating his political ambidexterity.
- Lilly did as much as any figure to fuse judicial astrology with the "ancient prophecy" genre, publishing collections such as *A Collection of Ancient and Moderne Prophecies* (1645) and *Monarchy or no Monarchy* (1651), and titling his almanac after Merlin.
- He weathered repeated political and legal jeopardy (indicted under the witchcraft statute in 1654, investigated over the 1666 Fire of London after a suspicious prophetic illustration) but retained a comfortable practice and reputation until his death.
- Thomas uses Lilly's voluminous case-books as primary evidence for the scale, social range, and everyday functions of astrological consultation in mid-seventeenth-century England.
