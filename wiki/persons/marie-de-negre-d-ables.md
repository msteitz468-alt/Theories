## Biographical Details from Source

The source refers to this person under several names: "Marie, Marquise d'Hautpoul de Blanchefort," "Marie de Blanchefort," and, in the index, "Blanchefort, Marie, Marquise d'Hautpoul de." She was a member of the noble Blanchefort family of the Languedoc, which the source identifies as having deep connections to the Knights Templar, the Cathar world, and ultimately the Prieuré de Sion.

## The Tombstone and the Encoded Message

The source places Marie de Blanchefort's grave at the centre of one of the most important cryptographic elements of the Rennes-le-Château mystery. In the churchyard at Rennes-le-Château, the source states, stood her sepulchre. The headstone and flagstone marking her grave "had been designed and installed by the Abbé Antoine Bigou — Saunière's predecessor of a century before, who had apparently composed two of the mysterious parchments."

The source describes the headstone's inscription as containing "a number of deliberate errors in spacing and spelling" that constituted "a perfect anagram for the message concealed in the parchments referring to Poussin and Teniers." The errors were "contrived precisely to make them do so." Saunière, upon recognising this, apparently defaced and eradicated the inscription to prevent others from reading it.

The source also notes that a curious signature — "P.S." — appeared both on one of the parchments and "on the tombstone of Marie de Blanchefort."

## January 17th

The source emphasises that January 17th appears on the tombstone of the Marquise d'Hautpoul de Blanchefort. This is the same date as Saunière's fatal stroke (January 17, 1917) and the feast day of Saint Sulpice. The authors write: "The date of January 17th is perhaps suspicious. The same date appears on the tombstone of the Marquise d'Hautpoul de Blanchefort — the tombstone Saunière had eradicated."

One later "Prieure document" also references "January 17th — the date of Nicolas Flamel's first alchemical transmutation, of Marie de Blanchefort's tombstone, of Saunière's fatal stroke."

## Role as Confessor's Charge

The source refers to Abbé Antoine Bigou as "curé of Rennes-le-Château and confessor to Marie de Blanchefort," speculating that a secret held by the Blanchefort family — possibly reaching back through Pope Clement V's mother, Ida de Blanchefort — was entrusted to Bigou in this capacity and encoded into the parchments.

## The Broader Blanchefort Family

The source treats the Blanchefort family as a whole as significant to its thesis: Bertrand de Blanchefort was fourth Grand Master of the Temple; the family had Cathar sympathies; and the lord of Blanchefort had fought alongside Raymond-Roger Trencavel. The source speculates that Pope Clement V (whose mother was Ida de Blanchefort) may have been "privy to some secret entrusted to the custody of his family."

## Primary Source Appearances

The source's index lists: "Blanchefort, Marie, Marquise d'Hautpoul de, 28, 30." She appears primarily in Chapter 1 and is referenced throughout in connection with the coded tombstone inscription.
