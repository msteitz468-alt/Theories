## Overview
An Irish gentleman and former officer in Cromwell's army, described by Thomas as "the most famous occult healer of the seventeenth century." After the Restoration, Greatrakes believed he was divinely inspired with the power to cure the King's Evil (scrofula) by touch, and later extended his healing to the ague and other ailments. He charged no fee beyond travel expenses.

## Role in the Source
Thomas uses Greatrakes as the culminating case study in his account of non-royal healing by touch (`Ch 7, §2 Healing by touch`). Brought to Ragley, Warwickshire, in 1666 to treat Anne, Viscountess Conway's headaches, he failed with her but performed many apparently successful cures on the crowds who besieged his lodgings, attracting support from Cambridge Platonists (Henry More, Ralph Cudworth, Benjamin Whichcote) and scientists (Robert Boyle, John Wilkins). He failed in a demonstration before Charles II and returned to Ireland within five months of his London triumph. Thomas suggests his healing career, beginning with an urge to cure the King's Evil, was plausibly "a veiled sectarian protest against the Restoration and the miraculous powers claimed by Charles II," given his Cromwellian past and interest in Boehme.
