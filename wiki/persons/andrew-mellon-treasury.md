## Biographical Details from Source

Mullins (Ch. 11) presents Mellon as Secretary of the Treasury through the 1920s and an ex-officio member of the Federal Reserve Board who "abetted in the credit expansion of the 1920s which culminated in the Great Depression." The source charges that through his tax-rebate policy he returned "more than twenty billions of dollars in tax rebates" to corporations in which he was a dominant partner, including US Steel's wartime corporate taxes. It identifies him as the biggest banker in Pennsylvania and owner of Gulf Oil and Aluminum Corp. of America (Alcoa), and partner with Baruch in several ventures.

The source states Mellon faced repeated demands for impeachment over the rebate policy and left office in 1932, succeeded by Ogden Mills. Mullins reports that on February 6, 1929, Montagu Norman of the Bank of England conferred with Mellon, after which the Federal Reserve abruptly shifted to a high-discount-rate policy ahead of the Crash; and that Mellon publicly assured the country brokers' loans were "not too high" before October 1929.
