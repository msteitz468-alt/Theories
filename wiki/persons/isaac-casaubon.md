## Biographical Details from Source

Per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], Isaac Casaubon was born in Geneva in 1559 to Huguenot refugee parents and became "widely regarded as the most learned man in Europe," with a speciality in classical languages. After a distinguished academic career in Switzerland and France — latterly as a royal librarian under Henri IV — he relocated to England following Henri's assassination in 1610, accepting an invitation from James I. He was buried in Westminster Abbey on his death in July 1614.

James commissioned Casaubon to write a rebuttal of Cardinal Caesar Baronius's *Ecclesiastical Annals*, the great Catholic argument for the primacy of the Roman Church. In the single completed volume — *De rebus sacris et ecclesiasticis* (1614) — Casaubon used philological analysis of the Greek of the *Corpus Hermeticum* to argue that the texts dated not from ancient Egypt but from the early Christian era. His argument was aimed at demolishing the Catholic theological tradition of regarding Hermes Trismegistus as a pagan prophet of Christ, but the effect was far wider: it undermined the entire Hermetic movement's claim to primordial Egyptian authority. The authors note the deep irony that Casaubon "had intended to demolish the Christian tradition that accorded Hermes a privileged place in pagan history, rather than to attack Hermeticism itself."

His son **Méric Casaubon** continued the family's inadvertent war on Hermeticism by publishing a hostile edition of John Dee's diaries in 1659, painting Dee as a necromancer in league with the Devil.
