## Biographical Details from Source

Thomas Campbell is an American physicist who describes himself in *[[My Big TOE]]* as having spent nearly thirty years actively exploring nonphysical reality before writing the trilogy. He earned a physics degree and went on to work in graduate physics research before taking "my first real job applying classical physics and mathematics to electromechanical and electromagnetic systems simulation" at what appears to be a Department of Defense facility in Virginia (context suggests this is in the region of Monroe's Whistlefield estate).

### Meditation Practice (Pre-Monroe)
Before encountering Monroe, Campbell had developed a personal meditation practice through college. He describes being given a mantra as part of Transcendental Meditation-style training and eventually discovering that "any two-syllable nonsense word ending in 'ing' (a resonant sound) worked as well as any other." He continued meditating throughout his professional life, eventually dropping the mantra and learning to enter meditative states instantly—"gone completely from their world with no residual awareness of their reality." He could do this while appearing to colleagues merely to be deep in thought.

### Meeting Robert Monroe (1972)
Campbell's boss, Bill Yost, gave him a copy of *[[Journeys Out of the Body]]* by [[Robert Monroe (1915–1995)|Robert A. Monroe]] in 1972. Campbell found it scientifically plausible if unverifiable and reserved judgment. When Yost organized a group to visit Monroe at Whistlefield estate, Campbell attended and quickly became the most assertive questioner. Satisfied that Monroe was "rational, intelligent, under control, and conservative" with "an engineer's personality," Campbell proposed on the spot: "If you will teach me what you know about out-of-body and altered states of consciousness, I will help you do legitimate scientific research."

An electrical engineer colleague (Dennis) made a similar offer simultaneously, and both were accepted.

### Early Monroe Institute Research
Campbell and Dennis built measuring equipment—including mobile EEG-type monitoring and audio systems—for Monroe's research, often working evenings and weekends to meet deadlines alongside Nancy Lea Honeycutt (Monroe's stepdaughter and team administrator). Campbell describes a weekend-long research session with naive subjects who produced a wide range of paranormal events: "reading numbers in sealed envelopes, remote viewing, manifesting lights in the sky, visiting their relatives, reading next week's newspaper headlines, and much more." Campbell describes this as exhausting but revelatory.

Campbell's background in physics, systems modeling, and meditation gave him what he considered a uniquely suited perspective for the research. He describes Monroe as being "distracted and knowing things paranormally," often a step ahead and correct before facts were established.

### Personality and Intellectual Approach
Campbell describes himself as constitutionally oriented toward objectivity and impartial observation, finding emotional crowd behavior puzzling. He identifies himself as a "perpetual outsider" who found this advantageous for scientific clarity. His intellectual approach is characterized by:
- Insistence that altered states must produce "consistently and universally" measurable results to qualify as "real"
- Conviction that subjective experience is valid data if it produces consistent objective effects
- Preference for bottom-up logical derivation over revelation or authority
- Willingness to follow implications wherever they lead, regardless of cultural comfort

### Thirty Years of Research
Campbell states that by the time of writing (2005), he had spent approximately thirty years exploring nonphysical reality through NPMR. The My Big TOE trilogy is the product of this research, representing his attempt to organize findings into a comprehensive, internally self-consistent theory of everything.
