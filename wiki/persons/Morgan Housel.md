## Biographical Details from Source

**Morgan Housel** is the author of *[[The Psychology of Money (Timeless Lessons on Wealth, Greed, and Happiness)]]* (2020). Within the book he identifies himself as a partner at the venture-capital firm **Collaborative Fund** and a former writer at **The Motley Fool** and **The Wall Street Journal**, who began writing about finance in early 2008, "the dawn of a financial crisis and the worst recession in 80 years."

Autobiographical details he supplies:
- He spent his **college years working as a valet** at a nice Los Angeles hotel — the source of the opening gold-coin story, the Ferrari/Man-in-the-Car observations, and the "Roger"/repossessed-Porsche anecdote.
- He took a **summer investment-banking internship** in Los Angeles in his junior year and lasted one month of a four-month placement, finding the loss of control over his time miserable (chapter 7).
- His **father became an Emergency Room doctor at age 40** after raising three kids while in medical school; his parents lived well below their means, and his father later simply quit — the origin of Housel's personal goal of financial **independence** (chapter 20).
- He met his **wife in college**; they deliberately froze their lifestyle expectations in their 20s, producing a high savings rate. They own their **house without a mortgage**, hold ~**20% of assets in cash**, and invest everything else in **low-cost index funds** (chapter 20).

## Contribution in the Source

- Author and narrator of all 20 chapters, the summary chapter, the confessions chapter, and the historical postscript.
- Coins or popularizes the book's named ideas: the [[Man in the Car Paradox]], [[Wealth is what you don't see (rich vs. wealthy)]], "appealing fictions," the "historians as prophets" fallacy, and the "different games" account of bubbles.
- States that the book builds on a 2018 report of the same name read by over one million people, "some short passages" of which appear unaltered.
