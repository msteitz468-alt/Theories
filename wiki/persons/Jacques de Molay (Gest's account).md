**Biographical Details from Source**
In Kevin L. Gest's *The Secrets of Solomon's Temple*, Jacques de Molay (referred to also as "James Burg de Molay") was the Grand Master of the Knights Templar and godfather to one of French King Philip the Fair's sons. Lured to France, he was arrested in a secretly organized raid on Friday, October 13, 1307, along with a large contingent of Templars, in an action Gest attributes to Philip the Fair's desire to seize Templar wealth. Gest reports (quoting James Orchard Halliwell's 1840 *Early History of Masonry in England*) that de Molay was executed by being "roasted alive" (burned) near the Cathedral of Notre Dame in Paris on March 19, 1314, at the direction of Pope Clement V, an event Halliwell calls "the assassination of the Grand Master... his murderers being Philip le Bel, Pope Clement V and Squin de Florion."

**Primary Source Appearances**
- Chapter Fifteen ("The Knights Templar Legacy and a New Hiram Abif") recounts de Molay's arrest, the charges (heresy, blasphemy, sodomy) which Gest states "are now widely understood to have been false," and his execution, as well as noting that some writers allegorically link the Masonic legend of Hiram Abif's murder to de Molay's death.
- Appendix 3's speculative chronology restates the 1307 arrests and 1314 execution as a turning point after which Templar-affiliated masons "inherited the residual knowledge" including heliocentric knowledge, later encoded in the Hiram Abif legend.
