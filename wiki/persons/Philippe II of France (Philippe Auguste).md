## Biographical Details from Source

The source references Philippe II primarily in two contexts.

**The Cutting of the Elm at Gisors (1188)**

The source states that in 1188 a meeting occurred between Henry II of England and Philippe II of France on the Champ Sacré (Sacred Field) adjacent to the fortress of Gisors. In the middle of this field stood an ancient elm said to be more than eight hundred years old and large enough that nine men linking hands could barely encompass its trunk. According to one account, Henry II's entourage took shade under the tree, leaving the French monarch — who arrived later — to the sun. For reasons the source describes as obscure and "garbled," a bloody quarrel over the elm ensued. The source identifies this event — "the cutting of the elm" — as marking the separation of the Prieuré de Sion from the Knights Templar, an event of central importance to the "Prieuré documents."

**Philippe IV (Le Bel) and the Templars**

The source makes a sharp distinction between Philippe II and Philippe IV (Philippe le Bel, r. 1285–1314). It is Philippe IV who is the more prominent figure in the source's narrative, as the king who, "by 1306 … was acutely anxious to rid his territory of the Templars." Philippe IV is described as planning the arrests of all Templars in France, issuing "sealed and secret orders to his seneschals throughout the country" to be opened simultaneously and implemented at dawn on Friday, 13 October 1307. The source characterises this as "a security operation worthy of the SS or Gestapo." Philippe IV's primary interest — the Order's immense wealth — eluded him: "It was never found."

The index entry distinguishes them: "Philippe II, King of France, 121" and "Philippe IV (Le Bel), King of France, 70-1, 73, 76."
