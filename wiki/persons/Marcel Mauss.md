## Biographical Details from Source

Marcel Mauss appears in *The Occult Mind* as, with Henri Hubert and (via Émile Durkheim) Durkheim,
the originator of the "mana" theory of magic. Lehrich reports that in the 1902 *General Theory of Magic*
(written with Hubert), Mauss proposed that magic is "first and foremost different": magicians are
powerful because they are different, and those who are different are ascribed magical powers. Mauss
referred this projection of power to the Polynesian-derived notion of "mana," a theory Durkheim then
borrowed in *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life*.

Lehrich notes that Durkheim and Mauss together assigned magic an "antisocial" character, in contrast to
E. E. Evans-Pritchard's later reading of magic as a means by which a society manifests tensions and
cleavage — two views Lehrich treats as "strongly differing" yet "founded on structurally equivalent
grounds," since both use "magic" as a class term for practices grouped by exterior criteria (Mauss/Durkheim)
or by the natives' own parallel classifications (Evans-Pritchard).

Lehrich further reports that subsequent scholarship (citing Radcliffe-Brown) argued that native
explanations, including native uses of "mana," should be treated with scholarly suspicion — a
"progressive repudiation" of the Mauss/Durkheim mana thesis. Lehrich also discusses [[Claude Lévi-Strauss]]'s
reinterpretation of Mauss's mana in his *Introduction to the Work of Marcel Mauss* (1950), where mana becomes
a "floating signifier" rather than a substantive supernatural force.
