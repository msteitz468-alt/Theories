## Biographical Details from Source

Mullins introduces Aldrich (Chs. 1–2) as one of a coalition of five Republican Senators ruling the Senate, tied to the Rubber Trust and Tobacco Trust, and "denounced many times for his callous disregard of his oath of office." He headed the [[National Monetary Commission]], appointed by Congress after the Panic of 1907, which spent nearly two years and more than $300,000 touring Europe.

Per the source, Aldrich led the group that travelled in a sealed railway car from Hoboken to [[Event: Jekyll Island drafting meeting (November 1910)]] to draft the central-bank bill. He insisted the legislation bear his name (the "Aldrich Plan") over Warburg's objection that his name would brand it as a Wall Street measure. After the Federal Reserve Act passed, Mullins quotes Aldrich writing in his magazine *The Independent*: "Before the passage of this Act, the New York bankers could only dominate the reserves of New York. Now we are able to dominate the bank reserves of the entire country."

The source recounts the muckraker attacks on Aldrich (Ida Tarbell, Louis Brandeis) and his partnership in the tobacco and rubber trusts.

## Primary Source Appearances

Central figure of Chapters 1–2; recurs in the account of the Aldrich Plan's defeat and reissue as the Federal Reserve Act.
