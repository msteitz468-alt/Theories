## Biographical Details from Source

According to the "Prieure documents" as reproduced and discussed by the source, Jean de Gisors was born in 1133 and died in 1220. He is listed as the first Grand Master of the Prieuré de Sion to be independent of the Knights Templar, having assumed his position "after the 'cutting of the elm' and the separation from the Knights Templar in 1188." His alleged tenure ran from 1188 to 1220.

The source describes him as "at least nominal lord of the fortress of Gisors in Normandy where meetings were traditionally convened between English and French kings and where, in 1188, a curious squabble did occur which involved the cutting of an elm." Until 1193 he was a vassal of the king of England — first Henry II, then Richard I. He also possessed property in England in Sussex, and the manor of Titchfield in Hampshire.

According to the "Prieure documents," he met with Thomas à Becket in 1169. The source notes that "no independent record of this meeting survives, but Becket was at Gisors in 1169 and must have had some contact with the lord of the fortress."

## The Authors' Assessment

The authors are candid about the limits of their research into Jean de Gisors: "Jean de Gisors, apart from a few bland details, proved virtually untraceable. He seemed to have left no mark whatever on history, save his existence and his title." They write: "If the list of Sion's purported Grand Masters was authentic, what, we wondered, did Jean do to earn his place on it? And if the list were a latter-day fabrication, why should someone so obscure be included at all?"

Their tentative conclusion is that Jean de Gisors's claim to the Grand Mastership rested on his membership in the dense network of interconnected noble families whose genealogies appear in the "Prieure documents," families "ultimately descended, supposedly, from the Merovingian dynasty."

## Connection to the "Cutting of the Elm"

The source recounts a curious incident at Gisors in 1188 involving the cutting of an elm tree, which external sources confirm but explain only vaguely. The "Prieure documents" treat this event as the moment of separation between the Prieuré de Sion and the Knights Templar. The source notes that Richard I was "frequently accompanied by knights of the Order" and that thirty years before the incident, Gisors had been entrusted to the Temple.
