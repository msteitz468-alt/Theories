## Overview
Daughter of the Earl of Castlehaven, married first to Sir John Davies and then to Sir Archibald Douglas. Thomas calls her "much the best-known" religious prophet in England before the Civil War. In 1625 she reported hearing a voice from Heaven declaring "There is nineteen years and a half to the Judgment Day," and from then until her death in 1652 pursued a continuous career of prophetic utterance, interrupted by repeated imprisonments.

## Role in the Source
Discussed in `Ch 5 Prayer and Prophecy, §3 Prophecy`. Contemporaries credited her with predicting the deaths of Charles I, Laud, Buckingham and her first husband. In 1633 the High Commission imprisoned and fined her for printing (at Amsterdam) a commentary on Daniel foretelling doom for Laud and Charles I; she later went "berserk" in Lichfield Cathedral, declaring herself Primate of England. Thomas uses her case to argue that prophecy was one of the few channels through which women, denied access to pulpit, press or university, could gain a public hearing — she published at least thirty-seven tracts between 1641 and her death. Her enemies dubbed her by the anagram "Dame Eleanor Davis: (never so mad a ladie)."
