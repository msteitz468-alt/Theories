## Biographical Details from Source

Earl William St. Clair is described in [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]] as "the figure who did the most to preserve and propagate Rex Deus traditions." He was the third St. Clair Earl of Orkney and Baron of Roslin, living in the 15th century.

His designation as "a Knight of the Cockle and Golden Fleece" (per the source) signals membership in two significant [[Rex Deus]] orders: the Knights of Santiago (the Cockle) and the Order of the Golden Fleece, founded by Philip the Good, Duke of Burgundy.

Contemporary sources cited in the text describe him as "one of the Illuminati, a nobleman with singular talents" and "a man of exceptional talents much given to policy, such as buildings of Castles, Palaces and Churches." He was also called "a Grand Master and an adept of the highest degree."

Scottish records confirmed he was not only hereditary Grand Master of the craftmasons, but of all the hard and soft guilds in Scotland — including shipwrights, papermakers, tanners, and foresters.

A true son of the Renaissance, Earl William corresponded regularly with René d'Anjou and, like Cosimo de' Medici, amassed a vast library of original manuscripts in Hebrew, Greek, Arabic, Turkish, and Latin, housed at Roslin Castle. The authors note an anecdote in which, during a fire at the castle (c. 1447), Earl William's first concern was the safety of his charters and writings — only reassured when his chaplain confirmed the documents had been thrown to safety.

The authors state: "The name Roslin translates from Scottish Gaelic as 'ancient knowledge passed down the generations'" (Tessa Ransford, Scottish Poetry Library), and argue that "spiritual insight runs like a river through the history of the St. Clairs."

## Role in Founding Rosslyn Chapel

Earl William founded [[Rosslyn Chapel]] between 1446 and 1450, bringing master masons from across Europe and paying them £40 per year (four times the standard rate). He exercised total design control: every carving was modelled in wood for his approval before being executed in stone. The source credits this rigorous oversight with the chapel's "symphonic harmony of design."

## Role in Founding Freemasonry

The authors argue at length (Chapter 18) that Earl William used the building of Rosslyn Chapel — and the community of craftsmen it assembled — as the vehicle for founding speculative Freemasonry as a Rex Deus vehicle for transmitting initiatory gnosis. See [[The St. Clairs of Roslin and the foundation of Freemasonry (Wallace-Murphy)]] for the full argument.
