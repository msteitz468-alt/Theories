## Biographical Details from Source

The source describes André de Montbard as one of the co-founders of the Knights Templar and, crucially, the uncle of Saint Bernard of Clairvaux. The source treats him as a key link in what it argues was a hidden, coordinated design behind the creation of both the Templar order and the Cistercian movement.

## Co-founder of the Knights Templar

The source states that "at least three of the nine founding knights, including Hugues de Payen, seem to have come from adjacent regions, to have had family ties, to have known each other previously and to have been vassals of the same lord." Among these, André de Montbard was Saint Bernard's uncle, and is consistently identified throughout the text as a co-founder of the Order.

The "Dossiers secrets," as reproduced by the source, lists the founders of the Order of the Temple as: "Hugues de Payen, Bisol de St. Omer and Hugues, Comte de Champagne, along with certain members of the Ordre de Sion, André de Montbard, Archambaud de Saint-Aignan, Nivard de Montdidier, Gondemar and Rossal." This is the first instance in the source where André is explicitly identified as belonging to the Ordre de Sion — "another Order, which predates the Order of the Temple and plays an instrumental role in the Temple's creation."

## The Uncle-Nephew Connection

The source makes much of the relationship between André de Montbard and his nephew Bernard. "In 1115 the count of Champagne donated the land on which Saint Bernard... built the famous Abbey of Clairvaux; and one of the nine founding knights, André de Montbard, was Saint Bernard's uncle." The authors write: "Under Hugues de Payen and André de Montbard, the Templars attained a military and administrative ascendancy in the Holy Land which quickly spread back to Europe. Behind the growth of both orders loomed the shadowy presence of uncle and nephew."

## André's Role vs. the Grand Mastership

The source explores a discrepancy between conventional historians, who list André de Montbard as Grand Master of the Temple between 1153 and 1156, and the "Dossiers secrets," which state he was never Grand Master. The source proposes that this discrepancy may arise because André was "regional Master of Jerusalem," which would give him the same charter designation ("Magister Templi") as a Grand Master, while he "would seem to have continued functioning — as he does all through his career — behind the scenes."

The source also notes the hypothesis that André's mentor — "some historians even list him as the Grand Master immediately preceding Bertrand" de Blanchefort — was a formative influence on Bertrand de Blanchefort.

## Connection to the Count of Champagne

The source explains: "In 1104 the count of Champagne had met in conclave with certain high-ranking nobles, at least one of whom had just returned from Jerusalem. Among those present at this conclave were representatives of certain families — Brienne, Joinville and Chaumont — who, we later discovered, figured significantly in our story. Also present was the liege lord of André de Montbard."
