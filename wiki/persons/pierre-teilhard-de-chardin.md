## Biographical Details from Source

Pierre Teilhard de Chardin (1881–1955), French Jesuit priest and palaeontologist. Per the book, Teilhard was "a paradoxical combination of Jesuit priest and evolutionary theorist." His fellow Jesuits posted him to China to prevent him lecturing on evolution; there he participated in the discovery of Peking Man (fossil Homo erectus over 500,000 years old). He was forbidden to publish philosophical works or apply for academic posts, went into self-imposed exile in New York, and his major work *The Phenomenon of Man* (Le phénomène humain) appeared posthumously (1955) when the Vatican's ban expired.

Per the authors, Teilhard "saw the universe as absolutely purposeful, the aim of matter being to engender life and the goal of life being to attain consciousness." He argued that human consciousness would eventually create a planetary spiritual entity — the noosphere — that would link with extraterrestrial intelligences, with all life and mind eventually permeating the universe. He called the end of this process the "Omega point": creation reuniting with its creator. He declared: "evolution is an ascent towards consciousness — therefore it should culminate forwards in some sort of supreme consciousness."

The book identifies a specific philosophical lineage connecting Teilhard to the Hermetic tradition: his formative influence was Henri Bergson's *Creative Evolution* (read before ordination in 1912); Bergson was in turn heavily influenced by Plotinus; and Plotinus, the book argues, was ultimately drawing on the religion of Heliopolis. The authors trace this as "the same underground stream" that runs through Hermeticism.

Theodosius Dobzhansky — founder of neo-Darwinian synthesis — is cited as being led to Teilhard *by* his evolutionary work rather than in spite of it, and as becoming President of the American Teilhard de Chardin Society. The book also notes that Barrow and Tipler wrote that Teilhard's "basic framework … is really the only framework wherein the evolving cosmos of modern science can be combined with an ultimate meaningfulness to reality." Christian de Duve (*Vital Dust*) is cited as taking Teilhard's ideas seriously.
