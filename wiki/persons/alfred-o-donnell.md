## Biographical Details from Source

Alfred "Al" O'Donnell (born 1922) is described by Jacobsen as an EG&G weapons test engineer and an early Manhattan Project member who armed, wired, and fired 186 nuclear bombs at the Nevada Test Site and the Pacific Proving Ground over the course of his career.

O'Donnell grew up in Boston. In 1941, while still in high school, he was recruited by the Boston Braves baseball team on the strength of his .423 batting average. The war intervened. He married Ruth, joined the Navy, and excelled in radio and electronics. After the war, back in Boston, he was mysteriously recruited to work for Raytheon Production Corporation, a defense contractor cofounded by Vannevar Bush. He did not know what the job entailed at the time of signing on. After about a month and the granting of a security clearance, he learned he had become part of the Manhattan Project. He was transferred to EG&G — the company named for MIT professors Edgerton, Germeshausen, and Grier. At EG&G, O'Donnell was personally trained to wire a nuclear bomb by Herbert Grier, the man who had invented the firing systems for the bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki.

**Operation Crossroads (1946):** O'Donnell was a leading member of the arming party tasked with wiring and firing the atomic bombs at Bikini Atoll. He was twenty-four years old at the time. He wired the twenty-three-kiloton Baker bomb, code-named "Helen of Bikini," connecting wires from a concrete bunker on the atoll out into the ocean to the device. On the day of the Baker detonation, he operated the instrument panel in the control room below deck on an observation ship, monitoring the auto sequence timer to the final second. He then raced to the ship's deck to watch the blast, forgetting to hold on to the railing; the shock wave, arriving approximately four minutes after detonation, threw him ten feet back against the bulkhead, leaving him badly bruised.

**Nevada Test Site and Pacific Proving Ground:** O'Donnell participated in numerous nuclear test series over the following decade, including Operations Greenhouse, Ivy, Castle, Plumbbob, and Hardtack. He wired many of Dr. Edward Teller's "Super" thermonuclear bombs in the Marshall Islands. He stood on Johnston Island on August 1, 1958, when the Teak bomb — a megaton-range weapon carried on a Redstone missile — detonated directly overhead due to a guidance failure rather than at its intended target twenty-six miles to the south. O'Donnell describes the fireball growing to ten miles wide in the first ten milliseconds and to more than forty miles wide at one second. He and the rest of the crew lost radio communications for eight hours. He also wired Operation Ivy's "Mike" bomb — the world's first thermonuclear device — firing it from the control room of the USS Estes, parked forty miles from Elugelab Island. Mike detonated at 10.4 megatons, nearly twice its predicted yield, and vaporized the island entirely.

**Operation Plumbbob (1957):** O'Donnell is mentioned as one of the engineers who had to climb metal towers three hundred to seven hundred feet tall to wire atomic bombs for atmospheric tests. He is credited with putting the "final touches on the bomb's firing system" of the Hood device — a 74-kiloton weapon, the largest atmospheric nuclear bomb ever exploded in the United States — just before it was detonated on July 5, 1957.

**Access facilitation:** Jacobsen notes that O'Donnell arranged a temporary security clearance for her so she could accompany him to federally restricted land connected to Area 51 and its vicinity. He was one of her primary interview sources, consulted on many occasions between May 2009 and June 2010.

## Primary Source Appearances

O'Donnell is one of Jacobsen's central interview sources throughout the book. He is quoted extensively on Operation Crossroads, Operation Ivy, the Teak detonation on Johnston Island, the nature of nuclear testing at the Nevada Test Site, and his early induction into the Manhattan Project and EG&G.
