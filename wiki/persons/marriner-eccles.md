## Biographical Details from Source

Mullins (Ch. 14) presents Eccles as "the Federal Reserve Board" in the public eye during the Roosevelt administration — a Utah banker, President of the First Securities Corporation, who (the source says) picked up banks cheaply during the Agricultural Depression of 1920–21. The source treats him as the System's most candid witness on how money is created.

Mullins quotes Eccles's congressional testimony at length as admissions: "Money is created out of the right to issue credit-money"; "When you sell bonds on the open market, you extinguish reserves"; "If there were no debts in our money system, there would be no money"; and, asked where the money came from to buy $2 billion in securities in 1933, "We created it ... Out of the right to issue credit money." Asked when a return to a free, open market might come, Eccles is quoted: "Never. Not in your lifetime or mine."

The source also quotes Eccles's 1939 statements opposing any bill proposing a stable price level, his role in consumer-credit control during WWII, and his advocacy for the International Monetary Fund ("An international currency is synonymous with international government"). Mullins says Eccles was replaced as Chairman by [[Thomas B. McCabe]] after clashing with the Truman–Snyder approach to the war-bond debt.
