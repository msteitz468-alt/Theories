## In Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)

A second source, *Custodians of Truth*, reads Leonardo as a transmitter of [[Rex Deus]] heresy in
encoded form: it interprets *The Last Supper* and *The Virgin of the Rocks* as concealing
Rex Deus doctrine (e.g. a James-not-Peter succession and the "heresy of the holy twins"). See
[[Rex Deus used Lombardy and the Italian Renaissance as a vehicle for spreading initiatory and heretical knowledge]].

## Biographical Details from Source

The "Dossiers secrets," as cited by the source, list "Leonard de Vinci" as Grand Master of the Prieuré de Sion from 1510 to 1519. His predecessor in the list was Sandro Filipepi (Botticelli), 1483–1510.

The source identifies Leonardo's name among the names on the Grand Masters list that "appear suspect" at first glance — too famous, too well researched to have exercised a covert Grand Mastership without word leaking out. Yet the authors also question why a forger would include such names alongside many obscure ones if the goal were merely to construct a plausible pedigree.

**Rosicrucian characterisation**

The source recounts that Frances Yates, in conversation with one of the authors' researchers, described Leonardo as a "Rosicrucian" — "using the term as a metaphor to define his values and attitudes." The source elsewhere notes that the work of Leonardo (along with that of Botticelli and other Renaissance artists) reflected a concern with esoteric, Hermetic themes, describing their paintings as works to which a particular statement made in connection with Poussin "might equally well be applied."

**The Renaissance and the Arcadian tradition**

The source discusses the broader cultural context in which Leonardo operated: the courts of the Medicis, the Sforzas and the Gonzagas — families whose genealogies figure in the "Prieuré documents" and who provided Sion with two other Grand Masters (Ferrante and Louis de Gonzaga). The source suggests that through these patronage networks, an esoteric tradition — an "underground stream" — was transmitted from Rene d'Anjou through the Renaissance to figures including Botticelli and Leonardo.

**A 1981 press report**

The source quotes a January 1981 item in the French press that explicitly states the Prieuré de Sion "has numbered among its Grand Masters Leonardo da Vinci, Victor Hugo and Jean Cocteau." This press item appeared after the Convent at Blois of 17 January 1981.

**The principle of selection**

The source explains what it takes to be the principle governing Grand Mastership: when there was no eligible claimant from the interlinked Merovingian genealogies, "the Grand Mastership, presumably in accordance with the procedures outlined in the statutes, was conferred on a chosen outsider. It would be on this basis that individuals like Leonardo, Newton, Nodier and Cocteau found their way on to the list."
