## Biographical Details from Source

According to the "Dossiers secrets" as cited by the source, Jean Cocteau is listed as Grand Master of the Prieuré de Sion from 1918 until his death in 1963. The source also reproduces the "Cercle d'Ulysse" pamphlet (1977) by Jean Delaude, which lists Cocteau as Grand Master from 1918 to 1963.

The source describes Cocteau as one of the most improbable entries on the list of Grand Masters: "More than Charles Radclyffe, more than Charles Nodier, Jean Cocteau seemed to us a most unlikely candidate for the Grand Mastership of an influential secret society. In Radclyffe's and Nodier's cases, however, our investigation had yielded certain connections of considerable interest. In Cocteau's we discovered very few."

**Background and milieu**

Born in 1889, Cocteau was raised in a milieu close to the "corridors of power" — "his family was politically prominent and his uncle was an important diplomat." The source states that Cocteau "at least ostensibly, abandoned this world, leaving home at the age of fifteen and plunging into the seedy sub-culture of Marseilles." By 1908 he had established himself in bohemian artistic circles, becoming associated in his early twenties with Proust, Gide and Maurice Barrès. He was also "a close friend of Victor Hugo's great-grandson, Jean, with whom he embarked on assorted excursions into spiritualism and the occult." He "quickly became versed in esoterica; and Hermetic thinking shaped not only much of his work, but also his entire aesthetic."

By 1912 he had "begun to consort with Debussy," to whom he alludes frequently in his journals. In 1926 he designed the set for a production of *Pelléas et Mélisande* because, according to one commentator, he was "unable to resist linking his name for all time to that of Claude Debussy."

**Public life and politics**

The source notes that Cocteau was "always acutely conscious of his public persona." He was made a Chevalier of the Légion d'Honneur in 1949. In 1958 he was invited by de Gaulle's brother to make a public address on France — "not the kind of role one generally attributes to Cocteau." He "denounced the Vichy government during the war and seems to have been quietly in league with the Resistance," though he "took no active part in the Resistance" during the Occupation. He retained contact with "individuals highly placed in aristocratic and political circles."

**Religious and esoteric interests**

The source notes that Cocteau's Catholicism "was highly suspect, highly unorthodox, and seems to have been more an aesthetic than a religious commitment." In the latter part of his life he "devoted much of his energy to redecorating churches — curious echo, perhaps, of Bérenger Saunière." His mural in Notre Dame de France, London, depicts a very singular Crucifixion: a black sun, an unidentified green-tinged figure, a Roman soldier with what appears to be an Egyptian rendering of Horus on his shield, and — most strikingly — the lower portion of the cross only, so that the identity of the crucified figure cannot be determined. "Fixed to the cross, immediately below the anonymous victim's feet, is a gigantic rose." The source describes this as "a flagrant Rose-Croix device."

**The statutes**

Most convincing to the authors was Cocteau's signature on a copy of the Prieuré de Sion's statutes, which the source reproduces in full. "Unless it had been executed by an extremely skilful forger, the signature was authentic." The statutes (signed "JEAN COCTEAU") establish the Prieuré de Sion as "an initiatory order of chivalry" with roots in Godfroi de Bouillon's foundation of 1099. The source states: "For us, the most convincing testimony of Cocteau's affiliation with the Prieuré de Sion resides in his work — in the film *Orphée*, for instance, in such plays as *The Eagle has Two Heads* (based on the Habsburg Empress Elisabeth of Austria) and in the decoration of such churches as Notre Dame de France in London. Most convincing of all, however, is his signature appended to the statutes of the Prieuré de Sion."

**Succession**

The Dossiers secrets were dated 1956, before Cocteau's death in 1963. After him, the "Cercle d'Ulysse" pamphlet names the Abbé Ducaud-Bourget as Grand Master, though the source notes conflicting accounts about whether his election was by proper quorum. A 1981 press item (quoted in the source) states that following the Convent at Blois on 17 January 1981, Pierre Plantard de Saint-Clair was elected Grand Master.

The source also notes an intriguing coincidence: Cocteau appeared on the Grand Masters list as "Jean XXIII," and when the College of Cardinals elected a new pope in 1959 (while Cocteau still presumably held the Grand Mastership), Cardinal Angelo Roncalli chose the name John XXIII.
