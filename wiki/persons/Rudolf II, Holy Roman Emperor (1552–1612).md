## Biographical Details from Source

Per [[Count Michael Maier (Craven)]], Rudolf II was the son of Maximilian II and Maria of Austria (daughter of Charles V), born in Vienna in 1552. His mother instilled in him "an ardent zeal for the Roman Church," strengthened by his early residence in Spain. He was crowned Emperor and King of Hungary in 1572, and King of Bohemia in 1575. Though he confirmed his father Maximilian's privileges granted to noble Protestants, he banished some of their preachers and restricted their meetings amid the spread of Lutheranism.

Rudolf resided at Prague, summoned Michael Maier there, ennobled him as Pfalzgraf (Count Palatine), and made him his private secretary and body physician. Devoted to science, Rudolf invited to his court Tycho Brahe, "greatly attached to the practices of judicial astrology and alchemy," whose prognostics warned Rudolf he would suffer great danger through "a prince of his own blood." Rudolf grew estranged from his own family, avoided marriage propositions, ceased appearing in public, and had covered galleries built to reach his gardens unseen, fearing assassination. He surrounded himself with "astrologers, chemists, painters, turners, engravers, mechanicians," and kept botanic gardens, natural history cabinets, and galleries of antiquities.

In 1611 his brother Matthias arrived at Prague; Rudolf, at a diet, offered to resign the crown on account of his age and dispensed his subjects from their oaths of fidelity. Matthias was crowned with great magnificence, and Rudolf retired to a pleasure house before being allowed to inhabit the palace at Prague with a pension of 400,000 florins. "Vexed and humiliated by what he had undergone," he died in his sedentary retirement in the 60th year of his age and 37th of his reign, on 20 January 1612. Craven describes him as "a man of elegant manners, affability, and easy conversation," possessing "a great knowledge of languages, both ancient and modern," and skilled "in painting and mechanical arts, in botany, zoology, and chemistry." Kepler was employed together with Tycho Brahe to arrange the calendars, "which have thence received the name of Rudolphine." His collections of precious stones, antiques, and pictures were said to remain "among the finest ornaments of the cabinets of Vienna."
