## Biographical Details from Source
In Éliphas Lévi's *Transcendental Magic*, Apollonius of Tyana appears as an exemplar of chastity among the great adepts — "Apollonius of Tyana never yielded to the seductions of pleasure" — cited alongside Isis, the Emperor Julian, Plotinus, Paracelsus, and Raymund Lully as proof that the licentious "orgies" sometimes practiced within Sabbath-type assemblies are not the practice of "veritable adepts." He is also credited with a specific technique of ecstatic divination: "Apollonius enveloped himself completely in a woollen mantle and fixed his eyes on his navel in the dark," a method Lévi compares to the magical mirror of Dupotet, hydromancy, and thumb-nail scrying, as pretexts for auto-magnetic withdrawal from exterior light so as to attend to "the interior light alone."

Apollonius is presented as the attributed author of the Nuctemeron, a text of "transcendent Assyrian Magic" describing twelve allegorical "Hours" of initiation, each governed by named genii. Lévi states the Greek text was first published from an ancient manuscript by Gilbert Gautrimis and reproduced by Laurentius Moshemius (Amsterdam, 1721), and that he himself has "translated and interpreted [it] for the first time." Lévi frames his act of textual recovery as itself a kind of necromantic evocation: "We have not merely evoked Apollonius, we have possibly resuscitated him." Within the Nuctemeron's explanatory notes, Apollonius is twice quoted as the source of specific doctrinal cautions — that the secrets of the Ninth Hour "must be passed over in silence, because it contains the great secrets of the initiate," and, in the parallel Hebrew Nuctemeron, that "the mysteries of the number nine are not to be revealed."
