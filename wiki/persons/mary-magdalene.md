**Biographical Details from Source**

The source treats Mary Magdalene not primarily as a biblical exegete's subject but as the key figure in the central bloodline hypothesis. The source notes that she appears in the Gospels in several anointing scenes that are argued to follow the ritual format of a Jewish wife anointing her husband (washing his feet and wiping them with her hair). These scenes are read as covert indicators of her marital relationship with Jesus.

The church at Rennes-le-Château, at the center of the mystery, is consecrated to Mary Magdalene (dedicated 1059, on Visigoth foundations from the sixth century). This dedication is treated as a significant link.

Medieval traditions cited by the source maintain that the Magdalene landed at Marseilles (southern France), bringing with her the Grail. René d'Anjou possessed a cup he claimed came from this Magdalene landing at Marseilles, bearing an inscription invoking her name: "He who quaffs at a single draught will see God and the Magdalene."

The source identifies the Magdalene as the person who, after the Crucifixion, was "smuggled to Gaul" with at least one child by Jesus. Several "Prieuré documents" reference a work attributed to "Madeleine Blancassal" entitled *Les Descendants mérovingiens et l'énigme du Razès* — directly connecting the Magdalene with the Merovingian bloodline and the Razès (the area around Rennes-le-Château).

The source notes that the extraordinary cult significance accorded the Magdalene during the Crusades is anomalous — far exceeding her canonical status — and is explicable if she was regarded as the bearer of the Holy Blood (Sang Real) rather than merely as a repentant sinner.
