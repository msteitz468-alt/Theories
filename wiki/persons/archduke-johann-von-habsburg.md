## Biographical Details from Source

The source identifies Archduke Johann von Habsburg as "a cousin of Franz-Josef, Emperor of Austria" who visited Bérenger Saunière at Rennes-le-Château. Bank statements, the source states, "subsequently revealed that Saunière and the archduke had opened consecutive accounts on the same day, and that the latter had made a substantial sum over to the former."

The source elsewhere — in a footnote — provides more detail: "The Habsburg archduke in question has since been revealed as Johann Salvator von Habsburg, known by the pseudonym of Jean Orth. He renounced all his rights and titles in 1889 and within two months had been banished from all the territories of the Empire. It was shortly after this that he first appeared in Rennes-le-Château. Said officially to have died in 1890 but in fact died in Argentina in 1910 or 1911."

The source uses the archduke's visit to illustrate that the mystery at Rennes-le-Château extended far beyond a simple treasure hoard: "They would not explain a priest's refusal to administer the last rites to a dying man, or the visit of a Habsburg archduke to a remote little village in the Pyrenees." The source further speculates: "We knew that he had received money from Johann von Habsburg. At the same time, however, the priest's 'secret' … seemed to be more religious in nature than political." The source raises the possibility that the archduke was merely "an intermediary, and the money he bestowed on Saunière actually issued from the coffers of Rome."

Later in the book the source returns to the financial question, stating: "We know that some of it seems to have been obtained through a financial transaction involving the Archduke Johann von Habsburg."

The source also notes that among Saunière's notable guests was "the Archduke Johann von Habsburg" — listed alongside Emma Calvé and the French Secretary of State for Culture as evidence of the priest's surprising social connections.
