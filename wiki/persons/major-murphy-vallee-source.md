## Biographical Details from Source

"Major Murphy" is the pseudonym Vallée gives to a retired U.S. intelligence officer who becomes
his intellectual guide through *Messengers of Deception*. Vallée notes "his actual rank is much
higher than that of Major." Murphy saw WWII action in Italy and ran counter-submarine and
counter-espionage operations in the Caribbean. Vallée met him at a contactee gathering, where
Murphy posed the question that reframed the whole investigation: "What makes you think that UFOs
are a scientific problem?"

## Role in the Source

Murphy argues that because the UFO phenomenon "could be controlled by alien beings" — or by a
human group — its study "doesn't belong in science. It belongs in Intelligence," i.e.,
counterespionage. He teaches Vallée the "price of information" (Hitler "had 95 percent of the
information about the landing in Normandy. But he had the wrong 95 percent"), the futility of
feeding biased UFO data into computers, and the tradecraft used inside UFO groups ("vacuum
cleaner," "little aunt," "cut-out," "useful idiots").

His counsel shapes the book's method: work outside the infiltrated UFO organizations, "look for
the irrational, the bizarre, the elements that do not fit," and look for the "Manipulators"
"right here, on Earth… among some of the occult groups." On the occult orders he draws the
distinction Vallée keeps: "I never said they were wrong. I said they were infiltrated." He also
advances the "psychotronic device" speculation linking 1946 Swedish "ghost rockets," captured
German secret-weapon research, and ELF/mind-control programs.

## Strict-extraction note

Murphy is presented under a pseudonym and his claims are unverifiable; the wiki records him as a
character/source within Vallée's narrative, not as an independently documented figure.
