## Biographical Details from Source

James Shreve Jr. (also rendered J. D. Shreve Jr. in documents cited by Jacobsen) is identified as the director of Project 57 — the 1957 Atomic Energy Commission and Air Force program that deliberately detonated an XW-25 nuclear warhead with conventional high explosives at Area 13, adjacent to Groom Lake, in order to study the dispersal of plutonium in the event of an accidental aircraft crash. He is affiliated with the Sandia Corporation.

Jacobsen notes that the project's public press release described it simply as "a highly classified safety test being conducted by Dr. James Shreve Jr., in April 1957," as quoted in the Las Vegas Sun, and that having a doctor's name attached gave the program what Jacobsen characterizes as "an almost wholesome ring to it."

In a classified memo, Shreve and Colonel E. A. Blue conducted an aerial inspection of the Groom Lake valley prior to January 18, 1957, to assess Area 13's suitability as the test location. Their memo includes gallows humor about spotting sixty to eighty head of cattle roaming the site who "hadn't gotten the word."

By the summer of 1958, after Project 57 had wound down, Shreve authored a classified report marked Secret–Restricted Data noting a potentially significant ecological hazard: earthworms ingesting plutonium at Area 13 could translocate it through the soil, and birds eating those earthworms could carry plutonium-laden material well beyond the fenced contamination boundary. Shreve cited Charles Darwin's observation that 53,000 earthworms can move 18 tons of soil per acre as the basis for his concern. He acknowledged in the same report that an ecological study of Area 13 had been considered as early as the summer of 1957, but that the relevant UCLA research group was too committed to Operation Plumbbob to take it on. The report is cited in endnotes as: Ref. Sym 5112-(127), Appendix A, Administrative Committee Report, J. D. Shreve, Sandia Corporation.

## Primary Source Appearances

Shreve is mentioned primarily in Jacobsen's chapter covering Project 57 and the contamination of Area 13. He is not quoted directly but is cited via declassified documents, including administrative committee reports and the post-project ecological report.
