## Biographical Details from Source

Athanasius Kircher (1602–80) is presented by Lehrich as a Jesuit polymath who wrote thirty-one major,
lavishly illustrated folios covering an extraordinary range of topics — astronomy, magnetism, geology,
music, numerology, Egyptology, cryptography, and Sinology among them. He was curator of the museum
at the Collegio Romano, a collection Lehrich treats as a key to understanding Kircher's larger
intellectual project: not simply an "encyclopedia" of curiosities but a constructive vision of the
world and of antiquity, organized through the collecting and connecting of objects and signs.

Kircher's culminating book, *Turris Babel* (1679), attempted to reconstruct the origins of language
from the Tower of Babel onward. In *Polygraphia Nova* (1663) he proposed cryptographic writing as the
basis of a perfect language; in the three-volume *Oedipus Aegyptiacus* (1652–54) he used allegorical
method to "decipher" Egyptian hieroglyphic inscriptions; in *China Illustrata* (1667) he argued Chinese
writing descended allegorically from Egyptian via the line of Noah's son Ham (identified with Zoroaster
and with Hermes Trismegistus).

Lehrich stresses that Kircher was controversial even in his own time — praised lavishly (his admirer
Peiresc initially hoped for great revelations, then grew disillusioned and accused Kircher of "some
jest or weakness of the author's mind... and this dismal magic") while critics such as Henry Oldenburg
called his work mere "Collections... of what is already extant" rather than new discovery. Modern
scholarship (Grafton, Findlen, Romano, Stolzenberg) has revived interest in Kircher while still
struggling, per Lehrich, to say coherently what his project actually was.
