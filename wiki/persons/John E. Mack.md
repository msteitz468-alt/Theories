## Biographical Details from Source

John Edward Mack (1929–2004), a Harvard Medical School professor of psychiatry and Pulitzer Prize winner (for a biography of T. E. Lawrence), became the most academically credentialed figure to investigate UFO abductions. He describes nearly forty years in psychiatry — training in adult and child psychiatry and psychoanalysis — before being introduced to the abduction phenomenon by artist-investigator **[[Budd Hopkins]]** in January 1990. By the end of 1992 he had seen more than a hundred people referred for "anomalous" experiences, seventy-six of whom met his strict criteria for an abduction case; *Abduction* (1994) presents thirteen of them in depth.

Mack's prior background shaped his approach: he was trained in **Stanislav Grof's holotropic breathwork** (he incorporates breath-centered relaxation into his sessions) and had long studied the psychology of the nuclear threat (informing his reading of the abduction "ecological warning"). He consulted his childhood acquaintance **Thomas Kuhn** (*The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*) on how to investigate data that did not fit the prevailing paradigm. He founded the Program for Extraordinary Experience Research (PEER) and, with Laurance Rockefeller's support, pursued the subject despite a 1994–95 Harvard committee inquiry into his work. He died in a road accident in London in 2004.

## Primary Source Appearances

Author, narrator, and clinical investigator throughout *Abduction*; co-author (with Dominique Callimanopulos) of the cross-cultural Appendix B.
