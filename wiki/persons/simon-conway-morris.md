## Biographical Details from Source

Simon Conway Morris, Professor of Evolutionary Palaeontology at Cambridge University's Department of Earth Science, Fellow of the Royal Society, committed Anglican. Per the book, Conway Morris is a "champion of directionality" in evolution who believes convergent evolution reveals that evolutionary outcomes are largely predetermined rather than random. He is described as equally critical of the Intelligent Design movement and of "ultra-Darwinists" such as Dawkins, whom he describes as "arguably England's most pious atheist."

His key work *Life's Solution* (2003) is cited extensively. Conway Morris argues that convergent evolution — where distantly related organisms independently evolve identical anatomical solutions — is far more prevalent than neo-Darwinian theory predicts, including at the molecular and biochemical levels. He contends this shows "evolution plays the same themes over and over again," implying the number of viable evolutionary pathways is severely constrained. The camera eye evolved independently at least seven times; C4 photosynthesis at least 31 times.

The book quotes Conway Morris's conclusion: "It seems to me that evolution very much has directionalities, and in that sense it has destinations." His further claim — that the constraints of evolution make "the emergence of something like ourselves a near-inevitability" — is presented by the authors as making humanity "the focus of the universe, the very reason it exists." In his 2007 Gifford Lectures at Edinburgh ("Darwin's Compass: How Evolution Discovers the Song of Creation"), Conway Morris declared that the emergence of life, intelligence, and intelligent beings "really is set into the entire fabric of the cosmos."

His statement on human uniqueness is also quoted: "incipient 'human-ness' is clearly visible in a wide variety of animals … Yet in no case has it 'crystallized'. We stand alone, feet on the ground, head towards the stars."
