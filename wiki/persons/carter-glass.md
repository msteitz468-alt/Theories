## Biographical Details from Source

Mullins (Ch. 5) presents Glass as the House sponsor who, with Wilson, "gets credit" for passing the Federal Reserve Act, while arguing the substance was Warburg's. As Chairman of the House Banking and Currency Committee, Glass guided the Owen-Glass Bill; the source states his committee struck out Senator Robert L. Owen's monetary-stabilization provision and deleted the bill's more idealistic, non-banker administration provisions.

The source quotes Glass's book *Adventure in Constructive Finance* defending closed Board meetings ("neither the public nor reporters should be present") and taking credit for the Act — a claim Mullins says prompted Samuel Untermyer to write that Glass "must be senile to put such a bald misstatement into print." Mullins notes Glass later listed himself in *Who's Who* merely as patron and sponsor of the bill. Glass is also presented as having been a Board member (as Treasury Secretary) at the [[Event: Secret Federal Reserve Board meeting (May 18, 1920)]] that raised the agricultural discount rate.

In 1935, when the Glass-Steagall separation of investment and deposit banking was repealed, Mullins quotes Glass responding to whether Morgan could re-enter investment banking: "Well, why not?"
