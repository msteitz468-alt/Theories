## Biographical Details from Source

According to the source, Nicolas Flamel was born around 1330 and worked for a time as a scrivener, or copyist, in Paris. By virtue of his occupation, many rare books passed through his hands, and he "acquired proficiency in painting, poetry, mathematics and architecture." He also acquired an interest in alchemy, and Cabalistic and Hermetic thought.

The source describes Flamel as "perhaps the most famous and well documented of medieval alchemists."

## The Book of Abraham the Jew

Around 1361, the source states, Flamel "happened upon the alchemical text that was to transform his life." Its full title is given as: "The Sacred Book of Abraham the Jew, Prince, Priest, Levite, Astrologer and Philosopher to that Tribe of Jews who by the Wrath of God were Dispersed amongst the Gauls." This work is described as "one of the most famous in Western esoteric tradition," with the original said to have been deposited in the Arsenal Library in Paris.

Flamel pored over the book for twenty-one years without success. At last, on a journey to Spain in 1382, he claimed to have met a converted Jew in León who elucidated the text for him.

## January 17th and Alchemical Transmutation

On returning to Paris, Flamel applied what he had learned and "is said to have performed his first successful alchemical transmutation at noon on January 17th." The source emphasises that this is the same date that "recurs so persistently in connection with Saunière and Rennes-le-Château" — it is also the date on the tombstone of Marie de Blanchefort, the date of Saunière's fatal stroke, and the feast day of Saint Sulpice.

## Wealth and Philanthropy

The source states that Flamel became "phenomenally wealthy" — owning "more than thirty houses and tracts of land in Paris alone" by the end of his life. The source also describes him as "a modest man who did not revel in power and lavished much of his wealth on good works." By 1413 he had "founded and endowed fourteen hospitals, seven churches and three chapels in Paris, and a comparable number in Boulogne." This altruism "endeared him to posterity" and he was still revered by Sir Isaac Newton in the eighteenth century, who "painstakingly read through his works, copiously annotated them and even copied one of them out by hand."

## Alleged Grand Mastership of Sion

According to the list of alleged Grand Masters in the "Dossiers secrets" as reproduced by the source, Flamel held the Grand Mastership of the Prieuré de Sion from 1398 to 1418. The source notes that Flamel "is the first name on the list of Grand Masters not to be affiliated by blood with the genealogies in the 'Prieure documents'," suggesting that with him "the Grand Mastership of Sion seems to have ceased being exclusively a family sinecure."

The source further notes a possible connection between Flamel and his alleged predecessor Blanche d'Evreux, who "is rumoured to have been a personal patron of Nicolas Flamel."
