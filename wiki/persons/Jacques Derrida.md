## Biographical Details from Source

Jacques Derrida is treated in *The Occult Mind* as the source of the deconstructive half of
[[Christopher I. Lehrich]]'s comparative method, paired with the structuralism of [[Claude Lévi-Strauss]].
Lehrich draws principally on *Of Grammatology* and Derrida's neographism "différance" — a term
that, through its orthography, collapses "to differ" and "to defer," and whose spoken indistinguishability
from "différence" gestures at a differance underlying both writing and speech. Lehrich argues that magic
is not identical to differance but "coexists with the thinking of or toward differance," holding a place
open for it and making its contours apparent differentially, because magic lends itself to permutation and
manipulation in a way pure differance (not itself a concept, object, or word) cannot.

Lehrich also discusses Derrida's reading of [[Claude Lévi-Strauss]]'s Nambikwara parable in *Tristes Tropiques*:
Derrida argues Lévi-Strauss's account of introducing writing to an "innocent" people is logocentric,
since it assumes writing is equivalent to inscription-on-a-medium rather than a more general structural
phenomenon the Nambikwara arguably already possessed.

Lehrich further notes Derrida's use of magical imagery and vocabulary throughout his work (necromancy,
specters, "exorcism," the occult movements of logocentrism), citing *Specters of Marx* as the fullest
extended treatment of these themes, though one entangled with an engagement with Hegel.
