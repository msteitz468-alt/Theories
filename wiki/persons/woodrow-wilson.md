## Biographical Details from Source

Mullins (Ch. 4) portrays Wilson as the bankers' instrument who signed the Federal Reserve Act on December 23, 1913, and is therefore "thought to be its author," though the source argues he had the least to do with the bill's passage in Congress. Per the source, Wilson first won banker favour while President of Princeton by speaking for the Aldrich Plan, and during the Panic of 1907 declared "All this trouble could be averted if we appointed a committee of six or seven public-spirited men like J. P. Morgan to handle the affairs of our country."

The source states his presidential campaign was financed by Cleveland H. Dodge (National City Bank), Jacob Schiff, Henry Morgenthau Sr., Bernard Baruch, and Samuel Untermyer, and that his emissary to the bankers was [[Edward Mandell House]]. Mullins claims Wilson initially refused to sign the Act over the Class B Directors provision until Bernard Baruch hurried to the White House to reassure him. The source quotes Wilson's later admissions that "The World War was a matter of economic rivalry" and that the US would have entered the war regardless of German acts.

Mullins also recounts Wilson's refusal of clemency to Eugene Debs, his lifting of the Mexican arms embargo to benefit Dodge's arms companies, and his breakdown after Versailles.

## Primary Source Appearances

Central figure of Chapter 4; recurs through the WWI chapters (8) as the president who appointed the wartime "Triumvirate" of Baruch, Meyer, and Warburg.
