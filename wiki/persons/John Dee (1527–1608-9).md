## Biographical Details from Source

Per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], John Dee was the leading English Hermeticist of the sixteenth century — "a great exponent of the magical art of memory for which [[Giordano Bruno]] was most famous," a friend and astrologer of Elizabeth I, and "a major figure behind the explosive expansion of the emerging English Empire." The authors note his royal codename was 007. His fortunes collapsed on James I's succession, when the new king's paranoid hostility to occultism caused him to withdraw Dee's royal patronage, leading to "a serious decline in the old man's fortunes and a sad slide into death."

His masterwork, *The Hieroglyphic Monad* (*Monas hieroglyphica*, 1564), presented a new symbol derived from astrological and magical glyphs that Dee believed encoded the secrets of the cosmos. The significance of this work to the Rosicrucian movement is shown by the fact that a Latin tract explaining Dee's philosophy (*A Brief Consideration of a More Secret Philosophy*) was used to preface the *Rosicrucian Confessio* (1615), and Andreae's *Chemical Wedding* was decorated with Dee's monas symbol. The Hermetic Jesuit Athanasius Kircher also "placed great importance on John Dee's *Monas hieroglyphica*," citing it repeatedly in both *Obeliscus Pamphilius* and *Oedipus Aegypticus*.

In his *Mathematicall Magick* (1648), John Wilkins — a key founder of the Royal Society — acknowledged that he based his mathematical works on Dee and Fludd. Isaac Newton owned and annotated copies of Dee's works. The authors describe Dee as "one of the greatest mathematical minds of all time," whose esoteric reputation was trashed for centuries by Méric Casaubon's 1659 hostile edition of his diaries.
