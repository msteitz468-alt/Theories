## Biographical Details from Source

Richard I appears in the source primarily in two contexts: his connection to the Knights Templar and his role in the incident at Gisors involving the "cutting of the elm."

## Richard I and the Knights Templar

The source states in a footnote: "King Richard I was a close friend of the Order, and lived with them during his stay in Acre. When he left the Holy Land in 1192, he left disguised as a Templar, setting sail in a Templar ship, and accompanied by four members of the Order."

The source also notes in its discussion of Jean de Gisors that until 1193, Jean was a vassal of the king of England — "Henry II and then Richard I." This places Richard in the chain of lords connected to the fortress at Gisors.

## The Cutting of the Elm at Gisors (1188)

The source recounts in some detail an incident that occurred at Gisors in 1188 which the "Prieure documents" treat as the moment of formal separation between the Prieuré de Sion and the Knights Templar. According to the source's account:

A dispute broke out between the French king Philippe II and the English king Henry II, involving the cutting of a large elm tree at Gisors. One account describes Philippe giving notice that he intended to cut the tree; Henry reinforced the trunk with bands of iron. The French armed themselves and formed five squadrons, advancing on the elm "accompanied by sling men as well as carpenters equipped with axes and hammers." The source states: "Richard Coeur de Lion, Henry's eldest son and his heir, participated, attempting to protect the tree and spilling considerable blood in the process. Nevertheless, the French held the field at the end of the day, and the tree was cut down."

The source acknowledges that no biography of Richard makes much of the affair, and that existing accounts are "too vague, too scant, too incomprehensible, too contradictory to be accepted as definitive." It adds: "It is extremely probable that Templars were present at the incident — Richard I was frequently accompanied by knights of the Order, and, moreover, Gisors, thirty years before, had been entrusted to the Temple."

## Connection to the Grail Romances

The source notes that Richard is associated in popular consciousness with the Crusades, but argues that in his own era, Godfroi de Bouillon enjoyed far greater prestige and acclaim: "Today, when one thinks of the Crusades, one thinks of Richard Coeur de Lion, King John, perhaps Louis IX (Saint Louis) or Frederick Barbarossa. But until relatively recently, none of these individuals enjoyed Godfroi's prestige or acclaim."

## Primary Source Appearances

The source's index lists: "Richard I, King of England, 122, 484." He appears primarily in Chapter 5 in connection with the Gisors incident and the Templars.
