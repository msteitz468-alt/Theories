## Biographical Details from Source

The source references Simon de Montfort primarily as the principal military leader of the Albigensian Crusade against the Cathars in the Languedoc.

**Role in the Albigensian Crusade**

The source states that "military operations were entrusted largely to Simon de Montfort — father of the man who was subsequently to play so crucial a role in English history." In 1218 "Simon de Montfort was killed besieging Toulouse." Despite his death, "the depredation of the Languedoc continued, with only brief respites, for another quarter of a century." According to the source, "by 1243, however, all organized resistance — in so far as there had ever been any — had effectively ceased."

**Relationship with the Templars and Cathars**

The source notes that the Knights Templar officially remained neutral during the Albigensian Crusade, but covertly provided refuge for many Cathar refugees: "An inspection of the Order's rolls towards the beginning of the Albigensian Crusade reveals a major influx of Cathars into the Temple's ranks where not even Simon de Montfort's crusaders would dare to challenge them."

In a footnote the source records that "Blanchefort was destroyed during the Albigensian Crusade, falling some time before 1215, at which date its lands were given by Simon de Montfort to Pierre de Voisins."

An anecdote from Jean de Joinville describes a curious episode: certain Albigensians allegedly invited Montfort to witness a miracle — "the body of Our Lord … become flesh and blood in the hands of their priest." Montfort "seems somewhat taken aback by the invitation" and "huffily" declares that his entourage may go if they wish, but he will continue to believe in accordance with "the tenets of 'Holy Church.'" The source leaves this anecdote unexplained, noting only its "disturbingly literal" quality.
