## Biographical Details from Source

The "Dossiers secrets," as cited by the source, list Isaac Newton as Grand Master of the Prieuré de Sion from 1691 to 1727. The source notes that at first glance this seemed improbable — Newton is hardly a name the twentieth century associates with the occult or esoteric. The authors' investigation, however, turned up "some astonishing results and consistency."

**Biography and connections**

According to the source, Newton was born in Lincolnshire in 1642. He "descended from 'ancient Scottish nobility,' he himself insisted, although no one seems to have taken this claim very seriously." He was educated at Cambridge, elected to the Royal Society in 1672, and made the acquaintance of Robert Boyle in 1673 (listed as his predecessor as Grand Master).

In 1689–90 Newton became associated with John Locke and "an elusive, enigmatic individual named Nicholas Fatio de Duillier," descended from Genevan aristocracy. Fatio de Duillier "appears to have worked as a spy, usually against Louis XIV of France" and was "Newton's single closest friend" for at least the next decade. In 1696 Newton became Warden of the Royal Mint and was subsequently "instrumental in fixing the gold standard." In 1703 he was elected President of the Royal Society. Around this time he also befriended Jean Desaguliers, who became one of the leading figures in the dissemination of Freemasonry across Europe.

The source states Newton was not himself a Freemason on record, but was a member of the semi-Masonic "Gentleman's Club of Spalding," which included Alexander Pope. Like many Masonic authors, he esteemed Noah more than Moses "as the ultimate source of esoteric wisdom."

**Alchemical and hermetic interests**

The source describes Newton as deeply steeped in Hermetic texts, describing his persona as "something of a revelation" compared with his twentieth-century image as a rationalist scientist. Boyle is said to have taught Newton "the secrets of alchemy," and the two met regularly to discuss alchemical works. Newton's library contained more than a hundred alchemical works; one, by Nicolas Flamel (listed elsewhere as a Grand Master of Sion), he had "laboriously copied in his own hand." He kept a "voluminous and cryptic correspondence" on alchemy with Boyle, Locke, Fatio de Duillier and others.

Newton's major work The Chronology of Ancient Kingdoms Amended attempted to establish the primacy of Israel over other ancient cultures, employing "Jason's quest for the Golden Fleece as a pivotal event" and interpreting it as "an alchemical metaphor." He sought Hermetic "correspondences" between music and architecture, and ascribed great significance to the dimensions of Solomon's Temple, believing them "to conceal alchemical formulae."

**Religious heterodoxy**

The source emphasises that Newton was "militantly, albeit quietly, hostile to the idea of the Trinity." He also "repudiated the fashionable Deism of his time," questioned the divinity of Jesus, "avidly collected all manuscripts pertaining to the issue," doubted the complete authenticity of the New Testament (believing certain passages to be "corruptions interpolated in the fifth century"), and wrote a study of a Gnostic heresy. Newton also displayed — prompted by Fatio de Duillier — "a striking and surprising sympathy for the Camisards, or Prophets of Cevennes," heretics who, like the Cathars, "queried Jesus's divinity" and had been suppressed by military force.

A few weeks before his death Newton, aided by close friends, "systematically burned numerous boxes of manuscripts and personal papers." His contemporaries "noted that he did not, on his death-bed, request last rites."

**Connections to the Sion lineage**

Boyle, on his death in 1691, "entrusted all his other papers to these two confidants" (Locke and Newton) "as well as samples of a mysterious 'red powder.'" The source notes that through Desaguliers, Newton had indirect links to Charles Radclyffe and to the spread of Freemasonry involving the house of Lorraine, whose genealogies appear in the "Prieuré documents."
