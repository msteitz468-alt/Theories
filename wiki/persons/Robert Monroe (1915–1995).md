## Biographical Details from Source

Robert Allan Monroe (1915–1995) was the son of a college professor and a medical doctor mother. He received his degree from Ohio State University after studies in engineering and journalism, then entered radio broadcasting as a writer and director. By 1939 he was in New York, where over twenty years he created and produced some 400 radio and television network programs. He composed all orchestral music for his productions, much of which remained in use in television and film.

Programs he created and produced included: *Rocky Gordon* (railroad adventure, NBC), *High Adventure* (George Sanders), *Nightmare* (Peter Lorre), *Starlight Theatre* (Madeleine Carroll), *Scramble* (Bob Ripley), *M-G-M Screen Test*, and quiz shows *Take a Number* and *Meet Your Match*. At peak operations, Robert Monroe Productions produced 28 radio network shows weekly. He later became vice-president of programs and director of Mutual Broadcasting System, Inc.

Monroe then became president of Laury Associates, acquiring radio stations in North Carolina and Virginia. He formed Jefferson Cable Corporation, guiding the construction and operation of cable-TV systems in Charlottesville and Waynesboro, Virginia (position held until April 1976).

Monroe began experiencing spontaneous OBEs in the fall of 1958 without apparent cause ("no drugs or alcohol were involved"). The experiences initially terrified him, leading to extensive physical examinations (all negative) and a recommendation of psychotherapy for "minor hallucinatory dysfunction" — which he rejected. He undertook self-directed research, documented in *Journeys Out of the Body* (1971).

*Far Journeys* (1985) covers Monroe's research from approximately 1972 onward, including the founding of the Monroe Institute (originally Whistlefield Research Laboratories), development of Hemi-Sync technology (patented 1975), the Gateway Program, and his personal OBE encounters with entities he identified as INSPECs. Monroe reports a body that rejects all chemicals including anesthetic (he describes waking on the operating table), prescription drugs, caffeine, and alcohol. LSD and mescaline had no effect on him in laboratory testing.

Monroe's INSPEC-assigned identifier is "Ashaneen." He uses "RAM" (his initials) as his self-identifier in OBE states in the narrative chapters. He notes his non-physical guides identified him as having a past life as a monk in a monastery in Coshocton, Pennsylvania — subsequently confirmed to have existed by a Catholic monsignor friend.

## From *Journeys Out of the Body* (1971)

Monroe's first book presents his own first-person account, beginning with the spring/May 1958 onset: living "a reasonably normal life" as a businessman in a country home, he was experimenting with sleep-learning tapes when, after listening to one, he felt a severe cramp beneath his rib cage followed by the "vibrations" and sensations he came to interpret as the onset of out-of-body capability. He approached the phenomenon with an engineer's method — detailed notes, reproducible induction techniques, and systematic phenomenology of consciousness separate from the body. He developed the concepts of [[Concept: The Second Body|the Second Body]], the [[Concept: Vibrational State|vibrational state]], the [[Concept: The Separation Process|separation process]], and the [[Concept: Locales in Non-Physical Reality|Locales]]. Publication in 1971, he said, "quite thoroughly blew my cover," exposing the president of a multimillion-dollar corporation as an OBE researcher. (See the dedicated source page [[Journeys Out of the Body]].)

## Biography (per Russell, 2007)

Ronald Russell's authorized biography fills in the life the autobiographies omit. Monroe descended from a Scottish prisoner, William Munro, shipped to the New World in 1651; he was born **October 30, 1915** in Lexington, Kentucky, to a professor of Romance languages (Robert Emmett Monroe) and one of the few female MDs of her generation (Georgia, whom his father forbade to practice). A quick, adventurous, left-handed child obsessed with music (harmonica), trains, cars, and especially **flying** (a qualified pilot by 17), he weathered the family's ruin in the 1929 crash, spent about a year as a Depression-era **hobo** riding the rails, and graduated from Ohio State after switching among engineering, journalism, and English while working in radio and student drama. A brief first marriage to Jeanette (a failed Pittsburgh summer-stock venture) ended in divorce; his sister Peggy was institutionalized for schizophrenia. His radio/television production career (Ch. 2) preceded the **1958 onset** of his OBEs (Ch. 3), after experiments with a sleep-learning audio tape and the now-famous "vibrations." He founded what became [[Monroe Institute|The Monroe Institute]], developed Hemi-Sync, and wrote three books. Monroe **died in 1995**.

## Primary Source Appearances

Monroe is the first-person narrator and sole author of *Far Journeys*; he is the biographical subject of Russell's *The Journey of Robert Monroe*. Key characterization points:

- He cannot use his own Hemi-Sync technology on himself (Ch. 2)
- His decision to surrender OBE navigation to his "total self" in spring 1972 transformed all subsequent OBE experiences (Ch. 1)
- He uses the lazy-man's "rollout method" of OBE induction — waking after 2–3 sleep cycles and detaching from the physical (Ch. 1)
- He identifies himself as a "Seeker" in Ed's taxonomy: a living human who enters the post-death ring zones while still in a physical body (Ch. 10/11)
- His INSPEC tells him his key attribute is "curiosity" and that this has been the driver of his entire OBE trajectory (Ch. 8)
