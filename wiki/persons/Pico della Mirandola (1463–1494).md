## Pico della Mirandola from Source

Yates presents Pico della Mirandola as the founder of Christian Cabala and the thinker who completed the Renaissance magus tradition inaugurated by [[Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499)]]. A contemporary of Ficino though younger, Pico absorbed from him the enthusiasm for magia naturalis but went decisively further by adding practical Cabala — Cabalist magic — as the necessary complement and superior supplement to natural magic.

In 1486, at the age of twenty-four, Pico went to Rome with his nine hundred theses (Conclusiones) drawn from all philosophies. These theses included twenty-six Conclusiones Magicae and seventy-two Conclusiones Cabalistae. The great public debate he had proposed never took place; Roman theologians raised objections, and several theses were condemned by Pope Innocent VIII. Pico published his Apology (1487) and the Oration on the Dignity of Man, incurring further difficulties, fleeing briefly to France, and eventually living in Florence under the protection of Lorenzo de' Medici, in a state of extreme piety and under the spiritual influence of Savonarola. He died in 1494, the day French armies entered Florence.

Yates argues that the Oration on the Dignity of Man is "the great charter of Renaissance Magic", opening with Hermes Trismegistus's words "Magnum, o Asclepi, miraculum est homo" and presenting Man the Magus — not the pious spectator of medieval theology, but the divine operator who marries earth to heaven. Pico's magic was two-fold: natural magic (magia naturalis) working through sympathetic links and magical characters, and practical Cabala invoking Hebrew divine names, angelic names, and the ten Sephiroth, which Pico called the "supreme part of natural magic". He argued that no magical operation is effective without Cabala annexed to it.

Pico is also credited by Yates as the first thinker to unite Hermetism and Cabalism systematically, perceiving structural parallels between the Hermetic Powers of Corpus Hermeticum XIII and the Cabalist Sephiroth, and asserting that Magia and Cabala together give more certainty of the divinity of Christ than any other science — a claim that caused the most theological controversy.
