**Biographical Details from Source**

French painter who spent much of his career in Rome. His painting "Les Bergers d'Arcadie" ("The Shepherds of Arcadia") is central to the Rennes-le-Château mystery because: (1) the encoded parchments found by Saunière reference "Poussin" and "Teniers" as holding "the key"; (2) the painting depicts a tomb near Rennes-le-Château that actually exists; and (3) Louis XIV went out of his way to acquire and sequester the original painting.

In 1656, Poussin was visited in Rome by the Abbé Louis Fouquet, brother of Nicolas Fouquet (Superintendent of Finances to Louis XIV). The abbé's letter to his brother described the meeting: "He and I discussed certain things, which I shall with ease be able to explain to you in detail — things which will give you, through Monsieur Poussin, advantages which even kings would have great pains to draw from him, and which, according to him, it is possible that nobody else will ever rediscover in the centuries to come. And what is more, these are things so difficult to discover that nothing now on this earth can prove of better fortune nor be their equal."

Shortly after this, Nicolas Fouquet was arrested and imprisoned for life; his correspondence was confiscated and personally reviewed by Louis XIV. Louis subsequently acquired the original of "Les Bergers d'Arcadie" and sequestered it in his private apartments at Versailles.

The inscription "ET IN ARCADIA EGO" on the tomb in the painting is argued to be an anagram of "I TEGO ARCANA DEI" — "Begone! I conceal the secrets of God."

**Primary Source Appearances**

Poussin is introduced in Chapter 1 via the encoded parchment messages; the Fouquet letter and the Arcadia motif are developed in Part Two. The painting functions throughout the book as a recurring symbol of the concealed secret.
