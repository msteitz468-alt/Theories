## Summary
Puritan preacher and self-appointed exorcist whose spectacular "dispossessions" by fasting and prayer — first in Derbyshire in 1586, then in Nottinghamshire, Lancashire and Staffordshire in 1596–7 — precipitated a major controversy within the Elizabethan Church of England, according to Keith Thomas's [[Religion and the Decline of Magic (Thomas)]]. Darrell was convicted by the High Commission in 1599 as an impostor who had coached his patients (notably William Sommers) to simulate possession. His Arminian opponents, led by Samuel Harsnet and Richard Bancroft, used the affair to argue that all claims of exorcism by fasting and prayer were fraudulent Puritan propaganda, culminating in Canon 72 of 1604, which banned unlicensed exorcism by Church of England clergy. Nearly every case Darrell handled also resulted in a named witch being blamed for the victim's affliction, linking the possession controversy directly to witchcraft accusation.
