## Biographical Details from Source

Mullins (Ch. 7, "The Hitler Connection") presents Hoover as a London-based mining promoter and Rothschild secret agent (rewarded with a directorship in the Rothschild enterprise Rio Tinto). Per the source, his fortune was guided by the Belgian financier Emile Francqui, with whom he was involved in "a number of scandals" (the Kaiping Coal Company affair said to have set off the Boxer Rebellion); Hoover had been "barred from dealing on the London Stock Exchange." He directed the Belgian Relief Commission and the U.S. Food Administration.

Per Mullins, "the London Connection decided to run Herbert Hoover for president" in 1928; because he had lived abroad and had no U.S. address, he listed the New York office of his associate Edgar Rickard (shared with Schroder partner Julius Barnes). Quoting Congressman McFadden, the source states Hoover's election was financed by "the German international bankers of Wall Street — that is Kuhn Loeb Company, J. & W. Seligman, Paul Warburg, J. Henry Schroder" in exchange for the Hoover Moratorium on German war debts — necessary, per Mullins, "so that Germany would have funds for rearming" for World War II. Hoover insisted on appointing [[Eugene Meyer Jr.]] to the Federal Reserve Board; McFadden introduced an impeachment resolution against Hoover (tabled).

Hoover is also the central figure of Ch. 12 of the earlier Mullins edition.
