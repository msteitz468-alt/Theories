## Summary
Self-styled professional "witch-finder" active in East Anglia (Essex, Suffolk, Norfolk) in 1645–7, during the disruption of normal judicial machinery caused by the Civil War, working with his associate John Stearne. According to Keith Thomas's [[Religion and the Decline of Magic (Thomas)]], Hopkins's campaign produced roughly 200 convictions — a sizeable fraction of all English witchcraft executions — and was exceptional both in its scale and in the frequency with which it extracted confessions of diabolical compact (rather than simple maleficium), through sleep deprivation and leading interrogation rather than the formal torture used on the Continent. Thomas rejects the view that Hopkins's campaign proves persecution was normally driven "from above": Hopkins had no known government backing, relied on the willing testimony of ordinary villagers (ninety-two individuals came forward in Essex alone), and exploited already-existing local suspicions rather than manufacturing them from nothing.
