## Biographical Details from Source

According to the source, Claude Debussy was born in 1862 into a poor family but "quickly established wealthy and influential contacts." While still in his teens he was performing as a pianist in the chateau of the French president's mistress. In 1880 he was "adopted by the Russian noblewoman who had patronized Tchaikovsky," and travelled with her to Switzerland, Italy, and Russia. In 1884, after winning a coveted musical prize, he studied for a time in Rome. Between 1887 and 1906 he lived mostly in Paris, with the years preceding and following devoted to extensive travelling.

## Alleged Grand Mastership of the Prieuré de Sion

According to the list of alleged Grand Masters in the "Dossiers secrets," Debussy held the Grand Mastership of the Prieuré de Sion from 1885 to 1918. Another "Prieure document" (the 1977 pamphlet Le Cercle d'Ulysse) confirms this, stating that Prieuré Grand Masters included "Claude Debussy from 1885 to 1918."

The source treats this claim with initial scepticism, noting that "Hugo, Debussy and Cocteau were immersed in such matters, they would seem to be too well known, too well researched and documented, to have exercised a 'Grand Mastership' over a secret order. Not, at any rate, without some word of it somehow leaking out."

## Secretive Associates

The source notes that Debussy was "curiously secretive about his aristocratic and political associates. Many of his letters have been suppressed; and in those that have been published important names and often whole sentences have been scrupulously excised."

## Connection to Saunière and Rennes-le-Château

The source states that Bérenger Saunière, when dispatched to Paris after discovering his parchments in 1891, was introduced through Émile Hoffet to a distinguished esoteric circle that included Stéphane Mallarmé, Maurice Maeterlinck, and "the composer Claude Debussy." Saunière is said to have had personal contact with Debussy at this time.

The source also argues: "Mere money, gold or jewels would not, in themselves, explain a number of facets to [Saunière's] story. They would not account for his introduction to Hoffet's circle, for instance, his association with Debussy and his liaison with Emma Calvé."

## Symbolist Circles and Esoteric Connections

The source describes Debussy as "an integral member of the symbolist circles, who dominated the cultural life of the French capital." These circles included Émile Hoffet, Emma Calvé, Stéphane Mallarmé (whose "L'Après-Midi d'un Faune" Debussy set to music), Maurice Maeterlinck (whose drama "Pelléas et Mélisande" Debussy turned into a world-famous opera), and the Comte Philippe Auguste Villiers de l'Isle Adam (who wrote the "Rosicrucian" play Axel, for which Debussy began composing a libretto — left unfinished at his death in 1918).

Among those who attended Mallarmé's famous Tuesday night soirées — and whom Debussy therefore encountered — were Oscar Wilde, W. B. Yeats, Paul Valéry, André Gide, and Marcel Proust. The source states that Debussy "consorted with virtually all the most prominent names in the so-called 'French occult revival'."

## Connection to Victor Hugo

The source states that Debussy made Hugo's acquaintance through the symbolist poet Paul Verlaine, and later set a number of Hugo's works to music — linking the two alleged consecutive Grand Masters of the Prieuré de Sion.
