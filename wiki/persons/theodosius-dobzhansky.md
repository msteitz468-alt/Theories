## Biographical Details from Source

Theodosius Dobzhansky (1900–75), Russian-born naturalized American biologist, described by the book as "the largely unacknowledged genius" who hardened the modern evolutionary synthesis. His 1937 book *Genetics and the Origin of Species* was the landmark work reconciling natural selection with genetics, effectively creating neo-Darwinian theory as it exists today. The book notes that Dobzhansky is conspicuously absent from popular accounts of evolution (absent from Dawkins' *The Blind Watchmaker* and *The Greatest Show on Earth*) while being the foundational figure cited by specialists.

Per the authors, the reason for this silence is that Dobzhansky "is something of an embarrassment because he was unashamedly a devout Christian" — an active member of the Eastern Orthodox Church who saw no incompatibility between faith and evolution. He wrote in 1970 that "man was and is being created in God's image by means of evolutionary developments" and regarded evolution as a "creative process." His vision was that the designer had "set in motion a system that enabled life to develop and find its own way," describing natural selection as "groping its way forward." His Greek name Theodosius means "God-giver" — noted by the authors as ironic given his invisibility in popular accounts.

The book traces how Dobzhansky was led, via his own evolutionary work, to Pierre Teilhard de Chardin's philosophy of purposeful cosmic evolution — eventually becoming President of the American Teilhard de Chardin Society in the 1960s. This lineage (Dobzhansky → Teilhard → Bergson → Plotinus → Heliopolis) is presented as evidence for the underground Hermetic stream running through even mainstream science.
