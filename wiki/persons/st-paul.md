## Biographical Overview from Source

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], St. Paul — born Saul of Tarsus — was simultaneously a Roman citizen, a Pharisee, and, crucially, a kinsman of the Herodian royal family. The authors argue that his Herodian connections have been consistently overlooked by mainstream Christianity and are the key to understanding both his theology and his political role.

### Herodian Identity

The source draws on Paul's own epistle to the Romans (16:10–11): "Greet those who belong to the household of Aristobulus. Greet Herodian my relative." The Aristobulus mentioned was the son of Agrippa I's brother, Herod of Chalcis. As a Herodian, Paul held Roman citizenship by birth (the original Antipater, father of Herod the Great, having been granted hereditary Roman citizenship by Caesar). This explains, the authors argue, his ease of travel, his multiple "miraculous" escapes from prison, his powerful political connections, and the extraordinary military escort (200 soldiers, 70 cavalrymen, 200 spearmen) deployed for his transfer to Caesarea — far more than warranted for a simple blasphemer.

### Role in the Persecution of Jesus's Followers

As a young man, Paul wielded power in Jerusalem as a member of the temple guard "authorized by the high priest to persecute the followers of Jesus," who as nationalistic, Torah-zealous Jews were a prime target for temple authorities seeking to suppress rebellion against Rome. The English author A. N. Wilson is quoted as suggesting Paul may have been in the temple guard at the time of Jesus' arrest.

### Theological Conflict with James

After his claimed Damascus road conversion and three years in Arabia, Paul joined [[James the Just]] in Jerusalem and then set out on evangelical journeys that increasingly diverged from James's teaching. The source catalogues the fundamental differences: Paul abolished the Torah, circumcision, dietary laws, and the prohibition on mixing with Gentiles, substituting faith alone as sufficient for salvation. For James, this was apostasy; Ebionite documents call Paul "an apostate of the Law," "a Spouter of wickedness and lies," and "the distorter of the true teachings of Jesus." The Kerygmata Petrou describes his Damascus visions as "dreams and illusions inspired by devils."

### Physical Attack on James

The source, citing Eisenman's *James the Brother of Jesus* and the Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions, states that Paul physically assaulted [[James the Just]], throwing him down the temple steps and breaking his legs — an event that led to Paul's protective arrest and subsequent removal under heavy Roman military escort.

### Political Function

The authors argue that Paul's theology of subservience to lawful authority — "render unto Caesar" — served Roman interests precisely: it de-nationalized Jesus, stripped the movement of its apocalyptic revolutionary fervor, and made it safe for the Roman imperial order. After the fall of Jerusalem in 70 C.E., the Pauline Christian communities (unlike the scattered Ebionites) were relatively stable, and the Roman-centered faction eventually won the battle for doctrinal supremacy. Without the destruction of Jerusalem, the Catholic historian Paul Johnson is quoted as saying, Paul's effort "would have been consigned to a minor footnote of history or, more likely, forgotten altogether."
