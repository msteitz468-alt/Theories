**Biographical Details from Source**

Born 1408; died 1480. Held an "awesome array of titles": Count of Bar, Count of Provence, Count of Piedmont, Count of Guise, Duke of Calabria, Duke of Anjou, Duke of Lorraine, King of Hungary, King of Naples and Sicily, King of Aragon, Valencia, Majorca and Sardinia, and — "perhaps most resonant of all" — titular King of Jerusalem (acknowledging a continuity extending back to Godfroi de Bouillon). One of his daughters, Marguerite d'Anjou, married Henry VI of England and figured in the Wars of the Roses.

The source identifies René as a man ahead of his time, anticipating the Renaissance Italian princes. Extremely literate, he wrote prolifically, illuminated his own books, composed poetry and mystical allegories, and employed Christopher Columbus at one point. His court included a Jewish astrologer, Cabalist, and physician, Jean de Saint-Rémy — alleged grandfather of Nostradamus. He was steeped in esoteric tradition.

The source argues René was a major impetus behind the Renaissance: through his friendship with the ruling Sforza family of Milan, he influenced Cosimo de' Medici to begin collecting ancient manuscripts in 1439, found Europe's first public library (San Marco, 1444), commission translations of Platonic, Neo-Platonic, Pythagorean, Gnostic, and Hermetic thought, and instruct the University of Florence to teach Greek.

René is credited with introducing the Arcadia motif into post-Christian Western culture. In 1449 at his court of Tarascon, he staged the "Pas d'Armes of the Shepherdess" — an Arcadian tournamen combining pastoral romance, the Round Table, and Grail mysteries.

The source notes René had a particular preoccupation with the Grail. He possessed a cup of red porphyry he claimed had been used at the Wedding at Cana and obtained at Marseilles "where the Magdalene, according to tradition, landed with the Grail." The cup's inscription: "He who drinks well will see God. He who quaffs at a single draught will see God and the Magdalene."

René's early career is tied to Joan of Arc: Joan's first stop before embarking for the Dauphin's court was to request an audience with the Duke of Lorraine (René's father-in-law), at which René was present. Contemporary chroniclers assert René accompanied Joan to Chinon and was present at the siege of Orléans.
