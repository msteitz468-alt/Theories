# Éliphas Lévi (Alphonse Louis Constant, 1810–1875)

## Biographical Details from Source

According to A. E. Waite's Biographical Preface to *Transcendental Magic*, "Éliphas Lévi Zahed" is a pseudonym adopted in his occult writings by Alphonse Louis Constant, said to be the Hebrew equivalent of that name. He was born in humble circumstances about 1810, the son of a shoemaker. His parish priest, noticing his unusual intelligence, placed him on the foundation of Saint-Sulpice, where he was educated free of charge with a view to the priesthood. He took minor orders, became a deacon, and was appointed a professor at the Petit Séminaire de Paris, but was expelled — the accounts are hazy — for holding opinions contrary to Roman Catholic teaching.

By 1839 he had formed literary friendships, including with Alphonse Esquiros, who introduced him to Ganneau, a "distracted prophet" preaching political illuminism as a second incarnation of Louis XVII; Constant and Esquiros, having come to scoff, became disciples. Constant's socialist-illuminist pamphlet *The Gospel of Liberty* earned him six months' imprisonment. Despite his diaconate, he made a runaway match with the sixteen-year-old Noémie Cadiot, who bore him two children (both died young), deserted him, obtained a legal annulment, and later became famous as a sculptor under the name Claude Vignon.

Waite dates Lévi's occult study, probably, to the period between his wife's desertion and the publication of the *Dogme de la Haute Magie* in 1855, followed by the *Rituel* in 1856, then the *Histoire de la Magie* (1860), *La Clef des Grands Mystères* (1861), *Fables et Symboles* (1862), *Le Sorcier de Meudon*, and *La Science des Esprits* (1865). In later life he supported himself through admirers and pupils, receiving remuneration for personal or written courses of instruction, commonly found *chez lui* in a species of magical vestment. He exchanged Socialism for "a sort of transcendentalized Imperialism," outlived the Franco-German war, and died in 1875 "amidst the last offices of the Church which had almost expelled him from her bosom," leaving many manuscripts and innumerable letters to pupils (Baron Spedalieri alone possessed nine volumes).

## Waite's Assessment (within the source)

Waite judges that "no modern expositor of occult claims can bear any comparison with Éliphas Lévi" and that no influence in occult literature during the last two centuries has been so great — yet also that Lévi was "without accuracy in detail," never deeply read the authorities he cited (his method was *parcourir*, not *approfondir*), and was "fundamentally a materialist" who approached atheism (God as a hypothesis "very probably necessary") and disbelieved in real communication with the world of spirits. Waite holds that Lévi was never part of any circle of occult initiation — the hypothesis of the Astral Light "belongs to the records of the past," which Lévi developed into what he believed was "the key of all occult phenomena." His later works qualified and stealthily retracted the aggressive occult dogmatism of the *Doctrine and Ritual*, allegorizing the Great Work and Universal Medicine, and devising "a one-sided and insincere concordat with orthodox religion."
