## Biographical Details from Source

Jesus of Nazareth is the subject of the source's central and most controversial hypothesis. The authors present this as a hypothesis — explicitly acknowledging they cannot prove it — but treat it as consistent with the evidence they have accumulated.

**The source's central claim**

According to the source, the authors concluded — on the basis of their investigation — that Mary Magdalene was "in fact Jesus's wife," that "their union produced offspring," and that "after the Crucifixion, perhaps the Magdalene, with at least one child, was smuggled to Gaul where established Jewish communities already existed and where, in consequence, she might have found a refuge." The source states: "Perhaps there was, in short, an hereditary bloodline descended directly from Jesus. Perhaps this bloodline, this supreme *sang real*, then perpetuated itself, intact and incognito, for some four hundred years … and perhaps in the fifth century Jesus's lineage became allied with the royal line of the Franks, thereby engendering the Merovingian dynasty."

**Marital status**

The source devotes considerable attention to the question of whether Jesus was married. It notes that in first-century Palestine, celibacy for a Jewish man was "virtually unthinkable" and that the Gospels present no explicit declaration of celibacy. The source argues that the wedding at Cana, at which Jesus's mother gave instructions to servants that were then obeyed by Jesus, implies a position of authority one would expect of the bridegroom — and thus, possibly, that Jesus was the groom. The source also notes that the Gospels' reference to Mary Magdalene anointing Jesus's feet and head with expensive ointment, and wiping his feet with her hair, reflects a specifically Jewish custom designating a ritual union between a rabbi and his wife.

**The crucifixion: survival hypothesis**

The source presents the hypothesis that Jesus may not have died on the cross. It marshals several arguments:

- The Gospel of Mark, in its original Greek, has Joseph of Arimathea asking Pilate for Jesus's *soma* (a word applied only to a living body), while Pilate consents using *ptoma* (corpse). The source suggests this may imply that Joseph deliberately requested a living man while the Roman administration chose not to notice.
- Crucifixion normally took two to three days to cause death; Jesus is said to have died within hours, surprising Pilate himself according to Mark.
- Pilate's ready consent to release the body was a "flagrant breach of procedure": under Roman law, crucified men were denied burial and left on the cross for carrion birds. The authors suggest Pilate's compliance implies "some complicity on Pilate's part."
- A lance thrust into Jesus's side drew blood and water — which the source argues would indicate a living body, not a dead one.
- The source also cites what it describes as the possibility that the spiced unguents applied at burial were used for healing wounds, not embalming.

The source acknowledges: "Such a charade would, of course, have necessitated some connivance and collusion on the part of Pontius Pilate or of someone influential in the Roman administration." Pilate is described as corrupt and "susceptible to bribes."

**The Barabbas passage**

The source notes that "Barabbas" literally means "son of the father" (*bar Abbas*) in Aramaic, and raises the possibility that Barabbas and Jesus may have been the same person or that the "release of Barabbas" episode reflects a political negotiation.

**The Nag Hammadi scrolls and the Gnostic tradition**

The source cites the Gnostic Gospels — especially the Gospel of Philip and the Gospel of Mary — as providing additional evidence that the Magdalene held a special, intimate status with Jesus and that early Christians disputed the nature of that status. The source presents the conflict between Peter (the adherent of the orthodox "message") and the Magdalene (associated with the "bloodline") as reflected in the canonical Gospels, where Peter is repeatedly antagonistic toward Mary Magdalene.

**The source's wider argument**

The source argues that if Jesus did not die on the cross and did have descendants, this would explain why the Merovingians were regarded as sacred, semi-divine priest-kings; why the Church — in its pact with Clovis — bound itself to the Merovingian bloodline in perpetuity; why Dagobert II's assassination was so carefully erased from history; and why the Prieuré de Sion has, according to the "Prieuré documents," laboured over nine centuries to preserve and restore a Merovingian monarchy.

The source summarises its hypothesis: "We have formulated an hypothesis of a bloodline, descended from Jesus, which has continued up to the present day."

**The "Precious Blood" letter**

The source also notes that Pope John XXIII, in June 1960, issued an apostolic letter specifically on "The Precious Blood of Jesus," which "ascribed a hitherto unprecedented significance to that blood" and "emphasized Jesus's suffering as a human being." The source comments: "If man's redemption was achieved by the shedding of Jesus's blood, his death and resurrection became incidental if not, indeed, superfluous. Jesus need not have died on the cross for the faith to retain its validity."
