## Biographical Details from Source

The source identifies Émile Hoffet as the nephew of the Abbé Bieil, Director General of the Seminary of Saint-Sulpice in Paris. Hoffet was the person to whom Bérenger Saunière brought his parchments in 1891.

**Biographical details from a footnote**

A detailed footnote in the source states that Hoffet was born at Schiltigheim, Alsace, on 11 May 1873. In 1884 he began his studies in Paris at the Maîtrise de Montmartre, continuing them at the Petit Séminaire de Notre-Dame de Sion, where he prepared to enter the Church. He began his novitiate at Saint-Gerlach in Holland and entered the religious Order of Oblats de Marie in 1892. He was ordained as a priest at Liège in 1898, then worked as a missionary — first in Corsica, then back in France. In 1903–4 he was in Rome. He returned to Paris permanently in 1914, and died there in March 1946. The source notes he "wrote prolifically, particularly for specialist magazines on religious history" and was "a linguist, fluent in Greek, Hebrew and Sanskrit."

The same footnote reports that a colleague (Descadeillas) in 1966 wrote to the authorities of the Oblats de Marie asking whether there was any proof that Hoffet had ever preached in Rennes-le-Château. The archivist replied: "Hoffet is the author of some very interesting studies on Freemasonry, of which he had made a particular study, and I have unearthed a number of his manuscripts … I have ordered that the particularly interesting documents be placed in security."

**Role in the Saunière affair**

The source describes Saunière being sent to Paris by his bishop to present the parchments to Abbé Bieil and to Hoffet. "At the time Hoffet was training for the priesthood." Saunière was "promptly and warmly welcomed into Hoffet's distinguished circle." The source recounts that Hoffet's circle included Stéphane Mallarmé, Maurice Maeterlinck, Claude Debussy, and the opera singer Emma Calvé, as well as Jules Bois, Papus, and Jules Doinel.

The source describes Hoffet as "the typical young Modernist scholar of his age" — steeped in the Catholic Modernist Movement and trained not at Saint Sulpice, but "in Lorraine. At the Seminary School of Sion: La Colline inspirée." Hoffet thus links Saunière to the Modernist network, to the esoteric circles of fin-de-siècle Paris, and to the mystery of Sion.

The source further connects Canon Alfred Leslie Liney — an Anglican cleric who claimed knowledge of "incontrovertible proof" that Jesus did not die on the cross — to Hoffet: Liney, as a young man working in Paris, had been acquainted with Hoffet.
