## Biographical Details from Source

According to the source, Henry II of England maintained close links with the Knights Templar. The Templars were said to be instrumental in trying to reconcile Henry II with his estranged archbishop, Thomas à Becket. The source notes that the Templars' political involvement extended to the highest levels of English governance and that the Master of the Temple was regularly called to the king's Parliament, taking precedence over all priors and abbots.

Henry II also figures in connection with the fortress of Gisors and the "Prieuré documents." Jean de Gisors, listed in those documents as the first independent Grand Master of the Prieuré de Sion, was a vassal of Henry II until 1193. The source notes that Jean de Gisors held property in England — in Sussex and the manor of Titchfield in Hampshire.

**The Cutting of the Elm (1188)**

The source recounts that in 1188 a meeting took place between Henry II of England and Philippe II of France on the Champ Sacré (Sacred Field) adjacent to the fortress of Gisors. In the middle of this field stood an enormous ancient elm, said to be more than eight hundred years old and so large that nine men linking hands could barely encompass its trunk. According to one account, Henry II and his entourage took shelter under the shade of this tree, leaving the French monarch, who arrived later, to the sun. For reasons the source describes as obscure, a bloody quarrel erupted over the elm, which was subsequently cut down. The source identifies this event — "the cutting of the elm" — as marking the separation of the Prieuré de Sion from the Knights Templar, a split that had major consequences for both organisations according to the "Prieuré documents."

A footnote in the source notes that Robert de Torigny, Abbot of Mont-Saint-Michel from 1154 to 1186, was a close friend of both Henry II and Becket, and given those men's associations with the Prieuré de Sion, the Templars and Gisors, the authors suggest Robert was likely also connected with them.

## Primary Source Appearances

The source references Henry II at pages 121–22 in the context of the Templars' political influence in England, and at the account of the 1188 incident at Gisors.
