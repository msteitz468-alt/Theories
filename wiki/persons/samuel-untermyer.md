## Biographical Details from Source

Mullins (Ch. 3) presents Untermyer as the wealthy New York corporation lawyer who "single-handedly carried on the entire investigation" as Special Counsel to the [[Event: Pujo Committee Money Trust hearings (1912)]], and as a main contributor to Woodrow Wilson's campaign fund. The source argues the Pujo hearings were a deliberately ineffective "farce": the eleven congressmen barely questioned witnesses, and Untermyer's questioning allowed Jacob Schiff and J. P. Morgan to avoid disclosing anything damaging about the [[The Money Trust]].

The source cites Untermyer's autobiography in *Who's Who in American Jewry* (1926) reporting a $775,000 fee for a single merger transaction, arguing "a man who could make nearly a million dollars in one operation would not be likely to sincerely attack the wealthy men of his own class." Mullins also names Untermyer (with H. Parker Willis and Paul Warburg) as a principal writer of the administration bill, and reports Untermyer's private letter calling Carter Glass's authorship claim a "bald misstatement."
