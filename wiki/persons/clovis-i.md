## Biographical Details from Source

The source describes Clovis I as "the most famous of all Merovingian rulers," a grandson of Merovee who reigned between 481 and 511. The source identifies Clovis as the pivotal figure through whom the Roman Church established its supremacy in Western Europe — a supremacy, the authors argue, that would endure unchallenged for a thousand years.

## The Pact with the Roman Church

The source treats the conversion and baptism of Clovis as one of the most consequential events in its entire narrative. By 496, the source argues, the Roman Church was in a precarious situation, "no greater than that of any other bishop" in authority, fighting for survival against rival forms of Christianity including Arianism. It therefore turned to Clovis, who by 486 had become the most powerful potentate in Western Europe, having struck out from the Ardennes to annex adjacent kingdoms.

According to the source, a number of secret meetings occurred between Clovis and Saint Remy in 496, after which an accord was ratified between Clovis and the Roman Church. In return for becoming Rome's secular arm — the "sword of the Church of Rome" — Clovis was granted the title "Novus Constantinus" ("New Constantine"), to preside over a new Holy Roman Empire as successor to Constantine's.

The source states that one modern expert describes Clovis, prior to his baptism, as having been "fortified ... with visions of an empire in succession to that of Rome, which should be the inheritance of the Merovingian race." Another is quoted: "Clovis must now become a kind of western emperor, a patriarch to the western Germans, reigning over, though not governing, all peoples and kings."

## The Baptism at Rheims

The source describes the formal baptism of Clovis by Saint Remy at Rheims in 496. At the ceremony's climax Saint Remy pronounced: "Mitis depone colla, Sicamber, adora quod incendisti, incendi quod adorasti" (Bow thy head humbly, Sicambrian, revere what thou hast burned and what thou hast revered).

The source emphasises that the baptism was not a coronation — "the Church did not make Clovis a king. He was already that, and all the Church could do was recognise him as such." Crucially, by so doing, the Church "officially bound itself not to Clovis alone, but to his successors as well — not to a single individual, but to a bloodline." The authors compare this to the covenant God makes with King David in the Old Testament — a pact which "can be modified ... but not revoked, broken or betrayed."

## Military Campaigns and Conquest

The source states that "with irresistible efficiency, faith was imposed by the sword" as Clovis expanded the Frankish kingdom to encompass most of modern France and much of modern Germany. His most important adversaries were the Visigoths, who adhered to Arian Christianity. In 507 Clovis "decisively defeated the Visigoths at the Battle of Vouillé." Thereafter Aquitaine and Toulouse fell into Frankish hands, and the Visigoths retreated southward, eventually establishing their last remaining bastion at Rhedae — now the village of Rennes-le-Château.

## Death and Succession

Clovis died in 511, and "the empire he had created was divided, according to Merovingian custom, between his four sons." The source argues that the main line of Merovingian descent effectively ended with the assassination of Dagobert II in 679 — at which point the "later Merovingians were of cadet branches, not scions of the main line descended from Clovis and Merovee."

## The Betrayal of the Pact

The source's central argument about the Merovingian bloodline turns on the Church's alleged betrayal of its pact with Clovis. When Pope Pepin III sanctioned the Carolingian usurpation, "the pact with Clovis and the Merovingian bloodline had been shamelessly betrayed." The source quotes one modern authority: "Rome showed the way by providing in unction a king-making rite that somehow cleared the consciences of 'all the Franks'." The source states that according to materials from the Prieuré de Sion, "the only authentic nobility is the nobility of Visigothic/Merovingian origin" and that "in consecrating Charlemagne in the year 800, the Church perjured itself, for it had concluded, at the baptism of Clovis, an alliance with the Merovingians which had made France the eldest daughter of the Church."

## Connection to Arthur

The source briefly notes that Arthur was "closely contemporary with Clovis" at "the peak of Merovingian ascendancy in Gaul," and that the Grail romances were set in this era. The source also speculates that just as the name "Ursus" (Latin for "bear") was associated with the Merovingian line, the Welsh name "Arthur" (meaning bear) may represent an attempt to confer comparable dignity on a British chieftain.
