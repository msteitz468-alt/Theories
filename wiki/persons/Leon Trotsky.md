## Biographical Details from Source

In *Wall Street and the Bolshevik Revolution*, Antony Sutton treats Leon Trotsky as a central
figure of the 1917 episode. Sutton focuses on Trotsky's three months in New York (January–March
1917), where he wrote for the Russian-language paper *Novy Mir* and earned, by Sutton's reckoning,
only a few hundred dollars — yet left for Russia in March 1917 with **$10,000** of unexplained
(officially "German") origin, a discrepancy Sutton highlights via the 1919 Senate Overman Committee
testimony.

Sutton documents that President Woodrow Wilson facilitated Trotsky's return with a **U.S. passport**
(see [[President Wilson gave Trotsky a U.S. passport to return to Russia; Trotsky was briefly detained at Halifax (Sutton)]]),
that Trotsky sailed on the S.S. *Kristianiafjord* on March 26, 1917 "in company with other
Trotskyite revolutionaries, Wall Street financiers, American Communists," and that he was removed by
British/Canadian naval personnel at Halifax on April 3, 1917, interned at Amherst, Nova Scotia as a
listed German prisoner of war (Canadian Army form LB-1, serial 1098, signed "Leon Bromstein
Trotsky"), then released to continue to Petrograd.

Sutton's interest in Trotsky is evidentiary rather than biographical: he uses the passport, the
$10,000, and the *Kristianiafjord* passenger list as threads in his "alliance of bankers and
revolution" argument, while cautioning that they "suggest… without at this point providing evidence"
a financier link.
