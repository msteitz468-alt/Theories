## Biographical Details from Source

The source mentions Pépin d'Héristal — referred to as "Pepin the Fat" — in the specific context of Dagobert II's assassination and the fall of the Merovingian dynasty.

The source explains that as power seeped from the hands of the later Merovingians, it "passed into the hands of the Mayors of the Palace — a process that had already commenced before Dagobert's reign." The source then states: "It was a Mayor of the Palace, Pepin the Fat, who engineered Dagobert's death." Dagobert II was assassinated in 679 (the source identifies the date as December 23rd, which is also described as the feast day of Saint Dagobert).

The source notes that Pepin the Fat was followed by his son, the famous Charles Martel, who checked the Moorish invasion of France at the Battle of Poitiers in 732. Curiously, the source observes, Charles Martel — "strong man though he was, never seized the throne — which certainly lay within his grasp. In fact he seems to have regarded the throne with a certain superstitious awe and, in all probability, as a specifically Merovingian prerogative."

The broader narrative context is the source's argument that Dagobert II's assassination was the critical blow to the main Merovingian bloodline: "the later Merovingians were of cadet branches, not scions of the main line descended from Clovis and Merovee. The main line of Merovingian descent had been deposed with Dagobert II."

The index also references "Pepin the Fat, 263" and "Pepin III, King, 266, 267-8, 414-15" as distinct individuals.
