## Biographical Overview from Source

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], John the Baptist was an Essene and a direct lineal descendant of the Zadokite priests of [[The twenty-four ma'amadot (high-priestly families)]]. The authors describe the entire Jordan valley between the Lake of Genasseret and the Dead Sea as "alive with holy eccentrics, many of whom had been to Qumran where they were imbued with Essene teaching," and place John squarely within this milieu.

### Mission and Teaching

John saw his mission, per the source, as creating "an elite within the elite" of Israel — a purified remnant to hasten the apocalyptic transformation that was the necessary prelude to the final battle of righteousness over evil. His own people believed he was Elijah returned (John 1:21), fulfilling the Old Testament prophecy of the "messenger of the covenant who would purify the sons of Levi" (Malachi 3:1–4) and the return of Elijah as reconciler (Malachi 4:5–6).

Citing the historian Josephus, the authors stress that John's baptism was not a ritual removal of sin but a physical and external cleansing that symbolized that internal purification had already taken place through righteous behavior. To be immersed by John, a follower first had to accept his teaching and already be living righteously.

### Relationship with Jesus

The authors assert that Jesus was John's disciple — a position the Church has always denied but which modern scholarship and the Rex Deus tradition alike affirm. Per the source, this teaching relationship has been "kept alive for 2,000 years among the hidden streams of spirituality preserved by the descendants of the ma'amadot, namely the Rex Deus families and their spiritual heirs, the Templars and the Freemasons." The source notes that if Jesus was John's pupil, by John's own definition Jesus must have been a sinner seeking restoration to righteousness before baptism — a theologically untenable conclusion for orthodox Christianity.

### Execution

Josephus records John's execution by Herod Antipas, attributing it to Herod's fear that John's oratorical power over crowds might provoke sedition. The Gospel account (execution prompted by Herodias and her daughter Salome) is judged by the authors to be compatible with Josephus rather than contradictory — both reflecting concurrent aspects of Herod's motivation.
