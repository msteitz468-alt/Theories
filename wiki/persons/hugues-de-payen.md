## Biographical Details from Source

According to the source, Hugues de Payen was the founder and first Grand Master of the Order of the Poor Knights of Christ and the Temple of Solomon (the Knights Templar). He was a nobleman from Champagne and a vassal of the count of Champagne.

A footnote in the source states that, according to one scholar, Hugues de Payen "was not born in Champagne but in the chateau of Mahun, near Annonay in the lower Rhône valley (Ardèche). His birth record has been found and the date of birth given is February 9th, 1070. Presumably he later moved to Champagne."

## Foundation of the Knights Templar

The source follows Guillaume de Tyre in placing the founding of the Order in 1118. According to this account, Hugues "unsolicited, presented himself with eight comrades at the palace of Baudouin I — king of Jerusalem." Baudouin received them "most cordially, as did the Patriarch of Jerusalem."

The source notes, however, a major anomaly: Fulk de Chartres, the official royal historian writing during the very years in question, "makes no mention whatever of Hugues de Payen, Hugues's companions or anything even remotely connected with the Knights Templar. Indeed there is a thunderous silence about Templar activities during the early days of their existence."

The "Dossiers secrets" as reproduced by the source further identifies Hugues de Payen as one of three primary founders (alongside Bisol de St. Omer and Hugues, Count of Champagne), along with "certain members of the Ordre de Sion" — meaning the Prieuré de Sion predated and helped create the Templar order.

## Council of Troyes and Grand Mastership

After nine years, in 1127, most of the founding knights returned to Europe. In January 1128, a Church council at Troyes officially recognised the Templars as a religious-military order. At this council, "Hugues de Payen was given the title of Grand Master." The source recounts that when Hugues visited England in late 1128, he was received "with 'great worship' by King Henry I." He donated his own properties to the Order, and required all new recruits to do likewise.

## Hugues's Shield and the Baphomet Connection

The source notes that in one account, the mysterious head allegedly worshipped by the Templars (the "Baphomet") was said to be "that of Hugues de Payen, the Order's founder and first Grand Master; and it is suggestive that Hugues's shield consisted of three black heads on a gold field." Another scholar, quoted in a footnote, "claims that the three black heads on the family shield of Hugues de Payen are such a device with a dual meaning" linked to the Arabic word for "wise."

## Joint Grand Mastership of Sion and the Temple

The source states that until 1188, "the Ordre de Sion and the Order of the Temple are said to have shared the same Grand Master. Hugues de Payen and Bertrand de Blanchefort, for example, would thus have presided over both institutions simultaneously." The source also cites a charter dated May 2, 1125, on which Hugues de Payen's name appears alongside that of a Prior Arnaldus of Notre Dame de Sion.

## Death

The source discusses at length the date of Hugues de Payen's death, weighing the "Prieure list" (which gives 1131) against modern sources (which give 1136). The authors conclude that the Prieuré list "appears to have the evidence in its favour" — specifically that the last charter signed by Hugues using his full name ("Hugo de Pagano") is dated 1130.

## Return to Palestine

When Hugues returned to Palestine in 1130, the source states, he brought "an entourage quite considerable for the time — of some three hundred knights," leaving behind in Europe vast tracts of territory in the custody of other recruits.
