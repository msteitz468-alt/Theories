## Biographical Details from Source

James Lovelock (b. 1919), described by the book as "even more of a maverick or independent thinker" than Fred Hoyle, and "that rare being, someone whose brilliance has actually changed the face of the world." An "independent scientist" who avoided academic and commercial institutions throughout his career, Lovelock discovered in the early 1970s that human-made chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) had penetrated the environment globally — directly resulting in the CFC-free world of today.

Per the book, Lovelock developed the Gaia hypothesis as a spin-off of NASA work in the 1960s on detecting life on Mars. He proposed that Earth's living systems (the biosphere) do not merely inhabit the planet but actively regulate it — keeping temperatures, atmospheric composition, and other conditions within the narrow range required for life, despite a 30 per cent increase in solar output over 3.5 billion years. He called Earth "a self-regulating entity" and a "superorganism." The hypothesis was named "Gaia" at the suggestion of his neighbour, novelist William Golding.

The book notes that Richard Dawkins "hated Gaia as much as he hates God" because it could not be explained by natural selection acting on individuals. Much of the controversial Gaia hypothesis has since become mainstream. The book is careful to note Lovelock's own caveats: he explicitly rejects New Age interpretations of Earth consciousness and uses "alive" metaphorically ("the planet is alive in the same way that a gene is selfish"). He applies the term "superorganism" technically: "bounded systems made up partly from living organisms and partly from non-living structural material."

The authors connect Gaia to the Hermetic concept of the anima mundi ("world soul") and present it as evidence that life, once established, is actively maintained — consistent with a designer universe.
