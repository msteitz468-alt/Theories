## Biographical Overview from Source

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], James the Just was the biological brother of [[Jesus of Nazareth]], the true successor Jesus appointed to lead his disciples, and a hereditary member of [[The twenty-four ma'amadot (high-priestly families)]]. The authors draw on the Gospel of Thomas (Nag-Hammadi), the Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions, Epiphanius, Clement of Alexandria, and Robert Eisenman's scholarship.

### Appointment as Successor

The source quotes the Gospel of Thomas (Nag-Hammadi, 1945): "Wherever you are, you are to go to James the Righteous, for whose sake heaven and earth came into being." This language deliberately echoes Kabbalistic descriptions of Noah as "the Foundation of the World." Epiphanius describes James as "The first to whom the Lord entrusted his Throne upon Earth." Whether by direct appointment or apostolic election, both strands of evidence confirm James — not Peter — as the true successor of Jesus. The "Petrine foundation myth" is described by the authors as a deliberate Church construction to proclaim Rome's supremacy.

### High-Priestly Role

As a Nazorite "dedicated to Holiness from his mother's womb," James was celibate — one of the few exceptions to the Jewish obligation to marry. Epiphanius records that James exercised the high priesthood "according to the Ancient Priesthood" and was permitted to enter the Holy of Holies once a year and to wear the High Priestly diadem. The Dead Sea Scrolls scholar Robert Eisenman, cited by the source, equates James the Just with the "Teacher of Righteousness" of the Essenes.

### Political Stance and Conflict with Paul

James led a strict, nationalistic, Torah-observant, anti-Herodian, and anti-Roman movement. He was at the center of temple agitation in the years before the Jewish War. His theological conflict with St. Paul is described in detail: James insisted on doing Torah, strict dietary laws, prohibition on mixing with Gentiles, and circumcision; Paul abolished all of these. The source uses Eisenman's analysis of the Dead Sea Scrolls to argue that the "Liar" figure in the Scrolls corresponds to Paul, and the "Teacher of Righteousness" to James.

The confrontation turned violent: Paul physically assaulted James, throwing him headlong down the temple steps and breaking his legs — an event recorded in the Pseudo-Clementine Recognitions and referenced by Epiphanius.

### Execution and its Aftermath

James was executed by the Sanhedrin under the new Sadducee high priest Ananus, appointed by King Agrippa II (Paul's relative). The Mishna Sanhedrin procedure was followed: he was jostled by priests, cast from the Temple wall, stoned, and given the coup de grace with a fuller's club. The Church fathers Origen and Eusebius, citing a now-lost version of Josephus, stated that the fall of Jerusalem was believed to be a consequence of James's death. After his murder, the Ebionites and surviving members of the ma'amadot crossed the Jordan to Pella under the leadership of James's "cousin," Simeon. Leadership of the group (the Desposyni) remained within the family of Jesus for over 150 years.
