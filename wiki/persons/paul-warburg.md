## Biographical Details from Source

Mullins presents Paul Warburg as the central architect of the Federal Reserve System. Per the source: a German immigrant who arrived in the United States in 1902 and within a few years became a partner in Kuhn, Loeb & Co. at a salary of $500,000 a year; his family house, M. M. Warburg & Co. of Hamburg and Amsterdam, was "the chief German representative of the great European banking family, the Rothschilds." He was naturalized in 1911 and, the source states, decorated by the Kaiser in 1912.

Mullins calls Warburg the "banking brain" of New York who spent his time writing and lecturing on monetary reform. The source identifies him as the principal drafter of the plan written at [[Event: Jekyll Island drafting meeting (November 1910)]], the man who coined the name "Federal Reserve System" to avoid the term "central bank," and the designer of the regional-reserve disguise. Mullins quotes Professor E. R. A. Seligman ("The Federal Reserve Act is the work of Mr. Warburg more than of any other man in the country") and J. Laurence Laughlin in support.

Warburg served on the first Federal Reserve Board of Governors (1914), resigning in May 1918; the source quotes a US Naval Secret Service report (Dec. 12, 1918) alleging he "handled large sums furnished by Germany for Lenin and Trotsky" and had a brother heading German espionage. After leaving the Board he continued, per Mullins, to dominate Federal Reserve policy as President of the Federal Advisory Council and Chairman of the Executive Committee of the [[American Acceptance Council]] until his death in 1932. The source credits him with organizing the International Acceptance Bank, Agfa Ansco, and American I. G. Chemical Corporation (I. G. Farben's US branch).

## Primary Source Appearances

Central figure of Chapters 6, 7, and 10; recurs throughout. Mullins quotes Warburg's March 1929 warning to stockholders of his International Acceptance Bank as the signal to exit the market before the Crash.
