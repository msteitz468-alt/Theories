## Biographical Details from Source

University of London physicist, "a protege of Einstein's and one of the world's most respected quantum physicists." Talbot recounts that Bohm did doctoral work under Robert Oppenheimer and, in 1951, was called before McCarthy's House Un-American Activities Committee to testify against Oppenheimer; he refused, lost his Princeton post, and never taught in the U.S. again (moving to Brazil, then London). Six months of conversations with Einstein at Princeton crystallized his dissatisfaction with the standard ("Copenhagen") interpretation of quantum theory. He developed the concepts of the **quantum potential**, **nonlocality**, and ultimately the **implicate order**, presented in *Wholeness and the Implicate Order* (1980).
