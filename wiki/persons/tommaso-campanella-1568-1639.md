## Biographical from Source

Per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], Tommaso Campanella (1568–1639) was born in Stilo, Calabria — then part of the Spanish-ruled Kingdom of Naples — and, like [[Giordano Bruno]], was a Dominican friar from a humble background who became a Hermetic radical. The book identifies him as "Bruno's spiritual heir," noting that "the similarities between the careers, philosophies and aims of Bruno and Campanella are so striking that they must have been working to the same plan."

After a two-year stay in Naples in the early 1590s, during which he was influenced by both [[Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499)]] and the polymath Giovanni Battista della Porta, Campanella arrived in Padua in 1592, where he met Gian Vincenzo Pinelli and the newly appointed Professor of Mathematics — [[Galileo Galilei (1564–1642)]]. He and Galileo "were to stay in touch for the rest of their lives."

Arrested by the Inquisition in early 1594 and transferred to the same Rome prison as Bruno, Campanella was released into house arrest after agreeing to abjure. In 1597 his superiors ordered him back to Naples, where he immediately organized the Calabrian revolt — an uprising aiming to overthrow Spanish rule and establish a republic run according to "magical and astrological principles." The revolt was betrayed and crushed in November 1599; Campanella and the other leaders were arrested. The book argues that Bruno was executed barely three months later partly as "an example to the Calabrian rebels," given his role as the revolt's spiritual inspiration.

Campanella escaped execution by feigning madness — maintaining the pretence even under prolonged torture — and was sentenced to life imprisonment. For the next twenty-seven years he was held in Neapolitan castle dungeons, yet enjoyed a remarkable degree of scholarly freedom: supplied with books and writing materials, he received a "steady flow of scholarly visitors, mainly from Germany," who carried his writings back for publication.

**Key works:**
- *City of the Sun* (*Civitas Solis*), written in the early 1600s, published Frankfurt 1623. A utopian dialogue describing a society "designed and run according to magical and astrological principles" — Campanella's ideal Hermetic-Egyptian republic, derived from the *Lament* in *Asclepius*. The book quotes specialist George Lechner: "In it, Campanella developed the notion of a new city-state, led by a philosopher-priest-king, and guided by Hermetic magical principles." The authors note Campanella was "the first person to call the infant Louis [XIV] the Sun King, as an acknowledgement of his great Hermetic potential."
- *Defence of Galileo* (1622) — written from prison in support of Galileo, using "language strongly reminiscent of Bruno" in describing heliocentricity as "a return to ancient truth and as portending a new age."

In 1626, Pope Urban VIII summoned Campanella to Rome to perform "protective magic to ward off the evil effects of an eclipse," freeing him after twenty-seven years. Urban even granted him permission to found a college to train missionaries in his religious and philosophical ideas. After a new Calabrian revolt in 1634 implicated one of his followers, Campanella fled to Paris, where he became a favourite of Cardinal Richelieu. He died in Paris in May 1639.
