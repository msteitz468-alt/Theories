## Biographical from Source

Per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], Nicolaus Copernicus (born Mikolaj Kopernik, 1473–1543) was a Polish church canon who proposed the heliocentric theory of the solar system in *On the Revolutions of the Celestial Spheres* (*De revolutionibus orbium coelestium*, 1543), traditionally considered "the very birth of science itself" (Paul Davies). He was born in Torun, Poland; his father died young, leaving an uncle — later a bishop — to raise him. He studied church law and then medicine at Padua in Italy, developing his passion for astronomy in his leisure time.

Copernicus developed his heliocentric theory in the first decade of the sixteenth century but was reluctant to publish for many years, circulating only a private account in the early 1510s. The source corrects the popular myth that he delayed out of fear of the Vatican; he was primarily concerned with academic controversy, and was actually urged to publish by a cardinal who had attended a lecture on the theory given to Pope Paul III's secretary.

**The Hermetic connection.** The book argues that Copernicus's heliocentrism was directly inspired by the Hermetica. On the thirty-first page of *On the Revolutions*, just below the famous diagram showing planets orbiting the sun, Copernicus explicitly invokes [[Hermes Trismegistus]], describing the sun as "Trismegistus' visible God." The source contends that all three revolutionary ideas in *On the Revolutions* — the Earth's motion in space, its rotation on its axis, and the orbiting of planets around the sun — appear in the Hermetica prior to Copernicus. In particular, Hermetic Treatise XVI states "the sun is situated at the centre of the cosmos, wearing it like a crown" and describes eight spheres that "depend from" the sun — language that, the authors argue, describes a heliocentric system taken for granted.

The book quotes [[Frances Yates]]'s assessment: that either the "intense emphasis on the sun" in the Hermetic tradition was "the emotional driving force which induced Copernicus to undertake his mathematical calculations on the hypothesis that the sun is indeed at the centre," or he wished to make his discovery acceptable "by presenting it within the framework of this new attitude — perhaps both."

Copernicus studied in Rome and Padua in the 1480s and 1490s, where Ficino's translation of the Hermetica "was on everyone's lips." His citation of Hermes was therefore, the authors argue, no casual flourish but evidence of genuine Hermetic conviction.
