## Biographical Details from Source

The source, based on the "Dossiers secrets" and the "Cercle d'Ulysse" pamphlet, lists Victor Hugo as Grand Master of the Prieuré de Sion from 1844 to 1885. The source describes him as one of several "illustrious and improbable names" on the Grand Masters list, noting that while Hugo and others like Debussy and Cocteau "were immersed in such matters, they would seem to be too well known, too well researched and documented, to have exercised a 'Grand Mastership' over a secret order."

**Background and connections**

According to the source, Hugo's family was originally from Lorraine — he himself later insisted on distinguished aristocratic descent. He was born in Besançon in 1802. His father was a general under Napoleon, but maintained cordial relations with Napoleon's political opponents. One of those opponents lived in the same house and was Hugo's godfather and mentor, giving Hugo early exposure to "the world of intrigue, conspiracy and secret societies from the age of seven."

By age seventeen Hugo was a fervent disciple of Charles Nodier (listed in the "Prieuré documents" as his predecessor as Grand Master). The source recounts that it was from Nodier that Hugo "acquired his erudite knowledge of Gothic architecture, which figures so saliently in The Hunchback of Notre Dame." In 1819 Hugo and his brother established a publishing house with Nodier, producing a magazine under Nodier's editorial direction. In 1822 Hugo married in a special ceremony at Saint Sulpice — a location the source identifies as central to the entire mystery. Three years later Hugo and Nodier, with their wives, travelled to Switzerland and to the coronation of Charles X. Hugo was one of the pallbearers at Nodier's funeral in 1845.

Like Newton, the source observes, Hugo was "a deeply religious man, but his religious views were highly unorthodox. Like Newton, he was militantly anti-Trinitarian and repudiated Jesus's divinity." Immersed in Gnostic, Cabalistic and Hermetic thought — a preoccupation shaped by Nodier's influence — Hugo is known to have been connected with a "Rose-Croix" order that also included Eliphas Levi and the young Maurice Barrès.

The source notes Hugo's complicated political attitudes: despite personal admiration for Napoleon, he was a staunch royalist who welcomed the Bourbon restoration but appears ultimately to have despised the Bourbons and was "particularly fierce in his condemnation of Louis XIV." The ruler Hugo most enthusiastically endorsed — and was close personal friends with — was Louis-Philippe, the "Citizen King," who was allied by marriage to the house of Habsburg-Lorraine (his wife was Maximilian de Lorraine's niece).

The source records that Claude Debussy made Hugo's acquaintance through the symbolist poet Paul Verlaine, and subsequently set a number of Hugo's works to music. Cocteau was a close friend of Hugo's great-grandson, Jean Hugo.

In 1833 Hugo served on a committee, along with Jules Michelet and Baron Emmanuel Rey, under the auspices of the Ministry of Public Education, which published hitherto suppressed historical documents pertinent to the history of France — including primary materials on the Prieuré de Sion.

Hugo was also a friend of Maurice Joly, a man described as a member of a Rose-Croix order and as sharing Hugo's antipathy to Napoleon III.
