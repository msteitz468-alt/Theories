## Biographical Details from Source

Sir Fred Hoyle (1915–2001), British astrophysicist and "celebrated — and to some, notorious — scientific maverick." Per the book, Hoyle is the pivotal modern scientist in the fine-tuning argument. In 1951 he predicted the precise energy resonance (the "triple-alpha process") required for carbon to form inside stars — a calculation confirmed experimentally by a team at Caltech. The authors note this was "a watershed event in the modern history of science." Despite the American experimental team receiving the Nobel Prize when the prizes were awarded in the mid-1980s, Hoyle was excluded — the book contends this was "almost certainly because by the time the prizes were awarded … Hoyle had championed two controversies too far": panspermia (life seeded from space) and his "intelligent universe" thesis.

Hoyle coined the dismissive term "big bang" — ironically his own team later found some of the best supporting evidence for it. The book quotes his 1957 Cambridge lecture: "the laws of nuclear physics have been deliberately designed with regard to the consequences they produce inside the stars. If this is so, then my apparently random quirks have become part of a deep-laid scheme. If not then we are back again at a monstrous sequence of accidents." He described the precise energy resonance as a "put-up job."

Together with Chandra Wickramasinghe (his long-term collaborator, from 1962), Hoyle advanced panspermia — the idea that life's building blocks, including what they called "protocells," were seeded onto Earth by comets. His 1983 book *The Intelligent Universe* argued for a purposeful, creative universe. He dismissed the mainstream response to fine-tuning as "a modern attempt to evade all implications of purpose in the Universe."
