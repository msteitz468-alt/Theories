## Biographical Details from Source

According to Lehrich, Alfred Watkins was a traveling salesman for his family's milling and brewing business in Herefordshire, England, as well as a respected amateur photographer who invented the Watkins exposure meter. In 1921, while standing on a high ridge top in his native Herefordshire and comparing the view to a map, Watkins reported a vision: he saw "that various prehistoric places, such as standing stones, earthen burial mounds, prehistoric earthworked hills, and other such features fell into straight lines for miles across country." He called this experience a "flood of ancestral memory."

## Primary Source Appearances

Lehrich summarizes Watkins's theory: "the early inhabitants of Britain deliberately placed mounds, camps and standing stones across the landscape in straight lines. As time went by later structures were added to these sites. Some Roman roads followed the leys, Christian churches were built on what had been ley markers... and the keeps of mediaeval castles were sited on mounds that had marked leys millennia before." Watkins's theory produced the Straight Track Club and many amateur ley hunters, but was flatly rejected by professional archaeologists and, per Lehrich (quoting Paul Devereux), the club had folded by 1948 from lack of interest before ley hunting was revived and transformed in the 1960s by figures such as Tony Wedd and John Michell.

Lehrich notes that Watkins himself "recognized the provisional nature of his work," in contrast to later occult elaborations of his theory.
