## Biographical Details from Source

Mullins (Ch. 8) presents Baruch as one of three "emperors" who ruled the United States during World War I. Per the source, Baruch first drew notice on Wall Street around 1890, organized the Consolidated Tobacco Co. (1896) and the Guggenheims' copper trust, and described his own profession before a congressional committee as "speculator." The source states he contributed $50,000 to Wilson's campaign and "as payment received control of America's heavy industry."

As Chairman of the War Industries Board and dominant member of the Munitions Price-Fixing Committee, Mullins says Baruch set the prices at which the government bought war materials from Baruch-connected firms and spent taxpayer money "at the rate of ten billion dollars a year." The source quotes his Nye Committee testimony (1937): "All wars are economic in their origin," and his account of compelling Judge Gary of US Steel with a presidential letter authorizing him to take over any plant.

Mullins reports Baruch made $750,000 in one day on US Steel stock after planting a false peace rumor in 1918, and that he later financed the Democratic Party between the wars and dictated (per the source) the rationing, price-control, and labor-control framework adopted in WWII through his lectures to the Nye Committee.
