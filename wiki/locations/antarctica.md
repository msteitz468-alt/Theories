## Geographical and Historical Context from Source

In *Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings*, Antarctica is the central evidentiary subject of Hapgood's thesis. He notes that conventionally the continent "was supposedly discovered only in 1818," and that although "a good many world maps of the 16th Century show an antarctic continent," belief in it "lasted until the time of Captain Cook, whose voyages into the South Seas demonstrated the non-existence of a southern continent at least in the latitudes where one appears on these maps." Geographers thereafter dismissed the depicted continent as an imaginative device "to balance off the concentration of land in the northern hemisphere."

Hapgood's investigation began with Captain Arlington H. Mallery's suggestion that the **Piri Re'is Map** of 1513 shows part of Antarctica — specifically "bays and islands of the Antarctic coast of Queen Maud Land now concealed under the Antarctic ice cap" — implying "that somebody had mapped this coast before the ice had appeared." Hapgood reports that Mallery's reading drew confidence from scientists including the Rev. Daniel L. Linehan, S.J. (who had been to Antarctica) and the Rev. Francis Heyden, S.J.

Working with MIT mathematician Richard W. Strachan, Hapgood and his Keene State College students reconstructed the map's projection grid. He argues that the apparent northward displacement of the Antarctic coast results from the mapmaker's omission of Drake Passage and a stretch of the South American coast, errors he says recur "on all maps of the Renaissance showing the Antarctic."

Hapgood states that the Piri Re'is testimony "does not stand alone." He singles out the **Oronteus Finaeus Map of 1531/1532**, of which he writes that on seeing it he "had the instant conviction that I had found here a truly authentic map of the real Antarctica": its general shape, the near-central position of the South Pole, and individualized coastal mountain ranges with natural drainage patterns "suggested … that the coasts may have been ice-free when the original map was drawn," while "the deep interior … free entirely of rivers and mountains" suggested the ice "might have been present there." He compares its proportions, and those of the Hadji Ahmed and Mercator maps, against modern maps of the continent.

Hapgood also cites cross sections of the Queen Maud Land ice cap (from *The Geographical Journal*, June 1954), arguing that sub-ice extensions of the coast correspond to islands and bays shown on the Piri Re'is Map.
