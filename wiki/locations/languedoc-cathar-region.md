**Geographical and Historical Context from Source**

At the beginning of the thirteenth century, the Languedoc was not officially part of France but an independent principality with closer cultural ties to Spain than to northern France. Its language, culture, and political institutions were distinct. It was ruled by the Counts of Toulouse and the powerful house of Trencavel.

The source describes it as "the most advanced and sophisticated [culture] in Christendom, with the possible exception of Byzantium" — a civilization characterized by: high literacy even among nobility; philosophy, Greek, Arabic, and Hebrew studies; thriving Cabalistic schools at Lunel and Narbonne; courtly love and poetry; Islamic and Judaic intellectual threads absorbed through maritime commerce and across the Pyrenees from Spain; religious tolerance practiced in marked contrast to the fanatical north.

The Roman Church's authority in the region was slack and its clerics notoriously corrupt — some churches had not held mass for thirty years; one Archbishop of Narbonne never visited his diocese.

By 1200 the Cathar heresy had penetrated the region so deeply that it threatened to displace Catholicism entirely. The Albigensian Crusade (1209–c.1250) was launched to extirpate it, and in the process destroyed the Languedoc's unique civilization, "plunging it back into the barbarity that characterised the rest of Europe."

**Textual Appearances**

The Languedoc provides the immediate historical and geographical context for the Cathars (Part One) and for the Rennes-le-Château mystery. Rennes-le-Château sits at the heart of what was Cathar territory; the parchment references to Dagobert II and the presence of Visigoth and Templar ruins all speak to the region's layered history. The source also notes that many modern-day residents of the region still openly proclaim Cathar sympathies.
