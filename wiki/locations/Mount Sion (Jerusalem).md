**Geographical and Historical Context from Source**

Mount Sion is the "high hill" just south of Jerusalem. In 1099, when Jerusalem fell to Godfroi de Bouillon's crusaders, there stood on this hill the ruins of an old Byzantine basilica — called "the Mother of all Churches" — dating supposedly from the fourth century. At Godfroi's express command, an abbey was built on the site of these ruins: the Abbey of Notre Dame du Mont de Sion.

According to a 1172 chronicler, the abbey was "extremely well fortified, with its own walls, towers and battlements." A 1698 historian states it was inhabited by "knights attached to the Abbey of Notre Dame de Sion who took the name of 'Chevaliers de l'Ordre de Notre Dame de Sion.'"

A charter signed by Prior Arnaldus dates from July 19, 1116. A Pope Alexander III Bull of 1178 officially confirms the Order's possessions, which included lands in Picardy, France (including Saint-Samson at Orléans), Lombardy, Sicily, Spain, Calabria, and the Holy Land including Saint Leonard at Acre.

When Jerusalem fell to Saladin in 1187, the abbey was lost and the Ordre de Sion returned en masse to France, triggering the "Cutting of the Elm" crisis of 1188.

**Textual Appearances**

Mount Sion is the physical origin point of the Prieuré de Sion and thus of the entire secret-society thread of the investigation. The authors note that the recurring references to "Sion" — in the encoded parchments, in the "Prieuré documents," in the title of the organization — all trace back to this location.
