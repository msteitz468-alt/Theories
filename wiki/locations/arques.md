## Geographical and Historical Context from Source

Arques is described in the source as a village approximately six miles from Rennes-le-Château and three miles from the chateau of Blanchefort.

## The Tomb near Arques and Poussin's Painting

The source's most significant treatment of Arques concerns a real tomb located on its outskirts that the authors argue is identical to the one depicted in Nicolas Poussin's famous painting "Les Bergers d'Arcadie" (also known as "Et in Arcadia Ego"). The authors write:

"In the early 1970s, however, an actual tomb was located, identical to the one in the painting — identical in setting, dimensions, proportions, shape, surrounding vegetation, even in the circular outcrop of rock on which one of Poussin's shepherds rests his foot. This actual tomb stands on the outskirts of a village called Arques — approximately six miles from Rennes-le-Château, and three miles from the chateau of Blanchefort. If one stands before the sepulchre the vista is virtually indistinguishable from that in the painting. And then it becomes apparent that one of the peaks in the background of the painting is Rennes-le-Château."

The source further states that there is no indication of the age of the tomb. According to local peasants, the tomb "has been there for as long as they, their parents and grandparents can remember," and there is said to be mention of it in a memoir dating from 1709. An American named Louis Lawrence of Boston, Massachusetts owned the land on which the tomb stands until his death in the 1950s; in the 1920s he opened the sepulchre and found it empty. His wife and mother-in-law were later buried in it.

## Cathar Connections

The source notes that even at the time of writing, "there is even a Cathar church and a so-called 'Cathar pope' who, until his death in 1978, lived in the village of Arques."

## Noble Ownership of Arques

The source traces ownership of Arques through several interconnected noble families. Henriette-Catherine de Joyeuse — the woman who married Charles, Duke of Guise — was the owner of Couiza and Arques. It is noted that Robert Fludd was specifically called to act as personal tutor to the sons of the duke of Guise; that after the duke conspired against the French throne and went into voluntary exile in Italy in 1631, his wife "was not allowed to return to France until she consented to sell Couiza and Arques to the crown."

Earlier, the source notes that Charles de Montpensier and de Bourbon's personal entourage included "one Jean de Joyeuse, who, through marriage, had become lord of Couiza, Rennes-le-Château and Arques, near where the tomb identical to the one in Poussin's painting stands."

The source also connects Charles, Duke of Guise, to the line whose legitimate claim to the French throne he asserted — noting that he "had married Henriette-Catherine de Joyeuse, owner of Couiza and Arques" — as part of its discussion of Merovingian dynastic ambitions in the sixteenth century.
