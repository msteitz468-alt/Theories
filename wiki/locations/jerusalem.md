## Geographical and Historical Context from Source

Jerusalem occupies a central place in the source's narrative as the focus of the Crusades, the site of the Temple of Solomon whose excavation by the Knights Templar is central to the book's thesis, and the seat of a kingdom that the authors claim was established by Merovingian blood.

## The Fall of Jerusalem (1099) and Godfroi de Bouillon

The source describes how in 1099 Godfroi de Bouillon's crusading army captured Jerusalem from the Saracens. Immediately after the capture, "a group of anonymous figures convened in secret conclave" to elect a king of Jerusalem. Although Raymond, Count of Toulouse, had a persuasive claim, the mysterious conclave offered the throne to Godfroi, who declined the title of king and accepted instead "Defender of the Holy Sepulchre." His brother Baudouin became the first official king of Jerusalem in 1100.

The source argues that Godfroi was a lineal descendant of the Merovingian dynasty (through Dagobert II's son Sigisbert IV), so that with his capture of Jerusalem "Jesus's lineage would have regained its rightful heritage."

## The Abbey of Notre Dame du Mont de Sion

According to the source, Godfroi de Bouillon ordered the construction of an abbey on Mount Sion — the "high hill" just south of Jerusalem — on the site of the ruins of an old Byzantine basilica known as "the Mother of all Churches." The source states: "According to one chronicler, writing in 1172, it was extremely well fortified, with its own walls, towers and battlements." This structure was called the Abbey of Notre Dame du Mont de Sion. The source reproduces a photograph of "The Tomb of David, Abbey of Notre Dame du Mont de Sion, Jerusalem" (photograph no. 10).

An earlier nineteenth-century historian is quoted as stating there were "knights attached to the Abbey of Notre Dame de Sion who took the name of 'Chevaliers de l'Ordre de Notre Dame de Sion'." The source also cites a charter signed by a Prior Arnaldus dated July 19, 1116, and another dated May 2, 1125, on which Arnaldus's name appears alongside that of Hugues de Payen.

## The Temple of Solomon and the Knights Templar

The source identifies the "Temple of Solomon" and the "Stables of Solomon" as the site beneath which the Knights Templar conducted secret excavations. According to the source's thesis, the Templars "were in fact sent to the Holy Land with the express purpose of finding or obtaining something" hidden beneath the Temple — possibly documents pertaining to Israel's royal line, or even Jesus's body or tomb. The source states: "They would seem to have accomplished their mission." The treasure of the Temple is also said to have been plundered by Titus's Roman legions in A.D. 70 and subsequently "found its way eventually to the Pyrenees."

## The Kingdom of Jerusalem as a Merovingian Project

The source argues that the Crusades are best understood not merely as symbolic acts of Christian piety but as a Merovingian political project. With the loss of the kingdom of Jerusalem in 1291 "the Merovingians were once again without a crown." The source asserts that during the period when Frankish kings sat on the throne of Jerusalem, "the Merovingian dynasty could sanction and even encourage hints about its true ancestry" — which would explain why the Grail romances appeared "precisely when and where they did."

## January 17th and Jerusalem

The source notes that one of the decoded messages concealed in Saunière's parchments alluded to "Dagobert II, King, and Sion" — connecting the mystery of Rennes-le-Château to both the Merovingian dynasty and Jerusalem.
