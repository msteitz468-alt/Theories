## Geographical and Historical Context from Source

The source refers to Saint-Sulpice primarily as a seminary in Paris with a recurring role in the mystery surrounding Rennes-le-Château. The authors identify it as an institution connected to the Prieuré de Sion's activities and to the story of Bérenger Saunière.

## Primary Textual Appearances

### Saunière's Visit and Abbé Bieil

When Saunière discovered the four parchments in a hollow Visigoth column at Rennes-le-Château in 1891, his bishop immediately dispatched him to Paris. There, according to the source, he "confided his parchments to the Abbé Bieil" — identified as "Director General of the Seminary of Saint-Sulpice" — and to Émile Hoffet. Through Hoffet, Saunière was introduced to a distinguished esoteric circle in Paris.

### Saint Sulpice and January 17th

The source notes that January 17th — the date on the tombstone of the Marquise d'Hautpoul de Blanchefort (which Saunière later defaced) — is also the feast day of Saint Sulpice. The authors write that "January 17th is also the feast day of Saint Sulpice, who, as we were to discover, figured throughout our story." The convergence of this date with Saunière's fatal stroke on January 17, 1917, is treated as suspicious.

### The Catholic Modernist Movement

The source states that Canon Alfred Leslie Liney "had maintained contacts with the Catholic Modernist Movement, based primarily at Saint Sulpice in Paris," and that "in his youth Liney had worked in Paris, and had been acquainted with Émile Hoffet. The trail had come full circle."

### Abbé Ducaud-Bourget

In its profile of the alleged late-twentieth-century Grand Master of the Prieuré de Sion, the source states that François Ducaud-Bourget "was born in 1897 and trained for the priesthood at — predictably enough — the Seminary of Saint Sulpice." He is thus said to have likely known many of the Modernists there and "quite possibly, Émile Hoffet."

### Index Reference

The source's index lists "Saint Sulpice, Seminary of (Paris), 180, 182, 196," connecting it to the activities of the Compagnie du Saint-Sacrament and to other esoteric networks described in the text.
