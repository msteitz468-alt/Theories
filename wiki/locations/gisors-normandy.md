**Geographical and Historical Context from Source**

A fortress town in Normandy that served as a traditional meeting place for the kings of England and France. The fortress of Gisors was entrusted to the Knights Templar approximately thirty years before the "Cutting of the Elm" incident (i.e., c. 1158). The adjacent land included the "Champ Sacré" (Sacred Field), deemed sacred since pre-Christian times, which in the twelfth century hosted numerous meetings between English and French kings.

The lord of Gisors was Jean de Gisors (born 1133, died 1220), described by the "Prieuré documents" as the first independent Grand Master of the Prieuré de Sion after the 1188 separation from the Templars. Jean was "an extremely powerful and wealthy landowner" who until 1193 was a vassal of the English king; he also possessed property in Sussex and the manor of Titchfield in Hampshire. He reportedly met Thomas à Becket at Gisors in 1169 (the source confirms Becket was there).

Robert Denyau, curé of Gisors, composed an exhaustive history of Gisors and the Gisors family in 1629, explicitly stating that the Rose-Croix was founded by Jean de Gisors in 1188 — independently confirming the Dossiers secrets claims.

**Textual Appearances**

Gisors is the site of the pivotal "Cutting of the Elm" (1188) and thus the symbolic birthplace of the Prieuré de Sion as an independent organization. It is repeatedly referenced in the "Prieuré documents" as a significant locus of Sion's activities.
