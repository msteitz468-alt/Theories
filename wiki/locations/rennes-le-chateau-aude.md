**Geographical and Historical Context from Source**

A tiny French village perched on a steep mountaintop. In Celtic times it was called Rhedae, a sacred site. In Roman times the area was a large thriving community important for its mines and thermal hot springs, regarded as sacred; researchers have found traces of several pagan temples. In the sixth century it was reportedly a town of 30,000 inhabitants, the northern capital of Visigoth territory straddling the Pyrenees. Subsequently it was the seat of the Comte de Razès for five hundred years.

During the Albigensian Crusade (13th century) the village and its region suffered devastating destruction. In the 1360s the local population was decimated by plague; shortly thereafter the village was destroyed by Catalan bandits. By 1885 (when Saunière arrived), only about two hundred people remained.

The surrounding landscape is dense with historical and legendary associations:
- A few miles southeast: Bézu, with ruins of a medieval fortress once a Templar preceptory
- A mile east: ruins of the Château de Blanchefort, ancestral home of Bertrand de Blanchefort, fourth Grand Master of the Knights Templar
- Approximately 6 miles away: the village of Arques, near which stands the tomb depicted in Poussin's "Les Bergers d'Arcadie"
- On the ancient pilgrim route from Northern Europe to Santiago de Compostela

The church at Rennes-le-Château is dedicated to Mary Magdalene, consecrated in 1059, standing on foundations of a Visigoth structure from the sixth century. Saunière redecorated this church bizarrely after discovering the parchments: a statue of the demon Asmodeus at the entrance; Latin inscription TERRIBILIS EST LOCUS ISTE ("This place is terrible") above the door; anomalous Stations of the Cross.

**Textual Appearances**

The entire book begins and radiates outward from Rennes-le-Château. It is the point of origin for the investigation of the Cathars, the Templars, the Merovingians, the Prieuré de Sion, and ultimately the Jesus bloodline hypothesis. The phrase "TERRIBILIS EST LOCUS ISTE" inscribed by Saunière over the church entrance is glossed by the source as the site of an overwhelming secret.
