## Overview from Source

Rosslyn Chapel (originally planned as a large collegiate church) was founded by [[Earl William St. Clair (c. 1410–1484)|Earl William St. Clair]], Earl of Orkney and Baron of Roslin, between 1446 and 1450. Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the chapel is far more than a Christian place of worship; the authors argue it is a deliberately designed "arcane library in stone" encoding the complete [[Rex Deus]] initiatory tradition, every known pre-Christian mystery teaching, and the surviving gnosis of the [[Knights Templar]].

To build it, Earl William brought master masons from every part of Europe, paid them at four times the standard rate (£40 vs. £10 per year), and exercised total design control: every carving was first modelled in wood for his personal approval before being executed in stone. The village of Roslin grew so rapidly to house the craftsmen that it was granted a royal charter by King James II in 1456.

The authors describe the chapel's essential character: "The sculptures in Rosslyn are magnificent manifestations of spiritual insight or vision, given substance in stone" (Trevor Ravenscroft, quoted in source). Douglas Sutherland (1982) called it "a medieval masterpiece of masonry, containing some of the most exquisite carvings ever fashioned in stone."

## Symbolic Programme (per Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)

The carvings encompass:
- **Pre-Christian and universal initiatory traditions**: the head of Hermes Trismegistos (equated with the Egyptian god Thoth); prolific Green Man carvings (spiritual death and rebirth); rosettes and five-pointed stars identical to those in ancient Babylonian temples dedicated to Ishtar and Tammuz.
- **Templar symbolism**: All six diagnostic signs catalogued by French scholar J-A Durbec — five-pointed star, two brothers on one horse, Agnus Dei seal, Mandylion (stylized face of Jesus as on the Shroud), dove with olive branch, floriated cross — appear within the chapel. The authors note this is unusual since Rosslyn was founded over a century after the suppression of the Templars.
- **Kabbalistic and Norse symbolism**: The Apprentice Pillar is interpreted as simultaneously the Tree of Life (Kabbala) and the Yggdrasil (Norse World Ash tree).
- **Masonic symbolism**: Three pillars (Apprentice, Journeyman, Master Mason) corresponding exactly to Masonic lodge symbolism; the phrase "Who will come to the aid of the son of the widow?" carved on the south wall of the clerestory; the maul-marked face of the murdered apprentice facing the Master Mason who killed him; memento mori including la danse macabre.
- **Heraldic connections**: The engrailed cross of the St. Clair family appears in vault carvings; at its centre is the Templar croix patté (cross of universal knowledge). The family's argent-and-sable heraldic colours match those of the Sforza of Milan and the Templar battle standard, the Beauseant.
- **Rex Deus heresy**: The authors contend that the two originally bearded faces (the apprentice and the master) were carved to depict the "heresy of the holy twins" and the "two Jesuses" — that one beard was subsequently chiselled off to suppress this reading.

## Templar Burial Stones

Two Templar burial stones from local graveyards are housed in the chapel: one in the crypt adjoining a 17th-century guild stone; the other in the north aisle, engraved with the name "William de Sinncler" and surmounted by a floriated cross — identified by the authors as commemorating the man who led the Templar charge at Bannockburn in 1314.

## The Three Pillars

The three pillars separating the retro-choir from the main body of the chapel carry direct Masonic symbolism:
- **Apprentice Pillar** (beauty): also the Tree of Life and the Yggdrasil; carries a Staffordshire knot identified by the authors as a Rex Deus symbol.
- **Journeyman's Pillar** (strength).
- **Master Mason's Pillar** (wisdom / sacred gnosis).

The legend of the murdered apprentice parallels both medieval guild initiation lore and the Masonic legend of Hiram Abif; the authors interpret the Hiram Abif story as an allegory for the murder of James the Just.

## Significance to Freemasonry (per source)

The authors argue that the construction of Rosslyn Chapel was Earl William's first move in founding speculative Freemasonry. Assembling master craftsmen from across Europe in a single community gave him the opportunity to merge Templar gnosis with craftmason tradition under St. Clair patronage. The chapel is described as "the unofficial core church of the worldwide craft" among Freemasons today.
