## Geographical and Historical Context from Source

The source distinguishes between "Gaul" as a Roman-era and early-medieval geographical concept and "the Languedoc" as the medieval southern French principality that forms a central setting for the book's narrative.

## The Languedoc as Independent Principality

According to the source, at the beginning of the thirteenth century "the area now known as the Languedoc was not officially a part of France. It was an independent principality, whose language, culture and political institutions had less in common with the north than they had with Spain — with the kingdoms of Leon, Aragon and Castile." It was ruled by the counts of Toulouse and the house of Trencavel.

The source asserts that this principality harboured "a culture which, at the time, was the most advanced and sophisticated in Christendom, with the possible exception of Byzantium." Learning was highly esteemed; philosophy, poetry, and courtly love flourished; and Greek, Arabic, and Hebrew were enthusiastically studied. Schools devoted to the Cabala thrived at Lunel and Narbonne. The Roman Church enjoyed low esteem due to the notorious corruption of its local representatives.

The Languedoc also practised what the source calls "a civilised, easy-going religious tolerance," allowing Islamic and Judaic thought to filter in through maritime centres like Marseilles and across the Pyrenees from Spain.

## The Albigensian Crusade

The source states that in 1209, Pope Innocent III ordered a Crusade against the Cathar heresy, which had taken root throughout the Languedoc. An army of some 30,000 descended "like a whirlwind." The source describes the resulting devastation as potentially "the first case of 'genocide' in modern European history." In the town of Béziers alone, at least 15,000 men, women, and children were slaughtered. Perpignan, Narbonne, Carcassonne, and Toulouse all fell. By 1243 all major Cathar towns had fallen, and by March 1244 the fortress of Montségur had capitulated.

## Gaul in the Merovingian Context

The source uses "Gaul" in its older sense when discussing the Merovingian dynasty. The authors note that Arthur (if historical) was "closely contemporary with Clovis" at "the peak of Merovingian ascendancy in Gaul." The source also references the title of a mysterious book associated with Nicolas Flamel: "The Sacred Book of Abraham the Jew, Prince, Priest, Levite, Astrologer and Philosopher to that Tribe of Jews who by the Wrath of God were Dispersed amongst the Gauls."

## The Languedoc and Jewish Communities

The source repeatedly notes that Languedoc had ancient Jewish communities. Marie de Saint-Clair's mother bore the surname Levis, which "would seem to be of Judaic origin" and "occurs frequently in the Languedoc, where there were Jewish settlements dating from before the Christian epoch." The index confirms: "Jews: in Languedoc, 50."

## The Languedoc and the Templars

The source identifies the Languedoc as a region where the Knights Templar had extensive connections to the Cathar world. Bertrand de Blanchefort, fourth Grand Master of the Temple, came from the Languedoc, and the source notes Templar links with the Cathars in that region. German miners were secretly brought to the vicinity of Rennes-le-Château, within the former Languedoc, to excavate or construct a hiding place under Blanchefort's auspices.
