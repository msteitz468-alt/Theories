## Source Description

*The Invisible College: What a Group of Scientists Has Discovered About UFO Influence on the Human Race* is a 1975 book by astronomer and computer scientist Jacques Vallée, the direct sequel to his [[Passport to Magonia: From Folklore to Flying Saucers (Vallée, 1969)]]. Where *Magonia* argued UFOs are a recurring folkloric phenomenon, *The Invisible College* advances Vallée's central interpretive thesis: that UFOs constitute a **control system** that conditions human belief and consciousness over historical timescales — "not unlike a thermostat" regulating the relationship between human consciousness and physical reality. The title refers to the informal network of ~100 scientists in several countries (a phrase Vallée and J. Allen Hynek borrow from the 17th-century forerunners of the Royal Society) who study the phenomenon seriously.

Vallée rejects both the "nuts-and-bolts" extraterrestrial hypothesis and the skeptics' debunking, arguing the phenomenon is simultaneously physical and psychic, and that its defining feature — a "constant factor of absurdity" / "meta-logic" — is functional: it conditions belief while evading scientific and institutional capture. He treats religious apparitions (Fatima, Lourdes), the rise of contactee/psychic figures (Uri Geller), and disinformation operations (UMMO) as expressions of the same system.

## Chapter Summaries

### Introduction
Vallée poses the book's central hypothesis: UFOs may constitute a "control system" that stabilizes the relationship between human consciousness and physical reality, "not unlike a thermostat in a house," rather than being either extraterrestrial craft or hoaxes and misidentifications. He frames the inquiry within "descriptive phenomenology" (citing Cynthia Nelson), confesses he has himself tracked UFOs with theodolites, and describes the "Invisible College" — ~100 scientists in five or six countries (a name he and Hynek borrow from the forerunners of the Royal Society). He recounts two "silent contactee" cases — a secretive engineer taken aboard a craft during a 1961 archaeological field trip (eighteen days lost, life permanently changed) and Uri Geller — and lays out the four elements (psychic component, triple coverup, patterns of belief, exploiting groups like UMMO) that form his new framework.

### Chapter One: The Psychic Component
Vallée argues that paranormal effects — telepathic "messages," space-time distortions, healing, and the sudden materialization/dematerialization of craft — are integral to UFO encounters, not incidental. He presents the thoroughly documented case of "Dr. X," a southern French physician whose leg wound and old war injuries healed after a November 1968 sighting of two merging disks, and who afterward developed a recurring triangular pigmentation and paranormal phenomena. Other cases (the "Over the Accelerator" red object near the Stanford linear accelerator, the Berkeley woman told telepathically "not to report" what she saw, Edgar Cayce's childhood vision, the Aveyron and Delphos landings with their sleep-disturbance and out-of-body after-effects) illustrate the "psychic component." He offers his five propositions — including that UFOs "are neither objects nor flying," have appeared throughout history under culturally appropriate guises, and exhibit a "constant factor of absurdity."

### Chapter Two: The Triple Coverup
Vallée describes three levels of suppression of the UFO phenomenon: official coverup (citing the Condon/University of Colorado study that destroyed its files and the Air Force closing Project Blue Book, plus foreign censorship like Brazil's Institutional Act No. 5), a second coverup in which scientists dismiss data without examining it ("swamp gas," "scared scientists"), and a third in which the phenomenon "negates itself" — its own absurdity makes witnesses reluctant to report (only one in twelve technically trained witnesses bothered to call the Air Force). He argues that the collective reaction to the reports is itself part of the phenomenon.

### Chapter Three: Memorandum for Archives
Vallée examines automatic-writing "contacts" with alleged space intelligences and treats them as borrowing entirely from the channels' own minds. He recounts the SPECTRA/Hoova messages received by Andrija Puharich and Uri Geller (riddled with pseudoscientific errors, e.g. using "light-years" as a unit of time, exactly as Geller does), and the authenticated 1959 Washington case in which intelligence officers received messages from an entity "AFFA from Uranus" via Commander Curtis's automatic writing — culminating in a disk seen over Washington. Vallée notes that in the Enochian language of Dr. John Dee (whom he profiles), "AFFA" means "empty," so the officers were "in direct communication with… emptiness." He shows that such messages never reveal anything beyond the participants' own knowledge.

### Chapter Four: The Function of OEMII
Vallée analyzes the UMMO affair — a Spanish organization claiming to represent extraterrestrials from the planet "Ummo," communicating by typed letters (received in the mail) and an insignia, which he treats as a deliberate, sophisticated disinformation operation exploiting the belief patterns documented earlier. He places it alongside SPECTRA and the Keech sect's "Guardians" as engineered contact designed to influence human affairs rather than genuine alien communication.

### Chapter Five: The Confrontation
Vallée presents his "Hilltop Theory": he defines seven "categories of strangeness" of encounter and argues that the kind of data an investigator receives is a function of his image among his audience — police and the Air Force get lights and craft-like objects, while amateurs and trusted figures receive the landing, occupant, and psychic cases. He cites the Betty and Barney Hill case as an illustration (the craft was reported officially; the creatures and time-loss only emerged with someone trusted). Drawing on the Brazilian cases of José Antonio (whose abduction matches the phases of a secret-society initiation ritual) and Paulo Gaetano (whose companion saw only a bus following them), and on Milton Erickson's "Confusion Technique," he proposes that the absurdity is functional — a means of inducing belief-change while the rational mind is disoriented.

### Chapter Six: The Winged Disk
Vallée traces the antiquity of the "signs in the sky," examining Phoenician amulets, Sumerian and Babylonian winged-disk iconography, and figures like the "Oxford Scorpion-Man," to argue that the phenomenon — and the god-like beings emerging from disks — has been depicted across cultures and millennia, consistent with his proposition that UFOs have appeared throughout history in culturally appropriate forms.

### Chapter Seven: A Morphology of Miracles
Vallée argues that famous religious apparitions are structurally identical to UFO events. He reconstructs the 1917 Fatima sequence in detail — preceded by 1915–16 visits from an "Angel of Peace," building through escalating crowds (3, 50, 4,500, 18,000, 30,000, 70,000) to the October 13 "miracle of the sun," a spinning silver disk casting colored beams that "plunged downward in zigzag fashion" — and notes the buzzing sounds, heat waves, "angel hair," healings, prophecies, paralysis, and amnesia all parallel UFO reports. He extends the analysis to Lourdes, Guadalupe, and the Mormon visions, proposing a "unified theory of apparitions."

### Chapter Eight: Who Are You, Mr. Geller?
Vallée gives a firsthand appraisal of Uri Geller from Parapsychology Research Group sessions, opening with Swedenborg's warning that spirits "lie." He recounts telepathy tests he personally ran (a "whale/fountain/fish" double-target drawing Geller could not have faked by peeking; an identical hastily-drawn "2") that convinced him some of Geller's abilities were genuine, while stressing that Geller does not control the effects and attributes their source to UFOs. He warns sharply against the "dangers of blind belief" and Geller's encouragement of followers to regard him as a higher emissary, and reproduces readers' accounts linking UFO sightings to telepathy, levitation, and out-of-body experience.

### Chapter Nine: The Control System
Vallée states his thesis fully: UFOs are not space visitations but a control system regulating human belief, operating like a thermostat by oscillating between opposite principles (rational/absurd, friendly/hostile) so that "no matter what approach I take, I can never explain more than half of the facts." Drawing on B. F. Skinner's work on schedules of reinforcement (periodicity combined with unpredictability produces slow, continuous, irreversible learning), he proposes UFO waves function as such a schedule, conditioning humanity toward "a higher image of man." He insists he does not mean a literal jailer or a redefinition of God — only that mythology rules at a social level over which political and intellectual action has no power — and argues UFO belief is becoming "the next form of religion."

### Conclusion
Vallée closes with Gordon Creighton's definition that "a myth is that which is TRUER THAN TRUTH": it may not be true that flying saucers are visits from space, but if enough people believe it, "certain things [will] change irreversibly." He reports that he once assumed a quiet, competent official investigation was underway, but having seen Blue Book's blunders and missing files, he no longer believes it — "if I still believed it today I would not be publishing this book." The answer, he concludes, lies "not… in some secret file in Washington," but "within ourselves."

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Vallée is explicit about his own uncertainty: he "has not determined whether [the control system] is natural or spontaneous… or if it is artificial in nature, and under the power of some superhuman will." He stresses he does *not* mean a literal jailer or a redefinition of God — only that mythology operates at a social level "over which normal political and intellectual action has no real power." He rejects the benign-space-intelligence reading of Puharich and Geller while affirming their experiences are real to the witnesses, and frames the whole inquiry as ending "within ourselves," not in science or a secret government file.
