## Source Description

*The Perennial Philosophy* (1945) by **[[Aldous Huxley]]** is an anthology-with-commentary arguing that the world's religious and mystical traditions share one underlying truth — the **perennial philosophy** (*philosophia perennis*). Huxley assembles passages from Hindu, Buddhist, Taoist, Christian, and Sufi mystics under thematic headings and shows that they converge on the same metaphysics and psychology: there is a **divine Ground** of all being; it can be **directly and unitively known** through a faculty deeper than the rational intellect; the human soul has an eternal spark identical with that Ground (**"Tat tvam asi" — That art thou**); and the purpose of life is the unitive knowledge of the Ground, attained only through **self-naughting**, humility, charity, and the death of the ego.

The book is the contemplative/mystical counterpart to Huxley's psychedelic writings and to the wiki's consciousness cluster; see [[The perennial philosophy]].

## Structure

Twenty-seven thematic chapters (e.g. "That Art Thou," "The Nature of the Ground," "Charity," "Self-Knowledge," "Grace and Free Will," "Time and Eternity," "Silence," "Contemplation, Action and Social Utility"), each weaving quoted scripture and mystics with Huxley's commentary.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Huxley presents perennial unanimity as an empirical fact drawn from the texts; critics note he selects and harmonizes passages across very different traditions, smoothing real doctrinal differences. He himself stresses the truth is verifiable only by those who fulfill the moral conditions (the "self-naughting") he describes.
