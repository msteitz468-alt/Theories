## Source Description

*Wall Street and the Bolshevik Revolution* is a documentary study by Antony C. Sutton (1925–2002), a British-American economist and former Research Fellow at the Hoover Institution on War, Revolution and Peace (Stanford) and Professor of Economics at California State University, Los Angeles. Drawing heavily on U.S. State Department Decimal File documents, British intelligence papers, and corporate records, Sutton argues that a syndicate of Wall Street financiers — centered on the Morgan-controlled Guaranty Trust Company and the address 120 Broadway, New York — financed and aided the Bolshevik Revolution, while also backing the anti-Bolshevik side.

The book is the first volume of Sutton's trilogy (followed by *Wall Street and FDR* and *Wall Street and the Rise of Hitler*). It is notably more documentary and restrained than the Mullins/Griffin works, and **explicitly rejects** the antisemitic "Jewish-conspiracy theory" of the revolution (Appendix II).

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Sutton's account directly contradicts Mullins and Griffin on a key point: where they claim [[Jacob Schiff]] (Kuhn, Loeb) financed Trotsky and the Bolsheviks, Sutton cites State Department documents that Schiff "was in fact against support of the Bolshevik regime," and attributes the promotion of the Bolsheviks to the Morgan-Rockefeller group instead. See [[Controversy: Did Jacob Schiff finance the Bolshevik Revolution?]]. Sutton also frames the motive as profit/monopoly ("an alliance of statists"), not ethnicity or ideology.
