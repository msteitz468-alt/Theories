## Source Description

*Wall Street and the Bolshevik Revolution* is a documentary study by Antony C. Sutton (1925–2002),
a British-American economist and former Research Fellow at the Hoover Institution on War,
Revolution and Peace (Stanford) and Professor of Economics at California State University, Los
Angeles. Drawing heavily on U.S. State Department Decimal File documents, British intelligence
papers, and corporate records, Sutton argues that a syndicate of Wall Street financiers — centered
on the Morgan-controlled Guaranty Trust Company and the address **120 Broadway**, New York —
financed and aided the Bolshevik Revolution, while also backing the anti-Bolshevik side.

The book is the first volume of Sutton's trilogy (followed by *Wall Street and FDR* and
*[[Wall Street and the Rise of Hitler (Sutton, 1976)]]*). It is notably more documentary and
restrained than the Mullins/Griffin works, and **explicitly rejects** the antisemitic
"Jewish-conspiracy theory" of the revolution (Appendix II). Sutton's stated thesis is not ethnic or
ideological but an "alliance of statists": financiers pursuing monopoly profit who were happy to
fund a centralized regime — and to fund *both sides* of the conflict.

## Strict-extraction note

Sutton's argument is *documentary* (he reproduces and footnotes archival sources) and he repeatedly
flags the limits of his own evidence ("suggests, though without at this point providing evidence").
The wiki records his attributed findings and his explicit cautions, and treats this book as the
catalog's chief *counter-weight* to the monolithic-cabal reading — see
[[Is history steered by a deliberate cabal or an emergent network? Models compared]].

## Structure and Chapter Summaries

**I. The Actors on the Revolutionary Stage** — Frames the cast: Wall Street financiers, the
financial district around 120 Broadway, and the revolutionaries, arguing the conventional
left/right and capitalist/communist categories obscure the actual alliances.

**II. Trotsky Leaves New York** — Documents Trotsky's New York period, his unexplained $10,000, and
Woodrow Wilson's provision of a U.S. passport that let him return to Russia "to carry forward the
revolution," followed by his brief detention at Halifax. See
[[President Wilson gave Trotsky a U.S. passport to return to Russia; Trotsky was briefly detained at Halifax (Sutton)]].

**III. Lenin and German Assistance** — Examines the case that Germany aided Lenin's return,
weighing the contested **Sisson Documents** and the "tug-of-war in Washington" over how to read
them — Sutton treats the forgery question cautiously rather than asserting the documents wholesale.

**IV. Wall Street and the World Revolution** — The financial core: American bankers and Tsarist
loans; **Olof Aschberg** ("the Bolshevik banker") and his Nya Banken; the joining of Nya Banken and
Guaranty Trust in Ruskombank; and Guaranty Trust's alleged links to German espionage in the U.S.,
1914–1917.

**V. The American Red Cross Mission in Russia (1917)** — Argues the mission "had only a nominal
relationship with the American Red Cross": privately funded by William Boyce Thompson, staffed
mostly by New York lawyers/financiers (only 5 of 24 were doctors — the "Haytian Army"), and that
Thompson gave the Bolsheviks $1 million. See
[[The 1917 American Red Cross Mission to Russia was a Wall Street business mission, not a medical one (Sutton)]]
and [[Federal Reserve Bank of New York director William Boyce Thompson gave $1 million to the Bolsheviks]].

**VI. Consolidation and Export of the Revolution** — Thompson's consultation with Lloyd George; the
"unofficial ambassadors" (Robins, Lockhart, Sadoul); and figures (Jacob Rubin, Robert Minor) tied
to exporting revolutionary activity.

**VII. The Bolsheviks Return to New York** — The 1919 raid on the Soviet Bureau in New York, its
corporate allies, and European banker aid to the Bolsheviks.

**VIII. 120 Broadway, New York City** — The geographic hub thesis: the American International
Corporation, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, and the American–Russian Industrial Syndicate
were clustered at or near 120 Broadway, alongside "establishment revolutionary" John Reed and the
Metropolitan Magazine. See [[120 Broadway was the hub of pro-Bolshevik Wall Street activity]].

**IX. Guaranty Trust Goes to Russia** — The commercial payoff: Wall Street positioning for Russian
business, Soviet gold and American banks, and **Max May** of Guaranty Trust becoming a director of
the Soviet Ruskombank. See [[Wall Street backed the Bolsheviks to capture the Russian market]].

**X. J.P. Morgan Gives a Little Help to the Other Side** — Documents that the same Morgan interests
funded *anti*-Bolshevik bodies ("United Americans" and its "startling disclosures on Reds"),
grounding the both-sides finding. See [[Wall Street financed both the Bolsheviks and their White opponents]].

**XI. The Alliance of Bankers and Revolution** — Synthesis: the financiers' motive was monopoly
profit and a managed, centralized state ("an alliance of statists"), not ideology — the basis on
which Sutton rejects the ethnic-conspiracy reading.

**Appendices** — I: directors of the major banks/firms; **II: The Jewish-Conspiracy Theory of the
Bolshevik Revolution** (explicit rejection — see [[Sutton rejects the Jewish-conspiracy theory of the Bolshevik Revolution]]);
III: selected government documents.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Sutton's account directly contradicts Mullins and Griffin on a key point: where they claim
[[Jacob Schiff]] (Kuhn, Loeb) financed Trotsky and the Bolsheviks, Sutton cites State Department
documents that Schiff "was in fact against support of the Bolshevik regime," and attributes the
promotion of the Bolsheviks to the Morgan-Rockefeller group instead. See
[[Controversy: Did Jacob Schiff finance the Bolshevik Revolution?]]. Sutton also frames the motive
as profit/monopoly ("an alliance of statists"), not ethnicity or ideology — the documentary basis on
which the wiki treats him as the counter-weight to the single-cabal reading.
