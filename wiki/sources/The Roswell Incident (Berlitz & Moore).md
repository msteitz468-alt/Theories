## Source Description

*The Roswell Incident* (1980) by Charles Berlitz and William L. Moore is the book that created the modern Roswell mythos. Drawing on interviews (many gathered with nuclear physicist Stanton T. Friedman) with the participants and townspeople, it argues that in early July 1947 an extraterrestrial craft crashed near Roswell, New Mexico; that the U.S. Army Air Force recovered both **unearthly debris** and the **bodies of dead alien crew**; and that the famous "weather balloon" announcement was a **cover-up** substituted within hours. It introduced to the public the figures and elements that became canonical Roswell lore — intelligence officer **Major Jesse Marcel**, rancher Mac Brazel, the Haut press release ("RAAF Captures Flying Saucer"), the substituted balloon debris, and General Ramey's reversal.

The authors situate the crash amid the dense atomic/rocket research of 1947 New Mexico (Los Alamos, White Sands, the 509th Bomb Group) and the wider flying-saucer flap that began with Kenneth Arnold (June 1947). In later chapters they weigh and reject alternative explanations — a secret AVRO disc and a "Russian connection."

## Chapter Summaries

### Introduction

The authors frame the Roswell crash of early July 1947 as a uniquely vital UFO case still "going on" — pointing to the pending Citizens Against UFO Secrecy (CAUS) Freedom-of-Information lawsuit against the CIA over crashed-disc files. They assert that material from the wrecked craft and its dead occupants (one reportedly alive when found) was shuttled under high security and is held at Langley, and that the recovered craft, hieroglyphic markings, and humanoid crew are still under study, presenting the case as possible proof of extraterrestrial contact rather than a mass delusion.

### Chapter 1: UFOs in the Sky and in Space

A historical and cosmic survey of UFO sightings — from Pharaoh's fiery circles, Ezekiel's "space capsule," and pre-1947 reports in weather and astronomy journals to Kenneth Arnold's June 1947 Mount Rainier sighting and alleged astronaut encounters in space (via NASA's Maurice Chatelain). The authors argue UFO frequency tracks human technological development and build toward a "science-fiction scenario" — a crashed disc with dead humanoids and unearthly writing — which, they say, was "rather convincingly carried out in New Mexico" in 1947.

### Chapter 2: Incident at Roswell

The opening scene: on July 7, 1947, KOAT teletype operator Lydia Sleppy began transmitting Roswell reporter Johnny McBoyle's scoop — "A flying saucer has crashed... they're saying something about little men being on board" — when the teletype was interrupted by an order: DO NOT TRANSMIT. The chapter sets the dense 1947 New Mexico atomic/rocket context (the 509th Bomb Group, White Sands, Los Alamos) and catalogues the regional flying-disc wave that preceded the crash.

### Chapter 3: The AAF Confronts a Crashed UFO and Dead Extraterrestrials

The canonical narrative: the Dan Wilmot sighting (July 2), rancher Mac Brazel's debris field, and the July 8 press release in which the 509th's intelligence officer Major Jesse Marcel announced the recovery of a "flying disc" ("RAAF Captures Flying Saucer") — only for Brigadier General Roger Ramey in Fort Worth to substitute a weather balloon within hours, displaying balloon debris for photographers. The authors stress Marcel's and others' insistence that the recovered material was unearthly, and argue the balloon story was a hastily improvised cover.

### Chapter 4: Witnesses Speak — the Town Remembers

Townspeople's recollections gathered decades later, centered on civil engineer Barney Barnett, who told friends (the Maltaises) that on July 3, 1947, he reached a crashed disc near Socorro and saw small, non-human dead bodies before the military sealed the area and removed the wreckage and crew. The authors vouch for Barnett's sober character to support the recovery account.

### Chapter 6: Holes in the Cover-up

A survey of leaks and rumors that, over the decades, repeatedly corroborated the recovery — retired officers, transport drivers, and autopsy-adjacent informants describing small bodies in close-fitting garments and human-like internal organs. The authors argue these lower-echelon disclosures, though sometimes contradictory, broadly agree and poke "holes" in the official denial.

### Chapter 7: The President and the Captured Saucer

The authors recount, via anonymous CIA-linked sources, that President Eisenhower made inquiries into the "Roswell saucer capture" and was startled to find that even he, a former Army general, lacked the clearances to access the information. They describe a split within the military over declassification, with secrecy justified by the national-security value of reverse-engineering the craft's propulsion.

### Chapter 8: "Top Secret" Forever — the AVRO Alternative

The authors explain why a genuine recovery would be kept "Top Secret" indefinitely — to deny rival powers the secret of fuel-less, high-velocity flight — and weigh a leading mundane alternative, the Canadian-American AVRO disc (Avrocar) program, to which the Air Force often deflected inquiries. They conclude the primitive AVRO craft cannot account for the 1947 Roswell object.

### Chapter 9: The Russian Connection

Surveying anomalous aerial crashes (the 1908 Tunguska event, the 1961 Lake Onega incident in the U.S.S.R. reported by Felix Ziegel), the authors consider and reject the possibility that the Roswell object was a secret Soviet craft, reaffirming the extraterrestrial interpretation. The chapter closes the book's weighing of rival explanations.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

The book is built largely on decades-later witness recollections (the authors themselves note witnesses who "couldn't find anyone who would talk about it," and others suspected of "latching on" for publicity). They explicitly consider and dismiss mundane and rival explanations (V-2 tests, the AVRO disc, a Russian craft). Notably, this extraterrestrial reading is directly contradicted within the wiki by Annie Jacobsen's claim that Roswell was a Soviet/Mengele craft ([[Roswell disc child bodies Stalin Mengele hoax per EG&G engineer Jacobsen source]]), and the official explanation later shifted to Project Mogul (contested in [[Dolan rejects the Air Force's Mogul-balloon and crash-dummy explanations of Roswell]]).
