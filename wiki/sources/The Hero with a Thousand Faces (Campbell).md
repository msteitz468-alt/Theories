## Source Description

*The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949) by comparative mythologist **[[Joseph Campbell]]** advances the **monomyth**: the claim that the hero myths of all cultures are local expressions of a single universal pattern, the **hero's journey** of **Departure, Initiation, and Return**. Drawing on a vast comparative survey of world mythology and folklore — and on the depth psychology of Jung and Freud — Campbell shows the hero leaving the ordinary world, crossing a threshold into a realm of supernatural ordeal (the road of trials, the meeting with the goddess, atonement with the father, apotheosis), seizing the **ultimate boon**, and returning to renew the community.

Campbell reads the figures and ordeals as **symbolic projections of the psyche**, and frames myth as a living function that guides psychological and spiritual transformation and reconciles the individual with the cosmos (the cosmogonic cycle). It is the wiki's anchor for comparative mythology and connects to shamanic initiation (Eliade) and the perennial/consciousness material. See [[The monomyth (the hero's journey)]].

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Campbell's universalism is its own controversy: critics argue he abstracts a pattern by downplaying the cultural specificity and divergence of myths. He frames the monomyth as a heuristic key to a deep psychological unity rather than a claim that all myths are identical.
