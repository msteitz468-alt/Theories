## Source Description

*Above Top Secret: The Worldwide UFO Cover-up* (1987) by British researcher Timothy Good is the landmark international, document-based study of official UFO secrecy. Built on records released under the U.S. Freedom of Information Act, official correspondence, and parliamentary debate, Good argues that governments around the world have secretly treated UFOs as a serious matter while publicly denying and ridiculing them. The book is organized by country — Part One (Great Britain), Part Two (France, Italy, Australia, Canada, China, the USSR, and others), and Part Three (the USA, with chapters on the DIA, CIA and NASA) — to demonstrate that the cover-up is genuinely worldwide. It was the first work to widely publish the **Majestic 12 (MJ-12)** documents.

Like Hynek, Good is measured: he accepts that 70–90% of sightings are mundane and stresses that "a bona fide UFO… does not necessarily imply an extraterrestrial spacecraft." His questions are narrower and evidential: are there unexplainable reports beyond present knowledge, and are governments concealing what they have learned? *Above Top Secret* became the foundational documentary source on which later cover-up historians (notably Richard Dolan) built.

Introduction · Prologue (ghost aircraft 1933–34; the 1942 Los Angeles air raid; WWII foo-fighters; the 1946 ghost rockets) · Part One: Great Britain (5 chapters) · Part Two: Around the World · Part Three: USA (incl. the DIA, CIA, NASA, and "Majestic 12") · extensive documentary notes and appendices.

## Chapter Summaries

### Introduction
Good poses the book's organizing question: are governments withholding evidence that UFOs exist as a serious reality? He sets two official denials against his own evidence — Chapman Pincher's 1981 assurance that UFOs are "entirely mythical" and that no secret service wastes resources on them, and Lord Strabolgi's 1979 House of Lords statement that "there is no cover-up and no security ban." Good concedes that 70–90% of sightings are explicable and that a bona fide UFO "does not necessarily imply an extraterrestrial spacecraft," but insists declassified FOIA documents prove intensive secret government research, reframing the question as whether unexplainable reports exist and whether governments conceal what they have learned.

### Prologue
A pre-1947 survey establishing official concern and concealment as long-standing. Good covers the 1933–34 Scandinavian "ghost aircraft" (Major-General Reuterswaerd's frank admission of unexplained intrusions), the 25 February 1942 Los Angeles air raid (1,430 anti-aircraft rounds fired, no planes downed; the suppressed Marshall memorandum to Roosevelt), the 1943–44 "foo-fighters" and the alleged British "Massey Project," and the 1946 Scandinavian "ghost rockets" (the Stockholm embassy telegrams, the press censorship imposed in Norway and Sweden, and Professor Santorini's account of a "world blanket of secrecy" after foreign officials halted the Greek investigation).

### Chapter 2: Room 801 — Top Secret (1955–59)
In April 1955 the Air Ministry announced that a five-year RAF investigation had been submitted to senior officers but would never be made public "for security reasons" — a striking move given the Ministry's claim that UFOs posed no security threat. Good reports that Air Minister George Ward later admitted to a friend that he was "obliged to cover up the facts," and covers the 1955 Kilgallen story (British scientists reportedly convinced a recovered craft was from another planet).

### Chapter 17: Above Top Secret
The book's culminating argument, centered on the National Security Agency. ## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Good distinguishes evidence from inference: he reproduces official denials in full (Chapman Pincher's "entirely mythical"; Lord Strabolgi's 1979 "there is no cover-up") before contesting them with documents; concedes most sightings are explicable; and, on MJ-12, writes only that "the group did indeed exist, and the document seems authentic enough" while noting his questions to a former CIA director and two ex-presidents went unanswered — a deliberately qualified verdict on documents whose authenticity has remained disputed.
