## Source Description

*UFOs: Generals, Pilots, and Government Officials Go on the Record* (2010) by investigative journalist Leslie Kean is a sober, evidence-first anthology in which high-credibility first-hand witnesses — generals, fighter and commercial pilots, air-traffic controllers, and senior officials from many countries — write their own accounts of UFO/UAP encounters and investigations. Kean's thesis is deliberately narrow and agnostic: a residue of well-documented cases involves real, physical, technologically unexplained objects, and the U.S. government has abdicated serious study of them. She does not assert an extraterrestrial origin; she argues for rigorous official investigation. The book carries a foreword by John Podesta calling for declassification, and culminates in a "Call to Action."

Kean's flagship case is the 1989–90 **Belgian UFO wave** (recounted by Major General Wilfried De Brouwer), with later chapters on JAL Flight 1628 (Alaska, 1986), the 1976 Tehran incident, the Trans-en-Provence physical-trace case, the Phoenix Lights (Governor Fife Symington), the 2006 O'Hare sighting, and the French **COMETA Report** (1999). The book became a key bridge from the classic era (Hynek, Keyhoe, Good, Dolan — all in her bibliography) to the modern disclosure era (Elizondo, Coulthart).

## Structure

Foreword (Podesta) + Introduction + Part 1: Objects of Unknown Origin (chs. 1–10) · Part 2: In the Line of Duty (chs. 11–20) · Part 3: A Call to Action (chs. 21–28), with first-person chapters by the witnesses themselves.

## Chapter Summaries

### Foreword (John Podesta) & Introduction (Leslie Kean)
Podesta, a longtime advocate for openness in government, endorses Kean's evidence-first approach and her call for "a small U.S. government agency" to study UFOs, insisting "the American people… can handle the truth." Kean recounts how the 1999 **COMETA Report** — written by thirteen retired French generals, admirals, scientists and a former head of CNES — concluded that about 5% of UFO sightings are "completely unknown flying machines with exceptional performances" and that the "extraterrestrial hypothesis" was the most logical explanation, though unproven. She defines a UFO by the Air Force's own definition, stresses that 90–95% of sightings are explainable, and sets out five premises: a physical phenomenon under apparent intelligent control exists; it raises aviation-safety and national-security concerns; the US government ignores it and issues false explanations; the extraterrestrial/interdimensional hypothesis is rational but unproven; and systematic scientific investigation with US support and international cooperation is warranted.

### Chapters 1–2: Majestic Craft with Powerful Beaming Spotlights / The UAP Wave over Belgium (Major General Wilfried De Brouwer)
Kean introduces the 1989–90 **Belgian UFO wave** as the book's flagship case, then De Brouwer (then Colonel and Chief of Operations of the Belgian Air Staff) gives a first-person account. On 29 November 1989, 143 sightings were reported around Eupen; federal policemen Nicoll and Von Montigny watched a triangular craft with three spotlights and a central red light hover, emit and retract "red light balls," and maneuver silently. Of roughly 2,000 reported cases over the wave, 650 were investigated and over 500 remain unexplained; the Petit-Rechain photograph (April 1990) was analyzed by NASA's Richard Haines and others and judged unfaked. De Brouwer scrambled F-16s on three occasions (recording extraordinary radar accelerations on 30–31 March 1990, though inconclusive), and a FOIA-released US memo confirmed no USAF stealth aircraft were operating there. He concludes the craft were of unknown origin and that "research should not exclude the extraterrestrial option."

### Chapters 3–5: Pilots: A Unique Window into the Unknown / Circled by a UFO (Capt. Júlio Guerra) / Unidentified Aerial Phenomena and Aviation Safety (Richard Haines)
Kean argues pilots are the best-trained observers of UFOs and that some encounters compromise aviation safety. She cites former NASA senior scientist **Richard Haines**, whose study documented over 100 pilot cases including 56 near-misses, electromagnetic effects on aircraft systems, and a database of over 3,400 firsthand pilot sightings; Haines co-founded NARCAP. Portuguese Air Force pilot Júlio Guerra describes a November 1982 encounter in which an oval metallic disc shot up to his altitude in seconds and repeatedly circled his Chipmunk, witnessed by two other Air Force pilots. United Airlines captain Neil Daniels recounts a 1977 incident in which a brilliant light pulled his DC-10 off course via magnetic interference, after which he was warned "bad things can happen to pilots who say they have these sightings."

### Chapters 6–10: O'Hare Airport / Gigantic UFOs over the English Channel (Capt. Ray Bowyer) / UFOs as Air Force Targets / Dogfight over Tehran (Gen. Parviz Jafari) / Close Combat with a UFO (Cmdte. Oscar Santa María Huertas)
A sequence of first-person military and pilot encounters. The 2006 O'Hare Airport incident (a disc hovering over a terminal, dismissed by the FAA as a "hole-punch cloud" despite above-freezing temperatures). Captain Ray Bowyer's 2007 sighting of two enormous brilliant objects over the English Channel, reported to the UK CAA. Iranian General Parviz Jafari's 1976 Tehran "dogfight," in which his F-4's instruments and weapons failed near a brilliant object. Peruvian commander Oscar Santa María Huertas's 1980 attempt to shoot down a UFO with cannon fire. Each witness emphasizes the objects' physicality and performance beyond known technology while avoiding claims about their origin.

### Chapters 11–12: The Roots of UFO Debunking in America / Taking the Phenomenon Seriously
Kean traces the official US debunking posture to the CIA-convened Robertson Panel of the 1950s and the public-relations character of Project Blue Book, contrasting it with the more serious handling of UFOs by other governments. She argues that decades of official ridicule created the "UFO taboo" that suppresses credible reporting, and that other nations' agencies have treated the phenomenon — and the extraterrestrial hypothesis — with appropriate rigor "as long as no other credible interpretation has been formulated."

### Chapters 13–14: The Birth of COMETA (Major General Denis Letty) / France and the UFO Question (Jean-Jacques Velasco)
French Major General Denis Letty recounts assembling the independent COMETA committee of high-ranking officers and experts who, after three years of analysis, produced the 1999 white paper "UFOs and Defense: What Should We Prepare For?" Jean-Jacques Velasco, who headed France's official UFO agency (GEPAN/SEPRA within CNES) for over twenty years, describes France's research-based government approach and concludes the extraterrestrial hypothesis deserves serious consideration, citing physical-trace cases such as Trans-en-Provence.

### Chapters 15–16: UFOs and the National Security Problem / "A Powerful Desire to Do Nothing"
Kean argues UFO incursions into restricted airspace pose national-security questions the US government ignores. She presents the 1982–86 **Hudson Valley wave** in upstate New York and Connecticut — large silent craft, "red light ball" probes, sightings over the Indian Point nuclear plant — as a close parallel to the Belgian wave that, unlike Belgium's, was met with total US government inaction. She draws on the late **Dr. J. Allen Hynek**, who investigated the wave and described the "UFO taboo" as a psychological mechanism that "blows the fuses" of the mind, leaving officials with "a powerful desire to do nothing."

### Chapter 21: Fighting Back: A New UFO Agency in America
Kean lays out the book's central recommendation: a small US government agency to investigate UFOs, liaise with other countries, and demonstrate the subject's scientific legitimacy. She reviews foreign models (France's GEPAN within CNES, Chile's CEFAA, Peru's OIFFA, the UK MoD office), recounts how even President Carter (himself a 1969 UFO witness) could not get NASA to study the evidence in 1977, and how a 1978 UN effort led by Grenada and J. Allen Hynek was undercut by the US delegation. She concludes that further scientific investigation is mandated, must be international, and cannot succeed without US participation.

### Chapters 22–23: The FAA Investigates a UFO Event "That Never Happened" (John Callahan) / Government Cover-up: Policy or Myth?
Former FAA division chief **John Callahan** describes the 1986 JAL Flight 1628 case over Alaska — thirty minutes of radar-corroborated UFO activity around a 747 — and a subsequent FAA/CIA briefing at which he was told "this event never happened." Kean then weighs the cover-up question agnostically: documents prove the phenomenon concerned the Air Force, CIA and FBI from the late 1940s, and several independent credible sources have told her of a deeply hidden UFO research program inaccessible even to high-level officials; but she argues that whether or not such a program exists is ultimately a "side issue" to establishing an open investigation.

### Chapters 24–25: Governor Fife Symington and Movement Toward Change / Setting the Record Straight (Fife Symington III)
Kean recounts the 13 March 1997 **Phoenix Lights** — a massive silent craft seen by hundreds or thousands across Arizona — and Governor Symington's mocking "spoof" press conference (his chief of staff in an alien costume) that overshadowed his real inquiries. In his own chapter, former Arizona Governor and Air Force pilot Fife Symington admits that he too personally witnessed "a massive, delta-shaped craft" that did not resemble any man-made object, that the National Guard's later flare explanation "defies common sense, for flares do not fly in formation," and he joins the call for the US to reopen its official investigation.

### Chapters 26–28: Engaging the U.S. Government / Militant Agnosticism and the UFO Taboo (Wendt & Duvall) / Facing an Extreme Challenge
Kean describes the Coalition for Freedom of Information she co-founded in 2002 (supported by John Podesta), whose FOIA suit forced NASA to release withheld documents. Political scientists Alexander Wendt and Raymond Duvall theorize the "UFO taboo" as a structural refusal by the modern state to even acknowledge a physically documented but unexplained phenomenon, advocating "militant agnosticism." Kean closes with a renewed appeal for an open, internationally cooperative, US-supported scientific investigation as the only way forward.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Kean is rigorously cautious: she accepts that the great majority of reports are explainable, keeps the "phenomenon" distinct from the extraterrestrial hypothesis, and lets her witnesses voice their own restraint — De Brouwer: "I stick to the facts and avoid extrapolations to possible extraterrestrial activities… Nevertheless… research should not exclude the extraterrestrial option." Her case for the Belgian wave's reality rests partly on FOIA-released U.S. documents confirming no American stealth aircraft were operating there.
