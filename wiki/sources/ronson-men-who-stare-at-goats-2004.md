## Source Description

*The Men Who Stare at Goats* (2004) is Jon Ronson's wry investigative account of the U.S. military's decades-long flirtation with psychic and New Age techniques — and how those ideas, sincerely held by people "filled with the kindest of intentions," resurfaced darkly in the War on Terror. Ronson traces a lineage from Major General Albert Stubblebine III (the army intelligence chief who tried to walk through walls), through the **First Earth Battalion** of Lt. Col. Jim Channon, the psychic-spy / remote-viewing programs, and the legendary attempts to kill goats by staring at them at Fort Bragg's "Goat Lab," to the use of music (Barney the Purple Dinosaur, Metallica) as an instrument of torture in Iraq and Guantánamo. He explicitly connects this thread back to the CIA's MKULTRA program and the death of [[Frank Olson]].

Ronson writes as a skeptical but sympathetic reporter with first-hand access to the principals; he repeatedly notes the difficulty of separating fact from self-promotion (e.g. Uri Geller's psychic-spy claims) and frames the book around the surreal collision of "dark intelligence secrets and familiar pop culture."

## Chapter Summaries

### 1. The General
In the summer of 1983, Major General Albert Stubblebine III — the U.S. Army's chief of intelligence, commanding 16,000 soldiers from his Arlington office — repeatedly tries and fails to walk through his office wall, reasoning that walls and bodies are mostly empty space. Convinced that psychic powers belong in the intelligence arsenal, he flies to Fort Bragg and pitches Special Forces commanders on psychic healing, spoon-bending, and bursting the hearts of animals just by staring; they feign indifference, and he takes early retirement in 1984. Unknown to him, Special Forces secretly thought his ideas excellent and were already hiding a hundred de-bleated goats in a shed nearby.

### 2. Goat Lab
Uri Geller — claiming he has been "reactivated" by a man called "Ron" — sets Ronson on the trail of a secret Army psychic-spy unit at Fort Meade, Maryland, where a half-dozen Black-Op soldiers tried to be psychic from 1978 until the CIA closed the unit in 1995. Retired Special Forces "Jedi Warrior" Glenn Wheaton describes Project Jedi's super-soldier levels (instant observation, intuition, invisibility) culminating in a master sergeant who stopped a goat's heart at Fort Bragg's "Goat Lab," injuring himself in the process. The man credited (Michael Echanis, then later Guy Savelli) leads back to one origin figure: Lt. Col. Jim Channon.

### 3. The First Earth Battalion
Vietnam veteran Jim Channon, traumatized by combat (including the death of a soldier whose comrades all instinctively fired high), is funded by the Pentagon in 1977-79 to tour 150 California New Age and Human Potential organizations — Esalen, biofeedback labs, ambient composers like Steven Halpern — seeking ways to make the Army "more cunning." He returns to write the 125-page *First Earth Battalion Operations Manual*, envisioning "Warrior Monks" who carry symbolic lambs, broadcast "words of peace," and aspire to walk through walls and stop their own hearts. He is appointed commander of the (largely conceptual) First Earth Battalion, and soldiers across the Army begin implementing his ideas, including Colonel John Alexander's "Sticky Foam."

### 4. Into the Heart of the Goat
Ronson visits martial-arts instructor Guy Savelli at his Cleveland dance studio. Savelli admits he "dropped a goat" at Goat Lab and claims to have recently killed his own hamster by staring; he shows Ronson a home video of a hamster behaving strangely under his gaze (though it revives), explaining that his wife told him not to show the footage of it actually dying. He produces a hand-drawn diagram of Goat Lab.

### 5. Homeland Security
The chapter traces how Channon's manual reached the top of Army Intelligence via Colonel Alexander and General Stubblebine, who took command of the new INSCOM in 1981 amid post-Vietnam budget cuts. Stubblebine's "supernatural war" with Panama's General Manuel Noriega features both men using sorcery and psychic spies; his spoon-bending alienates his devout Christian superior General Wickham (who believed Satan had bent the fork), hastening his retirement. The psychic-spy team (Lyn Buchanan, Joe McMoneagle, Ed Dames) is depicted in their condemned, coffee-budget-less Fort Meade building. A later thread links Channon's "First Earth Battalion" ethos, via martial artist Bert Rodriguez, to 9/11 hijackers Ziad Jarrah and Marwan al-Shehhi.

### 6. Privatization
After the unit's 1995 declassification, Major Ed Dames goes public on Art Bell's radio show with apocalyptic "remote-viewing" predictions (bovine AIDS, killer winds, pole shifts). His civilian students — including Prudence Calabrese and Dr. Courtney Brown of the Farsight Institute — psychically "confirm" a "companion object" trailing comet Hale-Bopp in 1996-97, fueling the Heaven's Gate cult's mass suicide of 39 people. The chapter shows how military psychic credibility leaked into the civilian world with lethal consequences.

### 7. The Purple Dinosaur
In May 2003, behind a disused railway station in al-Qa'im, Iraq, PsyOps soldiers blast captured Iraqis inside a shipping container with Metallica, "Burn Motherfucker Burn," and the "I Love You" song from Barney the Purple Dinosaur, alongside a flashing strobe light. Newsweek's Adam Piore reports it as a humorous item. Ronson interviews Channon, who insists this music use is a "legacy" of the First Earth Battalion, and Sesame Street composer Christopher Cerf, who jokes about royalties. A tour of Fort Bragg PsyOps headquarters reveals their sound-effects and music CD collection.

### 8. The Predator
Camp Pendleton martial-arts master Pete Brusso, a First Earth Battalion devotee, demonstrates "thought projection" by throwing Ronson across a room and torments him with "the Predator," a small plastic pain-compliance tool he designed for use in Iraq. ### 10. A Think Tank
Ronson learns that General Pete Schoomaker, the new Army Chief of Staff (and a former Fort Bragg Special Forces commander during the Jedi/goat-staring years), is considering bringing Channon back to seed a new think tank, echoing the early-1980s "Task Force Delta" and Colonel Frank Burns's "Meta Network." Channon confirms by email that the Army has requested his services to teach selected Majors and that he has submitted Iraq "exit strategy" plans. A British ex-detainee, Jamal al-Harith, recounts being played CDs at Guantánamo.

### 11. A Haunted Hotel

### 12. The Frequencies
Ronson follows the patent trail to Dr. Oliver Lowery's "Silent Subliminal Presentation System" (US Patent #5,159,703, 1992), whose schematics were classified by the U.S. government. In a strange phone call, Lowery claims silent-sound research is a "Manhattan Project"-scale effort used in the Gulf War. The chapter ties this to Russian scientist Dr. Igor Smirnov's "psycho-correction" work and his 1993 FBI consultation during the Waco siege — where the FBI blasted the Branch Davidians with chants, dying-rabbit noises and Nancy Sinatra — framing Waco as another "experiment" on available adversaries.

### 14. The 1953 House
Eric Olson's frozen-in-1953 Maryland house frames the story of his father, Army scientist Frank Olson, who fell from a New York hotel window on 28 November 1953. Decades later the Rockefeller Commission reveals Olson had been secretly dosed with LSD by the CIA as part of MK-ULTRA, overseen by Sidney Gottlieb — who also enlisted a Broadway magician to teach drink-spiking and ran an LSD "safehouse" brothel. A hotel manager's letter and a switchboard operator's account ("Well, he's gone." / "That's too bad.") suggest Olson may have been murdered.

### 15. Harold's Club or Bust!
Eric Olson pursues the truth of his father's death — exhuming the body in 1994 (the skull injury suggesting he was struck before falling) and preparing a news conference charging that Frank was killed to silence him about projects "Artichoke and MK-ULTRA." Ronson notes the eerie organizational closeness of the "light side" (psychic spies) and the "dark side" (Gottlieb, who administered the psychic unit before Stubblebine), and recounts Eric's boyhood cross-country bicycle trip ("Harold's Club or Bust!").

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Ronson is candid about evidentiary murk: he lays out "four possible scenarios" for Uri Geller's psychic-spy claims, notes psychic soldiers "chit-chatting all over town" and selling autobiographies, and treats the goat-staring legend as something he could never fully verify (the goat may already have been in "a shaky medical condition"). He presents the New Age originators (especially Channon) as well-meaning idealists whose ideas were repurposed by others toward ends they never intended.
