## Source Description

*Secrets of the Federal Reserve: The London Connection* is the expanded, retitled edition of Eustace Mullins's 1952 study (see the companion page [[A Study of the Federal Reserve and its Secrets (Mullins)]], which this wiki ingested first). This 1985 edition (Bankers Research Institute) reorganizes the material by topic into fourteen chapters plus appendices and adds substantial new content not in the earlier edition.

The Foreword states the book's origin explicitly: in 1949, while visiting the poet **Ezra Pound** — held without trial as a political prisoner at St. Elizabeth's Hospital — Mullins was asked by Pound to research the Federal Reserve at the Library of Congress, with Pound supplementing his income by ten dollars a week. Mullins names Pound's wartime antagonists Harry Dexter White, Lauchlin Currie, and Alger Hiss as "subsequently identified as being connected with Communist espionage."

**Note on extraction:** As with the other Mullins/Griffin works, this is a partisan, conspiracist account, and the "London Connection" and "Hitler Connection" theses (Rothschild control of the Fed via London, Wall Street/Bank of England financing of Hitler) are contested or rejected by mainstream scholarship. Claims are catalogued per the wiki's strict-extraction policy, without endorsement.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Mullins corrects an earlier researcher (William Guy Carr), noting the Morgan–Rothschild affiliation dated to 1835 (via Peabody), not 1899 as Carr supposed. He cites a wide range of authorities (Quigley, LaRouche, McFadden's congressional speeches, bankers' biographies) as mutually corroborating.
