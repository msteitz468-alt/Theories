## Source Description

*The Twelfth Planet* (1976) is the first volume of Zecharia Sitchin's *Earth Chronicles* series. Drawing on the Hebrew Old Testament and on Sumerian, Akkadian, Babylonian, Assyrian, Hittite, and Canaanite texts, Sitchin advances a single interpretive thesis: that the "gods" of the ancient Near East were the **Nefilim**, flesh-and-blood astronauts from a hitherto-unrecognised planet of our own solar system — the **Twelfth Planet** (counting the Sun and Moon as bodies in the Sumerian manner). Per Sitchin, these visitors arrived ~445,000 years ago to mine gold, genetically fashioned modern man from an existing hominid to serve as a labour force, and are remembered in scripture and myth as gods and "angels."

The book proceeds in fourteen chapters, moving from the "puzzle" of man's sudden appearance and the sudden flowering of Sumer, through the identities and family of the gods, the astronomy of the Twelfth Planet, a re-reading of the *Enuma Elish* creation epic as a literal cosmogony, the creation of man, and the Deluge.

**Note on extraction:** This is an ancient-astronaut / pseudo-archaeology work. Its central claims — a 3,600-year planet Nibiru, genetic engineering of *Homo sapiens*, and the literal-historical reading of Sumerian myth — are rejected by mainstream Assyriology, astronomy, and biology, and several of Sitchin's translations are disputed by Sumerologists. Per the wiki's strict-extraction policy, claims are catalogued **as the source presents them**, without endorsement. Sitchin's account is the upstream source for many later writers (e.g. Icke); see [[Anunnaki (Icke)]] for the distinct, reptilian reframing — Sitchin's Nefilim are biological humanoids, not shape-shifting reptilians.

## Chapter Summaries

### Chapter 1: The Endless Beginning
Sitchin opens with "exhibit number one": modern *Homo sapiens* himself, whom he calls "a stranger to Earth." He reviews the fossil record (ape ancestors ~25 million years ago, *Homo erectus*, Neanderthal) and argues that the abrupt appearance of *Homo sapiens* roughly 300,000 years ago — "many fossil collateral relatives but no progenitors," in Dobzhansky's words — is implausibly fast for ordinary evolution. He then traces the equally abrupt rise of civilization in the Near East: the regression recorded at the Shanidar cave, the sudden onset of agriculture and animal domestication circa 11,000 B.C., and the appearance of pottery, each occurring "suddenly, unexpectedly, inexplicably." A "mysterious hand," he proposes, repeatedly lifted Man out of decline.

### Chapter 2: The Sudden Civilization
A history of the rediscovery of Sumer: the decipherment of cuneiform, Botta and Layard's excavations of the Assyrian capitals (Khorsabad/Dur Sharrukin, Nineveh, Nimrud, Ashur), and the working-back from Assyria and Babylonia to Akkad (Sargon) and finally to the Sumerians, whose existence Jules Oppert proposed in 1869. Sitchin stresses that the Akkadians/Babylonians/Assyrians copied "olden texts" and that Sumerian was the fountainhead language and civilization. He marshals this to argue that the high civilization of Sumer flowered ~6,000 years ago with no precursor — a "gift of the gods."

### Chapter 3: The Gods of Heaven and Earth
Sitchin surveys the pantheons of the Greeks (the twelve Olympians, descended from Uranus and Gaea through the Titans), the Hindu Vedas (the twelve Adityas), and the Hittites, showing recurrent features: anthropomorphic gods who descend from the heavens, procreate, quarrel, and number exactly twelve. He treats these as branches of one original tradition rooted in the Near East, and identifies the Sumerian gods as the source — flesh-and-blood beings, each associated with a celestial body, who came down to Earth.

### Chapter 4: Sumer: Land of the Gods
Sitchin distinguishes gods "of the heavens" (Apsu, Tiamat, Anshar, Kishar — never on Earth) from gods "of Earth," and describes the Sumerian pantheon and its cult-cities. He details the great gods Anu, Enlil, and Enki/Ea and the division of realms among them, presenting the gods as physical rulers who governed Sumer's cities and directed its people.

### Chapter 5: The Nefilim: People of the Fiery Rockets
Sitchin presents his central reinterpretation: the biblical *Nefilim* ("Those Who Were Cast Down" / who descended) of Genesis 6 are the Sumerian gods, astronauts who came to Earth. He analyzes texts and depictions of the gods' "fiery rockets," space-suited figures, and celestial vehicles, and closes the chapter with the dramatic reveal that the Nefilim told humanity of the planet they came from — the Twelfth Planet.

### Chapter 6: The Twelfth Planet
The astronomical core. Using the Akkadian cylinder seal VA/243, which Sitchin reads as a complete twelve-body solar system (Sun, Moon counted, plus a planet between Mars and Jupiter), he argues the Sumerians knew of Uranus, Neptune, Pluto, and an additional large planet long before modern astronomy. He connects the asteroid belt to a shattered planet and concludes there exists a Twelfth Planet — the Nefilim's home world — on a great elongated orbit. The recurrence of the number twelve in calendars, zodiac, and pantheons is traced to these twelve solar-system bodies.

### Chapter 7: The Epic of Creation
Sitchin reads the Babylonian *Enuma Elish* not as myth but as literal cosmogony. The "gods" Apsu (Sun), Mummu (Mercury), and Tiamat are primordial bodies; the invading planet Marduk (Nibiru) is captured into the solar system, collides with Tiamat, and splits her — half becoming Earth and half the "Hammered Bracelet," the asteroid belt. He identifies each named god with a planet (Lahmu/Lahamu = Mars/Venus, Anu = Uranus, Nudimmud/Ea = Neptune, Gaga = Pluto) and reads the celestial battle as a real collision.

### Chapter 8: Kingship of Heaven
Following Marduk's victory and the construction of the "stations" (orbits) for the twelve bodies, Sitchin reconstructs the orbital period and chronology of the Twelfth Planet. He links the Sumerian *shar* (3,600) and the king-list reigns to the planet's long orbit, presenting the immense regnal numbers as measures of orbital time rather than mythic exaggeration.

### Chapter 9: Landing on Planet Earth
Sitchin argues the Nefilim's celestial knowledge (constellations, zodiac, planetary symbols on kudurru boundary stones and stelae) was taught to Man, and that certain symbols encode space-travel information — a "way station" on Mars, landing approaches, and a spaceport. He reconstructs the Nefilim's first arrival ~445,000 years ago and the establishment of a landing corridor toward Mesopotamia.

### Chapter 12: The Creation of Man
Enki and the mother-goddess Ninti/Ninhursag fashion "the Adam" by combining the genetic essence of a Nefilim with that of an existing hominid (*Homo erectus*), through a process Sitchin reads as in-vitro fertilization and cloning described in the texts. Man is created expressly as a *lulu*, a "primitive worker," to bear the gods' toil. Sitchin aligns this with the Genesis "let us make man in our image" plural.

### Chapter 13: The End of All Flesh
Sitchin reads the Garden of Eden, the "forbidding" of the fruit, and the granting of procreative ability as stages in Man's biological development under the gods, and recounts the deteriorating relations between the gods and a multiplying Mankind. He frames the gods' resolve to let Mankind perish — and Enlil's role in withholding warning — as the prelude to the Deluge, identifying the *Nefilim*-fathered "sons of the gods" of Genesis 6.

### Chapter 14: When the Gods Fled from Earth
The Deluge. Sitchin (citing Woolley's flood stratum at Ur) argues the Flood was a real, natural catastrophe — a giant tidal wave caused by the slippage of the Antarctic ice sheet, an event the Nefilim foresaw (as the Twelfth Planet neared) and concealed from Mankind. Enlil bound the gods to secrecy, but Enki warned his human (Ziusudra/Noah), who survived. The gods, having fled to orbit during the flood, return to a devastated Earth, and Sitchin closes with Mankind ultimately "crowding his gods off the Earth."

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Sitchin repeatedly harmonises competing texts (e.g. the Babylonian *Enuma Elish* names Marduk as the victorious planet/god, whereas Sitchin treats the underlying Sumerian tradition as crediting Nibiru and the elder gods). He treats biblical *Elohim* (plural) and the Sumerian pantheon as the same plural reality. He also notes that the "monster" Tiamat of the epic is, on his reading, simultaneously a goddess and a literal planet — the source itself flags this dual register.
