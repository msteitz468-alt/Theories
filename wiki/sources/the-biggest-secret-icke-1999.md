## Source Description

*The Biggest Secret: The Book That Will Change the World* (1999) is David Icke's foundational statement of his **reptilian-bloodline** thesis, the predecessor to the already-ingested [[Children of the Matrix (Icke, 2001)]]. Across 21 chapters it argues that a "race within a race" of interbreeding bloodlines — which Icke names the **Babylonian Brotherhood** — descends from a reptilian extraterrestrial race (the Anunnaki / "Serpent Race"), has controlled human civilization from ancient Sumer to the present, and created (not merely infiltrated) the institutions of government, banking, religion, and media to advance a "Great Work of Ages" toward centralized global control.

The book is best known for two signature contributions beyond the general reptilian thesis: (1) its detailed claim that **Princess Diana was ritually murdered** by the Brotherhood at the Pont de l'Alma, an alleged ancient sacrificial site to the Moon goddess Diana; and (2) the testimony of **Arizona Wilder**, a self-described "Mother Goddess" who claims to have conducted elite blood rituals at which royal and political figures shape-shifted into reptiles.

**Note on extraction:** This is a conspiracy-esoteric work whose central claims (a reptilian ruling species, the ritual murder of Diana, hereditary Satanic abuse by named living royals and politicians) are rejected by mainstream scholarship and have been widely criticised; some passages (e.g. the "El-lizard-birth," "Rothschild/Bronfman 'Jewish crime family'" framing) have drawn accusations of antisemitic coding. Per the wiki's strict-extraction policy, claims are catalogued **as the source presents them**, without endorsement. Much of the general reptilian/bloodline/Jesus-myth/blood-drinking material is already catalogued under the *Children of the Matrix* ingest; this source page focuses on what is distinctive to *The Biggest Secret*.

### Introduction & Chapter 1: The Martians have landed?
Icke states he has chosen to "communicate all the relevant information" however bizarre, treating readers as adults. He sets out the book's thesis that a hidden force has manipulated humanity for thousands of years, and previews the Denver Airport material (Masonic symbols, reptilian gargoyles, an apocalyptic mural) as evidence of the Brotherhood's planned New World Order.

### Chapter 2: "Don't mention the reptiles"
Icke introduces the core reptilian thesis: the **Anunnaki** of the Sumerian Tablets created interbreeding bloodlines to rule humanity, and the European royal/aristocratic and US "Eastern Establishment" families are this same genetically-related "tribe." He argues their obsession with interbreeding is to preserve the genetic structure that gives them "the ability to shape-shift and manifest in other forms."

### Chapter 19: The Goddess and the King
The book's signature chapter. Icke argues the agencies of the interconnecting bloodline families "were responsible for the murder of Diana, Princess of Wales," and links it to the JFK assassination as the same ritual. He frames Kennedy's killing as the ancient fertility rite of the **"Killing of the King"** — Kennedy shot in the back, head and throat, "the same wounds suffered by the mythical Hiram Abiff" of Freemasonic legend — at Dealey Plaza near the 33rd parallel. He then argues Diana, "named after the ancient Moon goddess," was manoeuvred into the **Pont de l'Alma** tunnel — an ancient sacrificial site to the goddess Diana and a crossroads sacred to Hecate — where the car was driven into the 13th pillar (a programmed driver, Henri Paul, as "patsy"), her death timed to Hecate's sacrifice day (31 August) and surrounded by Osiris/Isis/Horus symbolism. He cites **Arizona Wilder** on Guy de Rothschild's claim to have been in the tunnel to "steal Diana's soul."

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Icke repeatedly concedes the extremity of his claims ("I know how extreme that sounds") and frames the reptilian thesis as where "the evidence takes me." He stacks many independent witnesses (Arizona Wilder, Cathy O'Brien, Christine Fitzgerald, Brice Taylor) whose mutual corroboration he treats as confirmation; he does not present mainstream rebuttals. The book also leans heavily on [[The Twelfth Planet (Sitchin)|Zecharia Sitchin's]] Sumerian translations, which it cites by name.
