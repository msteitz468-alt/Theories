## Source Description

*Science Set Free* (2012; UK title *The Science Delusion*) by biologist **[[Rupert Sheldrake]]** argues that science has been confined by a materialist belief system resting on **ten unproven dogmas** — assumptions treated as established facts (that nature is mechanical, that matter is unconscious, that the laws and constants of nature are fixed, that minds are confined to brains, that memories are stored as material traces, that psychic phenomena are illusory, and so on). Turning each dogma into an open question, Sheldrake contends that **nature behaves more like an evolving organism than a machine**, that the "laws" of nature may be better understood as **habits**, and that a collective memory operates through his hypothesis of **morphic resonance**.

He marshals evidence that the fundamental "constants" have varied, that biological form and instinct are shaped by resonance from past similar organisms rather than stored wholly in genes or brains, and that **minds are extended** beyond the body — defending the reality of telepathy and the sense of being stared at. The book is a philosophy-of-science pillar of the wiki's **post-materialist science** cluster ([[Post-materialist science]]).

## Structure

Prologue (Science, Religion and Power) + Introduction (The Ten Dogmas of Modern Science) + ten chapters, each examining one dogma as a question (Is Nature Mechanical? · Is the Total Amount of Matter and Energy Always the Same? · Are the Laws of Nature Fixed? · Is Matter Unconscious? · Is Nature Purposeless? · Is All Biological Inheritance Material? · Are Memories Stored as Material Traces? · Are Minds Confined to Brains? · Are Psychic Phenomena Illusory? · Is Mechanistic Medicine the Only Kind That Really Works?) + Conclusions.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Sheldrake frames his case as restoring open inquiry rather than rejecting science; he presents morphic resonance and the extended mind as testable hypotheses and reports experimental support, while acknowledging that the scientific mainstream regards morphic resonance as unproven and largely rejects it.
