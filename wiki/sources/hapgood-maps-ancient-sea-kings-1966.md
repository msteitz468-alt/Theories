## Source Description

*Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings: Evidence of Advanced Civilization in the Ice Age* is a book by geographer and historian Charles H. Hapgood, first published in 1966 by Chilton Books. The book is a detailed synthesis of cartographic and geological evidence, analyzing late-medieval and early-modern navigation charts (portolans) to argue that an advanced, worldwide maritime civilization existed during the last Ice Age and mapped the globe with high scientific precision before disappearing.

Hapgood argues that maps such as the Piri Reis Map of 1513 and the Oronteus Finaeus Map of 1531 contain accurate depictions of the coasts of Antarctica, South America, and northern regions before they were covered by ice sheets or altered by post-glacial sea-level rise. He asserts that these maps could not have been drawn by medieval or classical Greek navigators, but were instead copied from ancient prototypes. To explain how Antarctica could have had ice-free coasts in the relatively recent past, Hapgood proposes the mechanism of earth-crust displacement—the shifting of the Earth's entire lithosphere as a single unit over the semi-fluid mantle beneath, driven by the centrifugal weight of polar ice caps.

## Chapter Summaries

### Preface & Foreword
Hapgood outlines the transmission of ancient sea charts through history, suggesting they originated with an unknown prehistoric maritime people, were passed to the Minoans and Phoenicians, collected in the Library of Alexandria, copied in Constantinople, and finally carried to the West after the Fourth Crusade (1204 AD). In the Foreword, Dr. John K. Wright (former director of the American Geographical Society) characterizes Hapgood's work as a stimulating challenge to orthodox cartographical history.

### Chapter I: The Treasure Hunt Begins
Details the rediscovery of the Piri Reis Map of 1513 in Constantinople in 1929. Hapgood describes how Captain Arlington H. Mallery first suggested that the southernmost portion of the map represented the ice-free bays and islands of Queen Maud Land, Antarctica. Hapgood recounts enlistment of Weston Observatory director Daniel L. Linehan and Georgetown Observatory director Francis Heyden to examine the claims, leading him to initiate a student research project at Keene State College to analyze the map's mathematics.

### Chapter II: The Secrets of the Piri Re'is Map
Investigates medieval portolan charts, highlighting Swedish scholar A.E. Nordenskiöld's thesis that all portolanos were derived from a single prehistoric original (the "normal portolano"). Hapgood's students (Livengood, Estes, and Woitkowski) reconstruct the geometry of the Piri Reis projection, establishing that it was built on plane trigonometry using Eratosthenes's measurement of the Earth's circumference. The projection's center is located near Syene (Aswan) on the Tropic of Cancer, with a radius extending from the Tropic to the Pole (66.5 degrees), and its prime meridian is set at 20 degrees West Longitude.
* **Claims Extracted:**
  * [[The geographers of a lost civilization applied advanced mathematics, including spherical trigonometry and correct measurements of the Earth's circumference, to map the globe long before classical Greece or modern times]]

### Chapter III: The Piri Re'is Map in Detail
A section-by-section analysis of the Piri Reis Map's geography. Hapgood demonstrates that the map is a composite constructed by piecing together local maps of varying scales and orientations, which accounts for local distortions while preserving highly accurate relative longitudes that were impossible to calculate in 1513.

### Chapter IV: The Antarctic Maps of Oronteus
Analyzes the Oronteus Finaeus Map of 1531, which depicts the entire continent of Antarctica in detail, including mountains, rivers, and deep fjords, on a heart-shaped (cordiform) projection. Hapgood presents geological evidence from Ross Sea sediment cores to show that fine-grained river sediments were deposited on the Antarctic seabed as recently as 6,000 years ago, indicating ice-free coastal conditions when the original surveys were conducted.
* **Claims Extracted:**
  * [[The Piri Reis and Oronteus Finaeus maps show the coasts of Antarctica before they were covered by the current ice cap, indicating they were mapped during a pre-glacial or ice-free period]]

### Chapter V: Ancient Maps of the East and West
Examines other late-medieval and Renaissance charts, including the Hadji Ahmed Map of 1559. Hapgood points out that this map shows a land bridge connecting Siberia and Alaska, indicating that the original surveys were made during the Ice Age when sea levels were significantly lower.

### Chapter VI: The Ancient Maps of the North
Examines the Zeno Map (showing Greenland ice-free), the Benincasa Map, and the Ibn Ben Zara Map. The Ibn Ben Zara Map depicts Sweden and Germany covered by ice sheets and features glaciers in England and Ireland. It also shows a large bay at the mouth of the Guadalquivir River in Spain where a massive delta now exists, confirming that the map was drawn before delta-building occurred, during a period of lower sea level.

### Chapter VII: The Scientific Implications of the Maps
Synthesizes the scientific achievements preserved in the maps. Hapgood argues that the use of cordiform and spherical projections (such as the De Canerio Map of 1502) proves that ancient cartographers possessed an advanced understanding of spherical trigonometry. He traces the "twelve-wind system" (the division of the 360-degree circle into twelve 30-degree arcs) to extreme antiquity before the rise of Babylonian astronomy. Finally, Hapgood presents the physics of earth-crust displacement, utilizing calculations by engineer James Campbell to show how polar ice caps generate centrifugal force that periodically shifts the Earth's crust as a single piece over a fluid-like sub-layer.
* **Claims Extracted:**
  * [[The geographers of a lost civilization applied advanced mathematics, including spherical trigonometry and correct measurements of the Earth's circumference, to map the globe long before classical Greece or modern times]]
  * [[The mathematical division of the 360-degree circle into twelve 30-degree arcs (the twelve-wind system) originated in extreme antiquity before the rise of Babylonia or Phoenicia]]
  * [[The entire crust of the Earth shifts as a single unit over the liquid-like mantle beneath, driven by the centrifugal forces of eccentric ice caps]]

### Chapter VIII: A Civilization That Vanished
Postulates a highly advanced worldwide maritime civilization that existed at least 20,000 years ago. Hapgood challenges the linear view of progress (paleolithic to neolithic to bronze) and notes that civilizations rise and fall. He details how the destruction of ancient libraries (the Library of Alexandria by Caesar, Christian mobs, and the Arabs; and the Library of Carthage by the Romans) resulted in the loss of 90 percent of ancient scientific and cartographic knowledge.
* **Claims Extracted:**
  * [[An advanced worldwide maritime civilization with global commerce and sophisticated navigation instruments existed at least 20,000 years ago during the Ice Age]]
  * [[The repeated destruction of ancient library centers, such as Alexandria and Carthage, resulted in the loss of 90 percent of ancient scientific and cartographic knowledge]]
