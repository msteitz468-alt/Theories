## Source Description

*The Secret Societies of All Ages and Countries* (1897, new edition) by Charles William Heckethorn is a two-volume Victorian reference work — "a comprehensive account of upwards of one hundred and sixty secret organisations" from antiquity to the 1890s. **Both volumes are ingested here.**

**Volume I** covers the ancient and medieval societies: the ancient **Mysteries** (Mithraic, Indian, Egyptian/Serapis/Osiris, Greek/Eleusinian, Chinese, Mexican/Peruvian, Scandinavian), the **Gnostics**, the troubadours ("Gay Science") and Cathars, the **Assassins**, the **Knights Templars**, the Holy **Vehm** (Vehmgericht), the alchemists, mystics, and **Rosicrucians**, and the transition to Freemasonry. **Volume II** covers Freemasonry in exhaustive detail (its legend, rites, degrees, national branches, and persecutions) and then the secret political and religious societies of the 18th–19th centuries: the Comuneros, the **Carbonari**, the later Illuminati, Chinese and Turkish societies, the **Nihilists**, German societies, the Babis, and the Irish agrarian societies (White-Boys, Defenders, etc.).

Heckethorn writes as a sober, often **skeptical** cataloguer rather than a believer or alarmist. He strips Masonic and esoteric history of "the varnish and tinsel" of partisan writers, credits some societies' historical role in opposing despotism — and bluntly debunks the grandiose claims of modern Freemasonry. The book is a useful primary-era counterweight to the conspiratorial ([[The Illuminati (Marrs)|Marrs]]) and esoteric ([[The Secret History of the World (Black)|Black]]) treatments of the same organisations.

## Structure

**Vol. I** — Introduction (classification of secret societies); Book I, Ancient Mysteries; Book II, the Gnostics; Book III, the Gay Science / the Cathars; Book IV, the Assassins; Book V, the Knights Templars; Book VI, the Holy Vehm; Book VII, alchemists/mystics/Rosicrucians; Book VIII, the beginnings of Freemasonry. **Vol. II** — Book XI, Freemasonry (legend, derivation, rites, the Lodge, Royal Arch, Rose-Croix, Rites of Misraïm/Memphis, Modern Knights Templars, Freemasonry by country, persecutions, and "The Futility of Modern Freemasonry"); Books XII–XIV, the Comuneros, the Carbonari, Chinese/Turkish societies, the Illuminati, the Nihilists, German societies, the Babis, and Irish societies.

## Chapter Summaries

This two-volume work has very many short numbered sections (§) on individual societies, grouped into "Books." The summaries below follow the book's own Books, grouping the many thin society-articles under combined headings as the material warrants.

### Volume I — Introduction: The Nature and Classification of Secret Societies
Heckethorn frames secret societies not as accidental but as "the intelligible and foreseen result of known causes" — "the tree presupposes a root." He classifies them as religious, military, judiciary, scientific, civil, and political (with examples: Mysteries, Templars, Vehmgerichte, Alchemists, Freemasons, Carbonari), then reduces these to two broad divisions, religious and political. His governing thesis is that religion's secret societies arose when the "genuine knowledge" of the first men — the knowledge of the constitution of the universe — began to decay, and that the ancient Mysteries preserved a degraded "type" of that primeval wisdom. His rule for inclusion is any society with "secret rites and ceremonies," even where the society's existence is public (e.g. Freemasonry).

### Volume I, Book I — Ancient Mysteries (Magi, Mithraics, Brahmins, Egyptian, Greek, Chinese, Mexican/Peruvian, Druids, Scandinavian)
A survey of the initiatory Mysteries of antiquity: the Persian Magi and Zoroaster; the Mithraic mysteries and their rites of initiation; the Brahmins and Gymnosophists (the ineffable name Aum, the Lingam, the Lotos); the Egyptian mysteries of Serapis, Osiris, and Isis and their metamorphoses into the Dionysiac, Sabazian, Cabiric, and Eleusinian mysteries; Chinese and Japanese mysteries; the cruel Mexican and Peruvian mysteries with their human sacrifices; the Druids as "the Magi of the West"; and the Scandinavian Drottes. Throughout, Heckethorn reads the Mysteries as decayed survivals of a primeval knowledge, often under an astronomical aspect, and stresses the "uniformity of dogmas" across cultures.

### Volume I, Books II–III — The Emanationists and the Religion of Love (Cabala, Gnostics, Manichæans, the Gay Science, Cathars, Troubadours)
Book II covers the Cabala (its origin, the Book of Creation, the creation out of nothing) and the Gnostics (their character, doctrines, and the spirit of Gnosticism). Book III ("Religion of Love") traces Manes and Manichæism and its spread, then the medieval "Gay Science": the transition from ancient to modern initiations, the Albigenses and their tenets, the religion and poetry of the Troubadours with their degrees and "Courts of Love," and Chivalry as the "military apostles of the Religion of Love." Heckethorn treats the Cathars/Albigenses as a heretical survival of the dualist current.

### Volume I, Book IV — The Ishmaelites: Lodge of Wisdom, the Assassins, the Druses
The secret doctrines of Islam and the Lodge of Cairo, then the Order of the Assassins: the foundation of the order, the influence of Hassan-i-Sabbah, the catechism and graded devotion of the followers, the "Imaginary Paradise" used to secure their fanatical obedience, the sanguinary character of Hassan, and the extinction of the sect. ### Volume I, Book V — The Knights Templars
The Order's foundation in 1118 by nine knights who united "the monk and the knight," its rapid growth and commanderies, and then its destruction: the imputations and plots against it, the charges against the Templars (cursing and spitting on the cross, the worship of Baphomet), the burning of the knights, and the death of Grand Master Jacques de Molay. Heckethorn weighs the charges critically — examining the mysteries, doctrines, and initiation of the order, the Temple as a symbol of the Holy Spirit, the Templars as opponents of the Pope — and considers the order's claimed connection with later Freemasonry.

### Volume I, Books VI–VII — The Free Judges and the Alchemists (Holy Vehm, Beati Paoli, Alchemists, Rosicrucians)
Book VI covers the Holy Vehm (Vehmgericht): the origin and objects of the institution, its officers, secret language and procedure, the execution of sentences, and its decay; plus the Sicilian Beati Paoli. ### Volume I, Book VIII — The Beginnings of Freemasonry
Heckethorn separates "the plain history of Freemasonry" from its mythic genealogy. He recounts the Legend of the Temple (Hiram Abiff, Solomon, the Queen of Sheba) and the traditions of origin, but argues Freemasonry derived prosaically "from many sources" and the operative masons. He describes the rites and customs, the lodge and its officers, the distinction between "genuine and spurious" masonry, the ceremonies of initiation (Apprentice, Fellow-craft, Master, with the legend of Hiram's murder explained as a solar/Osiris allegory), the higher degrees (Royal Arch, Knight of Kadosh, Prince of Rose-Croix), the rites of Misraïm and Memphis, the Modern Knights Templars, Freemasonry by country, Cagliostro's Egyptian Masonry, adoptive and androgynous masonry, persecutions, and finally "The Futility of Modern Freemasonry."

### Volume II, Book XI — Freemasonry (full treatment) and "The Futility of Modern Freemasonry"
Volume II re-treats Freemasonry in exhaustive detail — the Legend of the Temple and the murder of Hiram, the origin and "true history" of masonry, rites and customs, the lodge, the degrees and their ceremonies, the rites of Misraïm and Memphis, the Modern Knights Templars, Freemasonry by nation (England, Scotland, France, Italy, etc.), Cagliostro, adoptive and androgynous masonry, schismatic rites, the diffusion of the order, and its persecutions. It culminates (§§495–500a) in "The Futility of Modern Freemasonry," where Heckethorn dismisses the "vain pretensions" of modern masonry: founded when knowledge was the privilege of the few, it is now "a retrograde institution," its ceremonial a "childish and mischievous" affectation that diffuses no knowledge and has decayed.

### Volume II, Book XII — The International, the Commune, and the Anarchists
A survey of nineteenth-century socialist organization: socialistic schemes, the history, objects, and aims of the International (in England and abroad), its relation to the Empire, the Franco-Prussian War, and the Paris Commune, the budget of the International, and the emergence of the Anarchists.

### Volume II, Book XIII — Political Secret Societies (Chinese, Comuneros, Hetairia, Carbonari, Italian, Napoleonic, French, Polish, Turkish, Nihilist, German, Babis, Irish)
The largest political section. It covers the Chinese Hung League and Ko-lao-hui; the Spanish Comuneros; the Greek Hetairia and Ipsilanti's rising; the **Carbonari** in full (history and real origin, the *vendita*, the ritual and degrees, the symbols, Carbonarism's spread through Italy, France, Germany, and Spain); miscellaneous Italian societies (Mazzini and Young Italy, the Sanfedisti); the Napoleonic and anti-Napoleonic societies — including the later **Illuminati** (§594), a French/Italian society founded to restore the Napoleon family, "not to be confounded" with Weishaupt's earlier Bavarian order; French, Polish, and Turkish societies; the **Nihilists** at length (the meaning of the term, Nechayeff, "going among the people," the Party of Terror, the assassination of the Emperor, trials and decline); the German Tugendbund; the Persian Babis; and the Irish agrarian and republican societies (White-Boys, Defenders, Ribbonmen, Molly Maguires, the Orangemen, and Fenianism with the Phoenix Park murders and dynamite outrages).

A long alphabetical catalogue of minor and exotic societies, each given a brief notice — from the ABC Friends, Abelites, and Academy of the Ancients through the Duk-Duk, the Egbo Society, the Grand Army of the Republic, the Heroine of Jericho, the Ku-Klux-Klan, Mumbo-Jumbo, the Odd Fellows, O-Kee-Pa, the Knights of Pythias, the Red Men, and many more. ## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Heckethorn distinguishes "the plain history of Freemasonry, without the varnish and tinsel Masonic writers have bedizened it with" from the mythic genealogies (Solomon's Temple, the ancient Mysteries) that Masons claim — while still granting Masonry inherited "Indian, Egyptian, Jewish, and Christian ideas, terms, and symbols." He is candid that he grows "more repelled by its pretences" the more he studies it, and treats sensational claims (e.g. Masonic "tribunals" condemning monarchs and the Pope) critically rather than credulously.
