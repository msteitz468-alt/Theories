## Source Description

*The Devil's Chessboard: Allen Dulles, the CIA, and the Rise of America's Secret Government* is an investigative biography/history by David Talbot (b. 1951), founder of *Salon* and author of *Brothers: The Hidden History of the Kennedy Years*. Published in 2015, it is a critical account of Allen Welsh Dulles (1893–1969) — Wall Street lawyer (Sullivan & Cromwell), OSS Bern station chief in World War II, and Director of Central Intelligence 1953–1961 — and of the rise of what Talbot, borrowing Peter Dale Scott's term, calls "deep politics" and the "deep state."

The book draws on the Dulles papers and calendars (Seeley Mudd Library, Princeton), the Mary Bancroft and Clover Dulles papers (Schlesinger Library, Radcliffe), declassified CIA/State Department documents, congressional investigation records (Church Committee, House Select Committee on Assassinations), and author interviews (including with Dulles's daughter Joan Talley). Its thesis is that Dulles repeatedly subverted the presidents he served, built an unaccountable intelligence apparatus, protected Nazi war criminals, ran coups and assassination programs, and was complicit in the assassination of President Kennedy and then steered the Warren Commission cover-up. ## Chapter Summaries

### Prologue

Talbot opens in Georgetown in September 1965, where the retired Allen Dulles, working with young Harper's editor Willie Morris on an article defending his Bay of Pigs record ("My Answer to the Bay of Pigs"), erupts with scorn at "that little Kennedy . . . he thought he was a god." Talbot frames the book's thesis: Dulles, a Wall Street lawyer turned spymaster, "undermined or betrayed every president he served," built an unaccountable secret government, and after being fired by Kennedy turned his Q Street home into "an anti-Kennedy government in exile," later lobbying Lyndon Johnson onto the Warren Commission to steer it toward "lone gunman" Oswald. He introduces Peter Dale Scott's term "deep politics" and quotes Dulles's daughter Joan Talley ("It seems we just went wild").

### Chapter 1: The Double Agent

Dulles arrives in Bern as OSS station chief in November 1942, "hiding in plain sight" while running what was effectively his own foreign policy. Talbot argues Dulles "was more in step with many Nazi leaders than he was with President Roosevelt," detailing the Dulles brothers' Sullivan & Cromwell ties to German cartels (IG Farben, Krupp), Foster's defense of Hitler lobbyist Gerhardt Westrick, and Allen's coordination with Thomas McKittrick of the Bank for International Settlements, which laundered looted Nazi gold. Dulles meets secretly with Himmler's envoy Prince Max von Hohenlohe, assuring him the Casablanca "unconditional surrender" declaration was "merely a piece of paper," and sabotages Project Safehaven's hunt for hidden Nazi assets.

### Chapter 2: Human Smoke

A portrait of the Dulles brothers' upbringing on Lake Ontario and their cold temperaments (Clover called Allen "The Shark"), juxtaposed with Foster's callousness toward his Jewish friends and his attempt to talk Eleanor out of marrying a Jew. Talbot traces the State Department's obstruction of Jewish refugees — the turned-away ship *St. Louis*, Breckinridge Long's visa-delaying memos — and argues Dulles received credible, detailed Holocaust intelligence from informants Eduard Schulte, Fritz Kolbe, and the Vrba-Wetzler Auschwitz report, yet his 300-plus Bern communiqués barely mentioned the killing of Jews and conveyed no urgency.

### Chapter 3: Ghosts of Nuremberg

Against the ruins of Nuremberg and the 1945–46 war crimes trial (the *Death Mills* film, Goering's cyanide), Talbot recounts FDR ally Herbert Pell's fight on the UN War Crimes Commission against State Department officials like Green Hackworth who resisted prosecuting Nazis and industrialists. Donovan and Dulles, as Wall Street lawyers, sought to control the trial's "economic case" — which would have let them shape the fates of former Sullivan & Cromwell clients — until prosecutor Robert Jackson blocked them. Dulles tells the Council on Foreign Relations that Germany cannot be fully de-Nazified ("you can't run railroads without taking in some Party members") and begins protecting war criminals through the subsequent trials.

### Chapter 4: Sunrise

The core account of Dulles's secret Operation Sunrise negotiations with SS general Karl Wolff — Himmler's former chief of staff and security commander in Italy, blamed for civilian reprisals and torture — for a separate German surrender on the Italian front, in defiance of FDR's policy and an explicit Washington order. When Wolff is surrounded by partisans at Villa Locatelli (April 1945), Dulles dispatches Gero von Schulze-Gaevernitz to mount a US–German rescue convoy that extracts the general. Talbot argues the maneuver deepened Stalin's distrust and helped open "the first icy fissures of the Cold War," while saving few lives.

### Chapter 5: Ratlines

The postwar protection and recruitment of ex-Nazis. SS colonel Eugen Dollmann, Wolff's Sunrise intermediary, is sprung from captivity, hidden by Cardinal Schuster, and installed by young counterintelligence officer James Jesus Angleton in a Rome safe house and offered to the CIA as an asset. Talbot describes Angleton's anti-Communist zeal, his Vatican ties, and Dulles's role as his mentor, framing the "ratlines" that moved war criminals to Spain, the Middle East, and South America, and the integration of Nazi intelligence figures into the emerging US apparatus.

### Chapter 6: Useful People

Opening with Clover Dulles and Allen's wartime mistress Mary Bancroft as patients of Carl Jung in Zurich, the chapter explores the women who tried and failed to decipher Dulles's "emotional impenetrability." It charts the postwar founding and consolidation of the CIA (which Dulles turned into "a Cold War colossus far more powerful . . . than anything Truman had imagined") and the recruitment of "useful people" — ex-fascists and assets enlisted without scruple about their pasts — into the new intelligence establishment.

### Chapter 7: Little Mice

The story of Operation Splinter Factor, told through the idealistic American Quaker relief worker Noel Field and his family. Field, his wife Herta, brother Hermann, and adoptive daughter Erica Wallach vanish behind the Iron Curtain; Wallach endures years in the Vorkuta gulag. Talbot presents Dulles's CIA as having fed disinformation that made Field appear to be an American spy, triggering Stalin's paranoid purges and show trials across the Eastern bloc and sacrificing the Fields as expendable pawns.

### Chapter 8: Scoundrel Time

Set in the McCarthy era, the chapter follows freshman congressman Richard Nixon's 1947 Herter Committee tour of Europe alongside Dulles, who is promoting the Marshall Plan (funds from which, Talbot notes, Dulles and Frank Wisner would skim for anti-Soviet operations). It traces Nixon's resentment-fueled ambition and his earlier rejection by Sullivan & Cromwell, and the relationship between Dulles and Nixon as the CIA navigates McCarthyism. Talbot recounts Dulles secretly financing Nixon's career in exchange for silence on Nazi money-laundering investigations.

### Chapter 10: The Dulles Imperium

The CIA's coups against elected governments. Talbot recounts the August 1953 overthrow of Iran's Mohammad Mossadegh and the restoration of the exiled Shah (sheltered by the CIA at Rome's Hotel Excelsior), then the 1954 ouster of Guatemala's Jacobo Arbenz, set in motion after a CFR study group urged action and orchestrated in part by E. Howard Hunt. He describes the CIA's relentless campaign to ruin and pursue the exiled Arbenz, illustrating Dulles's willingness to terminate governments "deemed insufficiently pro-American."

### Chapter 11: Strange Love

### Chapter 12: Brain Warfare

Dulles's April 1953 "brain warfare" speech denounced supposed Soviet mind control even as he authorized MKULTRA, the CIA's quarter-century "Manhattan Project of the Mind," run under Richard Helms with drugs (especially LSD) and hundreds of researchers treating subjects as "expendables." The chapter centers on CIA biochemist Frank Olson, who was secretly dosed with LSD by Sidney Gottlieb's team and days later fell to his death from a New York hotel window — a death Talbot frames as deeply suspicious.

### Chapter 13: Dangerous Ideas

### Chapter 15: Contempt

The Bay of Pigs disaster of April 1961, during which Dulles conspicuously absented himself at a Puerto Rico resort while the CIA-trained exile brigade was crushed. Talbot argues the agency expected Kennedy to be forced into committing US military force; when he refused, the operation collapsed. Kennedy took public responsibility but resolved to break the CIA's power, and forced Dulles into retirement — feeding the spymaster's contempt for the president.

### Chapter 17: The Parting Glass

Kennedy's final European tour of summer 1963 — the American University Peace Speech echoed in Rome, the "Ich bin ein Berliner" address, the audience with the Pope — dramatizes JFK's turn toward détente and peaceful coexistence. Talbot presents this peace agenda, and Kennedy's warnings against extremists "of the far right and the far left," as precisely what alarmed the hardline Cold War establishment that Dulles embodied.

### Chapter 22: End Game

The aftermath, centered on Robert Kennedy — grief-shadowed, privately tormented about his brother's death, and rising as "the second coming" of JFK before his own 1968 assassination. Talbot closes the arc of Dulles's reign and the unaccountable secret power he built, which outlived the presidents it subverted.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Talbot records that he could not fully corroborate the assassination thesis: E. Howard Hunt's account (relayed by his son Saint John Hunt) was "carefully parsed," self-exculpatory, and named [[William King Harvey]] and [[David Sánchez Morales]] while loyally *omitting* Dulles, whom Talbot argues was the true link to [[Lyndon B. Johnson]]. Talbot concedes "there is no evidence" that corporate figures such as David Rockefeller had foreknowledge, framing their role as hostility that "emboldened" Dulles. He also notes scholars' puzzlement (e.g. Neal H. Petersen) over Dulles's wartime silence on the Holocaust rather than asserting a single motive. See [[Controversy: Was Allen Dulles complicit in the Kennedy assassination?]].
