## Source Description

*Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition* (1964) by historian **[[Frances Yates]]** is the landmark
academic study of the **Renaissance Hermetic tradition** — how the (mis)dating of the **[[The Corpus Hermeticum|Corpus Hermeticum]]**
as primordial Egyptian wisdom drove a revival of magic, prisca theologia, and Hermetic philosophy from
**Ficino** and **Pico** through **Agrippa** to **Giordano Bruno**, and how **Casaubon's 1614 redating**
unraveled it. See the umbrella [[The Renaissance Hermetic tradition and the Yates thesis]].

This is the **scholarly backbone** of the wiki's existing Hermetic-revival material: David Picknett &
Clive Prince's *[[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]]* explicitly builds on (and extends) Yates,
so this ingest is the academic original beneath FU's pages on
[[Ficino's translation of the Corpus Hermeticum and the prisca theologia idea (Hermes as ancient sage)|Ficino's translation]],
[[Prisca theologia and the Renaissance Hermetic revival|the prisca theologia]],
[[The Hermetic origins of modern science (Forbidden Universe thesis)|the Hermetic origins of science]],
[[Bruno's infinite Hermetic universe|Bruno's universe]], and
[[The Casaubon redating of the Hermetica|the Casaubon redating]].

## Strict-extraction note

Yates's scholarly arguments are recorded as *what she argues*, attributed and **not adjudicated** — the
same neutral protocol used for the other secondary sources. (Her "Hermetic origins of science" thesis is
itself contested in later scholarship; recorded as her position.)

## Structure & scope

22 chapters. **I–IV** Hermes Trismegistus, Ficino's translation, and Hermetic/natural magic · **V–X**
Pico & Christian Cabala, Agrippa, *Renaissance Magic and Science* (the Yates thesis), objections, and
16th-c. religious Hermetism · **XI–XV** Bruno's Hermetic reform and philosophy (England, the Cabala) ·
**XVI–XX** Bruno's later travels, return to Italy, trial, and Campanella · **XXI–XXII** *After Hermes
Trismegistus was Dated* (Casaubon) and the Fludd controversies. *Heavy on the Hermetic foundation
(I–IV), the science thesis (VIII), and Casaubon (XXI–XXII); the Bruno biography is extracted for its
Hermetic doctrine rather than exhaustive travelogue.*

**Ch. I–IV — Hermes & Ficino** (**[[Hermes Trismegistus as the prisca theologia sage of the Renaissance (Yates)]]** · **[[Ficino's translation of the Hermetica and the revival of Hermetic magic (Yates)]]** · **[[Ficino's natural magic: talismans, spiritus, and astral music (Yates)]]**).
[[Yates argues the Renaissance dated the Corpus Hermeticum to a primordial Egyptian sage, giving it vast authority as the prisca theologia]] ·
[[Yates argues Ficino set aside Plato to translate the Hermetic Pimander first in 1463, under Cosimo de' Medici's orders, launching the Hermetic revival]] ·
[[Yates argues the Asclepius's god-making magic underwrote a new Hermetic magic in the Renaissance, rehabilitated through Neoplatonic interpretation]] ·
[[Yates argues Ficino's natural magic of talismans, planetary music, and the spiritus mundi constituted a new Hermetic religion of the world]].

**Ch. V–X — Pico, Agrippa & the science thesis** (**[[Pico della Mirandola and the birth of Christian Cabala (Yates)]]** · **[[Cornelius Agrippa and the Renaissance magus (Yates)]]** · **[[The Yates thesis: Renaissance Hermetic magic as a stimulus to modern science]]**).
[[Yates argues that Pico fused Ficino's Hermetic magic with Jewish Cabala to create Christian Cabala and the operative Renaissance magus]] ·
[[Yates argues that Agrippa's De occulta philosophia systematized the three worlds of Renaissance magic into a unified operative philosophy]] ·
[[Yates argues that the Renaissance magus's will to operate on nature — rooted in Hermetic-magical worldview — was a psychological stimulus toward the scientific revolution]] ·
[[Yates argues that theological and humanist objections to Renaissance magic were the counter-reaction to Pico's operative synthesis, but did not immediately dislodge it]].

**Ch. XI–XV — Bruno the Hermetic magus** (**[[Giordano Bruno as a Hermetic magus and religious reformer (Yates)]]** · **[[Bruno's Hermetic reading of Copernicanism and the infinite universe (Yates)]]**).
[[Yates argues Bruno was not a martyr for modern science but a Hermetic magus preaching the return of the Egyptian religion (the Giordanisti)]] ·
[[Yates argues Bruno read Copernicus's heliocentrism as a Hermetic hieroglyph of the solar divinity, not as mathematics]] ·
[[Yates argues Bruno's infinite universe and innumerable worlds express a Hermetic-animist cosmology, not modern science]].

**Ch. XVI–XX — Bruno's martyrdom & Campanella** (**[[Bruno's trial and execution as a Hermetic martyr (Yates)]]** · **[[Tommaso Campanella and the Hermetic City of the Sun (Yates)]]**).
[[Yates argues Bruno's German period and final works show his infinite-universe philosophy was a Hermetic religious programme, not proto-scientific inquiry]] ·
[[Yates argues Bruno's return to Italy and his 1600 burning are best understood as a Hermetic religious mission, not a science martyrdom]] ·
[[Yates argues Campanella continued the Hermetic tradition into the 17th century, performing astral magic with Pope Urban VIII and projecting a Hermetic solar utopia]].

**Ch. XXI–XXII — Casaubon & Fludd** (**[[Casaubon's 1614 redating of the Hermetica and the unraveling of the tradition (Yates)]]** · **[[The Fludd controversies and the late Hermetic-Rosicrucian moment (Yates)]]**).
[[Yates argues Casaubon's 1614 philological redating showed the Hermetica were post-Christian, collapsing the prisca-theologia authority of Hermes Trismegistus]] ·
[[Yates argues Robert Fludd carried on the full Renaissance Hermetic-Cabalist worldview in total defiance of Casaubon's redating]] ·
[[Yates argues the Fludd-Kepler and Fludd-Mersenne controversies mark the decisive parting between the dying Hermetic-magical worldview and the rising mathematical science]] ·
[[Yates argues Renaissance Hermetism went underground after Casaubon into Rosicrucianism, Freemasonry, and esoteric societies rather than disappearing]].
