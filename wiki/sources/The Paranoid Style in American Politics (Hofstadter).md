## Source Description

*The Paranoid Style in American Politics* (1964) by historian Richard Hofstadter ([[Richard Hofstadter]])
is the landmark scholarly essay that names and analyzes the **"paranoid style"** — a recurring mode of
political expression marked by "heated exaggeration, suspiciousness, and conspiratorial fantasy." It is
the wiki's key **meta-counterweight**: rather than debunking any single claim, it characterizes the
*rhetorical and cognitive mode* shared by much of the wiki's conspiracy corpus (Cooper, Icke, Marrs,
Coleman, Webster, Allen, Jeffries). *(Umbrella concept "the paranoid style in politics" pending — ingest incomplete.)*

Hofstadter stresses the style is about **how** ideas are held, not whether they are true; that it is
used by "more or less normal people," not clinical paranoiacs; that its central image is a **vast,
sinister conspiracy as the motive force of history**, framed apocalyptically as an all-out crusade; and
that it **recurs** across U.S. history with the same form but changing villains — the anti-Illuminati
panic (Robison and Barruel), anti-Masonry, anti-Catholic nativism, the Populist "international bankers"
conspiracy, McCarthyism, and the John Birch Society.
