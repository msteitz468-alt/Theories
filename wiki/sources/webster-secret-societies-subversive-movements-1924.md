## Source Description

*Secret Societies and Subversive Movements* (1924) by Nesta H. Webster is one of the
foundational texts of modern conspiracy theory. Building explicitly on John Robison and the
Abbé Barruel, Webster argues that a single continuous "secret force of revolution" has operated
through secret societies from antiquity to the present — channelling occultism, Cabalism, the
Templars, Freemasonry (especially the Grand Orient), and the Bavarian Illuminati into the French
Revolution and onward to 19th-century revolutions and Bolshevism. The book is also openly
**antisemitic**: it devotes a chapter to "The Jewish Cabalists" and weaves an alleged Jewish role
through the whole narrative; Webster frames this as "impartial" inquiry while equating
anti-Bolshevism with the charge of "anti-Semitism."

These are Webster's assertions, recorded here strictly as she frames them — flagged, attributed,
and neither sanitized nor amplified, and without external rebuttal. The text is a primary
influence on the later Illuminati/NWO literature in this wiki (Marrs, Coleman, Sutton). (This
widely circulated edition carries a modern preface by Jordan Maxwell.)

## Structure and Chapter Summaries

The book is in ten chapters tracing one continuous thesis from antiquity to 1924.

**I. The Ancient Secret Tradition** — Locates the origin of secret societies in the East (Egypt,
Babylon, Persia) and posits a "double line": associations pursuing *esoteric knowledge* and those
using secrecy for *political* ends. Surveys the Mysteries, Pythagoras, and — in heavily
antisemitic terms — the Jewish Cabala and Talmud, then argues that the Gnostics and Manicheans
*perverted* early Christianity by mingling it with the "perverted Cabala." (Sensitive; flagged.)

**II. The Revolt Against Islam** — The book's key structural chapter. Traces the Ismaili/Fatimite
"secret-society method" — Abdullah ibn Maymun's graded system in which initiates of the lower
degrees are taught one thing and higher initiates the reverse, so that "a multitude of men of
diverse beliefs were all working together for an object known only to a few of them." Culminates
in Hasan Saba's **Assassins** (Hashishiyn), Webster's prototype of organized murder through
fanaticism. She states this Eastern method became the model Weishaupt copied. See
[[The secret-society method uses graded initiation to make dupes serve a hidden aim (Webster)]]
and [[The Assassins were the prototype of organized murder through fanaticism (Webster)]].

**III. The Templars** — Recounts the 1307–1314 suppression and the confessions (denial of Christ,
spitting on the cross, worship of a bearded idol/"Baphomet," unnatural vice), arguing the Order
had absorbed Eastern/Assassin heresy. See
[[The Templars were charged with idol-worship and denial of Christ (Webster)]] and the existing
[[The Templars transmitted subversive doctrine into Freemasonry (Webster)]].

**IV. Three Centuries of Occultism** — Carries the "Dualist" heresy forward through the Cathari and
Albigenses, then the rise of Satanism/black magic, presenting a continuous occult-subversive
current from the medieval heretics to the seventeenth-century Libertines and the Illuminati.

**V. Origins of Freemasonry** — Notes that Masonry has *no agreed origin* (the Royal Masonic
Cyclopædia lists twelve theories) and argues a genuine esoteric/occult tradition was grafted onto
the operative masons' guilds, providing the vehicle later exploited for subversion.

**VI. The Grand Lodge Era** — Covers the organization of speculative Freemasonry from 1717 and the
proliferation of the "higher degrees," through which (Webster argues) Continental Masonry diverged
from its English form toward political ends.

**VII. German Templarism and French Illuminism** — The doctrine of the **"Unknown Superiors"**:
the Baron von Hundt's *Stricte Observance* and other orders were openly led by figureheads but
secretly "directed from a hidden centre" by concealed chiefs whose identity initiates were sworn
not to reveal — Webster's template for hidden direction of visible orders. See
[[Hidden "Unknown Superiors" secretly direct the visible secret societies (Webster)]].

**VIII. The Jewish Cabalists** — The book's explicitly antisemitic chapter, alleging a Jewish role
running through the secret-society tradition. (Sensitive; recorded only via the single flagged
claim above, not reproduced.)

**IX. The Bavarian Illuminati** — Argues Weishaupt did *not* originate Illuminism but imported the
Eastern graded-deception method (via "Kölmer," founded on Manichæism), modelling his "Insinuating
Brothers" on Abdullah ibn Maymun; that Weishaupt may himself have been the agent of an inner group
(the secret chapter of the Lodge Theodore, with Mirabeau as a co-founder); that the Order concealed
itself *within* Freemasonry as its "nursery garden"; and that its true aim — per Henri Martin — was
"the abolition of property, social authority, of nationality," masked as social reform. See
[[Illuminism concealed itself within Freemasonry as a hidden inner circle (Webster)]].

**X. The Climax** — Argues "illuminized Freemasonry made the French Revolution" (the Lodge of the
Amis Réunis as the convergence point; only ~27 real initiates, the rest dupes); that Illuminism is
"less an Order than a principle" that survived its 1786 suppression by working "under cover of
something else"; and that the Mirabeau "Projet de Révolution" is the first of a series of
"Protocols" recurring through to "the Protocols of the Elders of Zion, and the Russian Bolsheviks."
See [[Illuminism survived suppression as a hidden principle working under cover (Webster)]].

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Webster presents herself as a careful documentary historian — claiming to rely "principally on the
writings of the Jews themselves" and on secret-society members' own admissions, and to indicate
"the rôle of the Jews only where it is to be clearly detected." She concedes (Ch. VIII) that "the
revolutionary spirit has always existed independently of the Jews," and (Ch. IX) even defends the
**Jesuits** against the charge of being behind the Illuminati, arguing they were Weishaupt's chief
opponents. These self-exonerating caveats sit alongside, and do not blunt, the work's sustained
antisemitic thesis; the wiki records the caveats as the source's own framing, not as endorsement.
