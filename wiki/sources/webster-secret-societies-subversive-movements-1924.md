## Source Description

*Secret Societies and Subversive Movements* (1924) by Nesta H. Webster is one of the foundational texts of modern conspiracy theory. Building explicitly on John Robison and the Abbé Barruel, Webster argues that a single continuous "secret force of revolution" has operated through secret societies from antiquity to the present — channelling occultism, Cabalism, the Templars, Freemasonry (especially the Grand Orient), and the Bavarian Illuminati into the French Revolution and onward to 19th-century revolutions and Bolshevism. The book is also openly **antisemitic**: it devotes a chapter to "The Jewish Cabalists" and weaves an alleged Jewish role through the whole narrative; Webster frames this as "impartial" inquiry while equating anti-Bolshevism with the charge of "anti-Semitism."

These are Webster's assertions, recorded here strictly as she frames them — flagged, attributed, and neither sanitized nor amplified, and without external rebuttal. The text is a primary influence on the later Illuminati/NWO literature in this wiki (Marrs, Coleman). (This widely circulated edition carries a modern preface by Jordan Maxwell.)

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Webster presents herself as a careful documentary historian — claiming to rely "principally on the writings of the Jews themselves" and on secret-society members' own admissions, and to indicate "the rôle of the Jews only where it is to be clearly detected, but not seeking to implicate them where good evidence is not forthcoming." She even concedes (Ch. VIII) that "the revolutionary spirit has always existed independently of the Jews" and that it would be "absurd" to ascribe early revolts to them. These self-exonerating caveats sit alongside, and do not blunt, the work's sustained antisemitic thesis; the wiki records the caveats as the source's own framing, not as endorsement.
