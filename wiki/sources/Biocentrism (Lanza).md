## Source Description

*Biocentrism* (2009) by physician–scientist **[[Robert Lanza]]** with astronomer Bob Berman argues that **life and consciousness are not products of the universe but its ground** — that biology, not physics, is the true "theory of everything." Lanza presents a set of **principles of biocentrism**: that what we call reality involves a consciousness; that external and internal perceptions are intertwined; that the behavior of particles is inextricably linked to the presence of an **observer**; and that without consciousness, "matter" dwells in an undetermined probability state.

From the quantum measurement problem (the double-slit and delayed-choice experiments — "the most amazing experiment"), Lanza argues the **observer brings reality into being**; that **space and time are not external things but tools/forms of animal perception**; that the universe's apparent **fine-tuning** for life is explained once the observer is recognized as central; and that **death is an illusion**, since consciousness is not bound to a single body or moment in linear time. The book is the physics/cosmology pillar of the wiki's **post-materialist science** cluster ([[Post-materialist science]]).

## Structure

Introduction + ~20 short chapters building the seven principles of biocentrism (e.g. "Muddy Universe," "The Sound of a Falling Tree," "Bubbles in Time," "When Tomorrow Comes Before Yesterday," "The Most Amazing Experiment," "No Time to Lose," "Falling in Love with Spinoza"), moving from the quantum observer effect through space, time, fine-tuning, and the implications for death and consciousness.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Lanza presents biocentrism as an interpretation that resolves standing puzzles (the observer effect, fine-tuning) more economically than materialism, while conceding it is a philosophical reframing not yet a predictive physical theory; mainstream physicists dispute that the observer effect requires consciousness and reject the cosmological claims.
