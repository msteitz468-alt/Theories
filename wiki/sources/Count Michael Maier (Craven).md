## Source Description

"Count Michael Maier, Doctor, Alchemist, Rosicrucian, Mystic: Life and Writings" by the Rev. J. B. Craven, D.D., Rector of St Olaf's Church, Kirkwall, first published 1910 (reprinted by Dawsons of Pall Mall, 1968). It is a biographical and bibliographical study of the German alchemist and Rosicrucian apologist Michael Maier (1568–1622), giving a Life, a list of references to Maier in other works, a lengthy Introduction on the nature and history of alchemy and Hermetic philosophy, and detailed descriptions (with summaries and extended quotation) of Maier's individual works in turn, in the order in which they were composed and published.

## What the Source Contributes

The full ingest covers the "Life of Maier," "References to Maier and his Works," the "Introduction," and Craven's treatment of Maier's works in order: "Arcana Arcanissima," "De Circulo Physico, Quadrato," "Lusus Serius," "Examen Fucorum Pseudo-Chymicorum," "Jocus Severus," "Silentium Post Clamores," "Symbola Aureae Mensae," "Atalanta Fugiens," "Tripus Aureus," "Themis Aurea," "Viatorium," "Verum Inventum," "Tractatus de Volucri Arborea," "Septimana Philosophica," "Civitas Corporis Humani," "Cantilenae Intellectuales de Phoenice Redivivo," and the posthumous 1624 collection ("Ulysses," "Colloquium Rhodostauroticum," "Echo Colloquii Rhodo-Staurotici," Diaz's "Christianae Religionis Summa," "Scholasterium Christianum"), plus the appendix of contributory works Craven had not seen. It supplies biographical claims about Maier's birth, career at the court of Rudolf II, friendship with Robert Fludd and other English physicians, association with the Rosicrucian movement, and death at Magdeburg; and doctrinal/allegorical claims drawn from six of his works, including the alchemical reading of Egyptian and Greek myth, the Labours of Hercules, the Trojan War, the medicinal virtue of gold, a satirical mock-parliament of creatures, a warning against false alchemists, a bird tribunal, and a formal defense of Rosicrucian secrecy.

## Chapter Summaries

### Life of Maier
Craven's biographical account: Maier's birth at Rendsburg, Holstein, c. 1568; his medical graduation (probably at Rostock); his rise at the court of Emperor Rudolf II in Prague as body physician, Count Palatine, and private secretary; a lengthy digression on Rudolf II's own biography, patronage of science (Tycho Brahe, Kepler), estrangement from his family, abdication to Matthias, and death in 1612; Maier's post-1612 visit to England and friendships with Robert Fludd, Sir William Paddy, Sir Thomas Smith, and Francis Anthony; the tradition that Maier admitted Fludd to the Rosicrucian order and instigated Fludd's Rosicrucian apologetics; Maier's later career as physician to the Landgrave Moritz of Hesse (1619) and settlement at Magdeburg; his death there in 1622 and the subsequent destruction of Magdeburg's records in 1631; his Lutheran piety; and Craven's comparative assessment of Maier against Robert Fludd and Henry Khunrath.

### References to Maier and his Works

### Introduction
Craven surveys the historiography and reputation of alchemy generally: the popular caricature of the alchemist as a foolish gold-seeker; the eighteenth-century decline and nineteenth-century revival of interest; the two "epoch-making" nineteenth-century works, the anonymous "A Suggestive Inquiry into the Hermetic Mystery" (1850, attributed to Rev. T. South and his sister) and General Ethan Allen Hitchcock's "Remarks upon Alchemy and the Alchemists" (1857); both arguing alchemy conceals a moral/spiritual system in which man himself is the "Vas" (vessel) and the alchemical work symbolizes the perfection of the soul. Craven quotes extensively from the Smaragdine Table of Hermes, Arnold di Villanova, Basil Valentine, Morienus, Boehme, and A. E. Waite's "The Hidden Church of the Holy Grail," developing the identification of the alchemical mystery with the Christian Eucharist, and introduces Henry Khunrath as a figure who, unlike Maier, foregrounded the spiritual reading of alchemy. This section frames Maier's own hermeneutic (discussed further under "Arcana Arcanissima") as an instance of a broader claimed tradition linking alchemy, the Eucharist, and ancient mystery religion.

### Arcana Arcanissima
Maier's first published work (c. 1614, Oppenheim), dedicated to Sir William Paddy, in six books arguing that the false gods and sacred institutions of Egypt and Greece encode a single Egyptian "golden medicine" of soul and body. Book 1 treats Egyptian hieroglyphics, Osiris, Isis, Typhon, and sacred animal cults; Book 2 treats Greek myths (the Golden Fleece, the Apples of the Hesperides) as encoding the same medicine; Book 3 treats the genealogy of the gods as chemical philosophy; Book 4 treats ancient festivals and games (the Eleusinian Mysteries, the Olympic and Pythian games) as vehicles of the hidden science; Book 5 allegorizes the Labours of Hercules; Book 6 allegorizes the Trojan Expedition and, above all, Achilles, as the "key of the whole art." The work closes by declaring that Christians have had revealed to them in Christ the true medicine once hidden in pagan allegory.

### De Circulo Physico, Quadrato
A short twelve-chapter treatise (1616, Oppenheim), dedicated to Maurice, Landgrave of Hesse, teaching that gold is the terrestrial image of the sun and the "absolute circle" of Nature, that gold rules the earth as the sun rules the heavens and the heart rules the body, and that gold carries a medicinal virtue toward which all other metals, in their imperfection, are naturally striving.

### Lusus Serius
A satirical philosophical dialogue (Latin editions 1616/1619; English translation by Robert Hegge, 1654) staging a mock parliament of representative creatures — the Calf, Sheep, Goose, Oyster, Bee, Silk-Worm, and Flax — each pleading its services to mankind before Man as judge, who ultimately crowns Mercury King of all worldly things.

### Examen Fucorum Pseudo-Chymicorum
A 1617 Frankfurt treatise dedicated to Joachim Hirschberger, warning against four types of fraudulent "pseudo-chemists" ("fuci," or drones) who prey on genuine seekers of the alchemical art, citing Geber, Albertus Magnus, Khunrath, and Cornelius Agrippa as earlier authorities on such frauds.

### Jocus Severus
A 1617 Frankfurt satirical work, composed while Maier travelled from England to Bohemia, depicting a tribunal of birds presided over by the Phoenix in which the Owl is crowned queen for her wisdom and recognized as sacred to Pallas; its dedication addresses "that order still lying hidden," referencing the Fama Fraternitatis and Confessio.

### Silentium Post Clamores
A formal apologetic treatise (1617, Frankfurt; German translation 1617; 2nd Latin edition 1622) explaining and defending the Rosicrucian Fraternity's silence in the face of the "clamores" caused by publication of the Fama and Confessio Fraternitatis. Maier traces the Fraternity's legitimacy to a claimed lineage of ancient secret philosophical colleges (Egyptian priests, Orphic and Eleusinian mysteries, the Samothracian Cabiri, the Magi, the Brahmans, the Gymnosophists, the Pythagoreans), affirms that the contents of the Fama and Confessio are true, defends the Order against charges of necromancy and superstition, and explains the five-year novitiate as training members "to govern their tongues." Craven, following Waite, notes that Maier's specific excuses for unanswered petitions are not wholly satisfactory.

### Symbola Aureae Mensae duodecim nationum (1617)

Maier's largest work (over 600 quarto pages) casts the defense of alchemy as a banquet at a "Golden Table," dedicated to Ernest, Count of Holsatia. Twelve alchemists, one representing each nation, are seated in turn to celebrate "Chemia" against an unnamed Adversary: Hermes (Egypt), Maria Hebraea, Democritus (Greece), Morien (Roman age), Avicenna (Arabia), Albertus Magnus (Germany), Arnold of Villanova (France), Thomas Aquinas (Italy), Raymund Lullius (Spain), Roger Bacon (England), Melchior Cibinensis (Hungary), and an Anonymous guest. In his preface Maier gives four reasons for the work: recording Chemia's antiquity and fame, rescuing its true authors from calumny, exposing the fallacies of its adversaries, and vindicating Chemia as Queen of the Arts. Each guest's chapter carries a motto, an emblematic woodcut, a legendary biography tracing the transmission of the art (Hermes deriving wisdom from Seth; Moses, Solomon, and Maria Hebraea as Biblical "workers"; Democritus and Apollonius among the Greeks; Avicenna among the Arabs; and a chain of medieval "congentiles" continuing through each national tradition), and closing syllogistic "Argumenta et Responsiones." The German (Albertus Magnus) chapter includes a distinct excursus, "Collegium Philosophorum Germanorum R.C.," presenting eight doubts about the Rosicrucians answered by Maier, plus "Aenigmata a IX. Musis et Apolline." The book closes with a "Dessert" recapitulation, a mystical journey through Europe, America, Asia, and Africa, and the hymn "Hermetis Regenerationis" from the Pomander.

### Atalanta Fugiens (Oppenheim, 1617/1618)

An emblem book of fifty engravings uniting three modes of apprehension — sight (figure, epigram, and discursive notes), intellect, and hearing (a musical fugue in three voices per emblem: Atalanta as "vox fugiens," Hippomenes as "vox sequens," Pomum objectum as "vox morans"). The title-page and preface frame the whole through the myth of Atalanta and Hippomenes: Atalanta is "virgo mere chymica," and the lovers' union in the temple of Mars (the vessel) and their transformation into lions signify the alchemical work reaching its red stage. Individual emblems (numbered examples include the wind-borne fruit, the eagle's nest, the egg struck by a glowing sword, the whitening of Latona, the dragon devouring its tail, the Salamander unhurt in fire, the death of Adonis reddening the rose, the Vulture's unceasing cry, and the Coral hardening in air) each present a mystic alchemical proverb pointing toward the nature of the Philosopher's Stone, culminating in the view that "Man...is the true laboratory of the Hermetic art" and that self-knowledge underlies all alchemical tradition.

### Tripus Aureus (1618)

A compilation, first printed together by Maier, of three chymical treatises: the "Practica" with Twelve Keys and appendix of Basil Valentine (a Benedictine monk of Germany, translated from German); the "Crede Mihi" or "Ordinale" of Thomas Norton (English, translated from a manuscript some 140 years old, previously called "uneditus" by Maier in the Symbola); and the "Testamentum" of Cremer, Abbot of Westminster (published here for the first time, recounting Cremer's alleged tutelage under Raymund Lullius and their supplying gold to Edward III — a story Craven notes is "more than doubtful"). The Twelve Keys of Basil Valentine include notable emblematic cuts (the sixth key depicting an alchemical marriage of Sol and Luna; the eleventh depicting Orpheus and Eurydice on lions). The Tripus Aureus was later reprinted in the Musaeum Hermeticum Reformatum and translated into English by A. E. Waite.

### Themis Aurea (1618)

Divided into twenty chapters, Themis Aurea sets out "the Laws of the Fraternity of the Rosie Cross." Opening with the myth of the goddess Themis as personified Justice and Virtue (not a literal historical figure), the work states six laws prescribed by the Fraternity's Founder: gratis medical practice, no fixed habit, an annual meeting on "the Day C," each brother choosing his own successor, use of "R.C." as seal and cognisance, and a hundred years of concealment. The Brethren are portrayed as pious, temperate, secret, and skilled in true Astronomy, Physics, Mathematics, Medicine, and Chymistry, devoted to Natural Magic and to healing without personal profit. A chapter on hieroglyphics explains that the letters "R.C." were adopted so the Brethren "may not be without Name," and though popularly read as "Roses" and "Cross," the Brethren themselves said the letters symbolically denote the name of their first Author. Craven notes the story of Christian Rosencreutz's burial, discovery, and the alleged founding dates are "treated as facts" in the work, and speculates on the meaning of the enigmatic "Book M" and place "C." The 1656 English translation is dedicated to Elias Ashmole, with an account of Ashmole's own initiation into Rosicrucian secrets by William Backhouse — Craven characterizing Maier, Fludd, Backhouse, and Ashmole as a "succession of three Rosicrucians."

### Viatorium (Oppenheim, 1618)

A treatise, "De Montibus Planetarum Septem seu Metallorum," presented as a guide ("Viatorium") for the philosopher lost in the "Labyrinth" of chymic errors, framed by Ovid's allegory of Battus turned to a touchstone and the myth of the Cretan Labyrinth and Ariadne's thread. The work opens with Tacitus's cautionary tale of Caecilius Bassus's delusive vision of buried Carthaginian gold, applied to those who pursue the work without proper method. Seven chapters follow, each devoted to a planetary "mountain" corresponding to a metal — Mercury, Saturn (Lead), Jupiter (Tin), Venus (Copper), Mars (Iron), Luna (Silver), and Sol (Gold) — with each metal's relation to gold, the Tincture, and Medicine discussed in turn. The final chapter, "De Monte Solis," treats gold as the noblest medicine, source of all chymic virtue, repeating the claim that Moses knew the secret of "aurum potabile" in dissolving the golden calf.

### Verum Inventum (1619)

A patriotic treatise arguing that Germany's true gifts to the world were won "vi animi et corporis," not by chance or drink as a critic alleged. Divided into six chapters: the earliest political state of Germany and the Imperial dignity; continued political history through the Saxons and Ottos; the nature of the Imperial prerogative; German inventions in war (gunpowder, attributed to Berthold Schwartz); German literary inventions (transmission of writing from Egypt through Phoenicia, Greece, and Rome to Germany, and Gutenberg's printing, 1440); and German theological gifts, culminating in Luther's Reformation and its critique of Roman practice. Two further sections address German contributions "in Medicina" (Paracelsus as successor to Albertus Magnus) and "in Chymia" (thirty pages), the latter explicitly defending the real existence of the Brothers of the Rosy Cross against the charge that they are "fictitious phantasms of the German brain," referring back to the proofs already offered in the Symbola Aureae Mensae. The work closes with a defense of alchemical transmutation from Scripture (Moses and the golden calf) and a patriotic eulogy of German princes, nobles, and military strength.

### Tractatus de Volucri Arborea (1619)

Craven summarizes this "Little Book of Nature's Marvels" (full title: *Tractatus de Volucri Arborea, absque Patre et Matre, in Insulis Orchadum...seu De ortu Miraculoso potius, quam naturali*), dedicated to Johannes Hardtmuthus ab Hutten. Across sixteen chapters, Maier recounts and defends the legend of the Orkney "Tree-bird" (barnacle goose), tracing its history through Giraldus Cambrensis, Hector Boece, John Gerarde, Sir Robert Moray, Olaus Wormius, and John Johnston, and arguing the bird is generated without ordinary parents through a marvelous natural process akin to putrefaction-generation elsewhere in nature. He allegorizes the bird, via the Alcyone myth, as an emblem of Christ's virgin birth and the Cross. Later chapters catalog further "wonders contrary to Nature": the Tartarian Lamb (Bonaretz), the Dragon's Blood tree, the Phoenix, green children found in England, ancient giants, the story of Tages, Incubi and Succubi, Lycanthropes, and a closing chapter "De Geniis" on Sylvani, Lamiae, satyrs, and witches, including Maier's reading of Genesis 6 as referring to the lines of Seth and Cain rather than demons. The "Conclusio Tractatus" states the work's aim: to show such wonders are wrought by God's omnipotent power as part of His divine plan.

### Septimana Philosophica (1620)

Craven summarizes this work as a philosophic "week" cast in dialogue form: Solomon, the Queen of Sheba, and Hiram, Prince of Tyre, meet at Solomon's palace in Jerusalem, and Solomon devotes six days to instruction in natural philosophy — Heaven and Elements (day 1), Meteors (day 2), fossils and things of the earth (day 3), vegetable life (day 4), animals (day 5), and man as Microcosm (day 6) — reserving the seventh as a Sabbath of rest. Each day proceeds through numbered "Ænigmata" posed and resolved among the three speakers, reaching Ænigma 319 by the sixth day. Throughout, Solomon and the Queen of Sheba repeatedly read natural objects and classical myths (the rose, Apis, the phoenix, the crocodile, the Trojan horse, Atalanta's golden apples, Osiris and Isis) as hieroglyphs of alchemical processes — the Philosopher's Stone, philosophic Sulphur and Mercury, and the tincture — and describe man's body as a Microcosm mapped onto the continents (Europe as head, Africa as heart, Asia as belly/generative organs).

### Civitas Corporis Humani (1621)

Craven summarizes this medical treatise on gout, arthritis, and related joint diseases (full title: *Civitas Corporis Immani, a tyrannide Arthritica vindicata*), dedicated to medical men, students, and gout sufferers, dated Magdeburg, August 1620. Across twelve chapters, Maier develops an extended political allegory: the human body is a "Civitas" whose organs are its citizens, governed not as a pure monarchy but on the model of the Venetian Republic, with the heart as a prince of limited power presiding over an aristocracy of organs. Gout arises as a tyranny or sedition afflicting the extremities (hands and feet) furthest from the heart, caused by failures of the liver, stomach, and other organs, compounded by gluttony, lust, and drunkenness ("Venus and Bacchus are the greatest friends of gout"). Subsequent chapters detail methods of cure — purgation, vomiting, mercurial doses, external poultices, a special double oak vase for fomentation, and forty rules of life (moderate diet, limited sleep, exercise, freedom from mental perturbation).

### Cantilenae Intellectuales de Phoenice Redivivo (1622)

Craven describes this as "one of the most curious and rare of Maier's books," a work in rhymed anacreontic verse, dedicated to Frederick, Hereditary Prince of Norway (dated Rostock, 25 August 1622), the year of Maier's death (first published Rome 1622, Rostock 1623). The work presents nine "Triads" of songs, each structured in three parts — names, allegories, and the application of the Mysteries of Art to those of Religion — using the classical Phoenix myth (its death by fire on its nest at Thebes and rebirth) as the master allegory for the alchemical Great Work. Successive triads draw in myths of Psyche and Cupid, the Roc bird, Tamerlane's heir, a basilisk slain by Socrates via mirrors, and explicit Christian parallels: Adam formed of red earth, the Incarnation, the Crucifixion and the five wounds, the Eucharist, and the Ascension, closing with praise of gold as durable and incorruptible, "compared to Divine and Eternal Things."

### Ulysses, Colloquium Rhodostauroticum, Echo Colloquii Rhodo-Staurotici, Christianae Religionis Summa (Diaz), and Scholasterium Christianum (posthumous collection, 1624)

Craven treats this 1624 Frankfurt volume as a memorial collection published after Maier's death (Magdeburg, summer 1622). The editor's preface states Maier had entrusted the short tract "Ulysses" to a friend before his death; the editor appended three further pieces translated from German into Latin. The "Ulysses" tract (subtitled on wisdom as a "heavenly spark of blessedness") holds up Ulysses as the symbol of perfect human wisdom, enumerating seven virtues — astuteness, eloquence, prudence, ingenuity, valor in war, skill in counsel, and patient endurance — supported by quotations from Ovid and the Wisdom of Solomon. The editor's preface raises, without resolving, whether Maier was ever formally received into the Rosicrucian brotherhood, while affirming his devout Christian life.

The appended *Colloquium Rhodostauroticum* stages a dialogue among five figures (Quirinus, Polydorus, Tyrosophus, Promptutus, Politicus) on the Fraternity of the Rosy Cross and its manifestos, including a list of approved theological and alchemical books, and references Maier's own "Symbolum Aureae Mensae" and "Silentium." Its sequel, the *Echo Colloquii Rhodo-Staurotici* (dated March 1622), responds to the Colloquium and contains a passage (p. 168) that, per Waite's "Real History of the Rosicrucians" as cited by Craven, has been read as implying Maier's admission to the Order shortly before his death. Appended to the Echo are the "Canones declaratorii" (Ergon et Parergon, Fr. C. R.), ten canons defining God via the Trinity and the "tetradic manifestation of the elements," followed by 120 chemical propositions allegorized through classical myth (Demogorgon, the Phoenix, Hercules and Antaeus).

The collection also includes the *Christianae Religionis Summa* by Joannes Diaz, a Reformed/Lutheran summary admitting the three creeds and four general councils but denying the Eucharistic Sacrifice (Diaz, a Spaniard who embraced Reformed views, was murdered by his own brother in 1546; the treatise was first published in French at Lyons in 1562); and the *Scholasterium Christianum*, a short ten-chapter work on time, place, eternity, the six-thousand-year age of the world, and the nature of Christ's body and of angels.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Craven notes that the tradition of Maier admitting Fludd to the Rosicrucian order "is... buried in obscurity, if not in contradiction." He also records, without fully endorsing, the charge (from Waite's "Real History of the Rosicrucians") that Maier was "wiled away from the practical path he had long followed by 'some adepts'" and "sacrificed his health, his fortune, and his time in these 'ruinous absurdities'" — a claim in tension with Craven's generally admiring portrait, and one Craven connects speculatively to the warnings in Maier's own "Examen Fucorum Pseudo-Chymicorum." Craven, following Waite, also judges Maier's defense in "Silentium Post Clamores" of the Order's failure to answer petitioners as logically unsatisfactory, since an Order transcending space and time should not have been hindered by petitioners' physical distance.
