## Source Description

*Stalking the Wild Pendulum: On the Mechanics of Consciousness* is a book by biomedical inventor and designer Itzhak Bentov, first published in 1977 by E.P. Dutton and reissued in 1988 by Destiny Books (ISBN 9780892812028). The work presents a scientific-philosophical model that bridges vibration, quantum physics, and human consciousness. 

Bentov argues that all matter, including the physical body, is made up of electromagnetic fields vibrating at high frequencies, oscillating like a pendulum. At the extreme endpoints of these oscillations (the "null point" or "void"), matter collapses out of physical reality, achieving infinite velocity and nonlocality. Bentov develops a closed-loop toroidal model of the universe and describes how meditation and physiological resonance allow the mind to access this universal holographic database.

## Chapter Summaries

### Preface & Introduction
Bentov outlines his purpose: to build a simple, conceptual model that demystifies altered states of consciousness, out-of-body travel, and parapsychological phenomena. He emphasizes that the book is a synthesis of living-room discussions and should be read as a conceptual model rather than dogmatic science.

### Chapters 1–2: Sound, Waves and Vibration & A Look Through a Supermicroscope
Bentov establishes the physical foundation of his model. He explains how waves behave, detailing interference patterns, beat frequencies, and coherent light (lasers) as the basis of the hologram. He then analyzes matter at a microscopic scale, showing that what we perceive as solid matter is actually empty space (vacuum) permeated by pulsating, vibrating electromagnetic fields.

### Chapters 3–4: A Morse Code of Action and Rest & An Experiment with Time
Explores the oscillation of matter. Bentov details that atoms do not move continuously, but oscillate like a pendulum. At the turning points of this vibration, they halt temporarily, reaching a "null point" where their velocity becomes infinite, allowing them to blink off and on. He maps this to consciousness, explaining that in altered states (meditation, dreaming, hypnosis), subjective time dilates relative to objective clock time, allowing the psyche (observer) to expand and navigate non-physical space-time.

### Chapters 5–7: Quantity and Quality of Consciousness, Relative Realities, & The Parable of the Bicycle
Bentov defines consciousness as the capacity of a system to respond to stimuli. He measures the "quantity" of consciousness by the frequency range and complexity of its electromagnetic emissions, and its "quality" by the ratio of subjective to objective time. He classifies different kingdoms of consciousness—mineral, plant, animal, human, and spiritual—as developmental bands within a single cosmic spectrum.

### Chapters 8–10: A Model of the Universe, The "How To" of Intuitive Knowledge, & Some Reflections on the Creator
Outlines the toroidal model of the universe. The cosmos is modeled as a closed loop shaped like a self-reflecting torus (or smoke ring) that constantly turns itself inside out. Matter flows out of a central core and is recycled back into it. Intuitive knowledge is accessed when the mind is quiet, allowing the individual to resonate with the universal holographic database. The Creator is defined as the absolute void or source of intelligence that upholds this structure.

### Epilogue & Appendix: Stresses and the Body
Bentov concludes by tracing the return of individual consciousness to the Creator. In the Appendix, he details the physiological basis of meditation (kundalini syndrome). The micro-motion of the heart's pumping produces a standing wave resonance of approximately 7 Hz in the body. This resonance entrains the brain's sensory-motor cortex, stimulating a circular current that activates pleasure centers and locks the body into phase with the Earth's electrostatic Schumann resonance.
