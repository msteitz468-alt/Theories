## Source Description

*Religion and the Decline of Magic: Studies in Popular Beliefs in Sixteenth- and Seventeenth-Century England* (1971) by **[[Keith Thomas (b. 1933)|Keith Thomas]]** is a landmark work of social history examining the systems of belief — the magic of the medieval Church, Protestant providence, astrology, witchcraft, magical healing, divination, ancient prophecies, ghosts and fairies — that were current in early modern England but that "no longer enjoy much recognition today." Drawing heavily on the studies made by modern social anthropologists of similar beliefs in Africa and elsewhere, Thomas explains why these beliefs were taken seriously by intelligent people and traces their decline in prestige across the period from the Reformation to the dawn of the Enlightenment.

The book's central argument is that these varied beliefs shared a common function — offering explanations for misfortune and a means of redress at times of adversity — and so bore a close relationship to the religious ideas of the period, sometimes parasitic upon Christian teaching and sometimes in sharp rivalry to it. Its organizing thesis is captured on the concept page [[The decline of magic (Thomas thesis)]].

## Strict-extraction note

Thomas is a historian writing a scholarly analysis. His arguments and interpretations are recorded as **what he argues / documents**, attributed to him and **not adjudicated**. Where he reports the beliefs of early modern people, the claims record what Thomas says those people believed, not endorsements of the beliefs themselves.

## Structure & scope

The book is organized into a Prologue and eight thematic groups of chapters:

- **PROLOGUE** — 1. The Environment
- **RELIGION** — 2. The Magic of the Medieval Church · 3. The Impact of the Reformation · 4. Providence · 5. Prayer and Prophecy · 6. Religion and the People
- **MAGIC** — 7. Magical Healing · 8. Cunning Men and Popular Magic · 9. Magic and Religion
- **ASTROLOGY** — 10. Astrology: its Practice and Extent · 11. Astrology: its Social and Intellectual Role · 12. Astrology and Religion
- **THE APPEAL TO THE PAST** — 13. Ancient Prophecies
- **WITCHCRAFT** — 14. Witchcraft in England: the Crime and its History · 15. Witchcraft and Religion · 16. The Making of a Witch · 17. Witchcraft and its Social Environment · 18. Witchcraft: Decline
- **ALLIED BELIEFS** — 19. Ghosts and Fairies · 20. Times and Omens
- **CONCLUSION** — 21. Some Interconnections · 22. The Decline of Magic

## Chapter Summaries

### PROLOGUE

**Ch. 1 — The Environment.** The material conditions of Tudor and Stuart England — endemic disease, fire, plague, dearth, and premature death, against feeble medical and technical resources — created a pervasive sense of insecurity that made systems for explaining and coping with misfortune indispensable.
- [[Thomas argues Tudor and Stuart England's insecure environment shaped its preoccupation with explaining misfortune]]
- [[Thomas documents that orthodox Galenic medicine offered little effective help against most contemporary disease]]
- [[Thomas argues fire and plague were uncontrollable, capricious threats that heightened the era's sense of insecurity]]

### RELIGION

**Ch. 2 — The Magic of the Medieval Church.** The medieval Church offered a reservoir of supernatural power for everyday earthly needs — saints as specialist wonder-workers, consecrated objects as talismans, the Host as detachable potency — blurring its own theoretical line between prayer and spell.
- [[Thomas argues the medieval Church functioned as a reservoir of magical power for practical earthly problems]]
- [[Thomas documents that saints were venerated as specialist wonder-workers assigned to particular afflictions and trades]]
- [[Thomas argues holy water, the agnus dei, and other consecrated objects functioned as popular protective talismans]]
- [[Thomas argues the consecrated Host was popularly believed to carry a detachable magical potency]]
- [[Thomas argues the medieval Church blurred the theoretical distinction between prayer and magical spell]]

**Ch. 3 — The Impact of the Reformation.** Protestant reformers first drew the sharp theoretical line between prayer and spell, branded Catholic ritual as sorcery, and stripped away ecclesiastical magic — yet Catholic magical practice survived widely, and profanation expressed popular rejection.
- [[Thomas argues Protestant reformers first articulated the sharp theoretical distinction between prayer and spell]]
- [[Thomas documents that Protestant polemicists repeatedly branded Catholic ritual as witchcraft and sorcery]]
- [[Thomas argues Hobbes systematized the Protestant case that Catholic consecration was mere conjuration]]
- [[Thomas documents that Puritans stripped the Rogation-week perambulation of its magical fertility associations]]
- [[Thomas documents extensive Catholic magical survivals persisting in England after the Reformation]]
- [[Thomas documents that ordinary people expressed rejection of Catholic ritual through deliberate profanation of holy objects]]

**Ch. 4 — Providence.** Post-Reformation theology denied chance, tracing every event to God's providence — a self-confirming, unfalsifiable scheme expressed in judgement-catalogues and in the tradition of a curse on buyers of monastic lands.
- [[Thomas argues post-Reformation theology denied the very existence of chance or fortune]]
- [[Thomas argues the doctrine of providence was psychologically self-confirming and unfalsifiable]]
- [[Thomas documents that clergy and laity routinely traced local disasters to specific communal sins]]
- [[Thomas argues belief in providential prodigies and portents was compatible with contemporary science]]
- [[Thomas documents a genre of published Theatres and Mirrors systematically cataloguing God's judgements]]
- [[Thomas argues a widespread tradition held that a divine curse fell upon purchasers of dissolved monastic lands]]

**Ch. 5 — Prayer and Prophecy.** Petitionary prayer worked as a self-confirming system; sortilege, sectarian miracle-working, and prophetic enthusiasm (the Hacket affair, millenarianism) discharged latent social functions, and prophecy opened rare public space to women.
- [[Thomas argues that petitionary prayer functioned as a self-confirming system of belief]]
- [[Thomas documents that early modern people used Bible sortilege and lots to seek divine guidance]]
- [[Thomas argues that the Civil War sects revived the miracle-working tradition of medieval Catholicism]]
- [[Thomas documents the Hacket, Copinger and Arthington affair as a prototype of Civil War prophetic enthusiasm]]
- [[Thomas argues that religious prophecy gave women one of the few channels to public expression]]
- [[Thomas argues that millenarian prophecy served as a validating myth for radical political programmes]]
- [[Thomas argues that public prayer and fasting discharged a latent social function beyond their stated purpose]]

**Ch. 6 — Religion and the People.** The Anglican Church was a total social institution, but the loss of the confessional, pervasive religious ignorance, poor attendance, and latent scepticism left gaps that rival systems of belief filled.
- [[Thomas argues that the Anglican Church functioned as a total social institution coextensive with society itself]]
- [[Thomas argues that the abolition of the Catholic confessional left a gap that Protestant clerical counselling never fully filled]]
- [[Thomas documents pervasive religious ignorance among the English poor]]
- [[Thomas documents that actual church attendance fell far short of the legal ideal]]
- [[Thomas argues that religious scepticism and practical atheism were widespread beneath the surface of nominal conformity]]
- [[Thomas argues that gaps in institutional religion opened space for cunning folk and rival systems of belief]]

### MAGIC

**Ch. 7 — Magical Healing.** Village charms, sympathetic techniques, the royal touch, and celebrated healers like Greatrakes offered cures whose efficacy Thomas locates in psychological and social mechanisms rather than the supernatural.
- [[Thomas documents the healing charms and techniques used by village cunning folk]]
- [[Thomas argues that girdle-measuring and similar techniques reflect the ancient logic of sympathetic magic]]
- [[Thomas documents the royal touch as the official ceremony for curing the King's Evil]]
- [[Thomas documents unofficial healers, especially seventh sons, who imitated the royal touch]]
- [[Thomas documents Valentine Greatrakes as the most celebrated occult healer of the seventeenth century]]
- [[Thomas argues that the efficacy of magical healing rested on psychological and social rather than supernatural mechanisms]]

**Ch. 8 — Cunning Men and Popular Magic.** Cunning folk provided thief-detection (a psychological ordeal), standard divinatory techniques, and deterrence through fear — practices Thomas compares to African divination and traces to Neoplatonic legitimation.
- [[Thomas argues that magical thief-detection functioned as a psychological ordeal confirming existing suspicion]]
- [[Thomas documents the sieve-and-shears, key-and-book, and other standard techniques of English popular divination]]
- [[Thomas argues that English cunning men's divinatory practice paralleled that of modern African diviners]]
- [[Thomas argues that Renaissance Neoplatonism gave learned intellectual legitimacy to magical practice]]
- [[Thomas argues that cunning men deterred theft through fear as much as through actual detection]]

**Ch. 9 — Magic and Religion.** The Church's magic/religion boundary was self-serving; cunning folk rivalled clergy; Protestant and Catholic hostility to magic differed little in substance; learned magi (Dee, Fludd) and even parish clergy practised magic devoutly.
- [[Thomas argues that the Church condemned lay magic while reserving supernatural legitimacy for its own rituals]]
- [[Thomas documents that ecclesiastical courts punished cunning folk only lightly compared to convicted witches]]
- [[Thomas argues that the cunning man rivaled the parish clergyman as a source of practical help and comfort]]
- [[Thomas argues that Protestant and Catholic hostility to popular magic did not differ sharply in substance]]
- [[Thomas documents that learned magicians such as John Dee and Robert Fludd pursued magic as a devout religious quest]]
- [[Thomas documents that parish clergy themselves frequently practiced or tolerated magic]]
- [[Thomas argues that religion could defeat magic only by promoting self-help and labour, not by outcompeting it with counter-magic]]

### ASTROLOGY

**Ch. 10 — Astrology: its Practice and Extent.** Astrology was woven into the educated worldview, with four branches of judicial practice, elite patronage, mass-circulation almanacs, and a huge and socially wide consulting practice serving mainly practical queries.
- [[Thomas argues astrology was integrated into the educated worldview of early modern England]]
- [[Thomas documents four branches of judicial astrology practised in early modern England]]
- [[Thomas documents extensive patronage of astrologers by Tudor and Stuart elites]]
- [[Thomas documents the mass circulation of English astrological almanacs]]
- [[Thomas documents the huge volume and social range of astrologers' consulting practices]]
- [[Thomas documents that clients sought astrologers chiefly for lost goods, missing persons, and personal decisions]]

**Ch. 11 — Astrology: its Social and Intellectual Role.** Astrology filled an intellectual vacuum before the social sciences, relieved personal guilt, defended itself against falsification, exerted self-fulfilling political influence — and lost scientific standing to the astronomical revolution, not to exposure of fraud.
- [[Thomas argues astrology filled an intellectual vacuum before the rise of the social sciences]]
- [[Thomas argues astrology relieved individuals of guilt for their own misfortunes]]
- [[Thomas documents astrology's built-in defences against falsification]]
- [[Thomas documents astrology's self-fulfilling political influence during the Civil War]]
- [[Thomas argues the astronomical revolution rather than exposed fraud destroyed astrology's scientific standing]]

**Ch. 12 — Astrology and Religion.** Theological opposition targeted astral determinism (Puritans most fiercely, given its proximity to predestination); astrology and clergy were rival pastoral agencies, yet astrology allied with radical sects and some clergy practised it.
- [[Thomas argues theological opposition to astrology centred on astral determinism, not pagan origins]]
- [[Thomas argues Puritans opposed astrology most fiercely because of its doctrinal proximity to predestination]]
- [[Thomas argues astrology and the clergy were rival pastoral agencies]]
- [[Thomas documents astrology's unexpected alliance with radical Protestant sects during the Interregnum]]
- [[Thomas documents clergy who personally practised astrology despite official condemnation]]

### THE APPEAL TO THE PAST

**Ch. 13 — Ancient Prophecies.** Deliberately obscure and infinitely adaptable, ancient prophecies were a recurring tool of rebellion (peaking in the Civil War, fused with astrology by Lilly), repeatedly criminalized, and retroactively reinterpreted to fit outcomes.
- [[Thomas characterizes ancient prophecy as a deliberately obscure and infinitely adaptable genre]]
- [[Thomas documents ancient prophecies as a recurring tool of rebellion from the Tudors through the Civil War]]
- [[Thomas documents repeated government criminalization of prophecy as evidence of its perceived political danger]]
- [[Thomas documents the Civil War as the peak of ancient prophecy publishing, fused with astrology by William Lilly]]
- [[Thomas argues ancient prophecies were retroactively reinterpreted to fit whatever political outcome occurred]]

### WITCHCRAFT

**Ch. 14 — Witchcraft in England: the Crime and its History.** English witchcraft centred on maleficium (harm), not the continental sabbath/pact; statutes graded punishment by harm; roughly 1,000 were executed (1560–1680); Thomas rejects both the "imposed from above" and fiscal-motive explanations.
- [[Thomas argues that English witch-belief distinguished maleficium from the continental doctrine of devil-worship]]
- [[Thomas documents that the sabbath and diabolical pact were rare in English witch-trials]]
- [[Thomas argues the three witchcraft statutes graded punishment by actual harm done rather than proof of a diabolical pact]]
- [[Thomas estimates roughly 1,000 executions for witchcraft in England concentrated between 1560 and 1680]]
- [[Thomas rejects Robbins's thesis that witch persecution was imposed on the people from above]]
- [[Thomas argues English witch prosecutions lacked the fiscal motives that drove continental witch-hunting]]

**Ch. 15 — Witchcraft and Religion.** The Reformation intensified belief in an immanent Devil while removing protective magic; the Darrell controversy dramatized Puritan exorcism; the Reformation, not the statutes, set the timing of persecution, which was not simply Puritan-driven.
- [[Thomas argues Reformation theology intensified belief in an immanent, personal Devil]]
- [[Thomas documents the Darrell controversy as a struggle over Puritan exorcism by fasting and prayer]]
- [[Thomas argues Protestantism stripped away protective ecclesiastical magic while retaining fear of the Devil]]
- [[Thomas argues the Reformation, not the witchcraft statutes, explains why English persecution began when it did]]
- [[Thomas argues witch-prosecution was not primarily driven by Puritan religious zeal]]

**Ch. 16 — The Making of a Witch.** Belief in effective cursing by the wronged poor, unreliable pact/familiar confessions mirroring poverty and despair, and overwhelmingly poor female defendants — set against Thomas's rejection of Margaret Murray's witch-cult thesis and against village pressures to conform.
- [[Thomas argues cursing by the poor and injured was widely believed to be genuinely effective]]
- [[Thomas rejects Margaret Murray's witch-cult thesis as unsupported by the English trial evidence]]
- [[Thomas argues confessions of pact and familiars are largely unreliable evidence]]
- [[Thomas argues the Devil's temptations to witchcraft mirrored the poverty and despair of the accused]]
- [[Thomas documents that accused witches were overwhelmingly poor women]]
- [[Thomas argues village life enforced conformity through intense communal surveillance and shaming]]

**Ch. 17 — Witchcraft and its Social Environment.** Witch-belief filled medical explanatory gaps; accusation required prior hostility and typically followed a refused request for charity; large-scale disasters were rarely blamed on witches; the belief upheld charity norms as manorial relief eroded.
- [[Thomas argues witch-beliefs filled explanatory gaps left by inadequate medicine]]
- [[Thomas argues witchcraft accusation required a pre-existing relationship of presumed hostility]]
- [[Thomas argues most witch accusations followed a refusal of charity to a poor neighbor]]
- [[Thomas argues large-scale disasters were rarely blamed on witches because they lacked a personal target]]
- [[Thomas argues witch-beliefs functioned as a conservative force upholding norms of charity]]
- [[Thomas argues the erosion of manorial poor relief intensified the tensions behind witch accusations]]

**Ch. 18 — Witchcraft: Decline.** Prosecution declined from evidentiary difficulty rather than disbelief; the mechanical philosophy withdrew intellectual support; the institutional Poor Law dissolved the guilt feeding accusations; Reginald Scot supplied the sceptical typology; popular belief persisted after 1736.
- [[Thomas argues the decline of witch prosecution reflected difficulty of proof, not disbelief in witches]]
- [[Thomas argues mechanical philosophy undermined intellectual support for witch-belief]]
- [[Thomas argues the institutionalized Poor Law dissolved the guilt that fed witch accusations]]
- [[Thomas documents Reginald Scot's fourfold typology of witches as the foundational sceptical position]]
- [[Thomas argues popular belief in witchcraft persisted long after formal prosecution ended]]

### ALLIED BELIEFS

**Ch. 19 — Ghosts and Fairies.** The Reformation transformed ghost-theology and re-classed fairies as demonic; ghost- and fairy-beliefs enforced moral and social norms, were exploited by cunning folk, and were self-confirming, declining as deference to the dead waned.
- [[Thomas argues that the Reformation transformed the theology of ghosts]]
- [[Thomas documents that ghost stories served to enforce social and moral norms]]
- [[Thomas argues that ghost-beliefs declined as society ceased to defer to the wishes of the dead]]
- [[Thomas documents that fairy belief functioned as a sanction for domestic tidiness and moral conduct]]
- [[Thomas documents that cunning folk and wizards exploited belief in ghosts and fairies]]
- [[Thomas argues that fairies were reclassified as demonic after the Reformation]]
- [[Thomas documents the self-confirming and elusive character of fairy belief]]
- [[Thomas documents the connection between ghost belief and witchcraft accusation]]

**Ch. 20 — Times and Omens.** Lucky/unlucky days reflected an agrarian experience of uneven time; Church-calendar taboos persisted while Puritan sabbatarianism introduced "even" time; omens proliferated — and Thomas weighs three explanations of superstition, finding each insufficient.
- [[Thomas argues that belief in lucky and unlucky days reflects an agrarian experience of uneven time]]
- [[Thomas documents the persistence of Church-calendar time-taboos after the Reformation]]
- [[Thomas argues that Puritan sabbatarianism introduced a modern conception of even time]]
- [[Thomas documents the proliferation of omens and prohibitions governing everyday conduct]]
- [[Thomas evaluates three explanatory approaches to superstition and finds each insufficient]]

### CONCLUSION

**Ch. 21 — Some Interconnections.** Magic, astrology, and witchcraft formed one interlocking system; magic and religion performed parallel functions sharing an assumption of guilt; clergy treated cunning folk and astrologers as rivals; religion outlived magic through comprehensiveness and institutions — and was itself purged of magic.
- [[Thomas argues that magic astrology and witchcraft formed a single interlocking system of belief]]
- [[Thomas argues that magic and religion performed parallel but distinct social functions]]
- [[Thomas argues that misfortune explanations across magic and religion shared an assumption of guilt]]
- [[Thomas argues that the clergy viewed cunning folk and astrologers as rivals for pastoral authority]]
- [[Thomas argues that religion outlived magic because of its comprehensiveness and institutional Church]]
- [[Thomas argues that the religion which survived the decline of magic was itself purged of magical elements]]

**Ch. 22 — The Decline of Magic.** Magic's belief-systems were self-confirming; the mechanical philosophy destroyed their intellectual basis; crucially, intellectual rejection *preceded* technological replacement (insurance, fire-fighting, probability). Thomas rejects the agrarian/urban equation and stresses a change in mental aspiration, while noting magic's popular survival.
- [[Thomas argues that magical belief systems were self-confirming and immune to disconfirmation]]
- [[Thomas argues that the mechanical philosophy of the scientific revolution destroyed the intellectual basis of magic]]
- [[Thomas argues that intellectual rejection of magic preceded technological replacement]]
- [[Thomas documents the rise of insurance fire-fighting and advertising as institutional substitutes for magic]]
- [[Thomas argues that probability theory supplanted mystical explanations of chance misfortune]]
- [[Thomas rejects the equation of magic with agrarian life and rationalism with urban life]]
- [[Thomas argues that the decline of magic was primarily a change in mental aspiration not technical achievement]]
- [[Thomas argues that magic and quasi-magical belief survived popularly long after elite abandonment]]
