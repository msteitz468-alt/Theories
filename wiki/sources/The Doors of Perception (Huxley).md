## Source Description

*The Doors of Perception* (1954) is **[[Aldous Huxley]]**'s short, first-person account of taking **mescaline** in May 1953 under Humphry Osmond's supervision, and his reflections on what the experience reveals about mind and reality. Huxley reports that under the drug ordinary objects blazed with significance and "**Istigkeit**" (is-ness), that he perceived "what Adam had seen on the morning of his creation," and that this implies the brain and nervous system act as a **"reducing valve"** filtering a vastly larger field he calls **"Mind at Large"** down to the trickle needed for biological survival. He frames psychedelics (and other "doors") as ways the valve is bypassed, connecting the visionary experience to art, religion, and the "antipodes of the mind."

The essay is the experiential companion to Huxley's [[The perennial philosophy|Perennial Philosophy]]; its title comes from William Blake ("If the doors of perception were cleansed every thing would appear to man as it is, Infinite").

## Structure

A single continuous essay (no chapters): the mescaline session and its perceptual revelations, interwoven with Huxley's "reducing valve" theory of mind, reflections on visionary art and religion, and the limits and value of the experience.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Huxley is candid that the mescaline state, for all its beatific vision, left him indifferent to human relations and practical action — he weighs its contemplative value against this withdrawal, and does not claim the chemical shortcut is equivalent to earned mystical realization.
