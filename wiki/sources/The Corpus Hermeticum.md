## Source Description

The **Corpus Hermeticum** is the core collection of the Greek *Hermetica* — philosophical-religious
treatises (1st–3rd c. AD) presented as revelations of **[[Hermes Trismegistus]]** to disciples (Tat,
Asclepius, Ammon) — together with the Latin **Asclepius**. This ingest uses Brian Copenhaver's
standard 1992 translation. It is the **primary source text** of the Hermetic tradition that the wiki
otherwise meets only at second hand: the *[[The Kybalion (Three Initiates, 1908)]]* claims to expound
its doctrine, *[[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]]* makes it the hidden root of modern
science, and Hall and Blavatsky fold it into their "wisdom-religion." See the umbrella
[[The Hermetica: Egyptian-Greek revelations of Hermes Trismegistus]].

Its central treatise, **Poimandres (C.H. I)**, gives the Hermetic cosmogony (the divine **Mind/Nous**;
Light and Life; the Word; the heavenly **Man** made in God's image who descends into Nature) and the
**ascent of the soul** through the seven planetary zones to "be made god." Other treatises teach that
**God is Mind**, that **knowledge (gnosis) divinizes**, and (C.H. XIII) the **rebirth** that expels the
twelve torments. The **Asclepius** declares "a human being is a great wonder" (*magnum miraculum est
homo*), describes the Egyptian **art of making gods** (ensouling statues), and contains the **Lament**
prophesying the abandonment of Egypt's holy religion.

Greek treatises) · the Latin Asclepius · (followed by Copenhaver's extensive notes, out of scope). The
wiki ingests the **treatises and the Asclepius** (the doctrine), not the philological apparatus.
