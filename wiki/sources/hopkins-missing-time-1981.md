## Source Description

*Missing Time: A Documented Study of UFO Abductions* (1981) by artist-investigator Budd Hopkins is the foundational systematic study of the UFO-abduction phenomenon. Its central concept is **"missing time"** — an unexplained gap of (usually) an hour or two following a UFO encounter, which Hopkins argues is the tell-tale sign of an abduction whose memory has been deliberately blocked. Working with hypnotists and psychologists (notably Dr. Aphrodite Clamar) and co-investigator Ted Bloecher, Hopkins recovers these suppressed memories through **regressive hypnosis**, and from nineteen cases (thirty-seven people, seven cases at the book's heart) argues that abductions form a consistent pattern amounting to "some kind of systematic 'research' program, with the human species as subject."

Building on the Barney and Betty Hill case (1961, published 1965), and drawing on J. Allen Hynek's Center for UFO Studies and its HUMCAT catalogue, Hopkins contends abductions are far more common than known — an "epidemic" — and leave physical traces (childhood scars / "scoop marks"). The book launched the modern abduction-research field that Whitley Strieber, John Mack, and others later extended.

Introduction + chapters built around individual investigated cases (e.g. "Steven Kilburn," Virginia Horton, the central multi-witness case), interleaved with the recurring pattern and physical evidence; appendices on scale and method.

### Introduction
Hopkins surveys the UFO field, noting tens of thousands of reports since World War II and that perhaps 90% are misidentifications. He distinguishes the unexplained residue — citing the 1964 Socorro, New Mexico landing investigated by Officer Lonnie Zamora — and states the book's thesis: that abductions are far more common than realized. He reveals that of the seven cases at the book's heart, three subjects (all born in 1943) were abducted as seven-year-old children in the summer of 1950 with incisions made in their bodies, and none had any conscious memory until hypnosis. His central conclusion: "anyone could have been abducted, with no memory of it."

### Chapter 1: Cape Cod Genesis
Hopkins recounts how he became involved: in late August 1964, while living in Truro, Massachusetts, he and two companions watched a dull-aluminum elliptical object hover and then move against the wind over Route 6. The sighting drew him into reading UFO literature, where he encountered (and at first dismissed as "ridiculous") the Betty and Barney Hill abduction case — the touchstone he would later return to. The chapter establishes his entry from skeptical painter to investigator.

### Chapter 2: The Landing in North Hudson Park
Hopkins investigates "George," a New Jersey shopkeeper who, driving home through deserted North Hudson Park around 1–2 a.m. in January, saw a roundish ~30-foot lit craft land in a playing field. Nine or more small helmeted figures "like kids in snowsuits" descended a ladder, dug soil samples with spoon-like tools into bags, and departed northward in under four minutes. Hopkins reads George's reluctance, terror, and specific homely detail (boots of one piece with the trouser legs) as marks of authenticity rather than invention.

### Chapter 3: A Sharp Right Turn on the National Road
The central Steven Kilburn case. Kilburn had only a vague dread of a particular stretch of Maryland highway and no memory of any UFO. Under hypnosis (with Dr. Franklin, then Dr. Aphrodite Clamar) he relived being stopped, gripped by a rounded metal clamp on his shoulder, and examined on a chest-high table that "grew out of the floor like a mesa" by short white-skinned beings with smooth, nail-less "perfect fingers." Hopkins draws the disturbing inference that, since beneath a mere "feeling" lay a full abduction, there could be a vast hidden population of abductees with no conscious clue at all.

### Chapter 4: The Blue Light in the Pine Barrens
The Howard Rich case (a second abduction scenario: taken from home rather than a car). After a vivid bedroom blue-light experience that left him deeply, disproportionately frightened, "Howard" underwent hypnosis and relived being floated out of his house and examined. Hopkins notes the matching "establishment of external control" sequence — dread, a telepathic-seeming order, paralysis, transfer aboard — and an "emotional plateau" in which the captive is tranquilized and made to feel calm during the examination, closely paralleling Kilburn's account.

### Chapter 5: NBC — The Catalyst
An NBC News documentary on the phenomenon becomes a catalyst, drawing in further witnesses — David Oldham (who consciously recalled a UFO and a two-hour time lapse) and "Mac" McMahon, whose March 1979 account of an on-board examination preceded and independently matched details later given by Kilburn, Rich, and Oldham. McMahon accepted only the facial image of Kilburn's televised reconstruction but rejected its skinny body, describing instead powerfully built beings with hard, "plastic"-like skin — a concordance Hopkins treats as evidence the witnesses were not simply copying one another.

### Chapter 6: The Virginia Horton Case
Virginia Horton contacted Dr. Clamar after the NBC segment, having a childhood "blood of unknown origin" memory. Under hypnosis she recalled a childhood abduction in which a being took a tissue sample from her leg, leaving a scar, and a second incident (a disappearance at a family picnic in France). Like Kilburn she had no conscious recall of any UFO. Hopkins notes her treatment — kept conscious, gently questioned, intelligence-tested — resembles Betty Hill's, contrasting with the male subjects who, like Barney Hill, are rendered semi-conscious and handled more roughly.

### Chapter 7: Pittsburgh and the Mountaintop
The Philip Osborne case. Prompted by the NBC program and a recent episode of nocturnal paralysis, "Philip" recalled an odd childhood trip to the Great Smokies and a perfectly straight three-inch "scar of unknown origin" at his hip whose origin no one could remember. Born in 1943 like Virginia Horton, he too may have been abducted at age six in summer 1950 and again as a young adult. His hypnotic sessions were marked by long pauses, hesitations, and self-doubt, which Hopkins presents candidly to the reader.

### Chapter 8: The Alsatian Deer
Virginia Horton's deeper recall, centered on her childhood memory of a "beautiful deer" that looked at her "in a very conscious kind of way" — reinterpreted under hypnosis as a screen memory masking an abduction. She describes a being roughly her uncle's height, very bright lights uncomfortable to her eyes, an "instrument box," and a tool that made the incision in her leg. The recurring scars, nasal probes, and tissue sampling across Horton, Andreasson, and others are knitted together as a pattern.

### Chapter 9: Speculations Both Grim and Hopeful
Hopkins's synthesis. He argues that simple information-gathering cannot explain why roughly five hundred abductions (and likely thousands more hidden) have occurred, since the questions asked of abductees are trivial and a handful of subjects would suffice. He proposes the abductors are either "taking something besides information, or … leaving something behind," and frames the whole as "some kind of systematic 'research' program, with the human species as subject" — while conceding the ultimate purpose, benign or otherwise, is unknown.

### Afterword (by Aphrodite Clamar, Ph.D.)
The clinical psychologist who hypnotized the subjects describes her method: she had no prior UFO interest and developed her own procedures, beginning each session with a life-history interview and a neutral "trial" hypnosis to acquaint the subject with the state, emphasizing that hypnosis is not unconsciousness. She reports the process gave the witnesses relief regardless of the literal truth of their accounts, and that more than a dozen men and women had been brought to her. The book closes with a "What Can Be Done?" appeal inviting witnesses to write in.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Hopkins is candid about the evidentiary problems: the missing "central" proof (no "captured UFO… to kick the tires on"); reliance on hypnotically recovered memory; the difficulty of separating the "purely psychic" from the physical ("what part of the experience may be purely psychic… we can only guess, but that [abductions] have physical dimension seems to me beyond doubt"). He notes some witnesses were polygraph-tested, and concedes the cases are a self-selected, hard-to-verify minority (70–80% of even the sightings go unreported).
