## Source Description

*The First Global Revolution: A Report by the Council of the Club of Rome* (1991) was written by Alexander King (the Club's co-founder and then president) and Bertrand Schneider (its secretary-general). It is the Club of Rome's own programmatic statement two decades after its 1972 report *The Limits to Growth* — a **primary source**, in contrast to the hostile external account in [[The Club of Rome (Coleman)]].

The book is built around two coined terms: the **"world problematique"** (the Club's trademark — the interlocking tangle of global problems) and the **"world resolutique"** (a coherent, simultaneous, comprehensive response). Part I diagnoses the problematique (population, environment, development, the post–Cold War "vacuum"); Part II proposes the resolutique (the "Three Immediacies," improved global governance, and a renewal of human "motivations and values"). It is the source of the much-quoted passage "The common enemy of humanity is Man."

## Chapter Summaries

### Foreword
King and Schneider recount the Club of Rome's founding in 1968 (the "Great Divide" year) by Aurelio Peccei and Alexander King at the Accademia dei Lincei in Rome, its initial theme "The Predicament of Mankind," and its membership of one hundred independent individuals from fifty-three countries. They set out the Club's three conceptual guidelines: a global approach to interdependent world problems, a longer-term perspective than governments allow, and attention to the cluster of intertwined problems they trademark as the *problematique*.

### Introduction: The Wheel of Humankind
The authors frame the present moment as "the first global revolution" — unlike the agricultural or industrial revolutions, this one is happening everywhere simultaneously, has no ideological basis, and is driven by a mixture of social, economic, technological, cultural, and ethical upheavals. They stress humanity's "double challenge": to understand the new world and to learn to manage it rather than be managed by it. The end of the Cold War, awakening nationalism, and stark world economic inequalities are presented as signs of this revolution.

### Chapter 1: A Whirlwind of Change
A survey of the accelerating, interacting global changes — geopolitical, demographic, technological, environmental — that constitute the *problematique*. The authors emphasize that no single problem can be understood in isolation, introducing the interlocking character of global difficulties that the rest of Part I elaborates.

### Chapter 2: Some Areas of Acute Concern
The authors illustrate the interaction among elements of the *problematique*, using population growth in poor countries as a worked example: more mouths require more food, which pressures soil and water, drives deforestation, diverts hard currency to imports, and degrades the environment. Population pressure and environmental impact are presented as the central, mutually reinforcing concerns.

### Chapter 3: The International Mismanagement of the World Economy
A selective overview of the world economy focused on key actors — the USA, Japan, the European Community, and groups of developing countries — arguing that the rapid changes and discrepancies of the global economy are being mismanaged and that flagrant inequalities (extreme poverty beside great wealth) generate tensions and conflicts.

### Chapter 4: Intimations of Solidarity
Against the dangerous trends of the preceding chapters, the authors point to positive, hopeful signs — recalling the Club's 1985 declaration that humanity faces either "a bright and fulfilling future" or "a slow and painful decline." They argue humanity is becoming aware of its problems and possesses the capacity for global solidarity, though "time is now running out."

### Chapter 5: The Vacuum
The authors diagnose a collapse of the social cohesion once supplied by patriotism, religion, and respect for the state, leaving a moral and political "vacuum" in the post–Cold War world. The chapter contains the much-quoted passage in which they search "for a common enemy against whom we can unite," propose pollution, global warming, water shortages, and famine for the role — then explicitly call this a "trap" of "mistaking symptoms for causes," concluding "the real enemy then is humanity itself."

### Chapter 6: The Human Malaise
A catalogue of the global "symptoms" of the great transition: urban violence, international terrorism, the rise of international mafias, drug addiction and drug-related crime, and the exploitation of deviant behaviour by the media and advertising. The authors describe a pervasive loss of values and points of reference, a worldwide malaise born of complexity, uncertainty, and unsolved problems of environment and underdevelopment.

**Claims Extracted**

### Chapter 7: The Three Immediacies
Opening Part II (the *resolutique*), the authors define the resolutique as a "simultaneous and comprehensive attack on the main problems at many levels," counter to traditional piecemeal policy. They identify three urgent priorities ("immediacies"): the conversion of arms industries to civil uses following disarmament; securing "an environment for survival"; and addressing the development of the poor countries.

### Chapter 8: Governance and the Capacity to Govern
The authors argue that mastering interlocking global problems requires policies that account for their mutual impact, and that existing political, institutional, and administrative systems are inadequate to the task. "Deficiencies of governance" are themselves at the root of many strands of the *problematique*; the resolutique therefore demands improved governance at national and international levels.

- [[The resolutique requires improved global governance]]

### Chapter 9: Agents of the Resolutique
The chapter asks who will carry out the resolutique, framing the fundamental challenge as a permanent capacity to *learn* and adapt. It surveys the agents of change — teachers and education, the scientific community, and the mass media (noting media's role as a balancing power in democracies) — as the actors who can help individuals and societies adapt to continuous change.

### Chapter 10: Motivations and Values
The closing chapter returns to the dominance of technology in shaping modern aspirations and lifestyles, and argues that the resolutique ultimately depends on a renewal of human motivations and values. Material progress alone is insufficient; the authors call for a transformation of attitudes and behaviour as the deepest level of response to the global revolution.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

The authors take pains to qualify claims for which they are widely cited: they insist the Club "never intended" to advocate "a zero growth economy" and "fully accepted the pressing need for material growth in the poor countries," warning only against unthinking growth by industrialized nations. In the famous "common enemy" passage they explicitly call casting pollution/warming/famine as "the enemy" a **"trap"** of "mistaking symptoms for causes," concluding the real cause is human behaviour. This primary text underlies — and is more qualified than — the conspiratorial readings in [[The Club of Rome treats overpopulation as the enemy and population reduction as policy]] and [[The Club of Rome's goal is to deindustrialize the United States under a Post-Industrial Zero-Growth policy]].
