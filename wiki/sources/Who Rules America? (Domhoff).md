## Source Description

*Who Rules America?* (8th ed., 2022) by sociologist **[[G. William Domhoff]]** is the classic academic "power-structure" analysis of who holds power in the United States. Its answer: a wealthy ownership class — the top 0.5% who own most of the country's financial wealth — dominates federal policy through a **power elite** drawn from an interlocking "corporate community" and a socially cohesive "upper class," operating four documentable networks (corporate, policy-planning, opinion-shaping, and candidate-selection). Domhoff argues the United States is a case of **class domination**, rooted in the early historical ascendancy of business over government, army, and church.

The book is the wiki's **sober, non-conspiratorial counterpoint** to the New-World-Order / international-banker cluster: Domhoff explicitly contrasts power-structure *research* (visible, institutional, class-based, studied through public records) with *conspiracy theory* (secret, small, behind-the-scenes plotters), and rejects the Illuminati / Bilderberg / UN-world-government / "deep state" theories by name. It is an analytical ally of [[The Paranoid Style in American Politics (Hofstadter)]], [[A Culture of Conspiracy (Barkun)]], and [[Real Enemies (Olmsted)]].

## Chapter Summaries

### Introduction — Setting the Stage
Domhoff frames the 2020s as a contest between an inclusionary, increasingly diverse Democratic coalition and a white-nationalist ultraconservative Republican bloc — but argues that, whichever way that fight goes, the wealthiest 0.5% (the owners and managers of large banks, corporations, real-estate firms, and agribusinesses) will remain the most powerful group. The corporate rich are held together by five shared goals: weaken labor unions, expand international trade and investment, cut taxes on high incomes and corporations, limit business regulation, and restrain government social insurance. They are challenged by an "inclusionary alliance" of communities of color, feminists, environmentalists, climate activists, liberals, and unions.

### Chapter 1 — Concepts, Definitions, and Power Indicators
Defines power as a *relationship* with two dimensions (collective and distributive power), surveys the social psychology of power (affordance and its abuse), and lays out the **four major power networks** (economic, political, military, religious/ideology) — noting that in the US the *economic* network has been dominant since colonial times. It explains the American cultural denial of class and power, the roles of caste and colorism, and "cultures of resentment." It then draws the book's pivotal distinction between **power-structure analysis and conspiracy theory**, and introduces the **three power indicators**: *Who governs?*, *Who benefits?* (wealth/income distribution), and *Who wins?* (union density).

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Domhoff is careful to distinguish his claims from both conspiracy theory (the corporate rich rule through visible institutions, make mistakes, and number in the thousands) and from any view that power is total: he stresses that power structures constantly change, that the inclusionary alliance has won major victories, and that "the other 99.5% of Americans will decide the direction the country takes." He concedes the *who-wins* indicator is the hardest to measure, and that the corporate community's greatest power may lie in keeping issues off the agenda entirely ("non-issues").
