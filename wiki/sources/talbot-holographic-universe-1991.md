## Source Description

*The Holographic Universe* (1991) by Michael Talbot synthesizes two independent bodies of work — physicist **David Bohm**'s theory of the *implicate order* and neuroscientist **Karl Pribram**'s *holographic brain* model — into the claim that both the brain and objective reality are organized like a **hologram**: the tangible world is a projection unfolded from "a level of reality so beyond our own it is literally beyond both space and time." Talbot argues this "holographic paradigm" can make scientific sense of a wide range of otherwise inexplicable phenomena: telepathy, precognition, psychokinesis, near-death and out-of-body experiences, lucid dreams, synchronicity, mystical oneness, and "miraculous" mind-body healing.

Talbot is careful to label the idea variously a "model," "paradigm," "analogy," or "metaphor," stressing it is "highly controversial and by no means accepted by a majority of scientists," and that Bohm and Pribram themselves "do not embrace all of the views and conclusions" he draws from it. He grew up in "a psychic family" and includes his own experiences as openly anecdotal.

## Chapter Summaries

### Introduction
Talbot opens with the *Star Wars* hologram of Princess Leia as a metaphor: some scientists believe the universe itself is a hologram, "a splendidly detailed illusion." He introduces the two architects — physicist David Bohm (a protégé of Einstein) and neurophysiologist Karl Pribram — who arrived independently at the idea that reality and the brain are holographic, and notes that the model claims to explain telepathy, precognition, near-death and out-of-body experiences, lucid dreams, synchronicity, and psychokinesis (citing Ring, Grof, Wolf, Peat, and the Aspect 1982 experiment and Jahn–Dunne PEAR work). He stresses the model is "highly controversial," not accepted by most scientists; that Bohm and Pribram do not embrace all his conclusions; that science is "not prejudice-free" (people are "addicted to their beliefs"); and that he grew up "in a psychic family" and includes his own experiences as anecdotal.

### Part I, Chapter 1: The Brain as Hologram
The case for Pribram's holographic brain. Talbot traces Pribram's puzzle — where memory is stored — through Wilder Penfield's engram experiments and Karl Lashley's finding that rats retained maze memories no matter how much brain tissue was removed. A 1960s *Scientific American* article on holography gave Pribram his model: like a hologram (where every fragment of film contains the whole image), memory and vision are distributed, not localized. He explains interference patterns, the "whole in every part" property, and how the model accounts for memory's vastness, associative recall, recognition, photographic memory, transfer of learned skills, and phantom-limb sensations. Experimental support comes from Paul Pietsch's salamander "Shufflebrain" experiments, Fourier transforms (Gabor's Nobel-winning math), and the DeValois Fourier-analysis findings; Bernstein's wave-form analysis of dancers' movements closes the chapter.

### Part I, Chapter 2: The Cosmos as Hologram
The case for Bohm's holographic universe. Talbot follows Bohm from his plasma physics ("the electron sea was alive"), through his six months of conversations with Einstein at Princeton, to his break with Niels Bohr's interpretation of quantum theory. The chapter explains wave-particle duality, the observer effect, the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen (EPR) paradox and quantum interconnectedness, and Bohm's response — the "quantum potential," a subquantum field — culminating in his theory of the **implicate (enfolded) and explicate (unfolded) order**: tangible reality is unfolded from a deeper undivided wholeness "beyond both space and time," in which consciousness is a subtler form of matter. Talbot cites the Aspect experiment as support and notes the physics community's largely negative reaction.

### Part II, Chapter 3: The Holographic Model and Psychology
Talbot applies the model to the mind. Bohm sees consciousness as the prime example of "undivided flowing movement." The holographic interconnectedness explains Jung's collective unconscious (illustrated by the 1906 schizophrenic's "sun's penis" vision matching a 2,000-year-old Persian text) and Robert Anderson's "personal resonance." Montague Ullman's Maimonides ESP dream experiments (the Tamayo and Chagall target-painting dreams) are read as evidence of dreaming interconnectedness; Ullman also interprets psychosis (schizophrenic loss of boundaries, manic identification with infinite potential) as untamed glimpses of the implicate order, and Fred Alan Wolf reads lucid dreams as "real images" / visits to parallel universes.

### Part II, Chapter 4: I Sing the Body Holographic
The mind's holographic control over the body. Talbot recounts O. Carl Simonton's cancer-visualization patient "Frank" (throat cancer vanishing in two months) and Simonton's 159-patient follow-up doubling survival time; Jeanne Achterberg's experiments training subjects to raise specific white-blood-cell counts (neutrophils vs. T-cells) and her 95% accuracy predicting survival from patients' drawings; Bernie Siegel's "soma-significant" disease cases; and the imagery/athletic-performance studies (Breznitz's marching Israeli soldiers, the Soviet visualization-vs-training experiment at the 1980 Lake Placid Games). All are framed via Bohm ("every action starts from an intention in the implicate order") and the holographic brain.

### Part II, Chapter 5: A Pocketful of Miracles
Talbot turns to "miracles": the twice-yearly liquefaction of San Gennaro's blood in Naples, stigmata (Therese Neumann's gravity-defying blood flow, Padre Pio), and psychokinesis. Bohm's "active information" / "soma-significant" view of meaning (the shadow-on-the-street example, the electron guided by its quantum wave "like a ship on autopilot") is offered as a context for PK as a nonlocal "resonance of meanings." Robert Jahn and Brenda Dunne's Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research (PEAR) experiments — the random event generator and the marble-cascade machine, with individual "signatures" and the "Pauli effect" / "Gremlin effect" — provide the laboratory evidence.

### Part II, Chapter 6: Seeing Holographically
Perception as construction. Talbot opens with his own experience of misreading a "No Parking" sign and the eye's blind spot to argue the brain edits and fills in reality (less than 50% of what we "see" comes from the eyes). In Pribram's view reality is a "frequency domain" the brain converts into the objective world, and mystics catch glimpses of that domain directly. The chapter explores the human energy field / aura as a holographic "blueprint," doctors and clairvoyants who report seeing it, and the possibility that much of reality lies beyond our edited perception.

### Part III, Chapter 7: Time Out of Mind
If reality is holographic, time is illusory. Talbot recounts psychic archaeology and retrocognition: Stefan Ossowiecki psychometrizing stone tools for Poniatowski (correctly describing Magdalenian hairstyles and oil lamps later confirmed by excavation), George McMullen locating an Iroquois longhouse for Norman Emerson, and Gerard Croiset, Eileen Garrett, and Clarence Weiant's Tres Zapotes find. Bohm's idea that the past is "active in the present as a kind of implicate order" — that as the present enfolds it returns to the implicate "cosmic storehouse" — supplies the model, extended to precognition.

### Part III, Chapter 8: Traveling in the Superhologram
Space, too, is illusory. Talbot surveys out-of-body and near-death experiences: cross-cultural OBE beliefs (Shiels, Bourguignon), incidence surveys, the Gabbard–Twemlow–Jones study (85% pleasant; OBEers psychologically normal), Kimberly Clark's "tennis shoe on the ledge" case, Michael Sabom's cardiac-arrest verification study, and Robert Monroe's controlled OBEs (*Journeys Out of the Body*, *Far Journeys*). Laboratory OBE work by Charles Tart ("Miss Z" reading a five-digit number), Osis and Mitchell, and Robert Morris's purring kitten is cited. Such phenomena, Talbot argues, are accessible because consciousness and the holographic record share the implicate order.

### Part III, Chapter 9: Return to the Dreamtime
Talbot argues the holographic model was "foreshadowed" in ancient traditions: the Tibetan Buddhist void/non-void, Milarepa's "conditioned" perception, Zen indivisibility, the Hindu Brahman and maya, Kabbalistic illusory creation (the Zohar's *baro*), and shamanic cosmologies (Hawaiian kahuna web, Aboriginal dreamtime, Dogon, Sufi "macrocosm is the microcosm," Hermes Trismegistus's "as above, so below," Indra's net, Fa-Tsang's jeweled network, Black Elk's "anywhere is the center of the world"). He closes by suggesting the model points toward an evolutionary thrust of consciousness and an "omnijective" universe blurring subjective and objective.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Talbot foregrounds the model's contested status and his own non-neutral standpoint. He notes the holographic idea is "still very much an idea in the making… a mosaic of many different points of view," that science "is not prejudice-free" (people are "addicted to their beliefs"), and that his personal experiences "can only be viewed as anecdotal evidence." He distinguishes Bohm and Pribram's core theories from the more speculative extensions by other researchers (Ring, Grof, Wolf, Peat, Jahn & Dunne) that he reports.
