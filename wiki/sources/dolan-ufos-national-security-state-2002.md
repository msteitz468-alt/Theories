## Source Description

*UFOs and the National Security State: Chronology of a Cover-up, 1941–1973* (2002) by historian Richard M. Dolan (foreword by [[Jacques Vallée]]) is a sober, document-based history of the U.S. government's involvement with the UFO problem. Drawing on three source types — "previously classified documents released through the Freedom of Information Act," primary sources from participants, and contemporary scholarship — Dolan argues that by 1947 American military and intelligence organizations had concluded there were real, unexplained objects violating sensitive airspace, that the matter became "a deep secret," and that a sustained **cover-up** followed, consistent with the secretive, deceptive behavior characteristic of the post-war "**national security state**."

Dolan is deliberately measured: he does not claim certainty that UFOs are extraterrestrial, nor that a single "control group" still manages the secret ("perhaps yes, perhaps no"); he infers the cover-up from the documentary record and from how large secret organizations demonstrably behave. The book proceeds as a year-by-year chronology from the wartime "foo fighters" to the 1969 closure of Project Blue Book and the 1973 wave.

## Chapter Summaries

### Foreword (Jacques Vallée) & Introduction
Vallée's foreword commends the book as a sober documentary history. Dolan's introduction lays out the thesis: military personnel worldwide have encountered UFOs for fifty years amid heavy secrecy, and by 1946-47 U.S. national security authorities concluded that real, unexplained objects were violating sensitive airspace — neither American nor Soviet — and made the matter "a deep secret." He distinguishes "official culture" from "unofficial culture" (illustrated by Stalin's falsified census and the Redmond, Oregon, case), rebuts the standard skeptical objections to alien visitation, and describes his three source types: FOIA-released documents, primary sources from participants (Project Blue Book, APRO, NICAP/Keyhoe), and contemporary scholarship.

### Chapter 1: Prologue: To 1947
Dolan surveys pre-war sightings (the 1882 Maunder object, the 1897 "airship" wave, Roerich's 1926 China sighting) and argues the Second World War transformed the problem by making aircraft and radar global. He sketches "the national security connection" — the Manhattan Project and its black budget as the model for institutionalized secrecy, the FBI's domestic surveillance, the rise of the OSS — into which the UFO problem was thrust, and reviews the wartime "foo fighters" (including the Battle of Los Angeles, Feb. 1942, whose Marshall memorandum stayed classified until 1974) and the 1946 Scandinavian "ghost rockets."

### Chapter 2: Saucers in the Skies: 1947
The 1947 wave: the buildup of sightings (including over the Oak Ridge nuclear facility), Kenneth Arnold's June 24 sighting that coined "flying saucer," the FBI's entry, an early media clampdown, and the Maury Island saga. Dolan recounts the Roswell episode — the RAAF's "flying disc" press release, General Ramey's same-day weather-balloon retraction, and the FBI teletype — and devotes extended analysis to rejecting the Air Force's later official explanations: the 1995 "Project Mogul" balloon report and the 1997 "crash test dummies" report, arguing the Mogul debris (balsa, tinfoil, rubber) cannot match witness descriptions and that the security level reported around the crash exceeded any ordinary Mogul launch.

### Chapter 3: Managing the Problem: 1948 to 1951
The institutional response: the 1947 National Security Act reorganization, the Twining letter affirming the objects were "real," the establishment of Project Sign and its 1948 "Estimate of the Situation" endorsing the extraterrestrial thesis (rejected by General Vandenberg), the 1948-49 green-fireball flap over New Mexico, the Air Force's public debunking (Project Grudge), the 1949 death of James Forrestal, and the Lubbock Lights. Dolan documents the pattern of internal belief in real objects paired with public dismissal.

### Chapter 4: Crisis and Containment: 1952 and 1953
The 1952 wave, when Grudge became Project Blue Book under Edward Ruppelt and sightings grew so frequent that some in the Air Force feared invasion. Centerpiece is the July 1952 Washington, D.C., radar-visual sightings over the Capitol and the resulting press conference. Dolan then narrates how the CIA stepped in and convened the secret **Robertson Panel** of January 1953, which recommended debunking and surveillance of civilian UFO groups — a turning point in formalizing the cover-up.

### Chapter 5: Shutting the Lid: 1953 to 1956
The tightening of secrecy after the Robertson Panel: the silencing regulations (AFR 200-2), Blue Book "cooking its books," and the mid-1950s humanoid and global sighting waves. Dolan situates the UFO cover-up alongside the era's other secret abuses, recounting the CIA's MKULTRA mind-control program and the LSD-related 1953 death of Army scientist Frank Olson as evidence of the kind of state capable of such concealment.

### Chapter 6: The Fight to End Secrecy: 1956 to 1962
The birth of NICAP under Donald Keyhoe and its push to force open congressional hearings, against an Air Force that parried the organization, censored Keyhoe on television, and "turned" Ruppelt (who revised his book before his death). Covers worldwide military aircraft encounters, the 1957 Sputnik-era global wave, the Morris Jessup case, Project Ozma, the 1961 Hill abduction case, and Hillenkoetter's resignation from NICAP — Dolan's portrait of a losing battle to end the secrecy.

### Chapter 8: Winners and Losers: 1966 to 1969
The Air Force funds the University of Colorado study (the **Condon Committee**) to settle the question. Dolan chronicles the project's internal collapse — leaked memos revealing a predetermined negative conclusion, the firing of the "pro-UFO" staff, the airing of "dirty laundry" — alongside 1967 cases (Falcon Lake, Shag Harbor, an early animal mutilation, the Schirmer abduction). The Condon Report's negative verdict enabled the Air Force to close Project Blue Book in December 1969, after which NICAP and civilian ufology declined.

### Chapter 9: The Problem Renewed: 1970 to 1973
With Blue Book closed, the "national security state triumphant," yet UFOs persist: Dolan covers the 1973 wave, an "underwater thesis" (UFO/USO activity over the world's oceans), and the 1971 death of atmospheric physicist and UFO advocate James McDonald. The official infrastructure of investigation is gone, but the phenomenon — and the secrecy around it — continues.

### Conclusion
Dolan summarizes: from the 1940s to the 1970s military personnel encountered UFOs hundreds or thousands of times, treated by the CIA, NSA, and military intelligence as serious while a public fiction of non-concern was maintained through Blue Book and a compliant media. He argues the cover-up is "nothing unique" for a state that ran mind-control experiments, biological spraying, mass surveillance, and assassinations, and weighs the two remaining explanations — a revolutionary human technology (which he rejects as unsupported) or an alien technology — noting every official study drifted toward the extraterrestrial conclusion before being shut down.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Dolan distinguishes inference from proof throughout: he notes the 1948 "Estimate of the Situation" is attested only by "several insiders" with "no official papers proving this," concedes a central UFO control group may no longer exist, and frames the extraterrestrial question as open ("either this was something real and alien, or… 'conventional' but as yet unknown"). He pre-empts the "conspiracy theory" dismissal by grounding it in ordinary, documented organizational secrecy rather than speculation, and cites the official Air Force position (Blue Book's three negative conclusions) in full before contesting it.
