## Source Description

*Magicians of the Gods* (2015) by **[[Graham Hancock]]** is the direct sequel to *[[Fingerprints of the Gods (Hancock)]]*, written twenty years later to incorporate new evidence Hancock argues vindicates his lost-civilization thesis. Its centerpiece is the **Younger Dryas Impact Hypothesis**: that a disintegrating comet struck the Earth around 12,800 years ago, triggering the Younger Dryas cold snap, catastrophic floods, and the extinction of a forgotten advanced civilization. Survivors — the "magicians of the gods" — are said to have carried the knowledge of agriculture, astronomy, and monumental architecture to hunter-gatherer peoples around the world.

Hancock builds the case from **Göbekli Tepe** (Turkey), the ~12,000-year-old megalithic site whose carved Pillar 43 ("the Vulture Stone") he reads as a memorial recording the date of the cataclysm; from Baalbek, Gunung Padang, the Giza plateau, and worldwide flood myths; and from the work of catastrophist researchers (Firestone, Kennett, West, and his collaborator Randall Carlson). He closes with a warning that fragments of the same comet, embedded in the Taurid meteor stream, threaten a recurrence. It belongs to the wiki's lost-civilization cluster and is directly opposed by the skeptical archaeology of *[[Frauds, Myths, and Mysteries (Feder)]]*.

## Structure

Eight Parts across nineteen chapters: I Anomalies (Göbekli Tepe) · II Comet (the Younger Dryas impact) · III–IV (the cataclysm, flood myths, survivors) · V (Baalbek) · VI (Göbekli Tepe's astronomy and Pillar 43) · VII (further sites) · VIII (the returning comet / a warning). Heavily endnoted, with appendices.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Hancock acknowledges that Klaus Schmidt, Göbekli Tepe's own excavator, does *not* endorse the lost-civilization reading (Schmidt frames the site as hunter-gatherers who "invented agriculture," "adding an important chapter to existing history," not rewriting it). The Younger Dryas Impact Hypothesis itself is contested in the scientific literature, which Hancock discusses.
