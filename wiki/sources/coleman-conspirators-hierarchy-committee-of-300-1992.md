## Source Description

*Conspirators' Hierarchy: The Story of the Committee of 300* (1992) by John Coleman — who presents himself as a former British intelligence officer — is the foundational text of the "Committee of 300" conspiracy thesis. Coleman argues that a supreme hidden body of 300 powerful individuals (which he says calls itself **"the Olympians"**), with roots in the British East India Company, secretly rules the world through an interlocking network of organizations — the Round Table, the Royal Institute for International Affairs, the Club of Rome, NATO, the Tavistock Institute, the German Marshall Fund, the Venetian Black Nobility, the Mont Pelerin Society, and others — pursuing a deliberate "open conspiracy" to impose a One World Government / New World Order by deindustrialization, depopulation, drugs, terrorism, and psychological warfare.

This is Coleman's central work; his later *The Club of Rome* (2008), already in the wiki, applies the same framework. The book is sweeping and polemical, naming named individuals and institutions as conspirators, and at points trafficking in prejudicial and bigoted characterizations; its claims are recorded here strictly as Coleman frames them.

## Structure

Foreword + an overview and case histories, followed by Coleman's enumeration of the Committee's "21 goals," its constituent organizations and think tanks, banking and intelligence arms, and concluding membership/organization charts.

## Chapter Summaries

The book is not divided into numbered chapters; it is a continuous polemic followed by reference catalogs. The summaries below follow Coleman's own top-level divisions (Foreword, the main expository body "An Overview and Some Case Histories," and the closing list-sections).

### Foreword (November 1991)
Coleman presents his credentials as a "professional intelligence officer" who, while serving as a political science officer in Angola, glimpsed top-secret documents that launched his lifelong quest to uncover "what power it is that controls and manages the British and United States governments." He says that on returning to the U.S. in 1969 he found Americans ignorant of bodies such as the Round Table, the Club of Rome, the German Marshall Fund, the Venetian Black Nobility and the Mont Pelerin Society, and that he began exposing them in monographs and cassette tapes despite harassment, financial loss and threats.

### An Overview and Some Case Histories
The book's main body. Coleman argues that a supreme hidden body — the **Committee of 300**, which calls itself "**the Olympians**" — secretly rules the world through an interlocking network (the RIIA, Club of Rome, NATO, Tavistock, the Black Nobility, banks and think tanks), pursuing an "open conspiracy" for a One World Government / New World Order. He enumerates the Committee's **"21 goals,"** including a unified world church and currency, destruction of national identity and Christianity, mind control via "technotronics," a "post-industrial zero-growth society," legalization of drugs and pornography, depopulation (the "Global 2000" plan to cull billions of "useless eaters"), crisis management via FEMA, Tavistock-engineered rock music and cults to demoralize the young, and total control of U.S. policy and education. He details the Tavistock Institute as the Committee's mass psychological-warfare arm (claiming it trained CBS's William Paley and produced "The Aquarian Conspiracy"), and presents case histories of the Committee assassinating or coercing leaders who defied it — most prominently Italy's Aldo Moro (whom he says Kissinger threatened and P2 Masonry murdered), Pakistan's Ali Bhutto and General Zia ul-Haq, plus the killings of Kennedy and the "Watergating" of Nixon as warnings that "nobody is beyond our reach."

### Closing Catalogs (Organizations, Banks, Foundations, and Membership Lists)
The final third of the book is reference material rather than narrative: Coleman lists the banks, insurance companies (Assicurazioni Generali, Eagle Star), and brokerage houses he says the Committee controls; enumerates "Past and Present Institutions/Organizations… Directly Under Influence of the Committee of 300"; catalogs "Special Foundations and Interest Groups," "Legal Associations and Lawyers," and "Tavistock Institutions in the United States" (Brookings, Hudson Institute, RAND, Stanford Research Institute, etc.); and provides a roster of "Past and Present Members of the Committee of 300" followed by a bibliography. These lists are presented as the documentary backbone of the thesis.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Coleman presents the thesis as the fruit of his "career as a professional intelligence officer" with "access to highly classified documents," and as confirmed by predictions he says came true — but offers no verifiable membership roster and acknowledges that Senate committees he approached claimed to have "never heard of" his sources. The work is presented as exposé rather than falsifiable evidence; the wiki records its assertions without endorsing them.
