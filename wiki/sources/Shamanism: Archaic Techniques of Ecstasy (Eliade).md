## Source Description

*Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy* (1951; English 1964) by historian of religions **[[Mircea Eliade]]** is the foundational comparative study of shamanism. Eliade defines shamanism precisely as a **"technique of ecstasy"**: the shaman is the specialist who, in controlled trance, sends the soul out of the body to **ascend to the heavens or descend to the underworld**, dealing with spirits as healer, diviner, and **psychopomp**. He distinguishes this from spirit-possession and from magic generally.

The book documents, across a vast range of cultures (Siberian and Central Asian heartlands, then North and South America, Southeast Asia, Oceania, and Indo-European antiquity), the recurring structures: the **election** of the shaman (heredity or **initiatory crisis** — sickness, symbolic death, **dismemberment**, rebirth); the acquisition of **helping spirits** and a secret language; the cosmology of **three zones** joined by an **axis mundi** (World Tree, Pillar, Mountain); and ecstatic feats — **magical flight**, the **mastery of fire**, the perilous descent. Eliade interprets shamanic ascent as a nostalgia for the lost primordial paradise. It is the scholarly root of the wiki's modern shamanism/entheogen material; see [[Shamanism as archaic technique of ecstasy (Eliade)]].

## Structure

~13 chapters plus introduction and conclusion: general considerations and recruiting of shamans; initiatory sicknesses and dreams; obtaining shamanic powers; initiation (Siberia, Central Asia, North America); the symbolism of the costume and drum; séances; celestial ascents and descents to the underworld; shamanism and cosmology (the World Tree, the three zones); regional surveys (the Americas, Southeast Asia, Oceania, Indo-Europeans); conclusion.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Eliade insists on a narrow definition (ecstasy/soul-flight) and treats spirit-possession as a later or peripheral development — a delimitation later scholars dispute. He builds a unified "archaic" model from very diverse ethnographic materials, and his thesis of a degenerated original celestial religion reflects his broader history-of-religions framework.
