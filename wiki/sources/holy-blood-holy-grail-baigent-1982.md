**Source Description**

Originally published in 1982 by Jonathan Cape, London. Three co-authors: Henry Lincoln, a BBC television writer who first encountered the Rennes-le-Château mystery in 1969 via Gérard de Sède's *Le Trésor Maudit*; Richard Leigh, novelist and comparative literature scholar; and Michael Baigent, psychology graduate and former photojournalist with prior research into the Knights Templar. The book grew from three BBC Chronicle television documentaries (1972, 1974, 1979) Lincoln made on Rennes-le-Château and the Templars. This edition is a Corgi 1983 paperback reprint.

The book is in three parts: **Part One: The Mystery** (Rennes-le-Château, the Cathars, the Knights Templar); **Part Two: The Secret Society** (the alleged Prieuré de Sion and its purported Grand Masters); **Part Three: The Bloodline** (the Holy Grail as dynastic bloodline descended from Jesus through Mary Magdalene and the Merovingian dynasty). The authors explicitly frame their central thesis as a hypothesis assembled from documentary, circumstantial, and archaeological evidence. They rely heavily on a corpus of documents they call the "Prieuré documents" or "Dossiers secrets," deposited in the Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris.

**What the Source Contributes**

This source supplies the foundational claims for a cluster of interconnected conspiracy-historical theses that have had substantial influence on popular esoteric and alternative-history literature. The principal contributions:

- Detailed account of the Saunière mystery at Rennes-le-Château and the encoded parchments
- The claim that the Prieuré de Sion is a real, continuous secret society from 1090 to the present
- A list of alleged Grand Masters of the Prieuré de Sion including Leonardo da Vinci, Isaac Newton, Victor Hugo, Claude Debussy, and Jean Cocteau
- The argument that "Sangraal" / "Sangreal" (Holy Grail) originally meant "Sang Real" (Royal Blood), not "San Graal" (Holy Cup)
- The central hypothesis that Jesus and Mary Magdalene were married; that the Magdalene carried their offspring to Gaul after the Crucifixion; and that this bloodline eventually merged with the Frankish royal line to produce the Merovingian dynasty
- Analysis of the Cathars, the Albigensian Crusade, and the Knights Templar as successive custodians of this secret
- The "Cutting of the Elm" at Gisors (1188) as the moment the Prieuré de Sion formally separated from the Knights Templar
- The alleged political goal of the Prieuré de Sion: a theocratic United States of Europe ruled by a restored Merovingian dynasty descended from Jesus

**Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts**

The authors explicitly acknowledge they cannot prove their central hypothesis; they present it as a "tentative hypothesis" tested against available evidence. They note that the "Prieuré documents" (Dossiers secrets) are of uncertain provenance and cannot always be independently verified, though they record multiple instances where the documents were confirmed by external archival sources (Orleans municipal archives, Inquisition records, etc.). They note the list of Grand Masters "immediately provoked our scepticism" and they remain uncertain about some attributions. They also flag that their scenario of Jesus's survival or the Magdalene's flight to Gaul rests on inference rather than direct evidence.
