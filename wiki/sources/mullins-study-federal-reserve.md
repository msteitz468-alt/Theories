## Source Description

*A Study of the Federal Reserve and its Secrets* is a polemical history of the United States Federal Reserve System by Eustace Clarence Mullins (ISBN 978-1-62755-880-8; Wilder Publications, 2014 edition). In the Preface, Mullins states the work originated in a Library of Congress research project begun in the fall of 1949 that grew into nineteen months of labour, undertaken at the suggestion of (and the broader project associated with) Ezra Pound. The Preface is dated January 12, 1952.

The book is organized into sixteen chapters, each built around a central figure in the founding or operation of the Federal Reserve: Nelson Aldrich (Chs. 1–2), Samuel Untermyer (Ch. 3), Woodrow Wilson (Ch. 4), Carter Glass (Ch. 5), Paul Warburg (Chs. 6–7, 10), Bernard Baruch (Ch. 8), Albert Strauss (Ch. 9), Andrew Mellon (Ch. 11), Herbert Hoover (Ch. 12), Franklin D. Roosevelt (Ch. 13), Marriner Eccles (Ch. 14), Herbert Lehman (Ch. 15), and Thomas B. McCabe (Ch. 16). A bibliography of primary banking documents, congressional hearings, and bankers' memoirs closes the book.

## Thesis (as presented by the source)

Mullins argues that the Federal Reserve Act of 1913 was not a popular monetary reform but a central-bank plan secretly drafted by a small group of New York and international bankers at Jekyll Island, Georgia, in November 1910; that the "Federal Reserve System" name and regional structure were deliberate disguises; that the System is privately owned and operated for its stockholders; that its primary function is war finance; and that it deliberately engineered the Agricultural Depression of 1920–21 and the Great Depression of 1929–31. The book treats Paul Warburg as the principal architect and the Rothschild banking interests (via Kuhn, Loeb & Co.) as the ultimate sponsors.

**Note on extraction:** This wiki records the claims as the source presents them. The book is a partisan, conspiracist account; many assertions (e.g., motive attributions, Rothschild control, deliberate engineering of depressions, and ethnically framed characterizations) are contested or rejected by mainstream historiography. Per the wiki's strict-extraction policy, only the source's own content is catalogued here, without external endorsement.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

The book is single-authored and argues one thesis throughout, but it repeatedly quotes participants whose statements it treats as admissions — e.g., Frank Vanderlip's and Paul Warburg's own accounts of Jekyll Island, Carter Glass's and Warburg's competing claims to authorship of the Act (Mullins notes Untermyer privately called Glass's authorship claim a "bald misstatement"), and Marriner Eccles's congressional testimony on money creation. Mullins presents these as mutually corroborating rather than conflicting.
