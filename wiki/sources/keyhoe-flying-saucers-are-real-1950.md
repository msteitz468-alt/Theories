## Source Description

*The Flying Saucers Are Real* (1950) by Major Donald Keyhoe — a retired Marine Corps aviator and journalist — is the founding text of UFO ufology and of the UFO "cover-up" thesis. Commissioned by *True* magazine (editor Ken Purdy) and expanded from his landmark January 1950 *True* article, Keyhoe reports a six-month investigation concluding that flying saucers are **real, interplanetary craft** observing Earth, that their appearances surged after 1947 (which he links to atomic-bomb testing), and that the **U.S. Air Force knows this and is concealing it** behind official secrecy. Predating Hynek's scientific work and Dolan's documentary history by decades, it set the template — the extraterrestrial hypothesis plus a government cover-up — for everything that followed.

Keyhoe writes as a sympathetic but evidence-citing reporter who crossed trails with the Air Force's Project "Saucer" (Sign) teams and the FBI, examined the major early cases, and argues the secrecy is partly a deliberate "Air Force policy" of cautiously preparing the public against panic rather than pure malice.

## Structure

20 short chapters, opening with Keyhoe's *True* assignment and conclusions, then working through the key 1947–1949 cases (the Mantell death, the Chiles–Whitted and Gorman encounters, the Harmon Field photo), the contradictions in Project "Saucer," and his interplanetary argument.

## Chapter Summaries

The book's twenty chapters are untitled in the text; they form a single continuous first-person investigation narrative running from Keyhoe's *True* assignment to his final conclusions. Adjacent chapters covering one theme are grouped below.

### Author's Note
Keyhoe opens with a chronology of contradictory Air Force statements between April 1949 and March 1950 — the April 27, 1949 Project "Saucer" call for "constant vigilance," repeated denials that saucers exist, the Key West and Wright Field radar/visual cases, and the December 30, 1949 release admitting "it will never be possible to say with certainty that any individual did not see a space ship." He states his thesis: the contradictions are "part of an intricate program to prepare America — and the world — for the secret of the disks."

### Chapter 1
Keyhoe receives the May 1949 telegram from *True* editor Ken Purdy assigning him the "Washington end" of the flying-saucer story, with leads on the Mantell case and a secret Harmon Field photograph. He summarizes the six-month investigation and the conclusions published in the January 1950 *True*: the earth has been observed by visitors from another planet, observation increased markedly in the past two years, and the only alternative — that the saucers are earth-made high-speed craft — would require "an almost incredible leap" the Air Force denies. He reports recent 1950 sightings (Tucson, Key West, the Chilean Antarctic report, Dayton) and frames the secrecy as an "elaborate program."

### Chapter 2
The death of Captain Thomas Mantell over Godman Field, January 7, 1948. Keyhoe reconstructs the radio messages ("It looks metallic — and it's tremendous in size"), the half-hour chase, and the disintegration of Mantell's P-51. He recounts the Lockbourne (Columbus) sighting two hours later and his early Pentagon inquiries. A long scene in Purdy's office lays out the Saturday Evening Post (Sidney Shallett) articles that brushed the saucers off, the Air Force's contradictory April report, and the Venus explanation given to Shallett for Mantell's death.

### Chapter 5
A detailed two-week analysis of the Mantell case. Keyhoe — a former balloon pilot — dismantles both the Venus and the Navy cosmic-ray balloon explanations using speed, altitude, and simultaneity figures (the object was seen from points 175 miles apart, implying it was over 30 miles high and enormous). He quotes Professor Hynek's astronomical analysis and concludes "this particular saucer had been real." A luncheon with Steele convinces Keyhoe that Steele is steering him toward the Soviet-missile answer to force silence.

### Chapters 12–13

Redell explains the Gorman light as a transparent rotating disk under remote control, and walks through why no earth-made guided missile could explain the major cases — citing the White Sands sighting (later identified as Cmdr. ### Chapter 14
Keyhoe constructs his central argument by "reversing" America's own space-exploration plans to model how visitors would survey the earth — orbiting bases, unmanned observer units, atmosphere sampling, luring aircraft into pursuit, avoiding contact. ### Chapter 18
Keyhoe dissects the Project "Saucer" case summaries and finds the published "answers" are mostly conjecture contradicting the investigators' own admissions. He shows the Mantell (Case 33) and Santa Fe (Case 175) reports handle Venus inconsistently, that the Twin Falls report calls a sighting one of "the other bona fide disk sightings," and that of ~228 summarized cases the Air Force lists all but 34 as "explained" despite internal notes saying "no astronomical explanation." He concludes the Air Force has known the truth since Mantell and that the December 1949 "all explained" release was false.

### Chapter 20
Keyhoe's summary conclusions: the Air Force was alarmed in 1947, began to suspect the truth after Mantell, and set up Project "Saucer" both to investigate and to conceal; a 1949 policy shift aimed to let the facts gradually "leak out"; the public's calm reaction to *True* prompted release of the case summaries; and Boggs was forced to defend the Venus answer he knew was wrong. He concludes the earth has been under interplanetary observation for at least two centuries, sharply increased after 1945, and that Americans "should be told the truth, now."

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Keyhoe frames his interplanetary conclusion as the best of limited options — "the only other possible explanation… is that the saucers are extremely high-speed, long-range devices developed here on earth," an "almost incredible leap" the Air Force denies. He notes *True* published "certain alternate conclusions," that the Air Force did not block publication, and reads the secrecy charitably as a gradual public-education policy as much as concealment. He relies heavily on press reports, pilot testimony, and a contested reading of Air Force documents.
