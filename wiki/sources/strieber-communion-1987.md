## Source Description

*Communion: A True Story* (1987) by novelist Whitley Strieber is the first-person memoir that brought UFO abduction — and the large-eyed grey "visitor" face of its cover — into mass culture. Strieber recounts "a shattering assault from the unknown," centered on the night of **December 26, 1985** at his secluded cabin in upstate New York, when he was apparently taken by small non-human beings and subjected to an examination (including a needle inserted into his brain and an intrusive probe). Crucially, Strieber refuses to call the beings "aliens" or "extraterrestrials" — he names them **"the visitors,"** and holds their nature genuinely unknown: they could be ET, the human dead, beings from another dimension or time, or a phenomenon of the mind. He titles the book for what he senses they seek: "the very depth of the soul; it seeks communion."

Strieber worked with abduction investigator **Budd Hopkins** (whose *Missing Time* had just appeared) and psychiatrist Dr. Donald Klein; he submitted to psychological/neurological testing (found in the normal range) and passed a polygraph. As a horror novelist (*The Wolfen*, *The Hunger*), he is acutely aware of — and argues against — the obvious objection that he invented it.

## Structure

Prelude ("The Truth Behind the Curtain") + six chapters (One: The Invisible Forest; Two: Down the Cave of Mind; Three: The Color of the Dark; Four: The Sky Beneath My Feet; Five: Alliance of the Lost; Six: A Structure in the Air) + Epilogue and appendices (hypnosis transcripts; polygraph results).

## Chapter Summaries

### Prelude: The Truth Behind the Curtain

Strieber frames the book as "the story of one man's attempt to deal with a shattering assault from the unknown," insisting it is true "as true as I know how to describe it." He reports that after fearing he was losing his mind he was examined by three psychologists and three psychiatrists, tested within the normal range, and passed a polygraph. He stresses he had been an indifferent UFO skeptic, that most of his memory was conscious before hypnosis, and that scoffing at abductees "is as ugly as laughing at rape victims." He closes with the book's title image: the visitors seem to seek "the very depth of the soul; it seeks communion."

### One: The Invisible Forest

The central narrative, drawn from a journal written before any hypnosis. On the night of December 26, 1985, at his secluded log cabin in upstate New York (burglar alarm armed), Strieber woke to a "whooshing, swirling noise," saw a compact three-and-a-half-foot figure edge around his bedroom door, then found himself paralyzed and moving through the woods. He describes being taken to a small, messy round chamber, examined by four types of beings (a robotlike leader, stocky blue-coveralled figures, and tall slender beings with "prominent and mesmerizing black slanted eyes"), having a needle inserted into his brain after he screamed, asking to "smell" one of them as an anchor in reality, and undergoing a rectal probe and a finger incision. He woke on the 27th with a false memory of a barn owl at the window — but found no owl tracks in the snow. He also reconstructs a related disturbance of October 4, 1985.

### Two: Down the Cave of Mind

Strieber recounts seeking out a therapist — deliberately one with no prior involvement in the abduction field, on a referral path away from Hopkins's usual hypnotists — and choosing Dr. Donald Klein of the New York State Psychiatric Institute for his scientific rigor. He addresses head-on the objection that hypnosis manufactures abduction memories, citing a *Science News* study but noting its questions were deliberately leading, and concedes hypnosis "is not a completely trustworthy scientific tool." He reproduces verbatim transcripts of regressions covering October 4 and December 26, 1985, worrying aloud that he might unconsciously *want* powerful visitors to appear to save a troubled world.

### Three: The Color of the Dark

A more reflective chapter on the psychological toll. Strieber walks the streets of New York "trapped" between two unacceptable alternatives — that he is the victim of a rare disorder, or that "my whole life might have proceeded according to a hidden agenda." Reading Hopkins's *Missing Time* and joining a colloquy of others who recall being taken, he finds that lifelong, multiple-episode patterns are commonplace. He recalls his own pre-memory unease — buying expensive burglar alarms in summer 1985 and a shotgun in October — and notes the staring, owl-like faces that now haunt him.

### Four: The Sky Beneath My Feet

Strieber journeys back through his past for earlier traces of the phenomenon, struggling with the implication that his "conscious life was nothing more than a disguise for another reality." He surfaces a buried childhood memory (summer 1957) of apparently being taken from a train, among other odd incidents, and reviews the years for hints — finding, disturbingly, that he had felt even more fearful in central Texas, where he grew up, than in New York.

### Five: Alliance of the Lost

The family dimension. Strieber describes how he initially hid everything from his wife Anne and his son. Anne is hypnotized (March 13, 1986) by a different psychiatrist, Dr. Robert Naiman, with Budd Hopkins present, specifically so the questioner would not carry prior knowledge. Her sessions suggest she was "made to believe that my mental health depends on her not remembering" — that she had been instructed to provide a "safe haven in ordinary reality." He recounts the March 15 sighting of a little girl at the country house, after which Anne, characteristically curious, took over the intellectual direction of their inquiry.

### Six: A Structure in the Air

The speculative and historical chapter. Strieber surveys a thousand years of disk sightings, airships, and "little men," and lays out the possibilities for what the visitors could be: from another planet; from earth but radically different; from another dimension or from this dimension but another time; from within us (gods we created turning out to be real); or a side effect of a natural electromagnetic phenomenon. He situates the experience alongside fairy folklore and historical visitor encounters (e.g., the 1491 Cardan case), insisting the answer cannot be a recent, simple extraterrestrial visit.

### Epilogue

Strieber declines to settle the question: "I do not have it in me to be a believer. Nor can I be a true skeptic." He notes the recurrent apocalyptic undertone in abduction accounts, compares science's openness to new things with theology's resistance (citing Einstein's reversed 1932 view on nuclear energy), and reports that the visitors did not strike him as superior so much as "wiser, but also more simple and unformed" — even "frail, limited individuals far from home." He reads the two triangles inscribed on his arm as a symbol that "we are each a tiny, complete universe."

### Appendix I & II: Klein Statement and Polygraph Results

Dr. Donald F. Klein, MD, Director of Research at the New York State Psychiatric Institute, states Strieber is "not suffering from a psychosis," shows no anxiety, mood, or personality disorder, and adapted to "a high level of uncertainty" in a psychologically healthy way. The second appendix reports Strieber's October 31, 1986 polygraph by Ned Laurendi (25 years' experience), which he passed — having secretly lied on two control questions to test the examiner, who detected both. Strieber stresses the result confirms only that he honestly described what he believes he perceived, not that his recollection is objectively correct.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Strieber is exhaustively self-skeptical: he seriously entertains hypnopompic hallucination, a drug-like state (he even names tetrodotoxin), "terror amnesia," temporal-lobe/brain mechanisms, an emerging belief-system/"religion," and mass hysteria — alongside the literal-visitor reading. He frames the core dilemma as "either visitors are actually here, or the human mind is creating something… close to a physical reality," concluding only that "it is not presently understood by science" and, of the visitors, "I do not know what it is." He stresses much of his memory was conscious *before* hypnosis, to rebut the "hypnosis-artifact" charge.
