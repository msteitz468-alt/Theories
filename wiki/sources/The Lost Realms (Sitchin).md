## Source Description

*The Lost Realms* (1990) is Book IV of the *Earth Chronicles*, extending Sitchin's thesis to the **Americas**. He argues that the civilizations of Mesoamerica and the Andes (Olmec, Maya, Aztec, Inca) were not independent developments but later outposts of the same [[Nefilim (Sitchin)|Anunnaki]] enterprise — established for **gold and tin**, and presided over by the same gods under new names. His central identification is that the feathered-serpent god **Quetzalcoatl/Kukulcan** was the Sumerian-Egyptian god **Thoth/Ningishzidda**, exiled from the Old World and bringing calendar, pyramid, and metallurgical knowledge to the New.

Sitchin reads the colossal **Olmec stone heads** as portraits of Africans brought by the Anunnaki as laborers, finds Near-Eastern parallels in Andean monuments (Tiahuanaco, Sacsahuaman, the gold-working of the Incas), and treats New World "white bearded god" traditions as memories of the same visitors. Per the wiki's strict-extraction policy, claims are catalogued **as presented**; these diffusionist readings are rejected by mainstream Americanist archaeology (see [[Pre-Columbian contact and the Mound Builder myth assessed (Feder)]]).

## Structure

Three parts — *El Dorado* (the conquistadors' gold and its divine associations), *Realm of the Serpent Gods* (Mesoamerica; Quetzalcoatl), and *Skywatchers in the Jungles* / *Strangers from Across the Seas* (astronomy, transatlantic contact) — across roughly a dozen chapters.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Sitchin's case is transatlantic-diffusionist: he treats parallels between Old and New World monuments, calendars, and "bearded white gods" as proof of contact, where mainstream archaeology reads them as independent invention. He acknowledges relying on contested colonial-era chronicles for some Mesoamerican traditions.
