## Source Description

*The Mothman Prophecies* (1975) is John A. Keel's first-person account of the wave of paranormal events he investigated in and around Point Pleasant, West Virginia, in 1966–67 — sightings of a winged, red-eyed creature dubbed **Mothman**, UFO lights, **Men in Black**, contactee encounters, poltergeist activity, and prophetic premonitions that, in Keel's framing, culminated in the December 1967 collapse of the **Silver Bridge** (46 dead). Far from arguing for nuts-and-bolts extraterrestrials, Keel advances his **"ultraterrestrial"** thesis: the phenomena are produced by non-human intelligences that have always coexisted with humanity and manifest in whatever form the percipient's era and beliefs expect.

Keel writes as a skeptical investigative journalist who says he is "not concerned with beliefs but with the cosmic mechanism which has generated and perpetuated those beliefs," and that he shelved roughly fifty reports for every one he published. The book is a foundational text of the **interdimensional / control-system** school of ufology, closely kindred to Jacques Vallée's work already in this wiki.

## Structure

19 chapters, opening with "Beelzebub Visits West Virginia" (Keel's own bearded-stranger anecdote) and proceeding through "The Creep Who Came in from the Cold," "Mothman!", the Men in Black, the contactees, and the prophecies, to the Silver Bridge disaster.

## Chapter Summaries

### Chapter 1: Beelzebub Visits West Virginia

Keel opens by debunking his own legend: the bearded "devil" who knocked on a Point Pleasant farmhouse door one rainy November 1967 night — and whose appearance preceded the Silver Bridge deaths of the couple inside — was just Keel himself, lost after his car ran off the road. From this he draws his method and thesis: he is "not concerned with beliefs but with the cosmic mechanism which has generated and perpetuated those beliefs," and he chose Point Pleasant as a "microcosm" of concentrated paranormal activity. He introduces the idea that the phenomena (which he and Ivan Sanderson group as OINTs) have always coexisted with humanity, are "not from outer space," and may be tulpas or aberrations of the human mind, and he dismisses Erich von Däniken's ancient-astronaut claims as "simplistic" and dishonest.

### Chapter 2: The Creep Who Came in from the Cold

Set in the grief-stricken Point Pleasant of December 1967 a week after the Silver Bridge collapse, the chapter centers on reporter Mary Hyre, the town's information hub, beset by newsmen. Two short, dark-complexioned men in black overcoats appear in her office asking about "flying saucer activity," opening Keel's treatment of the Men in Black as part of the phenomenon rather than government agents.

### Chapter 3: The Flutter of Black Wings

A historical survey of winged-man and giant-bird sightings — the 1877–80 "winged human form" over Coney Island, the 1948 wave of plane-sized birds across Illinois, "birdmen" in Washington and Texas, and cross-cultural folklore of Thunderbirds, the Garuda, and Mexican flying ikals. Keel argues these recurring red-eyed, bat-winged "Mothman"-type apparitions are a normal, perennial condition of the planet, ignored by saucer enthusiasts fixated on disks.

### Chapter 5: The Cold Who Came Down in the Rain

The contactee case of Woodrow Derenberger, a Mineral Wells, West Virginia, appliance salesman who in November 1966 reported being stopped on the highway by a grinning humanoid named Indrid Cold, who claimed to come from a place called Lanulos and communicated mentally. Keel uses Derenberger to examine the contactee pattern and the absurd, belief-confirming character of such encounters.

### Chapter 6: Mothman!

The founding sighting: at 11:30 P.M. on November 15, 1966, two young couples — the Scarberrys and Mallettes — driving through the TNT area (a derelict WWII explosives site of igloos and tunnels in the McClintic Wildlife Station) saw a gray, man-shaped creature over six feet tall with folded wings and two glowing red eyes "like automobile reflectors," which pursued their car. The press dubbed the creature Mothman.

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Keel is pointedly self-critical about evidence and belief. He opens by debunking his *own* legend (the "Beelzebub" stranger was just Keel, lost in the rain), warns that "sincere eyewitnesses can—and do—grossly misinterpret what they have seen," and argues that "once you have established a belief, the phenomenon adjusts its manifestations to support that belief and thereby escalate it." He explicitly criticizes Erich von Däniken's ancient-astronaut claims as "simplistic" and "deliberate misrepresentations," and rejects the extraterrestrial hypothesis in favor of his ultraterrestrial one — while conceding the alternative is that these beings "do not exist at all" and are "aberrations of the human mind."
