## Overview

The catalogue of 72 spirits known as the **Ars Goetia** — the first book of the *Lemegeton* — is
treated very differently by the wiki's sources that describe it. One presents it as a working
operative manual; one reprints its earlier ancestor; one is the modern edition that fixed its
canonical form; and one catalogues it as folklore within worldwide demonology. This page compares how
each frames *the same 72 spirits* — their number, their nature, and what the text is *for*. Each
position is attributed and **not adjudicated**; the ranks, seals, and rituals are recorded
descriptively, not endorsed.

## Side-by-side comparison

| Source | What the Goetia *is* here | Count | Framing of the spirits | Ritual apparatus given |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Lemegeton / *Lesser Key* (Peterson ed.)** | The operative grimoire itself — a manual for evoking and constraining the spirits | **72** ("seaventy two Mighty kings or Princes") | Ranked infernal nobility (kings, dukes, princes, marquises, earls, presidents) whom Solomon bound in a brass vessel and cast into a lake at Babylon | Full: circle & triangle, seals in specific metals, three-stage conjurations, the curse, the four governing kings (Amaymon, Corson, Ziminiar, Gaap) |
| **Weyer, *Pseudomonarchia Daemonum*** (reprinted in Peterson's Appendix 2) | The earlier *ancestor* list from which the Goetia grew | **69** princes | Demons and their "offices," with an exorcism/summons — but **no seals** | Partial: summons/conjuration, offices; omits the sigils |
| **Mathers & Crowley, *The Goetia*** (1904 ed.; cited by Bane & the Crowley corpus) | The modern critical/occult edition that fixed the canonical text and popularized it | **72** (Mathers added 3 to Weyer's 69) | Same infernal nobility; Crowley reframes evocation psychologically (spirits as portions of the human brain) | Inherits the *Lemegeton* apparatus; Crowley supplies the wider theory of evocation |
| **Bane, *Encyclopedia of Demons*** | A reference *catalogue* placing the 72 within worldwide demonology | **72** ("Spirits of Solomon") | Nearly all 72 described as **Fallen Angels**; entries give culture, rank, legions, appearance, specialty, banishing saint | Descriptive only — appearance, capabilities, method of binding per demon |

## Where they converge

1. **The same ranked hierarchy.** All four give the infernal nobility of kings/dukes/princes/
   marquises/earls/presidents, each spirit with a number of commanded **legions**, a described
   **appearance**, and an **office** or specialty — see
   [[The Ars Goetia catalogs 72 spirits — kings, dukes, princes, marquises, earls, and presidents — bound by Solomon in a brass vessel]]
   and Bane's [[Goetic grimoire demons (Bane)]].
2. **A shared textual lineage.** Both Peterson and Bane trace the line **Weyer (69) → Mathers (72,
   1904) → Crowley** — see
   [[Weyer's Pseudomonarchia Daemonum is an earlier catalogue of demons and their offices, a direct textual ancestor of the Goetia]].
   The "72" is a later stabilization of an unstable list.
3. **Practical orientation.** Every version is oriented toward *summoning*: exact appearance,
   capabilities, and means of binding. Even Bane, a folklore reference, preserves this operative data.
4. **Convergent detail across independent entries.** Bael/Baal (king in the East, three heads incl.
   cat and toad, 66 legions, grants invisibility) and Asmoday/Asmodeus (three-headed, under Amaymon,
   72 legions) match closely between the primary *Lemegeton* text and Bane's catalogue.

## Where they sharply diverge

- **The number.** Weyer's 69 vs the canonical 72 — the difference is the three spirits Mathers added
  in 1904, per Bane.
- **What the text is *for*.** The *Lemegeton* is an operative manual (
  [[Goetic evocation compels spirits within a protective circle and triangle by divine names and the threat of the brass vessel]]
  ); Weyer's *Pseudomonarchia* is famously a *skeptic's* exposé that deliberately **withholds the
  seals** (blunting its practical use); Bane is a neutral reference catalogue; Crowley turns it into a
  psychological system.
- **Ontology of the spirits.** The *Lemegeton* treats them as bound external spirits; Bane classes
  them as **Fallen Angels** within Christian demonology; Crowley (in the wiki's
  [[Magick in Theory and Practice (Crowley)]] corpus) treats evocation as operating on parts of the
  magician's own mind — a psychological rather than literal-demonic model.
- **Seals.** Present and essential in the *Lemegeton* (made in specific metals) and its Mathers/Crowley
  descendant; **absent** in Weyer's ancestor list; incidental in Bane.

## Notes on source posture

A primary operative grimoire in a modern critical edition (Peterson), a 16th-century demon-catalogue
that was itself an argument *against* witch-hunting (Weyer), the early-20th-century occult edition that
canonized and psychologized the text (Mathers/Crowley), and a 21st-century folklore reference (Bane).
The wiki records what each treats the Goetia *as*, without ruling on the reality of the spirits.
