## Overview

Many sources in this wiki agree that a small group at the top wields outsized power over American life. They divide on the **epistemological** question — *what kind of thing* that power is, and *how we could know it*. Is elite rule a **visible, documentable power structure** (class domination through institutions you can study in public records), a **hidden conspiracy** (a small, secret, intentional cabal executing a plan), or is "the cabal" itself best understood as a **recurring style of belief** to be studied rather than asserted?

The 2022 ingest of G. William Domhoff's *Who Rules America?* makes this comparison newly possible: Domhoff is a scholar who insists elite power is **real and highly concentrated** — agreeing with the conspiracists about the *outcome* — while rejecting their *method* and siding with the skeptics on what counts as evidence. Per the wiki's contradiction protocol, each position is attributed to its source and **not adjudicated**.

This page is the *method/evidence* companion to two content-focused pages: [[Is history steered by a deliberate cabal or an emergent network? Models compared]] (causation within the conspiracy-history sources) and [[Models of the New World Order Elites Compared]] (a taxonomy of who-is-on-the-list claims).

## The three poles

1. **Power-structure social science.** *[[Who Rules America? (Domhoff)]]* — the anchor. Elites dominate through four visible networks (corporate community, social upper class, policy-planning network, candidate-selection) that furnish a documentable **[[The power elite]]**; leaders are fallible, number in the thousands, and are studied through legally required public records. Domhoff explicitly contrasts this with conspiracy theory: [[Domhoff: power-structure analysis is not a conspiracy theory]].
2. **Conspiracy theory.** *[[None Dare Call It Conspiracy (Allen & Abraham, 1971)]]*, *[[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]]*, *[[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]*, *[[Conspirators' Hierarchy: The Story of the Committee of 300 (Coleman)]]* — power is a small, secret, intentional cabal (the "Insiders," a hidden government, Majesty Twelve, the Committee of 300) executing a long-range plan.
3. **Meta-analytic / skeptical.** *[[The Paranoid Style in American Politics (Hofstadter)]]*, *[[A Culture of Conspiracy (Barkun)]]*, *[[Real Enemies (Olmsted)]]* — these do not draw up a list of rulers; they study conspiracy *belief* itself (its psychology, structure, and the real government secrecy that feeds it).

**Carroll Quigley** (*[[Tragedy and Hope (Quigley)]]*) sits between poles 1 and 2: an establishment historian who documented a *real* semi-open network (the Rhodes–Milner Round Table → CFR) yet, like Domhoff, refused the monolithic-conspiracy reading — even as the pole-2 authors cite him as their proof.

## Where Domhoff bridges the divide

Domhoff is the hinge that makes the three poles legible at once, because he splits what the others fuse — *concentration* of power from *conspiracy* as its mechanism.

- **With the conspiracists, on the outcome.** Domhoff's data say power is genuinely concentrated: [[Domhoff: power is measured by three indicators — who governs, who benefits, who wins]] and the class-domination thesis [[Domhoff: the United States is a case of class domination rooted in the early ascendancy of business]] would not embarrass Allen or Marrs. His [[The policy-planning network]] even covers the *same institutions* (CFR, foundations, think tanks) that the conspiracists name — compare [[Domhoff: a policy-planning network of foundations and think tanks shapes the policies that become law]] with Cooper's [[Claim: Cooper asserts the CFR controls the entire US government and every CIA director has been a member]] and Coleman's [[The Committee of 300 ("the Olympians") is the supreme hidden world government]].
- **With the skeptics, on the method.** Yet Domhoff's four-part test for *why it is not a conspiracy* — leaders act from institutional/profit motives not a lust for power, they are fallible not omniscient, they number in the thousands not hundreds, and their actions are documented not secret — is precisely the diagnostic the meta-analysts apply to belief. It dovetails with Barkun's account of self-sealing logic ([[Barkun argues that NWO superconspiracy theories develop an internal logic of expanding inclusion that makes them nearly unfalsifiable and prone to absorbing all organizations as enemy fronts]], [[Barkun argues stigmatized knowledge operates through a proof-by-rejection inversion in which rejection by mainstream institutions is taken as evidence of truth]]), Hofstadter's [[Hofstadter argues the central image of the paranoid style is a vast conspiracy as the motive force of history]], and Olmsted's [[Olmsted argues that official secrecy and the government's own conspiracies are the real threat to democracy and the primary cause of conspiracy culture]].

## The crux: same facts, opposite readings

The Council on Foreign Relations is the sharpest test case. The *same* institution is:
- a node in a **studyable policy-planning network** for Domhoff ([[Domhoff: the power elite is the leadership arm of the corporate rich]]);
- a **documented but non-conspiratorial** Round Table outgrowth for Quigley ([[The Round Table Groups founded the RIIA and the Council on Foreign Relations]], [[Cecil Rhodes founded a secret society in 1891 to extend British rule]]);
- the **command center of a hidden government** for the conspiracists ([[A modern interlocking network of elite bodies forms a hidden government (Marrs)]], [[History is planned and engineered by a self-perpetuating clique of international bankers known as the Insiders]]);
- and an **artifact of conspiracist belief-structure** for the meta-analysts.

What separates the poles is therefore not the *facts* of elite networks — all sides accept those — but the *inference* drawn from them: visible class domination, secret cabal, or a style of belief. Domhoff's distinctive move is to claim the first while explicitly refusing the second and supplying the skeptics' method for telling them apart.
