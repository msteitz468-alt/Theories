## Overview

Five scientifically credentialed authors in this wiki argue that the **materialist/physicalist worldview is incomplete** and that consciousness is fundamental rather than a by-product of matter — but each reaches that conclusion from a *different discipline and a different kind of evidence*. This page lines up their **arguments and evidential strategies**: what each treats as the decisive data, what they say materialism gets wrong, and what they put in its place. Per the wiki's contradiction protocol, positions are attributed to their sources and **not adjudicated**.

This is the **evidential-strategy** companion to [[Metaphysical Reality Models Compared]], which compares *experiential / esoteric* reality systems (Campbell, Zeland, Talbot, Hicks, Monroe) by their cosmology and manifestation mechanics. The two overlap only at **Donald Hoffman**, who appears in both because his work is simultaneously an empirical-science argument and a full metaphysics.

## The five arguments

1. **Empirical / parapsychological — Dean Radin, *[[The Conscious Universe (Radin)]]*.** The case is *data-first*: a century of controlled psi experiments, pooled by **meta-analysis**, shows mind reaching beyond the brain and biasing matter — effects materialism says are impossible, therefore materialism is incomplete.
2. **Philosophy of science — Rupert Sheldrake, *[[Science Set Free (Sheldrake)]]*.** The case is *diagnostic*: materialism rests on **ten unproven dogmas**; once they are reopened, nature looks like an evolving organism with inherent memory (**morphic resonance**) and minds that extend beyond brains.
3. **Physics / cosmology — Robert Lanza, *[[Biocentrism (Lanza)]]*.** The case is *interpretive*: the quantum **observer effect** and cosmic **fine-tuning** are best resolved by making the observer primary — life and consciousness create the universe, and space and time are modes of perception.
4. **Evolutionary cognitive science — Donald Hoffman, *[[The Case Against Reality (Why Evolution Hid the Truth from Our Eyes)]]*.** The case is *formal*: his **[[Fitness-Beats-Truth (FBT) Theorem]]** argues natural selection hides objective reality behind a perceptual "interface," so space-time and objects are species-specific icons, and what underlies them is a network of **conscious agents** ([[Conscious Realism]]).
5. **Systems philosophy / unification — Ervin Laszlo, *[[Science and the Akashic Field (Laszlo)]]*.** The case is *consilience-driven*: a catalog of **"puzzles of coherence"** across quantum physics, cosmology, biology, and consciousness can only be explained, he argues, by a cosmos-wide **information field** rooted in the quantum vacuum — the **[[The Akashic field (Laszlo)|Akashic field]]** — which records and conveys "in-formation" and makes the universe an integral, self-remembering whole.

## Where they converge

- **Consciousness is the ground, not the product.** All five invert the materialist order. Compare [[Lanza: biocentrism holds that life and consciousness create the universe]], [[Consciousness is fundamental; the world is a network of conscious agents (conscious realism)]], [[Sheldrake: materialism is a belief system, not a proven fact]], and Laszlo's insistence that a real theory of everything must include mind ([[Laszlo: an integral "theory of everything" must include consciousness, not just matter]]) with Radin's evidence that mind is not brain-bound ([[Radin: the accumulated scientific evidence shows psi is real]]).
- **A memory/information field in nature — Sheldrake and Laszlo converge most tightly.** Sheldrake's **morphic resonance** ([[Sheldrake: morphic resonance gives nature an inherent collective memory]]) and Laszlo's **A-field** ([[Laszlo: nature stores and conveys "in-formation" that makes evolution coherent and cumulative]]) are near-twin proposals: a field that feeds the past back into the present to explain the coherence of form and the speed of evolution. They arrive at the same posited mechanism from a biologist's and a systems-philosopher's starting points respectively.
- **Space and time are not the bedrock.** Lanza ([[Lanza: space and time are forms of animal perception, not external realities]]) and Hoffman ([[Interface Theory of Perception (ITP)]]) independently demote space-time to a perceptual construct — a point the esoteric systems on the [[Metaphysical Reality Models Compared]] page reach experientially.
- **Materialism is a commitment, not a result.** Sheldrake ([[Sheldrake: modern science is constrained by ten unproven dogmas]]) and Hoffman both argue the physicalist default is an unexamined assumption rather than a finding.
- **The mind is not confined to the brain.** Sheldrake's extended mind ([[Sheldrake: minds are extended beyond the brain (telepathy and the sense of being stared at)]]) and Radin's non-local psi land on the same conclusion from philosophy and from lab data respectively — and Laszlo folds transpersonal/nonlocal mind into the broader coherence the A-field is meant to explain ([[Laszlo: deep coherence across quantum, cosmic, biological, and conscious domains points to an information field]]).

## Where they differ — the real fault lines

- **How much weight rests on contested data.** Radin's case stands or falls on the validity of **meta-analysis** and the answer to the file-drawer objection ([[Radin: meta-analysis defeats the chance and file-drawer objections to psi]]); Hoffman's rests on a **mathematical theorem** about perception, not on anomalous phenomena at all. They could both be right, but almost no evidence is shared between them.
- **Does the argument need the paranormal?** Radin and Sheldrake actively embrace psi/telepathy as evidence; Lanza and Hoffman build their cases entirely from *mainstream* results (quantum mechanics; evolutionary game theory) and do not require psi — a sharp strategic divide over respectability and risk. **Laszlo straddles it**: he leans on mainstream anomalies (entanglement, cosmic fine-tuning) *and* on transpersonal/psi coherence, treating both as data for the same field — which broadens his base but also exposes him to both camps' objections at once.
- **How far the metaphysics goes.** Hoffman, Lanza, and Laszlo offer a complete ontology (conscious agents / biocentric universe / an in-formed Metaverse — [[Laszlo: the universe is one of a cyclic series — an in-formed Metaverse]]); Sheldrake offers a reformed science (habits + morphic fields) that stops short of idealism; Radin is the most cautious, arguing the *data* without committing to a full theory of reality.
- **Where consciousness sits: created, revealed, or recorded.** Lanza says the observer *brings reality into being* ([[Lanza: the observer brings physical reality into being]]); Hoffman says evolution *hides* a pre-existing reality of conscious agents behind the interface; Laszlo offers a third stance — reality is *recorded and fed back* by an information field in the quantum vacuum ([[Laszlo: the quantum vacuum (zero-point field) is the physical basis of the Akashic field]]), so coherence is field-mediated rather than observer-made. Creation vs. concealment vs. cosmic memory.

## Notes on source posture

The cluster spans a parapsychologist (Radin), a biologist-heretic (Sheldrake), a physician–stem-cell scientist with an astronomer co-author (Lanza), a tenured cognitive scientist (Hoffman), and a systems philosopher (Laszlo). Their shared adversary is the same — physicalism — but they agree least on *what counts as evidence*, which is exactly what this comparison surfaces: the case "against materialism" is really five different cases that happen to point the same way. All five are disputed by the scientific mainstream, on grounds specific to each (the robustness of psi meta-analyses; the status of morphic resonance; whether the observer effect requires consciousness; whether the FBT theorem licenses its metaphysical conclusion; and whether the quantum vacuum can be read as an information field at all).
