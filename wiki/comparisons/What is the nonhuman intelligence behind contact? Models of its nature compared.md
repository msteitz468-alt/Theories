## Overview

This page compares what the intelligent **entities** behind UFO/contact experiences are *said to be* —
their nature and identity — rather than the explanatory models of the phenomenon as a whole (for that,
see [[Competing models of the UFO phenomenon compared across sources]]). Strikingly, most of these
sources **reject the simple "extraterrestrials in spaceships" image**, and several connect the
entities to older categories of spirits, fairies, and demons. Each position is attributed and **not
adjudicated**.

## Where they converge

1. **Something intelligent and nonhuman is real and interacts with humans** — even the cautious Hynek
   treats it as a genuine unknown.
2. **It is not simply "ET in a spaceship."** Vallée's
   [[Vallée argues the extraterrestrial hypothesis is an insufficient explanation]], Keel, Strieber,
   and Pasulka all push past the nuts-and-bolts ET reading.
3. **It shapes itself to human belief and culture** — Vallée's
   [[The phenomenon's secondary features adapt to the witness's culture]], Keel's ultraterrestrials,
   and Pasulka's media/confirmation-bias argument all say the entities mirror the observer.
4. **Continuity with older folklore** — fairies and Magonia (Vallée), omens and tricksters (Keel),
   angels and demons (Pasulka's experiencers, Bane's traditions). **Hancock states this continuity most
   explicitly**: the spirits met in shamanic trance, the fairies of folklore, and the abduction "greys"
   are, he argues, one phenomenon — and he roots it in the *visionary/entheogenic* state, identifying
   the DMT "machine elves" with the "ancient teachers of mankind" ([[Hancock: DMT "machine elves" are the "ancient teachers of mankind"]]). Eliade supplies the scholarly backbone for the oldest member of
   that series — the shaman's helping spirits and the "technique of ecstasy" itself.
5. **A strong psychic / consciousness / religious component** rather than a purely physical one — for
   Hancock and Dispenza the beings are reached through altered states of consciousness, and Hancock
   leaves open that the visionary realm is an objectively real parallel dimension
   ([[Hancock: the visionary spirit world may be an objectively real parallel dimension]]).

## Where they sharply diverge — the identity axis

- **Extraterrestrial** (the popular view most here reject) vs **interdimensional / ultraterrestrial**
  (Vallée, Keel) vs **spiritual / demonic** (Bane's traditions; the *ángeles y demonios* of Pasulka's
  witnesses) vs **"us" — nonlocal consciousness, future humans, or AI** (Dispenza; Pasulka's Simone)
  vs **deliberately unknown / agnostic** (Hynek, Strieber).
- **Disposition.** Malevolent trickster / manipulative control system (Keel, Vallée's
  [[The absurd behavior of UFO entities keeps science away and gives the myth religious overtones]];
  Bane's demons) vs genuinely ambiguous (Strieber) vs potentially benevolent/evolutionary (Dispenza;
  parts of Pasulka).
- **Physical vs. psychic.** "Real and physical but not extraterrestrial" (Vallée) vs primarily a
  phenomenon of consciousness (Dispenza, Pasulka).

## Notes on source posture

Astronomer-scientist (Hynek), scientist-skeptic-of-ETH (Vallée), investigative journalist of the
paranormal (Keel), first-person experiencer (Strieber), religious-studies scholar (Pasulka),
meditation teacher (Dispenza), a folklore/mythology reference (Bane), an alternative-history writer
drawing on his own entheogenic experiences (Hancock), and a historian of religions (Eliade) whose
*Shamanism* is the scholarly source the continuity-theorists build on. The wiki records each account's
claims as attributed positions.
