## Overview

Four of the wiki's ingested sources make overlapping but distinct arguments about how the U.S.
Federal Reserve came to be, who really owns and directs it, and what its deeper purpose is. This
page sets their accounts side by side. Following the wiki's contradiction protocol, each
position is attributed to its source and **not adjudicated**; the page records where the books
agree and where they diverge.

- **Eustace Mullins** — *[[A Study of the Federal Reserve and its Secrets (Mullins)]]* and
  *[[Secrets of the Federal Reserve: The London Connection (Mullins)]]*: a muck-raking financial
  history framing the Fed as a privately owned instrument of a London–Rothschild banking
  dynasty.
- **G. Edward Griffin** — *[[The Creature from Jekyll Island (Griffin)]]*: a structural/economic
  critique treating the Fed as a self-serving banking **cartel** legalized by government.
- **Carroll Quigley** — *[[Tragedy and Hope (Quigley)]]*: an establishment historian documenting
  how investment bankers built central-bank control as part of "financial capitalism."
- **Jim Marrs** — *[[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]]*: a synthesist who folds the Fed into a larger
  genealogy of secret-society and elite networks.

## 2. Where the sources converge

All four agree on several points (recorded as their shared assertions, not as established fact):

1. **The Fed was conceived in secrecy at Jekyll Island.** See
   [[The Federal Reserve Act was drafted in secret at Jekyll Island in 1910]] and
   [[The Aldrich Plan and the Federal Reserve Act were the same plan under different names]] (Mullins).
2. **The same banker, Paul Warburg, was central to its design.** See
   [[Paul Warburg was the principal author of the Federal Reserve Act]] (Mullins).
3. **Credit creation was removed from democratic/Congressional oversight.** Griffin's
   [[Banks create money out of nothing via the Mandrake Mechanism]] and
   [[The Federal Reserve is a banking cartel with five objectives]] meet Quigley's
   [[Investment bankers controlled the central banks from behind the scenes]] and
   [[Financial capitalism aimed to create a world system of control via the central banks]].
4. **The institution serves private banking interests over the public.** Compare Griffin's
   [[The Federal Reserve is a lender of last resort that funds bailouts through inflation]] and
   [[Inflation is a hidden tax that transfers wealth to government and banks]].

## 3. Where they diverge

- **Conspiracy vs. cartel vs. network.** Mullins frames a deliberate dynastic *conspiracy*
  controlled "from London via Rothschild gold-price fixing" (see
  [[The Federal Reserve is controlled from London via Rothschild gold-price fixing]] and
  [[J. P. Morgan was a secret Rothschild agent originating with George Peabody]]). Griffin
  frames a *cartel* pursuing rational self-interest, not a bloodline plot. Quigley — an
  insider-historian who *approved* of much of what he described — frames a *network* of bankers
  building a system, explicitly resisting the "single conspiracy" reading. Marrs *synthesizes*
  all of these into one continuous hidden-government story.
- **Foreign control vs. domestic capture.** Mullins stresses *foreign* (British/Rothschild)
  ownership; Griffin stresses a *domestic* American banking cartel; Quigley stresses an
  *Anglo-American* establishment.
- **Causation of crises.** Mullins asserts the Fed *deliberately* engineered downturns —
  [[The Federal Reserve deliberately caused the Great Depression of 1929-31]] and
  [[The Federal Reserve deliberately caused the Agricultural Depression of 1920-21]] — a claim
  of intent that Quigley's account treats more as mismanagement within "financial capitalism."

## 4. Notes on source posture

A key asymmetry: **Quigley is an establishment historian** who documents the banker network with
approval and on the basis of archival access, whereas **Mullins, Griffin, and Marrs write as
critics/exposers**. That several adversarial popular works and one approving academic converge on
the *factual skeleton* (Jekyll Island, Warburg, banker control of credit) while diverging sharply
on *intent and ownership* is itself the most useful thing this comparison surfaces.
