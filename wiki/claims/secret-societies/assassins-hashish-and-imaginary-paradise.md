## Claim Statement

Heckethorn recounts that the **Assassins** — an Ishmaelite order founded by **Hassan-i-Sabbah**, the "Old Man" or "Lord of the Mountain," from his fortress of Alamut — secured the fanatical obedience of their killers by two means: intoxicating them with **hashish** (whence the very name *Assassin* derives), and deluding selected devotees with a **simulated, drug-assisted "paradise."** So conditioned, the "self-sacrificers" would murder any victim and die without complaint.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

On the name and the drug (§136): "The term Assassins is a corruption of Hashishim, derived from hashish (the hemp plant), with which the chief intoxicated his followers when they entered on some desperate enterprise." Hassan reduced the nine degrees of the Lodge of Cairo to seven and placed himself at the head as *Seydna* (§§135–137).

On the devotion of the killers (§138): the followers were divided into "self-sacrificers" and "aspirants"; the first "joyfully gave their lives whenever it pleased the great master," going on their missions "clothed in a white tunic with a red sash, the colours of innocence and blood." Conrad of Montferrat was an early victim, stabbed by two Assassins who let themselves be baptized to get near him.

The "Imaginary Paradise" (§139) is described in detail: in a Persian valley a "most delightful spot" was made into "the most luxurious gardens, with pavilions splendidly furnished," peopled by "the most lovely and charming women." The chosen man "was first made drunk, and in this state carried into the valley," where on waking he believed he was in Elysium; he was then drugged again and carried home, and told that if he did his master's bidding "he could luxuriate in the same delights for the rest of his life," so that "the dupe… was ready to commit whatever crime was required of him." Heckethorn illustrates the resulting obedience (§141) with the lord ordering two of the faithful to stab themselves before a terrified count, and another to leap from a tower, claiming "Seventy thousand followers obey me in the same manner." The sect was destroyed when "the Mongolians, led by Prince Hulagu in 1256, attacked and overthrew the Assassins" (§143).

## Contexts Within the Source

Heckethorn frames the order as the "sanguinary and indelible" outgrowth of the secret Lodge of Cairo, whose doctrines "ended with denying all truth, morality, and justice." He treats the paradise tale with mild reserve ("The story goes…"; "it is said") and notes the figure of "seventy thousand" is "probably" exaggerated, "never estimated above forty thousand" by some writers — many of whom were "only aspirants," not the truly "faithful."
