## Claim Statement

Heckethorn presents the **Carbonari** ("charcoal-burners") as a 19th-century revolutionary secret society organised on the model of, and partly out of, the older French *Fendeurs* ("hewers"). Its members — "Good Cousins" — met in lodges called **vendite** (subdivided into *baracche*), used a charcoal-burning vocabulary and Catholic-Christian symbolism as a cover, and pursued the political object of liberty: the overthrow of tyranny and foreign (Austrian) oppression, and the unification of Italy as a free republic ("Ausonia").

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Heckethorn traces the society's mythic genealogies (a pretended descent from Philip of Macedon; the legend of Francis I of France initiated by charcoal-burners in a forest) but locates its "real origin" in twelfth-century leagues of charcoal-burners provoked by "severe forest laws," related to the Jura *Fendeurs* whose corporations "le bon cousinage" supplied the lower degree (§§543–544). He notes "powerful lords, members of the persecuted Order of the Temple" entered secret treaties with them, and that the Carboneria was introduced into Southern Italy by returning Neapolitan exiles, the first head *Vendita* formed at Capua in 1809, its rules "written in English, because the English Government desired to employ the society as a lever for the overthrow of Napoleon."

The ritual (§§545–552) is given at length: the lodge is "a room of wood in the shape of a barn"; the initiate is "a man lost in the wood" seeking "to conquer my passions"; symbols (block, cloth, water, fire, salt, crucifix, crown of thorns, furnace, the tree with roots in the air) are first given pious meanings, but their true revolutionary meanings are reserved for the highest degree. There Heckethorn states plainly: "The forest represents Italy… infested with wild beasts — that is, foreign oppressors. The tree with the roots in the air is a figure of kingdoms destroyed and thrones overthrown." In the **Grand Master Grand Elect** degree the symbols are decoded as instruments against the tyrant: "The cross serves to crucify the tyrant that persecutes us… The axe will separate his head from his body… The shovel will scatter his ashes to the wind." The summarised Carbonarist manifesto (§553, "The Ausonian Republic") lays out a republican constitution for a free Italy — twenty-one provinces, elected assemblies, abolition of hereditary titles and feudal rights, toleration of all creeds.

## Contexts Within the Source

Heckethorn treats the Carbonari sympathetically, as one of "the few secret associations" with a genuine liberating purpose: "the spirit that breathed in them was permanent and universal; and that it was the spirit of liberty and justice can scarcely be denied." He concedes "there was something theatrical in all this," but defends the ritual's psychological effect on initiates. He notes its ceremonies were "probably strictly followed on particularly important occasions only" and "not always and at all places alike."
