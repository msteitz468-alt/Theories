## Claim Statement

Heckethorn describes the **Holy Vehm** (*Vehmgerichte*) as the secret judicial tribunals of medieval Westphalia — a *judiciary* species of secret society, distinct from the religious and military orders — which arose amid the "violence and anarchy" of the German empire to "seize the guilty… punish them before they were aware of the blow," operating through initiated members ("the knowing ones"), secret signs, oaths and procedures, and executing its sentences (chiefly hanging) clandestinely.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

On origin and object (§166): the tribunals "arose during the period of violence and anarchy that distracted the German empire after the outlawry of Henry the Lion," when "might and violence took the place of right and justice"; their object was "the chastisement of crime," making the society "the instrument of public vengeance."

On organisation (§§167–168): Westphalia "comprehended the country between the Rhine and the Weser"; three degrees are named (*Stuhlherren*, *Schöppen*, *Frohnboten*); members "were called Wissende, 'the knowing ones,' or the initiated"; courts were "open" (*offenbares Ding*) or "secret" (*heimliche Acht*). The initiated had a secret language and recognition signs, and swore a fearful oath — "to serve the secret Vehm before anything that is illumined by the sun or bathed by rain," and "to denounce, if necessary, his parents and relations."

On procedure and execution (§§169–170): summonses sealed "with at least seven seals"; the accused who failed to appear was sentenced *in contumacia* and "the Imperial ban was pronounced," "followed by speedy execution… his goods and estates… forfeited, his wife a widow, and his children orphans." Declared *fehmbar*, he could be hanged by "any three initiated that met with him"; a knife left in the tree or in the wound marked a Vehmic execution. Betraying the secret of a man's condemnation "rendered the speaker liable to death."

On its terror and decay (§171): "the citation by a Westphalian free count was even more dreaded than that of the emperor"; in 1470 "three free counts summoned the emperor himself to appear before them." The institution "gradually degenerated" and was reformed, declining with the Roman law and Protestantism until "French legislation, in 1811, abolished the last free court in the county of Münster." Heckethorn adds the lurid tradition of execution by "Kissing the Virgin" — a spiked bronze statue and a pit of bladed cylinders (§172).

## Contexts Within the Source

Heckethorn explicitly introduces the Vehm as a new *type*: "an order of secret societies altogether different from preceding ones… judicial in their operations." He frames its existence as "amply justified" by the lawless conditions of the age and rests its authority "on… popular respect," while recording its later abuses and the "barbarous jurisdiction" it became.
