## Claim Statement

Heckethorn describes the **Albigenses** (and the closely-related **Cathari**) as a medieval dualist heresy — "the offspring of Manichæism" — that organised against the Church of Rome as a rival "religion of love," concealing its doctrines in degrees of initiation and (through the allied **Troubadours**) in obscure poetry, and that was ultimately "exterminated… by the sword of De Montfort and the fagots of the Inquisition."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

On their origin and aim (§§114–117): "none was more extensive and active than the Albigenses… the offspring of Manichæism," whose "blows were intended for Papal Rome alone." Toulouse "was the Rome of that church which had its pastors, bishops, provincial and general councils, like the official church," gathering "the dissenters of a great portion of Europe, all meditating the ruin of Rome." Heckethorn ties them to the Ghibelline / imperial cause ("The Albigenses were the Ghibellines of France"), to Frederick II and the Aragonese, and even to Dante, "imperialistic, tainted with that doctrine."

On secrecy and the Troubadours (§§118–122): the Troubadours are read as "the organizers of that vast conspiracy directed against the Church of Rome," their love-poetry a concealment in which "the mistress invoked… was the purified religion of love, personified as the Virgin Sophia"; the "Courts of Love" "may therefore have concealed far sterner objects," even the "'Lodges' of the Albigenses." They had four degrees and "secret signs of recognition."

On the Cathari (§§123–124): Milan, Florence, Orvieto and others were "active foci of the Cathari"; the sect "sought to conceal its operations," "bore great resemblance to Manichæism," believed in metempsychosis (seven transmigrations to reach the light), and "had communistic tendencies, and were averse to marriage."

On their destruction (§122): "these courts, as well as the race of troubadours, become extinct with the extinction of the Albigenses by the sword of De Montfort and the fagots of the Inquisition."

## Contexts Within the Source

Heckethorn sets the Albigenses within his broader thesis that the medieval heresies were the seedbed of later secret societies — "the sect of the Albigenses… fructified in its turn the germs of the Templars and Rosicrucians, and of all those associations that continued the struggle." He stresses that, unlike other sects, the Cathari concealed their doctrines "not only from the world at large, but even from its proselytes of inferior degrees," so that "we have only scanty notices of this sect."
