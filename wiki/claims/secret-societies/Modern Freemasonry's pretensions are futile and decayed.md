## Claim Statement

Heckethorn's verdict on present-day (1890s) Freemasonry is largely **dismissive**: it is "an institution which has outlived the object of its foundation," "a delusion and an anachronism." Since "all that is said and done in the lodges has for many years been in print," its promise of secrets is "a delusion" and its oaths "a farce"; it "diffuses no knowledge" and is in "decay." Yet he credits its *historical* value.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Heckethorn allows that when Masonry turned speculative it "opposed the political despotism then prevalent all over Europe and formed an anti-Inquisition to clerical obscurantism" — alongside the Illuminati — so that "the rapid progress achieved in modern times by humanity and toleration is undoubtedly due" in part to it. But now that knowledge is public, "a society that professes to keep knowledge for the few is but a retrograde institution." He cites Frederick III resigning the Grand-Mastership once "satisfied of the unsoundness and vanity of masonic pretensions," and condemns the "puerility of the symbolism" and "the paltriness of the secret when revealed." He treats sensational episodes — e.g. French/Italian/Spanish Masons "citing" Napoleon III, the Pope, and others before mock "tribunals" and condemning them to death — critically, as "insane excesses."
