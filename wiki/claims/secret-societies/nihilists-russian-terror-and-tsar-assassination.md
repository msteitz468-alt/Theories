## Claim Statement

Heckethorn traces the Russian **Nihilists** from an originally "literary and philosophical" movement into a revolutionary secret society that, after the failure of peaceful propaganda ("going among the people"), became "Nihilism militant" — a party of terror that, despite mass arrests and exiles, assassinated **Emperor Alexander II** on 13 March 1881.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

On the term and the founders (§§608–610): the word was coined by Turgenev in *Fathers and Sons* for a man "who respects nothing… who bows to no authority"; the "original Nihilists were not conspirators at all, but formed a literary and philosophical society" (c. 1860–1870), transformed by the Paris Commune and the International, with **Bakunin and Herzen** as "the real fathers of Nihilism." **Sergei Nechayeff**, who in 1869 slew his friend Ivanoff for threatening to leave the conspiracy, is treated at length, including his extradition, imprisonment in the fortress of Peter and Paul, and death.

On "going among the people" (§611): young men and women "of wealthy and aristocratic families renounced the comforts… to associate with the working classes and the peasantry," forming "thirty-seven revolutionary 'circles'"; but "among the peasantry their successes were not equal to their energy," and "the propaganda was stamped out with every circumstance of cruelty."

On the turn to terror (§612): "Nihilism doctrinaire having thus proved a failure, it became Nihilism militant"; a Moscow letter is quoted: "Kill, shoot, work, create riots!" Heckethorn then narrates the trials (Sophia Bardina and others) and the campaign against the Emperor.

On the assassination (§622): "The Executive Committee determined that now the emperor must die. Forty-seven volunteers presented themselves." On 13 March 1881 a first bomb (Ryssakoff) missed; the Tsar alighted and "a second bomb, thrown… by Ignatius Grinevizki… shattered both the legs of the emperor below the knees… Within one hour and a half the Tsar was dead." Sophia Perovskaia had given "the signal for throwing the bombs"; she and others were "hanged on the 15th April following."

## Contexts Within the Source

Heckethorn is notably sympathetic in tone, opening the section by quoting Wendell Phillips ("Nihilism is the righteous and honourable resistance of a people crushed under an iron foe") and the *Athenæum*'s description of "the most mysterious and romantic [movement] the world has ever known." He stresses the self-sacrifice of the propagandists and the conspirators, who "all died like heroes," while recording the Russian government's wholesale persecutions in detail.
