## Claim Statement

Heckethorn opens the work with a typology: secret societies fall under six heads — **Religious** (the Egyptian/Eleusinian Mysteries), **Military** (Knights Templars), **Judiciary** (the Vehmgerichte), **Scientific** (alchemists), **Civil** (Freemasons), and **Political** (Carbonari) — though, since the lines blur, he reduces them to "two comprehensive divisions of religious and political." He further argues that political secret societies are not mere conspiracies but "the providential temperers and safety-valves of the present and the powerful levers of the future."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He writes that without secret societies "the monologue of absolutism alone would occupy the drama of history," and that "every secret society is an act of reflection, therefore of conscience… in a certain manner the expression of conscience in history." This frames his treatment of the 19th-century revolutionary societies in Vol. II (Carbonari, Comuneros, Nihilists) as legitimate political reaction against despotism, not simply criminality.
