## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], Judaism is a direct evolution of the Egyptian religion of Atenism, and the biblical Moses is the historical pharaoh [[Akhenaten]] (or, in Freud's variant, an official in Akhenaten's entourage). The Exodus was not a flight of previously enslaved Hebrews but the expulsion or departure of Akhenaten's court, priesthood, and followers following the religious counter-revolution that restored the Amun cult.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The authors synthesize a wide range of scholars including Sigmund Freud (*Moses and Monotheism*), Ahmed Osman, Robert Feather, Dr. Karl Abraham, Manetho, Philo Judaeus, Flavius Josephus, and Justin Martyr — all of whom independently argued that Moses was born and raised as an Egyptian of noble or royal blood.

**Linguistic evidence cited:**
- The name "Moses" derives from the Egyptian *Mos* ("child")
- Abraham's original name *Abram* translates as "exalted father," a common ritual name for Egyptian kings; alternatively *Ab-ra-'am* = "the father of the House of Ra"
- Abraham's wife's new name *Sarah* is the Egyptian term for "princess"
- The word for "ark" or casket is the same in both Egyptian and Hebrew (described as a Hebrew "loan word" from Egyptian)

**Theological parallels cited:**
- Freud demonstrated that the Shema prayer is almost an exact copy of an Atenist prayer (Aten → Adonai via consonant shift)
- The Ten Commandments in Deuteronomy replicate Atenist strictures against graven images of multiple gods; those in Exodus mirror passages from the Egyptian Book of the Dead
- Psalm 104 parallels Akhenaten's Hymn to the Aten almost verbatim
- The Ark of the Covenant mirrors the ritual transport of the Aten at Amarna ceremonies
- Circumcision, mandatory in Judaism, had been obligatory among Egyptian royalty, the hereditary priesthood, and nobility since 4000 BCE
- The ten sephirot (attributes of God) in the Kabbala mirror the listed attributes of the Egyptian pharaohs
- The Amarna city was described as "the Holy Land" (Aketaten), prefiguring the Judaic usage
- Atenism sacrificed animals traditionally regarded as sacred — as did Moses

**Identification of Yuya as the patriarch Joseph:**
Ahmed Osman identified Joseph with Yuya, a powerful official whose tomb was discovered in the Valley of the Kings in 1905. The phrase "a father to Pharaoh" occurs both in Genesis 45:8 and in Yuya's own Book of the Dead. Yuya's daughter Tiye married Pharaoh Amenhotep III, making [[Akhenaten]] Yuya's grandson — and therefore of the bloodline of the patriarch Joseph.

**The "mixed multitude" and historical erasure:**
The authors argue that the emigration of Akenhaten's followers from Amarna was deliberately expunged from Egyptian records during Horemheb's reign. The treasure the Israelites took corresponds to the personal wealth of Akhenaten and the royal treasure of Amarna. The "mixed multitude" (Exodus 12:38) is identified as Akhenaten's court, priesthood, artisans, and foreign residents.

The conclusion: "the majority of scholars originating from different religious backgrounds agree that Judaism derived from Egyptian religious traditions. The similarities within both systems are so numerous that the case for the purely Egyptian origin and nature of early Judaism is proven beyond doubt."

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the central claim of Chapters 2–3 and establishes the Egyptian-gnostic pedigree that the book traces forward through the Temple priesthood and the Rex Deus families.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

- David Rohl argues the Exodus occurred during the reign of Dudimose (13th dynasty), not in Akhenaten's era — though this is characterized as a "minor difference of detail"
- Some scholars (cited as "several leading Israeli scholars") suggest the Exodus is pure mythology
- The historical invisibility of the Hebrew people in Egyptian records prior to the Mernephtah stele (1207 BCE) is acknowledged as a puzzle, though attributed to deliberate Horemheb-era erasure
- The authors acknowledge that the identification of Moses with Akhenaten "is still a matter of considerable debate"
