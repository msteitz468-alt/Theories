## Claim Statement

Wallace-Murphy and Hopkins argue that when the [[Knights Templar]] were suppressed in 1307 by Philip IV of France, only a fraction of the order was actually arrested. Of the 3,000+ Templars in France, only 620 were detained; the proportion arrested in other countries was even lower. The authors contend that advance warning of the arrests had leaked, allowing the majority to escape. Many of these survivors, especially those from [[Rex Deus]] families, subsequently infiltrated a network of existing and newly created chivalric orders, preserving and propagating Rex Deus tradition through alternative vehicles that were harder for Church or state to destroy simultaneously.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "Like a many-headed hydra, regrouped and redeployed by infiltrating other military and chivalric organizations. They had also learned the advantages that could accrue to their cause from having a well-disciplined executive arm positioned close to the seat of political power. However, this time they took great care to ensure that their new creations and affiliations differed from those of the Templars of old."

The orders identified by the authors as Rex Deus vehicles include:

- **The Scots Guard** (Garde Écossaise) — Created in France after 1424, drawn from St. Clairs, Setons, Montgomerys, Hamiltons, and Stewarts; the authors describe it as "a major conduit for Rex Deus traditions for three centuries."
- **Knights of Santiago** (Spain) — Provided refuge to fleeing Templars; Earl William St. Clair was a member. Its Reconquista credentials gave it "respectable façade" as cover for Rex Deus purposes.
- **Order of the Golden Fleece** (founded 1430 by Philip the Good of Burgundy) — Membership restricted to twenty-four knights from Rex Deus families; Pope Eugenius IV described them as "Maccabeans resurrected." The number twenty-four mirrors the twenty-four hereditary priestly families (ma'amadot) of the Temple at Jerusalem.
- **Order of the Croissant** (founded 1448 by René d'Anjou) — Required members to practise fraternal love, charity, and aid to the poor; eventually merged with another Rex Deus order to form the Order of the Fleur de Lys.
- **Imperial and Royal Court of the Dragon** (founded 1408 by King Sigismund of Hungary) — Twenty-four founding members pledged to "true and pure fraternity."
- **Order of the Bath** (England) — The authors' Rex Deus informant "Michael" alleged this may also have been a Rex Deus cover at its original foundation.
- **Knights of Alcantara and Knights of Calatrava** (Spain) — Founded with the involvement of Bernard of Clairvaux; the authors speculate they were designed from the outset as fall-back positions in the event of Templar destruction.
- **Knights Hospitaller** (Scotland) — The Hospitallers' Scottish actions in accounting for Templar lands as held in trust are cited as possible evidence of Rex Deus infiltration.

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter argues that Rex Deus learned from the catastrophic experience of having a single high-profile vehicle (the Templars). The new strategy was deliberately diversified: "use of so many orders also gives us an insight into the wisdom of these families, who, after the suppression of the Knights Templar, were not about to risk the failure of their divinely inspired mission by putting all their eggs in one basket."

The new orders also deliberately differed from the Templars: they owned no property, were ostensibly dependent on royal patronage, and "gave the illusion of being more interested in prestige than real power."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge a question they "cannot answer": for safety reasons, had the Rex Deus families now split into two or more completely separate groups that began to operate without knowledge of each other? They also note that their two self-declared Rex Deus informants possessed different ritual knowledge, suggesting distinct lines of transmission had evolved.
