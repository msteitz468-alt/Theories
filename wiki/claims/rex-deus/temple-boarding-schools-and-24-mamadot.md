## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the Temple of Jerusalem maintained two boarding schools — one for boys, one for girls — staffed by the twenty-four high-priestly families ([[The twenty-four ma'amadot (high-priestly families)]]). All pupils were drawn from the tribe of Levi. The high priests, ritually named according to rank (Melchizedek, Michael, Gabriel, etc.), were responsible for impregnating the female pupils upon reaching puberty. The girl Mary (Miriam) was impregnated by the high priest Gabriel; after her pregnancy was established she was married to Joseph of Tyre, a young man of Davidic descent. The child born was [[Jesus of Nazareth]].

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

This claim originates with the Rex Deus informant "Michael" ([[Prince Michael of Albany]]), who approached [[Tim Wallace-Murphy]] in 1994 and whose family had preserved this tradition for "over two thousand years." Wallace-Murphy states that despite the bizarreness of the account, Michael "seemed rational, well balanced, and completely sincere," and that the aspect of the story he expected to be hardest to corroborate — the existence of the temple schools and Mary's attendance — "proved, to our intense amazement, to be the easiest to corroborate."

The relevant passage from the Introduction:

> "Prior to the time of Jesus, he [Michael] claimed, the Temple of Jerusalem maintained two boarding schools staffed by high priests — one for boys and one for girls. Male graduates were destined to become high priests, rabbis, or leaders of Israel. All the pupils at both schools were drawn from the tribe of Levi, the hereditary priesthood of biblical times. The twenty-four ma'-madot, or high priests, who taught at the temple schools were the hereditary leaders of the Jewish religion."

And:

> "The high priests were not only responsible for administration and teaching, but for impregnating the female pupils when they reached puberty. Once the girls' pregnancies were established, they were married into the leading families of Israel."

And:

> "One pupil at the girls' school was Miriam, or Mary as she is known to history, the daughter of an earlier pupil named Anne. The high priest Gabriel impregnated Mary and, when her pregnancy was confirmed, arranged a marriage for her."

Mary rejected the first man chosen for her and accepted Joseph of Tyre (whose ancestor was Hiram of Tyre, known to Masonic legend as Hiram Abif), described as "a wealthy young man of Davidic descent." The child born of Gabriel and Mary was Jesus who, after childhood years in Egypt, returned to Jerusalem and attended the temple school himself.

The authors note that children born of these ritual unions were returned to the temple school at age seven, "further preserving the hereditary nature of the high priesthood and ensuring that the sacred bloodlines remained pure and unsullied."

The claim is presented not merely as family tradition but as an explanation for otherwise puzzling scriptural details, including the angel Gabriel's role in the Annunciation narrative — a priestly title, not a supernatural visitor.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the structural heart of the Rex Deus hypothesis as applied to Jesus: it explains his dual qualification as both a high-priestly initiate (through Gabriel) and of the Davidic bloodline (through Joseph of Tyre), making him a candidate for both the priestly messiah and the kingly messiah that the Essene and Pharisaic traditions both anticipated.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

- The source acknowledges there are "gaps in the historical record" and "in Michael's story as well"
- No external documentary corroboration of the temple boarding schools is cited beyond the internal corroboration Wallace-Murphy found in unspecified sources
- The source treats this as a family oral tradition held under oath of secrecy — inherently unverifiable by conventional historical methods
