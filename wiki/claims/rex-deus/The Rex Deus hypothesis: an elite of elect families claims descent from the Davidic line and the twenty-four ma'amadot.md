## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], a "widespread group of elect families" — known as [[Rex Deus]] — claim descent from the Davidic and Hasmonean royal families of biblical Israel and from [[The twenty-four ma'amadot (high-priestly families)]] who served the Temple of Jerusalem at the time of Jesus. These families preserved a sacred oral tradition under oath for over two thousand years, outwardly conforming to the dominant religion of their era while secretly following what they call "the true way." Their existence and traditions were first made public through the testimony of the informant referred to in the source only as "Michael" ([[Prince Michael of Albany]]).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The source describes Michael's approach to [[Tim Wallace-Murphy]] in 1994 following a talk on Rosslyn Chapel. Michael, a middle-aged Englishman who subsequently demonstrated descent from the family of [[Hugues de Payen (Templar founder)]], disclosed:

- The families' genealogies were originally inscribed on the walls of rooms under the Temple of Jerusalem
- After the Temple's destruction, the families fled to various destinations and were enjoined by sacred oath to keep the tradition alive
- Each family was obligated to keep an accurate genealogy from generation to generation
- Marital alliances were restricted to other members of the Rex Deus group, in the manner of the Cohenite priesthood
- The tradition was passed down through the eldest child or the child showing the highest level of spiritual awareness
- The oath of secrecy required initiates to swear: "May my heart be torn out or my throat be cut"
- The heraldic colors of the group are green and gold
- Rex Deus families had connections with important families in Byzantium (the eastern Roman Empire founded by Constantine in 330 CE) and held, until recently, a hereditary court appointment in England

The publication of [[The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail (Baigent, Leigh, Lincoln 1982)]] in 1982 prompted Michael to break his oath of silence, as he felt the essential framework of the secret was already in the public domain.

The authors tested the "Rex Deus hypothesis" derived from Michael's account against the medieval historical record and found it "worked well and provided a series of explanations for many otherwise insoluble puzzles" — including why the [[Knights Templar]] were founded only after Baldwin II became king of Jerusalem, how they knew where to dig under the Temple Mount, and why certain families formed inexplicable webs of political and marital alliances across Europe.

After *Rex Deus* (2000) was published, a separate correspondent (unnamed) contacted the authors, stating the account was "incomplete" and offering additional material, which forms the expanded basis of *Custodians of Truth* (2005).

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the framing claim of the entire book, presented in the Introduction. All subsequent claims about Egyptian gnosis, the Temple schools, Jesus's lineage, and the medieval Rex Deus activity in the Crusades and Templars depend on this foundational assertion.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

- Michael's claims rest on family oral tradition held under an oath of secrecy for two millennia — by definition unverifiable through conventional historical documentation
- The authors describe Michael as "seeming rational, well balanced, and completely sincere" but do not claim to have independently verified the tradition's antiquity beyond genealogical documentation of descent from Hugues de Payen
- The source notes there are "gaps in the historical record" and in Michael's own account
- The body reburial on the Temple Mount in the 4th century CE is flagged as "difficult to understand" motivationally — why would orthodox Jewish-descended families contravene the prohibition of burial on the Temple Mount?
