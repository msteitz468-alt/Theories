## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the founding of the [[Knights Templar]] in 1118 was not an improvised act of piety but the culmination of long-planned, coordinated action by [[Rex Deus]] families. The nine founding knights, all relatives connected to Count Hughes of Champagne and Bernard of Clairvaux, spent their first nine years not protecting pilgrims (their stated purpose) but excavating beneath the Temple Mount directly under their headquarters — searching for ancient documents and treasure whose location had been preserved through Rex Deus oral tradition for over a thousand years.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

[[Tim Wallace-Murphy]] argues the founding members — [[Hugues de Payen (Templar founder)]], André de Montbard, Geoffroi de St. Omer, and others — were "a tightly knit group of relatives who all belonged to Rex Deus." Hughes de Payen was cousin to both Bernard of Clairvaux and the Count of Champagne and bore the epithet "the Moor" due to lineal descent from the Prophet Muhammad, marking him as a Desposyni (descendant of the brothers of Jesus).

The evidence for the excavation mission:
- Lieutenant Warren of the Royal Engineers explored the tunnels in the late 19th/early 20th century and found Templar artefacts including a spur, lance remains, a small Templar cross, and part of a Templar sword.
- A carving on the Portal of the Initiates at Chartres Cathedral depicts the Ark of the Covenant on a wheeled cart — one esoteric tradition claims Hughes de Payen was chosen to retrieve it.
- The Copper Scroll from Qumran lists hiding sites for temple treasure pre-70 CE; several sites were excavated by John Allegro, who found 12th-century Templar artefacts but nothing from the Roman-era destruction.
- King Baldwin II granted the Templars quarters above the chosen excavation site "within a few weeks of the new king's accession" — indicating Baldwin was "obviously part of the conspiracy."
- Hughes de Champagne made preliminary visits to Jerusalem in 1104 and 1114 (before the order's formal foundation); Bishop Ivo of Chartres rebuked him in 1114 for joining a "knighthood of Christ" — suggesting the order existed before its official 1118 founding.

The documents allegedly retrieved are speculated to include copies of Dead Sea Scrolls material from Qumran and treatises on sacred geometry — knowledge that, per the authors, triggered the explosion of Gothic cathedral building when the Templars returned to Europe after 1128.

## Contexts Within the Source

The Council of Troyes (1128), where Bernard of Clairvaux wrote the Templar rule, is presented as the moment Rex Deus "went public for the first time." The subsequent cascade of land donations throughout Europe and the rapid expansion of the Cistercian order (300+ new abbeys in Bernard's lifetime) are treated as coordinated expressions of the same Rex Deus resurgence.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge they cannot definitively answer what the Templars found, whether they found anything, or precisely what the documents contained. They frame their scenario as the "only rational" explanation for the circumstantial evidence rather than proven fact. The sacred geometry / Gothic architecture link is offered as one hypothesis that the authors themselves note has some reservations (Egyptian and Hebraic architecture used lintels, not arches; the pointed arch appears instead to derive from Islamic mukhammas design encountered via Sufi contact).
