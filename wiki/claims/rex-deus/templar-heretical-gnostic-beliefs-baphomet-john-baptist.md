## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the [[Knights Templar]]'s initiated inner leadership — drawn exclusively from [[Rex Deus]] — maintained heretical beliefs that directly contradicted Catholic dogma. These included: rejection of Christ's redemptive crucifixion in favor of Jesus as a revealer of gnosis; veneration of Mary Magdalene as the bearer of the royal bloodline in the guise of the Black Madonna; elevation of John the Baptist above Jesus; and veneration of a bearded head (Baphomet) connected with the Baptist's reliquary. These beliefs were encoded in Gothic cathedral architecture and Grail literature rather than expressed openly, enabling the Templars to operate "right under the noses of the repressive Church authorities."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

[[Tim Wallace-Murphy]] marshals several categories of evidence:

**The cult of the Black Madonna:** Bernard of Clairvaux required Templar knights to make "obedience to Bethany and the House of Mary and Martha" — loyalty to the dynastic line of [[Mary Magdalene]] and Jesus. The authors cite Ean Begg's list of over fifty Templar-area churches dedicated to the Magdalene. At multiple symbolic levels, the Black Madonna represents: (1) Mary mother of Jesus, (2) Mary Magdalene, (3) Sophia / divine wisdom, (4) the Egyptian Isis with the Horus-child (i.e., Jesus' royal offspring).

**Rejection of the crucifixion:** Chartres Cathedral's west front bears thirty-eight scenes from the life of Jesus with no depiction of the crucifixion. No crucifixion carving from the 12th century exists anywhere in the cathedral. The authors describe this as "deliberate: it is a reflection of the Templar belief that Jesus came to reveal and not to redeem." Similarly, Templar churches and chapels throughout Languedoc and Provence lack contemporary crucifixion images.

**Veneration of John the Baptist:** Amiens Cathedral was built to house the reliquary of John the Baptist's severed head — described by Templar scholar Guy Jourdan as "la vrai tête Baphometique Templier." Bas-reliefs and carved panels throughout Amiens depict the Baptist's life, death, and decapitation, including a scene where the top of the severed head is pierced by a knife. Skulls found beneath Templar churches at Bargemon, Provence are similarly pierced in this manner. Throughout Languedoc and Provence, churches in former Templar territory are dedicated to John the Baptist.

**Baphomet / the Head:** The most credible charge at the Templar trial. The reliquary at Amiens, the Templecombe painting of a bearded head, and a possible connection to the Shroud of Turin (proposed by scholars Noel Currer-Briggs and J-A Durbec's "Mandylion" research) are cited. The authors argue the head venerated as Baphomet was fundamentally John the Baptist's, elevated above Christ in Templar belief.

**Sacred geometry as initiatory code:** Gothic cathedrals are described as "three-dimensional teaching boards" encoding the initiatory pathway of Rex Deus in visible, public form. The Children of Solomon (craftmason fraternity formally given a rule by the Templars in March 1145) built Chartres and the other Notre Dames using knowledge of sacred geometry retrieved from under the Temple Mount. Fulcanelli's interpretation of Gothic architecture as la langue verte (the language of initiation) is cited approvingly.

**The Holy Grail literature as coded doctrine:** The Grail romances created by Chrétien de Troyes (a relative of Hugues de Payen) and Wolfram von Essenbach were Rex Deus literary productions deliberately encoding the tradition in allegorical form. The Fisher King's wound represents the corruption of the Church; the true Grail is gnosis and initiatory enlightenment, not a physical cup.

## Contexts Within the Source

These claims are directly tied to [[The secret beliefs of the Knights Templar (Wallace-Murphy)]] concept page. They connect forward to the suppression narrative: the inner circle's heterodoxy, while real, was largely irrelevant to the formal charges — the authors argue the charges were mostly fabricated or irrelevant to the actual beliefs, but the Baphomet charge had genuine basis.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors emphasize that the vast majority of Templar knights were sincere Catholics and that the heterodox beliefs were held only by the inner Rex Deus circle. They also note that the charge of "denial of Christ" was standard heresy rhetoric applied to nearly all accused heretics throughout European history, weakening its evidentiary value.
