## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], following the Edict of Milan (312 CE) and the Council of Nicea, the Catholic Church forged a repressive church-state alliance that deliberately suppressed all rival access to spiritual knowledge, education, and the true initiatory teachings of Jesus. The authors argue that the Church's monopoly on literacy and education was not incidental but intentional: by restricting books and learning to the clergy, it maintained "total power and control over kings, emperors, and princes; over territories, peoples, and individuals; over this life and entrance to the next."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

[[Tim Wallace-Murphy]] argues that Augustine of Hippo (354–430) provided the theological framework for this repression by redefining heresy as "the distortion of a revealed truth by a believer or an unbeliever," while the Church itself exclusively defined what counted as revealed truth — a circular argument that established "a total monopoly on all access to the sacred." Constantine made Church benefits available only to "adherents of the Catholic Faith" and decreed that heretics would be stripped of civil rights. By the reign of Emperor Theodosius, over 100 statutes targeted heretics.

The authors describe the expulsion of Nestorius and the Greek classical scholars as deliberately wiping "all the benefits of centuries of Greek intellectual speculation and learning, philosophy, mathematics, and science…from the memory of European man." The Church destroyed or closed pagan temples and oracles, hijacked sacred sites, and rewrote tribal myths and histories to impose a Christian cultural mold.

The authors contrast this darkness with two surviving "glimmers of hope": Celtic Christianity (evangelized before Paul's doctrines took hold, preserving a hereditary, non-crucifixion-centered, married priesthood) and Moorish Spain (755–1492), where Islamic tolerance kept Greek, Jewish, and Arabic learning alive and accessible — the ultimate source from which knowledge eventually re-entered Christian Europe via Chartres.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim anchors the entire Rex Deus narrative: the scattered descendants of the twenty-four high-priestly families (the ma'amadot) went underground after their rebuke from Pope Sylvester and "learned to dissemble in order to survive," preserving "the true initiatory teachings of Jesus" in secret. The Church's repression is presented as the direct cause of the Rex Deus families' covert strategy that persisted for over a millennium.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge that even in the Dark Ages there were points of resistance — the Celtic Church and Moorish Spain — and that the Church could not entirely suppress what it feared. They also note that the Church did preserve and transmit some classical texts, if only in heavily glossed form.
