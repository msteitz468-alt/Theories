## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the Holy Inquisition, established by Pope Gregory IX in July 1233, was not a legitimate judicial institution but a deliberately constructed apparatus of terror. Led primarily by the Dominican order, it operated outside all established standards of justice, used institutionalized torture from the outset, profited financially from convictions, and created a self-reinforcing system of denunciation and confession from which no accused could escape. The authors present it as "the most elaborate, widespread and continuous legal barbarities recorded in civilized history" and connect its primary targets — the [[Cathars (Albigensians)]] and later the [[Knights Templar]] — to the broader [[Rex Deus]] tradition the Church sought to exterminate.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

[[Tim Wallace-Murphy]] recounts the Inquisition's structure and methods in detail:

- Two Dominican friars were appointed as "the first of a long line of serene untroubled persecutors of the human race" (quoting historian Peter de Rosa).
- Within a year of founding, the Inquisition was extended to Lombardy; by 1246 Franciscans joined, dividing Christendom into inquisitorial provinces.
- The accused had no right to know their accusers' names, no right to legal representation, and no right of appeal. Confessions were systematically extracted under torture.
- Torture received formal papal sanction in 1252 (Pope Innocent IV), though it was used from the beginning. The technique was designed to avoid shedding blood (to satisfy the maxim "Ecclesia non novit sanguinem") using the rack, thumbscrews, and red-hot pincers.
- The Inquisitors were paid from confiscated property of the convicted: half to the pope, half to the Inquisitors themselves. "The rich had even more to fear than the poor."
- The arch-Inquisitor Bernard Gui's "Black Book" (Libero Nero) instructed that if a person confesses only part of the charges, "he still ought to be regarded as guilty of the whole"; if they deny guilt entirely, they are "a victim of the devil" and deserve no compassion.
- There is "not one record of an Inquisitor ever losing a case."
- A 1239 Dominican sweep through the county of Champagne resulted in Bishop Moranis and over 180 of his flock being publicly burned — signaling that Rex Deus communities far outside Languedoc were at risk.
- In 1317, the Inquisitor Bernard Gui was sent to pacify northern Italy after Cathars fled there.

The authors quote Lord Acton (a Catholic): "Nothing short of religious assassination…the principle of the Inquisition was murderous for the popes were not only murderers in the grand style, but they made murder a legal basis of the Christian Church and a condition of salvation."

The Inquisition, per the authors, "still exists under the innocuous name of the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith" — citing Cardinal Ratzinger's 1990 statement that "The freedom of the act of faith cannot justify the right to dissent."

## Contexts Within the Source

The Inquisition chapter bridges the Cathar and Templar narratives: having failed to eliminate Catharism by the crusade, the Church deployed the Inquisition; the same Inquisition was then deployed as an arm of the French state against the Templars in 1307. The authors explicitly note that "King Philippe le Bel was scrupulous in his actions" in routing his suppression of the Templars through the Inquisitor-General of France.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge that some surviving Cathars fled and others "learned the arts of dissembling" — meaning the Inquisition did not achieve total eradication. They also note that Catharism as a "visible, coherent entity vanished forever in the 14th century."
