## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the [[Event: Albigensian Crusade (1209–1244)]] was not a military campaign against rebels but a deliberate, papally directed genocide against the [[Cathars (Albigensians)]] — a spiritually superior religion that had produced a flourishing civilization in Languedoc supported by [[Rex Deus]] nobility. The authors argue the Cathar faith represented the "true teachings of Jesus" as a gnostic, dualist, initiatory Christianity, making it an existential threat to the Catholic Church's monopoly on spiritual authority. Pope Innocent III's response was systematic mass murder, scorched-earth devastation, and the subsequent institution of the Inquisition.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

[[Tim Wallace-Murphy]] presents Catharism as an authentic rival to Catholicism, not a derivative or corrupt form of it: "The Cathars were not dissident Catholics. They drew their strength from a faith far more ancient and authentic than that of the Church." Key Cathar doctrines included: Jesus as revealer not redeemer; baptism by the Holy Spirit (the consolamentum) rather than water; denial of hell; acceptance of women as perfecti; and a dual-god cosmology in which the material world was created by an evil demiurge.

The Rex Deus nobility of Languedoc actively supported the Cathars. Count Raymond IV of Toulouse, the Count of Foix, and Viscount Roger Trençavel of Carcassonne and Béziers were all protectors; "Giraud, the Catholic historian, claims that all the minor nobility of the Lauragais…were Cathars, as were their subjects."

The crusade was triggered by the murder of papal legate Peter de Castlenau in January 1208. The papal legate Arnauld Aimery's instruction before the siege of Béziers — "Show mercy neither to order, nor to age, nor to sex…Cathar or Catholic—kill them all…God will know his own when they get to him!" — is quoted as defining the campaign's character. At Béziers (July 1209), over 20,000 people were slaughtered, including more than 7,000 who had taken sanctuary in the cathedral. The fall of Carcassonne, the hanging of eighty knights at Lavaur, the burning of 140 perfecti at Minerve, 400 at Lavaur, 60 at Les Casses, and 5,000 hacked to death at Marmande are documented.

Simon de Montfort's atrocities are detailed: at Bram in 1210, 100 defenders had their noses, lips, and ears sliced off, ninety-nine had both eyes gouged out, one was left with a single eye to lead his companions to the nearby Cathar stronghold as a psychological weapon.

The [[Knights Templar]] and Knights Hospitaller refused to participate in the crusade, citing the fact that their Languedoc holdings had been donated by noble families from the same Rex Deus group that supported the Cathars. The authors note Templar knights even gave shelter to Cathar refugees and buried perfecti on consecrated ground in defiance of papal orders.

The crusade's "official" end came with the fall of Montségur in 1244, where 225 perfecti — men and women — were burned alive. The authors suggest that, absent the crusade's destruction, Languedoc's civilization might have produced the Renaissance two centuries before Italy.

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter connects Catharism explicitly to the Rex Deus tradition: "In the light of Rex Deus beliefs and what we know of the true teaching of Jesus, it is hardly surprising that the Rex Deus nobility of Languedoc not only protected the Cathars but, in many cases, also joined them." The Cathar belief that "Jesus came to reveal not to redeem" is identical to the Templar doctrine elaborated in Chapter 11.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge that much of our information about Catharism comes from hostile Catholic sources and that tracing Cathar doctrinal origins is "notoriously difficult." They also note that Catharism was not extinguished by the Albigensian Crusade — it persisted in secret for decades and some Cathars found refuge with the Templars and later in Scotland.
