## Claim Statement

Wallace-Murphy and Hopkins argue that Lombardy and northern Italy became a second major sanctuary for Rex Deus tradition after 1307, alongside Scotland. Cathars fleeing Languedoc and Templars fleeing suppression both sought refuge in Rex Deus-ruled Italian territories, knowing these lands offered toleration. Over time the region became an important centre for the propagation of Rex Deus beliefs — expressed through the patronage of the Renaissance, the Tarot, and the encoded heretical iconography of painters like Leonardo da Vinci.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**On sanctuary in Lombardy:**
> "The Cathars who fled from Languedoc sought sanctuary in two principal locations: Scotland and Lombardy. Some years later, many knights of the persecuted Templar order did the same, because they knew, as did the Cathars, that lands ruled by Rex Deus nobility offered some semblance of safety and toleration."

**On the ruling families as Rex Deus:**
The Visconti of Milan and the counts of Savoy are explicitly identified as Rex Deus members. The Visconti intermarried with the Valois, Habsburgs, Tudors, Stuarts, and Bourbons. René d'Anjou — a leading Rex Deus figure — influenced Cosimo de' Medici through the Sforza connection; Sforza himself is identified as Rex Deus through his marriage to the last Visconti duke's daughter and initiation into René d'Anjou's Order of the Croissant.

**On the tarot as Rex Deus catechism:**
The authors cite Catholic theologian Margaret Starbird (unaware of Rex Deus, they note) independently identifying the Tarot as "a virtual catechism for the suppressed beliefs of the alternative Christian Church, the 'Church of the Holy Grail.'" The Visconti-Sforza tarot packs (c. 1440–1480) are identified as among the earliest known, and the cup symbol in them as closely resembling the Holy Grail. The authors interpret specific trump cards as: Temperance = Isis / the Black Madonna (as venerated by Templars); the Fool = Parzival; the Hermit = the Grail Hermit; the Hanged Man = the Fisher King / tortured Templars; the Tower = the Magdalene; the Moon = the Grail Bearer. One suit's symbolism refers to the St. Clair family of Lothian and Orkney.

**On Leonardo da Vinci's heretical iconography:**
The authors argue that Leonardo, working under the patronage of Duke Ludovico Sforza (a Rex Deus dynasty), encoded Rex Deus heresies in three major works:
- *The Last Supper*: Peter's disembodied knife-hand behind Judas points at James the Righteous — the authors interpret this as a heretical statement that James, not Peter, was designated successor to Jesus; the effeminate figure of John encodes the esoteric doctrine that the enlightened initiate becomes "a hermaphrodite."
- *The Virgin of the Rocks* (both versions): The child beside the Virgin is John the Baptist, not Jesus; Jesus gives the blessing — encoding the "heresy of the holy twins" and "the two Jesuses."
- The authors argue that the contractual instructions for *The Virgin of the Rocks* were drawn up by "a heretic within the priesthood."

> "In his paintings, da Vinci ceaselessly pursued both the visible form and the symbols that reveal the invisible spirit and the cosmos, including some that teach subtle forms of heresy in the guise of straight, Catholic devotional paintings."

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 17 situates Lombardy within a broader argument about how Rex Deus tradition diversified its transmission vehicles after the Templar suppression. The Italian stream ran in parallel with the Scottish stream (Chapter 18): "In Portugal, the refugees joined the Order of the Knights of Christ; in the Baltic States they joined the Teutonic Knights; in Lombardy, aided by the Cathars who had preceded them, they used their skills to strengthen the emergent banking system."

Northern Italy's role as haven for Jewish refugees from persecution across Europe is also noted — the authors attribute this to Rex Deus-influenced toleration by Visconti, Medici, and Sforza rulers.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors note that Lyn Picknett and Clive Prince's theory that the Shroud of Turin was faked by Leonardo is considered "very wide of the mark." They also acknowledge that multiple levels of interpretation coexist in great art: "it is sometimes difficult to know which is paramount — the insight of the artist or that of the patron."
