## Claim Statement

Wallace-Murphy and Hopkins argue that the 1988 radiocarbon dating of the Shroud of Turin — which dated the relic to between 1260 and 1390 CE and appeared to prove it was a medieval fake — has since been scientifically invalidated. American microbiologist Dr. Leoncio Garza-Valdes discovered that the linen threads of the shroud are completely enveloped by a bioplastic coating of living bacteria and fungi that was unknown at the time of the tests and that resisted all cleansing methods used. Since living organisms continue to absorb carbon from the atmosphere, the material being tested was slightly less than 40 percent shroud linen and more than 60 percent living organism, causing a massive distortion toward a falsely recent date. The authors conclude that the shroud's age and authenticity remain an open question, and that, assessed on all other forensic and scientific evidence, it is more probably the burial cloth of Jesus of Nazareth than a medieval forgery.

Beyond the authenticity question, the authors argue that the forensic evidence preserved in the shroud carries a heretical implication: that Jesus was alive when taken down from the cross, which would undermine the Church's doctrine of redemptive death.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**On the bioplastic invalidation:**
> "The extent of this contamination, which proved to be completely resistant to the cleansing methods used by the laboratories concerned, is such that what was, in fact, being tested was slightly less than 40 percent shroud material and more than 60 percent living organism."

Dr. Harry Gove of Rochester University, "the principal developer of the method used to carbon-date the shroud," is cited as responding to Garza-Valdes's findings: "This is not a crazy idea."

**On the broader forensic evidence supporting authenticity:**
- The cloth is woven in a three-to-one herringbone twill pattern common at the time of Jesus in the Middle East; Dr. Tite of the British Museum could not find a single piece of the same weave from the 14th century to use as a control for the tests.
- Pollen analysis by Dr. Max Frei identified six varieties of plants native to Palestine (including desert species that only grow around the Dead Sea shores), consistent with Frei's conclusion that "a forgery, produced somewhere in France during the Middle Ages…could not contain such characteristic pollen grains."
- Limestone dust from the image of the feet matched travertine aragonite specific to Jerusalem tomb limestone (Dr. Ricardo Levi-Setti, Enrico Fermi Institute).
- Anatomical analysis by Prof. Yves Delage (1902) and Dr. Pierre Barbet found the wound details — crucifixion nailing through the wrist at Destot's space, nail placement between foot metatarsals, contraction of thumb from median nerve damage, pleurisy-consistent chest wound — impossible for any medieval forger to have fabricated accurately.

**On the Rex Deus significance of the shroud:**
> "Rex Deus teaching, the long-standing traditions of Jesus' survival from his ordeal on the cross, and the message preserved in the image on the Shroud of Turin all point to the fact that the central belief of the Christian Church — that Jesus died on the cross as a sacrifice for our sins — is untrue."

## The Famous "Fake" Identified

Chapter 16 is titled "The Re-authentication of a Famous 'Fake'." The object is the **Shroud of Turin**. The 1988 announcement by Cardinal Ballestrero (13 October 1988) that radiocarbon dating showed it was manufactured between 1260 and 1390 CE (95% confidence) was the "fake" verdict; the authors argue this verdict has been scientifically overturned by the bioplastic coating discovery.

## Rex Deus Connection to the Shroud

The authors identify a chain of Rex Deus custody:
- First public exhibition in Lirey, France (mid-14th century), owned by the **de Charney family** — Geoffrey de Charney (d. 1356) was the nephew of Geoffroi de Charney, who was martyred with Jacques de Molay in 1314; the family connected to Brienne, De Joinville, D'Anjou, and Burgundy.
- In 1453, the shroud passed to Duke Louis of Savoy (a Rex Deus member) in exchange for gifts to Marguerite de Charney, the last of the de Charney line.
- The authors argue: "this group of Rex Deus families preserved the shroud because of the heretical message it contained."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge that the Catholic Church has refused to order a retest. They also dismiss several alternative theories about the shroud's origin: the Lomas/Knight theory (Jacques de Molay's burial cloth), the Picknett/Prince theory (a Leonardo da Vinci hoax), and the Kirsten/Gruber theory (Church conspiracy to discredit it), noting various factual errors or implausibilities in each.
