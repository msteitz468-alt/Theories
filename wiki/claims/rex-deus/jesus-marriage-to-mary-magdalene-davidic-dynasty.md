## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], [[Jesus of Nazareth]] was required by Jewish law to marry and produce an heir, as a rabbi and as a direct male descendant of the Davidic line. The authors argue that [[Mary Magdalene]] (Mary of Bethany) was his wife, that the wedding at Cana was his own wedding, and that after the crucifixion Mary Magdalene fled pregnant to Egypt (where she gave birth to a daughter named Sarah) before seeking refuge in southern France. Jesus's two-and-a-half-year-old son, James, was sent for safekeeping to King Abgar of Edessa in the care of Judas Thomas Didymus (Jesus's twin brother).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The legal argument:**
The source cites Father Jerome Murphy-O'Connor (Professor of New Testament Theology at the Ecole Biblique, Jerusalem) from a BBC radio broadcast: "Marriage is not a matter of choice for Jews, that's why you have so few in the early centuries who weren't married." The authors apply this reasoning to Jesus, noting that as a rabbi he was subject to the 613 strictures of the Law and bound to marry. As a man of the Davidic line, it was incumbent upon him to produce an heir. The few exceptions — including his brother James — required explicit designation as Nazorite "from the mother's womb."

**Scriptural evidence cited:**
- *The Wedding at Cana* (John 2:1–5): A. N. Wilson is quoted: "The story of the wedding feast at Cana contains a hazy memory of Jesus' own wedding." Muslim scholar Fida Hassnain states: "I believe that Mary Magdalene behaved as the chief consort of Jesus, and he also took her as his spouse." The key detail is that only the bridegroom or his mother could have the authority to give orders to servants at a wedding — and in the Cana narrative, Mary does exactly this, and Jesus complies.
- *Mary at Jesus's feet* (John 11:20, 28–29; Luke 10:39): Mary of Bethany (identified as Mary Magdalene) "sat at Jesus' feet," which the authors interpret via Jewish custom as indicating the role of dutiful wife — the only woman permitted to sit at a man's feet.
- *The alabaster jar anointing* (Matthew 26 / Mark 14): The Catholic theologian Margaret Starbird, who set out to *refute* the heresy and instead confirmed it, is cited: the king's head was ritually anointed with oil by the royal priestess or royal bride in the ancient rite of *hieros gamos* (sacred marriage). This ritual union was required for the king to be recognized as the "anointed one" — the messiah. Mary Magdalene performing this act identifies her as Jesus's royal bride.

**The Rex Deus dynastic account:**
The source states (Ch. 7): "The Rex Deus sagas recount that, years earlier, the children of Jesus were parted and sent to separate places of safety in order to ensure their survival. Jesus' two-and-a-half-year-old son, James, was entrusted to the care of Judas Thomas Didymus, Jesus' twin brother, and sent for safekeeping to King Abgar of Edessa. Mary Magdalene, the pregnant wife of the messiah, fled to Egypt, where she gave birth to a daughter called Sarah before seeking refuge in southern France."

The source also notes that the body of the messiah was reburied on the Temple Mount in the 4th century C.E. by returning Rex Deus families — the one location guaranteed to remain inviolate.

## Contexts Within the Source

The marriage claim builds directly on [[The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail (Baigent, Leigh, Lincoln 1982)]] and Margaret Starbird's subsequent confirmation. It is the foundation for the claim that the Rex Deus families preserve a genuine Davidic bloodline, not merely a priestly lineage.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

- The source acknowledges the Church has "signally failed" to engage with the legal argument for Jesus's marriage but offers this as evidence of suppression, not disproof
- The ossuary inscribed "James son of Joseph and the brother of Jesus" was "later proven to be a clever modern fake"
- The source relies primarily on inferences from Jewish social custom and on the Rex Deus oral tradition relayed through Michael
