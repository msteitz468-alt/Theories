## Claim Statement

Per [[Custodians of Truth (Wallace-Murphy & Hopkins)]], the scattered Rex Deus families — descendants of the twenty-four high-priestly families of biblical Judaism — used their natural literacy, Jewish trading contacts, and covert dynastic awareness to climb into positions of power across early medieval Europe. Under Charlemagne, they came to administer most of the Frankish empire, with the emperor himself deliberately placing Rex Deus members as counts, viscounts, and margraves across France, Septimania, Provence, northern Italy, and Saxony. The authors argue that the same families' tightly enforced marriage rules (endogamy within the Cohen high-priestly line) produced the "almost incestuous matrimonial arrangements" observable in the genealogies of medieval European nobility.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

[[Tim Wallace-Murphy]] presents the Carolingians themselves as a leading Rex Deus family, citing historian P. Munz's finding that Charlemagne "claimed succession from the biblical kings of Israel" and that he "deliberately engineered the situation in Septimania in order to arrange intermarriage between his family and that of the nasi [Jewish prince], who also descended from the Davidic line." Charlemagne created over 600 counties and staffed them preferentially with Rex Deus loyalists.

The St. Clair family is the book's primary illustrative case. William "the Seemly" St. Clair, a Norman knight, escorted the exiled Saxon princess Margaret to Scotland and received estates at Roslin. His son Henri accompanied Godfroi de Bouillon to Jerusalem in 1096. The Dukes of Normandy married into the Capetian royal house of France, the counts of Champagne, Brittany, Chartres, Flanders, and the House of Burgundy — all presented as Rex Deus families whose repeated alliances with one another are explained by their shared membership in the group. "All of their marriages are conducted from within a select group, and the same family names repeatedly appear in the genealogies of all of them every third or fourth generation."

The chapter also describes Rex Deus infiltration of the Church itself: Bishop Fulbertus of Chartres (installed 1007) taught the seven steps of Egyptian/Hebraic initiation disguised as the seven liberal arts, maintaining "a mystery school at Chartres…right under the noses of the repressive Church authorities." Bernard de Fontaine (later St. Bernard of Clairvaux), a Rex Deus family member, joined the struggling Cistercian order in 1112 accompanied by thirty-two relatives — an event the authors treat as a coordinated Rex Deus strategy to influence the Church from within.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim sets up the foundation of the Templar narrative in Chapter 10: Count Hughes I of Champagne, a Rex Deus magnate, made mysterious visits to the Holy Land in 1104 and 1114 and donated land at Clairvaux to the Cistercians before the founding of the Templars. The entire Templar founding conspiracy is presented as an expression of coordinated Rex Deus planning spanning multiple generations.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge that the claim about Charlemagne's Rex Deus lineage rests substantially on one historian (P. Munz) and on circumstantial genealogical patterns. They note that not all branches of the Carolingian line retained Rex Deus traditions: by Philippe IV's era, "the Rex Deus traditions had long since died within the royal family of France."
