## Claim Statement

Mack describes a method in which he treats each experiencer as a "co-investigator" whose well-being comes first, and recovers abduction material through a modified, breath-centered relaxation/hypnosis derived from Stanislav Grof's holotropic breathwork. He frames knowledge here as inherently intersubjective and even "co-creative" — gained through the joined consciousness of client and clinician rather than detached objective measurement.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"When a possible abductee comes to see me … I explain that I regard him or her as a co-investigator … my first responsibility is to their health and well-being." He uses "even the simplest of relaxation techniques" with "frequent return of attention to the breath," explaining "I am more interested in the integration of the recalled experiences as we go along than in 'getting the story.'" He follows Thomas Kuhn's advice to "suspend … all of these language forms and simply collect raw information."

On epistemology (ch. 16): "the information gained in the sessions is not simply a remembered 'item,' lifted out of the experiencer's consciousness like a stone from a kidney. It may represent instead a developed or evolved perception, enriched by the connection that the experiencer and the investigator have made … the experiencer and I may be participating in an evolution of consciousness." He pairs this with the claim that he does not lead: "abductees … are quite difficult to lead (they all seem to feel, as Sheila said, 'I know what I saw')."

## Contexts Within the Source

The procedural basis for every case chapter and the means by which Mack accesses the "ontological shock" of recovered abductions. It is also the method most attacked by critics, addressed at length in [[Mack: physical evidence corroborates but cannot prove abductions; the data is experiential]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Mack openly flags the central vulnerability: a "co-creative intuitive process such as this may yield information that is in some sense the product of the intermingling … of the consciousness of the two … people in the room … From a traditional dualistic perspective this might be called 'distortion.'" Appendix A answers the investigator-influence and hypnosis-accuracy objections directly, conceding "inaccurate material may be recovered under hypnosis" and that "ongoing research is needed."
