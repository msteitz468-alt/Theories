## Claim Statement

Hopkins presents the case of "Steven Kilburn" as a model of his method: a man with *no conscious memory* of any UFO encounter, troubled only by an inexplicable unease about a particular stretch of road, who under hypnotic regression recovered a detailed abduction — demonstrating that an abduction can hide behind nothing more than a vague, persistent disquiet.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hopkins describes Kilburn as "a shy, intense, and haunted man" who, during a general discussion, was "for no immediately apparent reason, uneasy." That free-floating unease — attached to a place where he sensed something had happened — was the only "symptom." When "a two-hour time period conveniently became available," Hopkins arranged a hypnosis session, and the regression unlocked the hidden experience. The case illustrates the book's thesis that the *absence* of memory, plus diffuse anxiety, is itself the investigator's clue.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

As throughout, the recovered account rests on hypnotic regression of a subject with no conscious memory — the evidentiary limitation Hopkins acknowledges.
