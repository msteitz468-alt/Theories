## Claim Statement

Mack reports that many experiencers come to feel they possess a dual identity — both human and alien — seeing the world at times from an alien perspective and sensing that they are "not 'from here.'" The alien self is sometimes felt to be a lost or abandoned soul once joined to a common source with the human self, so that the experiencer's task becomes the reintegration of the split-off alien self into wholeness.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Many of the abductees written about in this book have experienced a dual identity as both a human and alien being. As an alien they see the world from an alien perspective, and, as in Joe's case, carry out the alien side of the reproductive enterprise. Peter, on the other hand, was troubled to discover material about his mating as a human being with an alien female. The alien self is sometimes perceived as the lost or abandoned soul of the human self, linked once to a common source. The felt task then becomes one of integrating the split-off alien self or soul and becoming whole once again."

In chapter 1 Mack notes that some experiencers feel "that they are not 'from here' and that their human mother and father are not their true parents," having been told "by an alien female that she was their true mother." He lists "the experience by abductees of a dual human/alien identity … (for example, Peter, Joe, and Paul, in chapters 13, 8, and 10)" among the third-class phenomena requiring "another ontological paradigm."

## Contexts Within the Source

A "transformational/spiritual" phenomenon entwined with the reproductive program ([[Mack: a reproductive hybrid-breeding program is central to the abductions]]) and the felt bond with the alien "leader." Its reintegration arc feeds the transformation described in [[Mack: abduction is ultimately a spiritually transformative ego death and expansion of consciousness]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Mack places this among phenomena that "cannot be understood within the framework of the laws of Western science," reported through hypnotic regression and conveyed as felt identity ("difficult to put into words"). He weighs and sets aside a purely Jungian-archetypal reading (Appendix A), noting it would raise more questions than it answers without accounting for the phenomenon's physical elements.
