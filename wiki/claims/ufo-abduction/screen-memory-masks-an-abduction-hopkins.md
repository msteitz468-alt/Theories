## Claim Statement

Hopkins argues that abductors implant a pleasant, innocuous "cover" memory — a screen memory — over the real experience, so that the abductee consciously recalls only the benign image (in the key case, a "beautiful deer") while the abduction itself is hidden behind it. The cover story is installed by post-hypnotic suggestion, leaving the witness with euphoria and a bloody, unexplained gap rather than the truth.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the Virginia Horton ("Alsatian deer") case, the only conscious memory from a missing-time episode was: "the only thing I could remember is that I saw a beautiful deer in the woods. It was almost like a mystical deer." Under hypnosis she found the deer "was looking at me in a very conscious kind of way," and herself raised the doubt: "it could just as easily have been that I was hypnotized and thought I saw a deer to make it easy to have a story to tell" — what Hopkins elsewhere calls "an artificially induced image like the nonexistent deer."

Reconstructing the episode, Hopkins writes that after blood and tissue samples were "extracted from inside her nose," Virginia "is then taken into the woods and given post-hypnotic suggestions that she will forget everything except the fact that she saw a beautiful deer," returning to find "an odd time lapse" and "fresh blood on her blouse." He treats the accompanying euphoria ("a celebration in her honor") as a further "artificially induced emotion… some sort of powerful post-hypnotic suggestion," paralleling Barney Hill's strange elation after the Hill abduction.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hopkins is candid that the deer-memory's status is uncertain, weighing whether Virginia's recovered narrative was "an actual event or… something else — an interpolated dream or an artificially induced image," and noting that her account surfaced during "long digressions just before each breakthrough, as if the imposed amnesia were struggling to maintain itself." The whole rests on hypnotic regression, the evidentiary limit he acknowledges throughout.
