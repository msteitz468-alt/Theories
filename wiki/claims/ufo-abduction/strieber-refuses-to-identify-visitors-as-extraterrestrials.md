## Claim Statement

Strieber's signature position: he deliberately calls the beings **"the visitors"** rather than aliens or extraterrestrials, and holds their true nature genuinely unknown. He explicitly canvasses possibilities — that they are extraterrestrial, that they are "our own dead" (humans being a "larval form"), that they come "from another dimension, or even from another time," or that they are a phenomenon of the human mind/brain — and declines to choose.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He frames the dilemma: "Either… visitors are actually here, or the human mind is creating something that, incredibly, is close to a physical reality." He writes plainly "I do not know what it is," speculates the visitors "might really be our own dead" or a visit "from another dimension," and considers that a new "belief… on its way to becoming religious dogma" could be "a totally misunderstood biological process" of the brain (beyond "Jung's collective unconscious"). The title's "communion" expresses his sense that they seek "the very depth of the soul," not a nuts-and-bolts exchange.

## Contexts Within the Source

This agnosticism distinguishes Strieber from the extraterrestrial-craft school (Keyhoe) and aligns him, in spirit, with the interdimensional/psychic readings of Jacques Vallée and John Keel; it interprets the experience of [[Strieber recounts a first-person abduction by "the visitors" (1985)]].
