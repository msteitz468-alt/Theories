## Claim Statement

Mack argues that the abduction phenomenon is "daimonic" (Patrick Harpur's term) — it originates in an unseen reality but "crosses over" and manifests in the physical world, belonging cleanly to neither the objective nor the subjective domain of the Western "scientific materialist or … Newtonian/Cartesian paradigm." Because the phenomenon does not fit that ontology, he contends the rational response is not to explain it away but to expand the worldview to admit realities that science's restrictive epistemology excludes.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Faced with a phenomenon that does not fit one's ontological perspective, the choices are to ignore it, to force it somehow into the old mold … or to modify or expand the worldview itself." Mack adopts Harpur's "daimonic reality" — a world of unseen agency "neither spiritual nor physical, but both" — and writes: "The UFO abduction phenomenon appears to be a powerful example of a 'daimonic' reality. It affects experiencers profoundly (subjective), but at the same time manifests in the material world (objective). It appears to originate from an unseen reality, but 'crosses over' into the physical world."

He frames the materialist paradigm as a quasi-theology (citing Thomas Kuhn) that "determines what it is possible for us to see," and argues that to "exclude data because it does not fit a particular view of reality can only, in the end, arrest the progress of science." In Appendix A: the phenomenon "may not originate in our physical reality but penetrates variably into it," operating in "subtle, elusive, even 'tricky'" ways "as if a mischievous intelligence were at work."

## Contexts Within the Source

The ontological backbone of the book, generated by [[Mack: the alien abduction phenomenon is real and cannot be explained psychiatrically]] and elaborated in chapter 2's "three classes of information" (the third class requiring "another ontological paradigm"). It underwrites the spiritual reading in [[Mack: abduction is ultimately a spiritually transformative ego death and expansion of consciousness]] and the cross-cultural one in [[Mack: abduction parallels shamanic and cross-cultural encounter traditions]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Mack reproduces his critics at length — James Gleick's charge that "alien-abduction mythology" is a "leading case of the anti-rational, anti-science cults," and reviewers who warned that "the secular world and the spirit world must be kept as separate as church and state." He concedes the "daimonic" reading is "somewhat revolutionary and difficult to understand within our current modern secular worldview" and rests on his own clinical conviction rather than physical proof.
