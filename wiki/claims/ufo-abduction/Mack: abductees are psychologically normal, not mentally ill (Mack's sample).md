## Claim Statement

Mack argues that abductees, as a group, show no pattern of mental illness, personality type, or family structure that could account for their experiences. Drawn "as if at random" from all parts of society, they display a normal range of mental health; the only disturbances found are the secondary effects of trauma — making, in his view, a psychiatric explanation of the experiences themselves untenable.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"None of the efforts to characterize abductees as a group have been successful. They seem to come, as if at random, from all parts of society." His sample included "students, homemakers, secretaries, writers, business people, computer industry professionals, musicians … a prison guard, an acupuncturist, a social worker, and a gas station attendant." "Efforts to establish a pattern of psychopathology other than disturbances associated with a traumatic event have been unsuccessful. Psychological testing of abductees has not revealed evidence of mental or emotional disturbance that could account for their reported experiences."

Of four cases he had formally tested, one troubled young man showed disturbance in which "cause and effect in relation to the abduction experiences could not be sorted out," but "the other three tested in the normal range." In Appendix A: "Psychiatric examinations and numerous psychological tests have failed to reveal forms of mental illness that could, conceivably, explain the abduction phenomenon," and studies found experiencers "neither more hypnotizable nor more fantasy prone than the general population."

## Contexts Within the Source

The empirical underpinning of [[Mack: the alien abduction phenomenon is real and cannot be explained psychiatrically]]. It corroborates Hopkins's earlier finding in [[UFO abductions are a hidden "epidemic" far larger than reported]] and parallels the normality claims around [[Communion (Strieber)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Mack notes the sample is self-selected (those "with less of an economic and social stake in the society seem less reluctant to come forward"), that subtle distinguishing measures of openness or psychic ability "have yet to be developed or applied," and that biases from his own interests and the population that sought him out "must be considered." He acknowledges the suggested associations with sexual abuse and dissociation, while arguing dissociation "tells us nothing per se about the source" of the trauma.
