## Claim Statement

Mack argues that the abduction accounts of his clinically evaluated experiencers describe real events that no psychiatric explanation can account for. Having spent his career discriminating mental states, he finds these individuals psychologically sound, reporting with appropriate emotion and self-doubt experiences that nonetheless "simply could not be" within the Western scientific worldview — leaving, in his judgment, no plausible conventional explanation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Nothing in my nearly forty years in the field of psychiatry prepared me for what I have encountered in working with individuals reporting abduction experiences." The accounts, he writes, "had the characteristics of real events: highly detailed narratives that seemed to have no obvious symbolic pattern; intense emotional and physical traumatic impact, sometimes leaving small lesions on the experiencers' bodies; and consistency of stories down to the most minute details." His conclusion: "I was dealing with a phenomenon that I felt could not be explained psychiatrically, yet was simply not possible within the framework of the Western scientific worldview."

In Appendix A he restates it as the position reached "after many hundreds of hours of work": "I have no basis for concluding as yet that anything other than what experiencers say happened to them actually did … abduction experiencers have been visited by some sort of 'alien' intelligence which has impacted them physically and psychologically." He adds that "no plausible alternative explanation … has been discovered," and suspects the resistance to this conclusion exists only "because the phenomenon … violate[s] our scientific worldview."

## Contexts Within the Source

The thesis the whole book defends. It rests on the experiencers' normality ([[Mack: abductees are psychologically normal, not mentally ill (Mack's sample)]]), the consistent pattern ([[Mack: abductions follow a consistent pattern of grey beings, ships, and intrusive procedures]]), and corroborating but inconclusive physical evidence ([[Mack: physical evidence corroborates but cannot prove abductions; the data is experiential]]); its ontological consequence is [[Mack: abduction is a "daimonic" reality that breaches the materialist worldview]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Mack concedes there is "physical evidence but not proof," that knowledge rests on subjective and hypnotically aided report, and (in the 1995 edition) repeatedly stresses that accepting the *emotional* truth of an account "is not taking a position that validates the literal occurrence of these experiences in our physical reality." Appendix A reviews — without endorsing — psychopathology, sexual-abuse displacement, sleep paralysis, temporal-lobe epilepsy, fantasy-proneness, mass hysteria, and Jungian-archetype explanations.
