## Claim Statement

Mack reports that the "most common, and evidently most important" abduction procedures concern reproduction: sperm is taken from men and eggs removed or fertilized in women; experiencers are impregnated and later have an "alien-human or human-human pregnancy removed"; and on subsequent abductions they are shown hybrid fetuses in containers, incubators of hybrid babies, and older hybrid children they are told — or intuitively know — are their own, and are sometimes asked to hold or nurture.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"The most common, and evidently most important procedures, involve the reproductive system. Instruments that penetrate the abdomen or involve the genital organs … are used to take sperm samples from men and to remove or fertilize eggs of the female. Abductees report being impregnated by the alien beings and later having an alien-human or human-human pregnancy removed. They see the little fetuses being put into containers on the ships, and during subsequent abductions may see incubators where the hybrid babies are being raised (as do Catherine, Jerry, and Peter, among my cases)."

"Sometimes the aliens will try to have the human mothers hold and nurture these creatures, who may appear quite listless, or will encourage human children to play with the hybrid ones as, for example, Catherine is made to do." In Joe's case the experiencer "carr[ies] out the alien side of the reproductive enterprise"; Peter "was troubled to discover material about his mating as a human being with an alien female."

## Contexts Within the Source

The procedural heart of the consistent pattern ([[Mack: abductions follow a consistent pattern of grey beings, ships, and intrusive procedures]]) and a major source of the experiencers' trauma. It connects to the felt kinship behind [[Mack: many experiencers report a dual human/alien identity]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Mack reports these as experiencer accounts, not verified biology; the missing pregnancies and recovered "implants" are, by his own admission in Appendix A, "subtle, elusive, and difficult to prove" and serve only to corroborate. He notes the "rape-like nature of the abduction material may become altered" as the experiencer's relationship to the beings changes over the course of therapy.
