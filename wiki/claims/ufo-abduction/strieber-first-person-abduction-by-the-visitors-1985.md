## Claim Statement

Strieber gives a first-person account of being taken from his cabin on the night of December 26, 1985 by small non-human beings and subjected to an examination — including a needle inserted into his brain and an intrusive probe — an experience of overwhelming terror partly obscured by amnesia and later supplemented through hypnosis.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He describes being moved to a small, messy, domed chamber; the beings' rapid speech and a distinct odor that served as "an anchor in reality"; and "terror amnesia" over much of it. He stresses the core of the memory was conscious *before* any hypnosis (drawn "from journal material… written before undergoing any hypnosis"). For verification he cites a clean polygraph (a 30-year operator), interviews with three psychologists and three psychiatrists, and a neurological exam — all placing him "within the normal range." He reports recovering an earlier October 4, 1985 episode and finding many other experiencers (a scientist, a policeman, a federal officer) forming "a cross section of American society."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Strieber repeatedly entertains alternatives in the text — hypnopompic hallucination, a drug-induced state, brain/temporal-lobe mechanisms — and concludes only that the experience "is not presently understood by science."

## Contexts Within the Source

The experiential heart of the book; its interpretation is left open in [[Strieber refuses to identify the visitors as extraterrestrials]]. The encounter matches the "abduction syndrome" documented by Budd Hopkins — see [["Missing time" and the abduction syndrome]] — with whom Strieber worked.
