## Claim Statement

In Appendix B, Mack and Dominique Callimanopulos situate UFO abduction within a worldwide, millennia-old tradition of human contact with sky-beings and spirits. They argue that abductees are "continuing an amply documented tradition of ascent and extraterrestrial communication," and that the experience structurally resembles shamanic initiation — ego-dissolving ordeal, journey to another realm, rebirth, and the return with new sensitivities and a mission for one's people.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"The connection between humans and beings from other dimensions has been illustrated in myths and stories from various cultures for millennia … The contemporary Western tenet that we are alone in the universe … is, in fact, a minority perspective, an anomaly." Drawing on Mircea Eliade's ascension myths — in which "the gods came down to Earth and mingled with men," and after the "fall" only "shamans could continue to move between Heaven and Earth" — they conclude: "It would seem that today's UFO abductees are continuing an amply documented tradition of ascent and extraterrestrial communication."

"The UFO abduction experience … bears resemblance to other dramatic, transformative experiences undergone by shamans, mystics … the individual's ordinary consciousness is radically transformed. He or she is initiated into a non-ordinary state of being which results … in a reintegration of the self." They cite [[Jacques Vallée]]'s *Dimensions* and *Passport to Magonia* — "The UFO occupants, like the elves of old, are not extraterrestrials. They are denizens of another reality" — and report interviewing the Zulu healer Credo Mutwa, who described a classic "grey" abduction.

## Contexts Within the Source

The historical-comparative grounding of the book's ontology ([[Mack: abduction is a "daimonic" reality that breaches the materialist worldview]]) and its transformational reading ([[Mack: abduction is ultimately a spiritually transformative ego death and expansion of consciousness]]). It explicitly converges with [[Jacques Vallée]]'s thesis behind [[Magonia]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The appendix is candid that "The question of whether and why these events occur … remains unanswered. There is much debate even about how to frame such questions," and raises (without resolving) whether there is "any reality independent of consciousness" or whether abduction is "a product of … shared consciousness." Mack also notes abductions possess "their own uniqueness" and are not simply identical to the older traditions.
