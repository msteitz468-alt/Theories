**Claim Statement**
Lehrich describes Bruno's *De Imaginum* as constructing twenty-four mental "atria" (rooms), each named
and imaged (Altar, Basilica, Carcer/prison, etc.) so as to spell out the alphabet, each further
populated by twenty-four subordinate images leading to further rooms — a vast, rigidly structured
network of mental spaces for classifying and navigating all knowledge. Lehrich argues this
classificatory obsession is not a "turning back" from Bruno's cosmology but a serious, practical attempt
to found knowledge and stabilize meaning by imposing constant, navigable rules on an otherwise
"infinite universe of infinitesimals" — replacing the mathematics Bruno disdained with a semiotic and
mnemonic system. Bruno argued that discerning structural analogy between the system of signs and the
system of nature was possible precisely because the mind, itself constrained by signification, could
not simply invent classificatory order out of nothing.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"Each of the twenty-four atria has its own name and image: Altar, Basilica, Carcer (prison), and so on,
such that they spell out the alphabet. They are each filled with a further twenty-four images around the
exterior, and these in turn lead to further subjoined openings or rooms." Bruno, quoted: "We are
deliberately proposing a method which by no means concerns things but which treats, rather, the
significance of things... there exists beyond a doubt a productive power of all things." Lehrich:
"Therefore, Bruno suggests, the fact that system can be imposed on signs implies the possibility of a
structural analogy between the systems of nature and the systems of thought."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Lehrich closes this discussion by quoting Lévi-Strauss's remark that among "savage" peoples, plant and
animal species "are deemed useful or interesting because they are first of all known," commenting
"Giordano Bruno would surely have agreed" — directly linking Bruno's classificatory art of memory to
Lévi-Straussian structuralist anthropology.
