## Claim Statement

In concluding, Lehrich argues that magic "cannot be defined as différance," yet it repeatedly "plays
the part" of différance and haunts the very philosophical discourses (Derrida's included) from which it
seems excluded. Derrida's exclusion of magical self-legitimation from *différance* is "properly an
inclusion" — placing magic within the sphere of traditional philosophical discourse — so that magic
coexists with, and mirrors, the thinking of différance rather than being identical to it.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"To be sure, magic cannot be defined as differance, but it often plays the [part]…" Lehrich adds that
Derrida's exclusion "is properly an inclusion: it is differance that is (or was always already)
excluded from logic and reason." His closing image: "By encountering magical thought as theory, rather
than as an object to be analyzed through theory, we come to new understanding of a thought that looks
back at us from a fun-house mirror… one cannot know which is the original and which the distorted
reflection." (ch. 6, conclusion)

## Contexts Within the Source

The culminating move of the book, tying magic to Derrida's *différance* and to the mirror metaphor —
magic and theory as mutually reflecting, neither self-evidently the "original." Grounds the umbrella
thesis that magic should be encountered *as* theory.
