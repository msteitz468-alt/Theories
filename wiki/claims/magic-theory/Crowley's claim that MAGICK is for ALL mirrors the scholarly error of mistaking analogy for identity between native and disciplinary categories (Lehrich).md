## Claim Statement

Lehrich argues that [[Aleister Crowley]]'s claim in *Magick in Theory and Practice* that "MAGICK is for
ALL" — i.e., that magic is not fundamentally different from ordinary activity, and that ordinary people's
disdain for magic stems only from a failure to see this — repeats, in mirror-image form, the same
analytical error that A. R. Radcliffe-Brown made when he argued magic's inconsistency of classification
proved it did not exist. Just as scholars err by assuming purely differential native usage is
interchangeable with scholarly classification (mistaking analogy for identity), Crowley errs by
universalizing magic's identity with mundane activity from the same non-substantive, differential
character of the term.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Lehrich quotes Crowley: "My former work has been misunderstood, and its scope limited, by my use of
technical terms. It has attracted only too many dilettanti and eccentrics, weaklings seeking in 'Magic'
an escape from reality... And it has repelled only too many scientific and practical minds, such as I
most designed to influence." Lehrich's own comment: "Ignorant discrimination is hardly to be confused
with proper scholarly distinction. But in ignoring the latter and overextending the former, Crowley
simply repeats and turns to his own advantage a mistake of the same kind that A. R. Radcliffe-Brown made
when he claimed that magic's inconsistency of classification showed that it did not exist."

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich uses this juxtaposition (a practicing magician's universalizing claim and a skeptical
anthropologist's eliminativist claim) to show that both native/practitioner and scholarly discourse can
independently misuse magic's differential, non-substantive character to draw opposite but equally
illegitimate conclusions.
