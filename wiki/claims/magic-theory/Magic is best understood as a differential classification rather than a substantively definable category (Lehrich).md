## Claim Statement

Lehrich argues that repeated scholarly failure to substantively define "magic" (as against science or
religion) does not show the term is meaningless, but rather indicates that magic has "an unusual power to
manifest distinction and division." He proposes treating magic as fundamentally a differential — not
substantive — category: magic is "considered powerful because first of all it differentiates," and
"is not different because of its nature or its power." Building on Jonathan Z. Smith's "Trading Places,"
Lehrich argues that both attempts to define magic as a privative variant of science/religion, and
attempts to subsume magic into one of them by synonymy, founder on the same problem — an inability to
specify a difference precise enough to justify or dissolve the category. Magic, he concludes, is best
approached as a signifier of a differential dynamic, "a difference not different-from."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"The proliferation of definitions of magic, positive as well as negative, among scholars as well as those
whom they study, certainly attests to the confusion or diffusion of the term, but it also indicates in
magic an unusual power to manifest distinction and division." And: "Magic then is not different because
of its nature or its power; magic is considered powerful because first of all it differentiates." Quoting
Smith: "in academic discourse 'magic' has almost always been treated as a contrast term, a shadow reality
known only by looking at the reflection of its opposite ('religion,' 'science') in a distorting
fun-house mirror."

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich presents this as the conclusion of an extended argument surveying failed substantive definitions
(privative, evolutionary/chronological, "proto-science") and as the pivot toward his subsequent
Derridean formulation of magic via différance.
