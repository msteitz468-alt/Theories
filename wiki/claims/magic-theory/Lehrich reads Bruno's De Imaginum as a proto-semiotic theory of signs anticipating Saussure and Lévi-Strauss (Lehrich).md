**Claim Statement**
Lehrich argues that Bruno's *De Imaginum, Signorum, et Idearum Compositione* (1591) approaches something
close to modern semiotics: it distinguishes images (roughly, signifiers/percepts) from ideas
(signifieds/concepts) and treats the sign as the relational mediator between them, a move Lehrich
compares to Saussure's signifier/signified distinction and to Lévi-Strauss's remark that "images cannot
be ideas, but they can play the part of signs." Lehrich, following translators Doria and Higgins, notes
the historical curiosity that Saussure's formative period (1890s) coincided with the first collected
publication of Bruno's Latin texts, though this connection remains speculative. Lehrich stresses,
however, that Bruno's purposes were practical (memory and classification for confronting an infinite
Copernican universe) rather than linguistic in Saussure's sense.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Bruno, quoted from Book One, Part One, Chapter Ten: "Images do not receive their names from the
explanations of the things they signify, but rather from the condition of those things that do the
signifying... Therefore images are named not for those things they signify in intention, but for those
things from which they have been gathered." Lehrich: "Reading De Imaginum, especially the abstract and
theoretical book 1, part 1, we find that image is more or less equivalent to percept, idea to concept,
and that like Saussure Bruno understands the sign to mediate between these." He adds: "if Bruno has in
some sense invented semiotics—rather an overstatement... that is not to say he has the same purposes in
mind as do Saussure or Charles Sanders Peirce, or Lévi-Strauss."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Lehrich uses Bruno's elaborate demonstration on the opening line of Virgil's *Aeneid* ("Arma virumque
cano...") in *De Imaginum* Book 3 as evidence that signs, for Bruno, mediate freely and relationally
between images and ideas rather than fixing a single determinate meaning — a "protean," relational
semiotics rather than a stable code.
