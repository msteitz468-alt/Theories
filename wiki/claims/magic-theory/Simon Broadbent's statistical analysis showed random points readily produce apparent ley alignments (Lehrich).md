## Claim Statement

Lehrich reports that statistician Simon Broadbent, in a presentation to the Royal Statistical Society, demonstrated that the statistical likelihood of finding a passable straight-line alignment of three or four points within a random distribution of about fifty points is exceedingly high — far higher than intuition suggests — which undermines ley hunters' habit of treating a handful of aligned prehistoric sites as evidence of deliberate ancient construction.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "Broadbent's discussion... demonstrates conclusively that the statistical likelihood of finding a passable line of three or even four points within a random distribution of fifty or so points is exceedingly high, indeed a great deal higher than even a statistician might guess: 'Unaided intuition can in fact easily be surprised in this area. If 50 points are uniformly and independently distributed in a square, how many triads will we find at an acceptance angle of ½°?... it is shown below [in Broadbent's paper] that in this case the mean is 57.01 and standard deviation 8.34, so to observe 60 or even 70 triads is not really significant.'"

> "Ultimately, Broadbent shows that, contra Watkins and most ley hunters, it is not a question of finding so many points more or less in a line: this proves nothing. Simplistically, it is necessary to show that a given number of points fall in a line in a fashion significantly outside the statistical norm for all such points in that geographical region."

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich uses Broadbent's analysis as the first of two major objections to Watkins's ley theory (the second being ancient societies' presumed lack of organizational capacity for large-scale earthworking, which Lehrich notes has since been revised by archaeologists regarding sites like Stonehenge and Avebury). He concludes that "there are many possible leys, but the great majority of them are certainly accidental or entirely modern," and that any real leys would require external confirmation beyond mere alignment — additional data, or a plausible reason for their construction — which Lehrich says the occult ley-hunting tradition (Wedd, Michell, and the Earth Mysteries movement) supplied not through evidence but through nostalgic theorizing about Atlantis and UFOs.
