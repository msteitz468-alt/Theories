## Claim Statement

Lehrich argues that the musical metaphor pervading [[Claude Lévi-Strauss]]'s four-volume *Mythologiques* —
running "from 'Overture' to 'Finale,' by way of 'Bororo Song,' 'Well-Tempered Astronomy,' and 'The
Harmony of the Spheres'" — has been little examined seriously by scholars, yet careful analysis of it
"reveals a good deal more about his methods than one might expect, and also clarifies some of their
weaknesses." Lehrich uses the specific technical contrast between natural/harmonic tonal relations and
Schoenberg's serialism to show that Lévi-Strauss's critique of serialism reveals an underlying tension in
structuralism between an appeal to "natural" synchronic relations and a fully differential, arbitrary
sign system.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Oddly enough, there have to my knowledge been few serious attempts to make sense of the musical metaphor
in Lévi-Strauss... Yet careful examination of Lévi-Strauss's musical thought reveals a good deal more about
his methods than one might expect, and also clarifies some of their weaknesses." Lehrich further shows
that Lévi-Strauss's choice of Ravel's *Boléro* (rather than Bach) to exemplify musical "fugue" and
polyphony is an "aberrant" example that exposes strain in the analogy between mythological structure and
musical form.

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich frames this as an "alternate point of entry" into tarot's magical theory, using the fugue/tarot
comparison to interrogate Lévi-Strauss's structuralist method rather than merely to describe tarot.
