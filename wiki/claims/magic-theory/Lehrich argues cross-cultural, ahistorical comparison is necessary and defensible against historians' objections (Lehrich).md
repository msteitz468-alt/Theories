**Claim Statement**
Lehrich argues that historians' standard objection to cross-cultural ("ahistorical") comparison — that
it inevitably annuls difference — is not a decisive argument against comparison as such, since
historical contextualization is itself a comparative procedure (situating an object within a class
called "period" or "culture"). He argues historians who resist comparison on principle either
implicitly demand historians study only their "ancestral roots" (which would be absurd and, if pressed,
racist) or fail to see that "radical defamiliarization" — insisting on total uniqueness of the historical
object — equally destroys the possibility of interpretation. He concludes "it is long past time to
recognize that history is intrinsically comparative."

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"Traditional historians resist cross-cultural (so-called 'ahistorical') comparison but rarely present
the logical and methodological reasons for such resistance accurately... this argument presumes a
necessary contiguity of the historian's own position with that of the object studied. Taken seriously,
this objection requires historians to study only their ancestral roots, such that all historians of
China should be Chinese." "On the other hand, the recently more popular radical defamiliarization,
which insists on the uniqueness of its objects, risks incomprehensibility... this procedure destroys
the possibility of interpretation."

**Contexts Within the Source**
This argument sets up Lehrich's justification for comparing Dee's Monas to Japanese Noh theater and,
in Chapter 4, for treating Kircher, Bruno, and modern structuralism (Lévi-Strauss) as commensurable
objects of comparative analysis despite belonging to different historical periods and cultures.
