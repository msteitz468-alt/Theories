**Claim Statement**
Lehrich proposes understanding Kircher's entire intellectual output through the lens of his museum at
the Collegio Romano, which Kircher curated and which built on his own personal collection. Rather than
reading Kircher as an incoherent "encyclopedist" of curiosities (as some contemporaries and later
Enlightenment historians did), Lehrich argues Kircher should be read as a classifier and comparativist
who treated objects and collections themselves as signs, seeking underlying connections ("chains")
among disparate domains of knowledge — astronomy, magnetism, music, hieroglyphics, Chinese writing —
via a "science of the concrete." Antonella Romano's remark that "Kircher's life, world, and work belong,
without a doubt, to a universe to which we have lost the key" frames the interpretive difficulty; Paula
Findlen's contributors' recurring but under-analyzed use of the word "connections" signals the same gap.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"As a beginning, I suggest that we understand his intellectual project in light of yet another of his
achievements, the museum at the Collegio Romano, of which Kircher's own collection was the core, and
for which he was the curator." "If we are to understand Kircher cohesively, we must read his objects and
collections as signs, and recognize that his linguistics is often rooted in collecting." Henry Oldenburg
is quoted dismissing Kircher's work as "rather Collections... of what is already extant and known, yn
any new Discoveryes."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Lehrich distinguishes Kircher's project from both the modern "encyclopedic" tradition (Diderot, Bayle)
and from Foucault's (in Lehrich's view historically inaccurate) reading of pre-modern classification in
*Les mots et les choses*, arguing Kircher is better read as a precursor of the comparative/structural
tradition than of encyclopedism.
