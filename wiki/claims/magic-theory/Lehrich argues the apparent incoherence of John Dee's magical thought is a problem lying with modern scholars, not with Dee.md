**Claim Statement**
Lehrich argues that before Frances Yates, Dee appeared "wildly incoherent" — a scientist on some days
and a "superstitious madman" on others — and that even post-Yates scholarship (Clulee, Sherman,
Harkness, Håkansson, Szonyi) has failed to resolve basic questions about Dee's project: why the *Monas
Hieroglyphica* was unsatisfactory, why Dee shifted from mathematical-alchemical writing to the
linguistic/visionary *Libri Mysteriorum*, and how these relate to his political aims. Lehrich concludes:
"The problem lies with us, not with Dee." Magical thought, he argues, routinely undermines or ignores
the disciplinary divisions modern scholars project onto it.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"In most scholarship before Frances Yates, Dee appeared wildly incoherent: a serious scientist and
mathematician on Mondays, Wednesdays, and Fridays, he turned into a superstitious madman on Tuesdays,
Thursdays, and Saturdays." Lehrich lists four unresolved scholarly questions about the consistency of
Dee's magical project, then states directly: "The problem lies with us, not with Dee. As we have already
seen with Bruno, magical thought often undermines, challenges, or even ignores common divisions among
fields of knowledge or practice."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Lehrich frames this as part of his larger comparative-methodological project: the difficulty scholars
have understanding Dee stems from anachronistic disciplinary categories (mathematics vs. magic, science
vs. religion) that did not operate the same way for Dee's contemporaries.
