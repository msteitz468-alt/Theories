## Claim Statement

Lehrich argues that Frances Yates's historical method — especially in *The Rosicrucian Enlightenment* — of positing a hidden "line" or track connecting known figures (Dee, Maier, Ashmole, Newton) through cumulative speculation ("perhaps," "would have," "must have," culminating in "was") functions epistemologically like ley hunting: she sights hypothetical points from known ones and builds a self-reinforcing track, which Lehrich calls "bricolage rather than history," an epistemology drawn from occultism and ley hunting rather than ordinary historical source-validation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "Like a ley hunter, Yates sighted hypothetical points from known ones, then further hypothesized from the first, until she had produced a revelatory track through the byways of Renaissance ideas. As with Watkins and his 'flood of ancestral memory,' it seems at times that she had a vision from which she worked backward, tracing possible tumuli, mounds, and barrows filled with rich historical treasure."

> Quoting Brian Vickers on Yates's method: "In many places argument disappears altogether. Some of the recurrent words are 'if,' 'may,' 'perhaps,' 'would have,' 'surely,' 'must have,' a sequence which often culminates in the positive form 'was.'... [This] process is cumulative, as speculations at first tentative gradually harden and then become the base for further speculations."

> "In effect, this is bricolage rather than history... For precisely this reason, Yates's method of tracing the Rosicrucian lines buried beneath the familiar landmarks of early modern history depends on conceptions of evidence, even an epistemology, more familiar to occultists and ley hunters than to historians. It is not, then, that Yates is an occultist; rather, she translates into an historical idiom that mode of thought and analysis she perceives within her materials."

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich connects this to his broader "epistemology of occult history": Yates's language throughout *Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition* is saturated with the imagery of lines, roads, and tracks (Bruno as "landmark," Dee "in the line leading to the scientific advances"), and her own historical method — self-validating, tracking a hidden coherent line through history — is homologous to the object she studies (the Hermetic magus's belief in hidden correspondences). Lehrich links this structurally to Lévi-Strauss's concept of bricolage, in which the event is dissolved into synchronic structure, making conventional (diachronic) history "unthinkable," and notes that Eliade's "reactualization" is likewise, by extension, a form of bricolage.
