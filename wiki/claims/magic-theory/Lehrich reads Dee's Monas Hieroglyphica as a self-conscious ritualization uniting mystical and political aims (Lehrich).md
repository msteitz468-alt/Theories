**Claim Statement**
Applying Catherine Bell's performative theory of "ritualization," Lehrich argues that the *Monas
Hieroglyphica* is an "amazingly self-conscious, self-referential" ritual object: Dee constructs the
monad as an experiential entity with special, sacred status outside the physical world, then treats his
own analysis of it as a ritual encounter with the divine. Lehrich argues this reading resolves the
apparent split between Dee's mysticism and his stated "cosmopolitical" ambitions — Dee's 1564 dedication
to Emperor Maximilian II claims the monad holds "still greater mysteries... described in our
cosmopolitical theories," implying the monad itself functioned as an instrument of political
transformation, not merely private meditation.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"If we examine the Monas in terms of modern performative theories of ritual, the political levels of
which are to the fore, the text manifests an amazingly self-conscious, self-referential form of what
Catherine Bell has called 'ritualization.'" Dee's dedication states: "if your Majesty will look at it
with attention, still greater mysteries will present themselves... such as we have described in our
cosmopolitical theories." Lehrich concludes: "We now confront a Dee who was half private mystic, half
political actor. We require a political understanding of private mystical ritual."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Lehrich contrasts this with William Sherman's dismissal of Yates's "myth of the magus," arguing Sherman
overcorrects by separating Dee's mysticism from his politics just as sharply as Yates conflated them,
and that no categorical distinction separated political action from mystical meditation for Dee himself.
