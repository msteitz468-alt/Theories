## Claim Statement

Lehrich argues that Frances Yates and Mircea Eliade, in their scholarship on Renaissance Hermetism and comparative religion respectively, both analyzed the nostalgic myth of a lost magical golden age and simultaneously perpetuated it, projecting their own modernist nostalgia for an "enchanted" pre-disenchantment world onto the historical and mythological texts they studied.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "This myth is not entirely fantasy, either, for two scholars in particular have simultaneously analyzed and perpetuated this nostalgic story, and their visions inspire my examination... Neither scholar invented from whole cloth but rather rewove the threads of history and myth to reinvent a powerful, even magical, narrative. Simply, Yates and Eliade analyzed the Ægyptian nostalgias of former ages, and in the process projected their own modernist nostalgia onto the texts they analyzed."

Lehrich states this is not a project of "bashing" Yates and Eliade for their documented scholarly failings (Yates's questionable readings of Bruno, Eliade's reliance on dubious secondary sources and possible fascist sympathies), but an attempt to take seriously that "these great revolutionaries, who were also visionaries, saw or imagined something precious... in the texts they read."

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich frames this claim as the opening methodological move of Chapter 1: rather than debunking Yates's and Eliade's nostalgic constructions of a magical past, he proposes reading their scholarship itself as continuous with the object of study — nostalgia for Ægypt as a structuring principle of magical thought across eras, including the modern scholarly era.
