**Claim Statement**
Following the Renaissance Egyptological tradition (via Horapollo), Kircher held that Egyptian
hieroglyphs conveyed whole concepts through allegorical pictography (e.g., an ouroboros serpent
signifying the eternal, cyclical universe), fully comprehensible to an initiated priest without prior
knowledge of the specific glyph. He argued this allegorical logic could be run in reverse to reconstruct
Egypt's mental and cultural universe from its glyphs. Kircher extended this into a full diachronic
history of writing: a perfect Adamic language before Babel degraded into hieroglyphics, which then
passed — via Ham (identified with Zoroaster and with Hermes Trismegistus) — into Chinese characters,
which he held were structurally hieroglyphic (pictographic, one-character-per-concept) though he
insisted Chinese lacked the "occult," concept-referring depth of true hieroglyphs.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Kircher quotes Horapollo on the ouroboros: "When they wish to depict the Universe, they draw a serpent
devouring its own tail... By the scales they suggest the stars in the heavens..." On Chinese origins,
Kircher (in *China Illustrata*): "About 300 years after the flood... the first inventor of writing
[according to the Chinese] was the emperor Fu Xi. I can scarcely doubt that he learned this from the
sons of Noah... [Ham] was the same as Zoroaster... the elements of writing were instituted by Father Ham
and Mercurius [or Hermes] Trismegistus." Kircher distinguishes the systems: "the hieroglyphic figures do
not show simple syllables or names, but whole concepts... [signifying] the occult operations which its
archetype causes in the intelligible world. All of these things are completely lacking in the Chinese
characters."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Lehrich compares Kircher's method to Erwin Panofsky's iconology (reconstructing a culture's
*Weltanschauung* from its symbols) and notes that Kircher's conclusions, though methodologically
allegorical rather than philological, differ dramatically from those of modern Egyptologists.
