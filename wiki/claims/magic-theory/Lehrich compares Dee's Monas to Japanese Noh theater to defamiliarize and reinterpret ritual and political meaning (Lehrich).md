**Claim Statement**
Because Dee scholarship has failed by pursuing ever more historical "familiarization," Lehrich turns
to a deliberately cross-cultural, ahistorical comparison: Japanese Noh theater, especially Zeami
Motokiyo's theory of the actor as an emptied "marionette" manipulated by an unseen Actor/spirit. Lehrich
argues the *Monas* functions analogously: the monad is itself like a *shite* (the central masked
figure) — hollow, formally perfect, but animated by God or an angel who "pulls the strings," forcing
the meditating viewer to inhabit and be transformed by it, exactly as Dee sought his "fourth, great, and
truly metaphysical, revolution." Lehrich uses the later ideological capture of Noh by Japanese nativism
(kokugaku, Motoori Norinaga, Hirata Atsutane) as a parallel case for how a private ritual/aesthetic form
can be mobilized as a political-national instrument, illuminating Dee's fusion of mystical and
"cosmopolitical" aims.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"Zeami's view is more concordant with Dee's monad than with possession: the glyph is not divine in a
simple sense but rather an instrument through which the divine may manifest itself in a structured and
controlling manner to transform the meditating scholar." "By this reading, the monad itself was like a
shite. Empty itself, perfectly formed in the divine image but somehow spiritually hollow, the monad had
an actor who stood behind it... This Actor pulled the marionette's strings, forcing the sensitive viewer
to inhabit the shell." Zeami is quoted: "when we come to face death, our life might be likened to a
puppet on a cart... As soon as one string is cut, the creature crumbles and fades."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Lehrich explicitly frames the comparison methodologically, invoking Jonathan Z. Smith's two-step
comparative procedure (defamiliarize, then familiarize via a new class) and warning against both
"radical familiarization" (annulling difference, as in reducing Dee to social context) and "radical
defamiliarization" (insisting on total uniqueness, which "destroys the possibility of interpretation").
