## Claim Statement

Lehrich argues that tarot cards, like the myths analyzed by [[Claude Lévi-Strauss]], have a "double
structure": each card carries a fixed synchronic meaning (its place in the deck's hierarchy, its
independent iconographic meaning, its place in a cosmogonic cycle) while also entering a new, linear,
diachronic order in any given spread. Aligning these levels to formulate a spread as a "score" is, Lehrich
argues, a musical and mythological procedure — and just as a musical score requires performance to become
music, a tarot spread or a set of aligned myths requires a creative interpretive act to become meaningful.
Lehrich further suggests that this implies tarot diviners are themselves practicing a kind of structural
analysis, raising the possibility that "native" mythological thinkers are as intellectually sophisticated
and structurally rigorous as modern scholars, rather than merely expressing unconscious mental structures.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Tarot does indeed have the double structure Lévi-Strauss demands... By aligning the various levels and
seeking to formulate the spread as a 'score' made up of these elements, we have a musical activity, a
mythological procedure." And: "simply laying out the themes and variant structures does not in itself
constitute interpretation, much less art... it requires a creative moment of performance in order to be
heard as music." Lehrich illustrates this with de Mellet's reading of Pharaoh's dream (Genesis 41) as a
tarot "fugue," where the dream serves as subject and the diviners' drawn cards as answering lines that
must be brought into structural and harmonic parallel with it.

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich uses this analysis to push back against Lévi-Strauss's own tendency to see mythical thinkers as
operating only at an "unconscious level," proposing instead that native thought "really is that difficult"
and that tarot diviners may be understood as practicing structuralists.
