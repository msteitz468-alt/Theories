## Claim Statement

Lehrich proposes [[Jacques Derrida]]'s "différance" as an analogical model for the differential character
he has ascribed to magic. Différance's orthography puns on "to differ" and "to defer" simultaneously, and
in French speech "différance" is indistinguishable from "différence." Lehrich argues magic exhibits the
same "simultaneously active and passive differentiation and being-different-from" that characterizes
différance, making magic "a sort of relative of differance." However, Lehrich stresses this is an analogy,
not an identity: magic is a fixed sign that can be permuted and manipulated within systems of signs,
things, and actions, whereas différance itself is not a concept, an object, or even a word, and stands
prior to (rather than within) any system of classification.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"This is that most famous neographism of Jacques Derrida, 'différance.' The orthography draws on a kind
of double pun. First, the active, participial sense indicated by the a makes indistinguishable the root
meanings indicated in English as to differ and to defer... The characteristic qualities of magic too are
a simultaneously active and passive differentiation and being-different-from. Magic therefore can be seen
as a sort of relative of differance." And later: "To be sure, magic cannot be defined as differance, but
it often plays the part of its sign... Unlike differance, magic lends itself to a kind of permutation and
manipulation, allowing the possibility of thinking differance within the order of signs, things, and
actions."

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich frames this analogy as the chapter's central methodological move — "wrench[ing] the structural
stream of thought into the deconstructive" by substituting différance for Lévi-Strauss's bricolage — and
uses it to explain, via Derrida's reading of the Nambikwara episode in *Tristes Tropiques*, why writing
and magic are persistently entangled in Western discourse.
