## Claim Statement

Lehrich states that the tarot deck divides into fifty-six suited cards ("Minor Arcana") and twenty-two
unsuited trump cards ("Major Arcana"), and that the trumps — the distinctive mark of tarot as opposed to
ordinary playing cards — were first mentioned between 1440 and 1457, with the deck's invention now agreed
to be connected with the court of Filippo Maria Visconti, duke of Milan.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"The tarot deck is divided into two main groups: fifty-six suited cards and twenty-two trumps, commonly
known to occultists as Minor and Major arcana, respectively... The trumps are the distinctive mark of
tarot packs: twenty-two unsuited cards, bearing unique images and names. They are first mentioned
sometime between 1440 and 1457, the latter certainly a reference to tarot as such; it is now agreed that
tarocchi were invented in connection with the court of Filippo Maria Visconti, duke of Milan." Lehrich
adds that the suited cards resemble the 52-card Anglo-American deck but have four face cards (Page,
Knight, Queen, King) rather than three, with suits of swords, rods/wands, cups, and coins/pentacles.

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich presents this as neutral historical background before turning to the occult reinterpretation of
tarot, stressing that "the historical record reveals no occult associations to the tarot deck before the
late eighteenth century" — the occult tarot begins only with Antoine Court de Gébelin's 1781 essay in
*Le Monde Primitif*.
