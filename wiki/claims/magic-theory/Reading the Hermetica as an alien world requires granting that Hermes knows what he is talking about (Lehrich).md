## Claim Statement

Lehrich argues that to read the Hermetica as magical texts rather than merely as objects of ordinary historical-archaeological inquiry, the reader must depart radically from normal scholarly interpretation: rather than questioning the validity of Hermes's discourse, one must "grant that Hermes knows what he is talking about," treating the Hermetica as texts describing a genuinely different, "alien" world — Ægypt, not the historical Egypt of archaeology — on its own terms.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "But if we are to read these documents as magical, we must depart radically from the ordinary scholarly modes of interpretation. We must be cautious about questioning the validity and accuracy of Hermes' discourse — indeed, we must grant that Hermes knows what he is talking about, describes, and reflects upon a world different from our own. In short, we need to consider the Hermetica as texts from an alien world."

> "For us, though, mere historical and temporal distance will not suffice. In the history of magic, the Hermetica do not come from Egypt — if by Egypt we mean the historical time and place known to Egyptologists — but from Ægypt. In Ægypt, man and gods had constant communication, divinity and truth were always present, and magic worked."

## Contexts Within the Source

Lehrich explicitly contrasts this approach with "the obvious metaphor" of the normal archaeological approach, in which a scholar situates a text within its historical, material, and geological context (fitting the Hermetica into a picture of Egypt in the early Common Era). He argues this ordinary historicizing mode, while "the normal one," cannot capture what the Hermetica claim about themselves, and that the magical reading requires suppressing "that part of our critical faculties that immediately refers the texts to late Alexandria."
