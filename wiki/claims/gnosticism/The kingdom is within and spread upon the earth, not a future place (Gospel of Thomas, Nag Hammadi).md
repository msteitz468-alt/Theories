## Claim Statement

The Gospel of Thomas records Jesus explicitly rejecting the idea that the kingdom of the Father is a future or distant location — in the sky or the sea — and instead locating it within persons and spread invisibly across the earth, present now but unseen by the unawakened.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Logion 3 is the most direct statement: "If those who lead you say to you: 'Look, the kingdom is in the sky!' then the birds of the sky will precede you. If they say to you: 'It is in the sea,' then the fishes will precede you. Rather, the kingdom is inside of you, and outside of you."

The parallel passage in the two-column version preserved earlier in the same codex (Logion 113) makes the same point from the opposite angle. When the disciples ask "When will the kingdom come?", the text records: "It will not come by waiting for it. It will not be a matter of saying 'here it is' or 'there it is.' Rather, the kingdom of the father is spread out upon the earth, and men do not see it."

The kingdom is therefore not an eschatological future event but a present reality that ordinary perception fails to register. The blindness is not spatial but spiritual.

Logion 51 makes this explicit when the disciples ask about the resurrection of the dead and the coming of the new world: "That which you are awaiting has already come, but you do not recognize it." Recognition, not waiting, is the requirement.

Logion 49 ties entry to the kingdom to the identity of the seeker rather than to future action: "Blessed are the solitary ones, the elect. For you will find the kingdom. For you come from it and will return to it." The kingdom is origin and destination — already the seeker's home.

Logion 82 (in the columnar version) states: "He who is near me is near the fire, and he who is far from me is far from the kingdom." Proximity to Jesus is proximity to the kingdom — a relational and present condition, not a future reward.

Logion 22 describes the mode of entry in non-spatial terms: entering the kingdom requires making "the two into one" — inside and outside, above and below, male and female — a dissolution of the inner divisions that constitute the unawakened state.

Logion 27 offers one qualifier: "If you do not abstain from the world, you will not find the kingdom. If you do not make the Sabbath into a Sabbath, you will not see the Father." Abstaining from worldly attachment is presented as a precondition for perceiving what is already present.

## Cross-source connections

The teaching that the kingdom is present and interior rather than future and external stands in notable contrast to apocalyptic traditions and is a point of convergence between the Gospel of Thomas and the broader Gnostic worldview that the awakened soul already possesses what the uninitiated seek. The Corpus Hermeticum ([[The Corpus Hermeticum]]) similarly teaches that the divine realm is accessible inwardly to those who have received gnosis. Later alternative writers who draw on Gnostic motifs — including the idea that ordinary humanity is spiritually asleep, controlled by false teachers — include those treated in [[Children of the Matrix (Icke, 2001)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Several logia in Thomas do preserve an eschatological horizon. Logion 11 states "This heaven will pass away, and the (heaven) above it will pass away." Logion 57 describes a future harvest. The tension between present-kingdom and future-transformation is not fully resolved within the text. Some scholars read Thomas as teaching a realized eschatology; others see it as spiritualizing an originally apocalyptic tradition.
