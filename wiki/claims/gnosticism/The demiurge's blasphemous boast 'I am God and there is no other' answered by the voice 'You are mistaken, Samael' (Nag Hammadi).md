## Claim Statement

The Gnostic tractates of the Nag Hammadi library claim that the creator-god of the material world (Yaldabaoth / Sakla / Samael) committed a foundational sin of arrogance by declaring himself the only god — the same declaration attributed to the God of Israel in the Hebrew Bible ("I am God and there is no other"). This boast, in the Gnostic reading, is immediately rebuked by a voice from the true realm above, which names him **Samael** — meaning "god of the blind" — exposing his declaration as blasphemous ignorance rather than truth.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The **Hypostasis of the Archons** (Codex II — "The Reality of the Rulers") states, of the demiurge's first act of self-assertion:

> "Their chief is blind; because of his power and his ignorance and his arrogance he said, with his power, 'It is I who am God; there is none apart from me.' When he said this, he sinned against the entirety. And this speech got up to incorruptibility; then there was a voice that came forth from incorruptibility, saying, 'You are mistaken, Samael' — which is, 'god of the blind.'"

Later in the same tractate, the angel Eleleth elaborates the full scene to Norea:

> "Opening his eyes, he saw a vast quantity of matter without limit; and he became arrogant, saying, 'It is I who am God, and there is none other apart from me.' When he said this, he sinned against the entirety. And a voice came forth from above the realm of absolute power, saying, 'You are mistaken, Samael' — which is, 'god of the blind.'"

Immediately following, the demiurge's own daughter **Zoe (Life)** adds a second rebuke:

> "And Zoe (Life), the daughter of Pistis Sophia, cried out and said to him, 'You are mistaken, Sakla!' — for which the alternative name is Yaltabaoth. She breathed into his face, and her breath became a fiery angel for her; and that angel bound Yaldabaoth and cast him down into Tartaros below the abyss."

The same boast appears in the **Gospel of the Egyptians** (Codex III), attributed to Sakla after founding the world: "I, I am a jealous god, and apart from me nothing has come into being, since he trusted in his nature."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

- The *Hypostasis of the Archons* also narrates the repentance of **Sabaoth**, one of Yaldabaoth's own offspring, who condemns his father and is elevated to the seventh heaven — suggesting the Gnostic system is not simply dualist; transformation is possible even within the archonic hierarchy.
- The texts are internally consistent that Yaldabaoth's boast is false but do not claim the true Father is malevolent. The correction comes *from above*, indicating the real God exists and responds to the blasphemy.
- The **Sophia of Jesus Christ** (Codex III) frames the same dynamic differently, attributing Yaldabaoth's "bond of forgetfulness" to the will of Sophia so that "the matter might be revealed … concerning his arrogance and blindness" — i.e., his ignorance is itself part of the providential exposure of his deficiency.
