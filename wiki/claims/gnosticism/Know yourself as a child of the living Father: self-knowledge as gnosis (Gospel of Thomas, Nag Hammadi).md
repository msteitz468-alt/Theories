## Claim Statement

The Gospel of Thomas records Jesus teaching that knowing oneself is both the method and the substance of salvation: the one who knows themselves is simultaneously recognized by the Father, realizes they are "children of the living Father," and escapes the poverty of ignorance. Self-knowledge here functions as gnosis — saving knowledge of one's divine origin.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Logion 3 makes the connection between self-knowledge and divine identity explicit: "When you come to know yourselves, then you will be known, and you will realize that you are the children of the living Father. But if you do not come to know yourselves, then you exist in poverty, and you are poverty." The failure to know oneself is not merely ignorance — it is identified with poverty as such, a condition of spiritual destitution.

Logion 50 instructs disciples on how to answer, when they are asked where they come from: "Say to them: 'We have come from the light, the place where the light has come into being by itself, has established itself and has appeared in their image.' If they say to you: 'Is it you?' then say: 'We are his children, and we are the elect of the living Father.' If they ask you: 'What is the sign of your Father among you?' then say to them: 'It is movement and repose.'" Knowing where one comes from — a luminous, self-originated place — is the content of the self-knowledge being urged.

Logion 24 locates this light within the person: "Light exists inside a person of light, and he shines on the whole world. If he does not shine, there is darkness." The divine light is not external but constitutive of the "person of light" — one who has come to know their own nature.

Logion 67 (in the columnar version of the text) states: "Jesus said, 'If one who knows the all still feels a personal deficiency, he is completely deficient.'" Knowing the external totality without knowing oneself is worthless — self-knowledge is the indispensable core.

Logion 108 (columnar version) offers a formulation of union through gnosis: "He who will drink from my mouth will become like me. I myself shall become he, and the things that are hidden will be revealed to him." The disciples who receive and understand the hidden words are transformed into the teacher — the boundary between the knowing self and the knowing source dissolves.

Logion 28 diagnoses the failure of those who lack self-knowledge: "I found all of them drunk. None of them did I find thirsty. And my soul ached for the children of humanity, because they are blind in their heart, and they cannot see; for they came into the world empty, and they also seek to depart from the world empty. But now they are drunk. But when they shake off their wine, then they will change their mind." The drunkenness is a state of unknowing, of being absent to oneself and one's divine origin.

Logion 70 states: "If you bring forth what is within you, what you have will save you. If you do not have that within you, what you do not have within you will kill you." The saving reality is already within — the act of bringing it forth (i.e., knowing and articulating what one truly is) is salvation itself.

## Cross-source connections

The call to self-knowledge as the path to recognizing oneself as a child of the divine directly parallels the Hermetic tradition's "know thyself" injunction developed in the Corpus Hermeticum ([[The Corpus Hermeticum]]). The broader Gnostic framework, in which humanity contains a divine spark obscured by the material creation, is the cosmological context for this teaching — see [[Gnosticism: the unknowable Father, Sophia's fall, and the archon-creator (Nag Hammadi)]]. The idea that the divine spark is imprisoned in ignorance and must be awakened through revealed knowledge also underlies later treatments in [[Fallen angels, the Serpent, and Lucifer as misunderstood cosmic forces (Blavatsky)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Thomas does not develop a full cosmogony explaining how the divine spark came to be in ignorance of itself — that mythological background is supplied by other Nag Hammadi texts (the Apocryphon of John, On the Origin of the World) rather than by Thomas itself. Thomas's self-knowledge teaching can also be read in a non-Gnostic direction, as a wisdom tradition emphasis on self-examination rather than a specifically cosmological doctrine about the soul's pre-existent divine origin.
