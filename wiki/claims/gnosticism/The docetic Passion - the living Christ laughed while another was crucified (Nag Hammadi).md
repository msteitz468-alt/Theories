## Claim Statement

The Second Treatise of the Great Seth and the Apocalypse of Peter both teach that the true spiritual Christ did not suffer or die at the crucifixion. In the Great Seth, the Saviour declares explicitly that he did not die in reality but only in appearance, and that it was another — specifically naming Simon of Cyrene as the one who bore the cross — who endured the physical punishment while Christ "was rejoicing in the height" and "laughing at their ignorance." In the Apocalypse of Peter, Peter witnesses a vision of two simultaneous Christs: one being nailed to the cross and one standing apart, "glad and laughing on the tree" — and the Saviour identifies the crucified figure as merely "the fleshly part, which is the substitute being put to shame."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

### From the Second Treatise of the Great Seth (CG VII.2)

The Saviour's own account of the Passion substitution:

> "I did not die in reality but in appearance, lest I be put to shame by them because these are my kinsfolk. I removed the shame from me and I did not become fainthearted in the face of what happened to me at their hands. I was about to succumb to fear, and I \<suffered\> according to their sight and thought, in order that they may never find any word to speak about them. For my death, which they think happened, (happened) to them in their error and blindness, since they nailed their man unto their death." (trans. Bullard and Gibbons)

The text then names the substitutions at each point of the Passion narrative:

> "It was another, their father, who drank the gall and the vinegar; it was not I. They struck me with the reed; it was another, Simon, who bore the cross on his shoulder. I was another upon Whom they placed the crown of thorns. But I was rejoicing in the height over all the wealth of the archons and the offspring of their error, of their empty glory. And I was laughing at their ignorance."

The Saviour further explains his method of descent through the archons' gates:

> "For as I came downward, no one saw me. For I was altering my shapes, changing from form to form. And therefore, when I was at their gates, I assumed their likeness. For I passed them by quietly, and I was viewing the places, and I was not afraid nor ashamed, for I was undefiled."

The archons who believed they were crucifying Christ were in fact being deceived:

> "For it was ludicrous. It is I who bear witness that it was ludicrous, since the archons do not know that it is an ineffable union of undefiled truth... having proclaimed a doctrine of a dead man and lies so as to resemble the freedom and purity of the perfect assembly."

### From the Apocalypse of Peter (CG VII.3)

Peter, watching the scene of arrest, asks in confusion:

> "What do I see, O Lord? That it is you yourself whom they take, and that you are grasping me? Or who is this one, glad and laughing on the tree? And is it another one whose feet and hands they are striking?"

The Saviour replies with the definitive docetic statement:

> "He whom you saw on the tree, glad and laughing, this is the living Jesus. But this one into whose hands and feet they drive the nails is his fleshly part, which is the substitute being put to shame, the one who came into being in his likeness. But look at him and me." (trans. Brashler and Bullard)

A fuller explanation follows:

> "He who stands near him is the living Savior, the first in him, whom they seized and released, who stands joyfully looking at those who did him violence, while they are divided among themselves. Therefore he laughs at their lack of perception, knowing that they are born blind. So then the one susceptible to suffering shall come, since the body is the substitute. But what they released was my incorporeal body. But I am the intellectual Spirit filled with radiant light."

The crucified figure is further identified:

> "He whom they crucified is the first-born, and the home of demons, and the stony vessel in which they dwell, of Elohim, of the cross, which is under the Law."

## Cross-source connections

The docetic reading of the Passion has no direct parallel in the mainstream wiki sources, but the underlying logic — that the archons/material powers are fundamentally blind and cannot touch the true divine — is the same logic that drives the broader Gnostic cosmology covered by [[Gnosticism: the unknowable Father, Sophia's fall, and the archon-creator (Nag Hammadi)]]. Icke's "archons" (see [[Children of the Matrix (Icke, 2001)]]) are drawn from this Gnostic tradition; his controllers similarly cannot touch the true spiritual human. The Gnostic demiurge who boasts "I am God and there is no other beside me" appears by name in the Great Seth as "Yaldabaoth," contextualised in the broader archon framework.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The docetic position is presented as revealed gnosis — the secret truth behind the visible events — and no opposing voice is given within these tractates. The Great Seth does acknowledge that some who call themselves Christians "served two masters" and did not fully know the Saviour, implying that orthodox (non-docetic) Christians are among those who misunderstand. The Apocalypse of Peter likewise condemns future church leaders ("bishops and deacons") as "dry canals" who will mislead people by teaching about "a dead man."
