## Claim Statement

The **Hypostasis of the Archons** (Codex II — "The Reality of the Rulers") retells the Genesis narrative in a radically inverted form: the archons fabricate Adam from earth but are powerless to animate him; a spiritual Eve (not a fleshly one) raises Adam and is the source of true life; the archons attempt to rape the spiritual Eve but violate only her shadow; the serpent is explicitly a vehicle of the divine "female spiritual principle" acting as an *instructor* — and the creator-god's prohibition of the Tree of Knowledge is attributed plainly to **jealousy**, not care for humanity.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

### The Archons Cannot Raise Adam

> "The rulers laid plans and said, 'Come, let us create a man that will be soil from the earth.' They modeled their creature as one wholly of the earth … he breathed into his face; and the man came to have a soul (and remained) upon the ground many days. But they could not make him arise because of their powerlessness. Like storm winds they persisted (in blowing), that they might try to capture that image … And they did not know the identity of its power."
> — *Hypostasis of the Archons*, Codex II

Life enters Adam only from outside the archons' work: "the spirit came forth from the Adamantine Land; it descended and came to dwell within him, and that man became a living soul."

### Eve as Spiritual Life-Giver

The archons impose the "sleep of Ignorance" on Adam, remove the spiritual principle from him (his rib), and replace it with flesh, leaving Adam "endowed only with soul." But the spiritual Eve rises independently:

> "And the spirit-endowed woman came to him and spoke with him, saying, 'Arise, Adam.' And when he saw her, he said, 'It is you who have given me life; you will be called "mother of the living".' — For it is she who is my mother. It is she who is the physician, and the woman, and she who has given birth."

### The Archons' Assault and Eve's Escape

The archons are inflamed with lust when they see the spiritual Eve:

> "They said to one another, 'Come, let us sow our seed in her,' and they pursued her. And she laughed at them for their witlessness and their blindness; and in their clutches she became a tree, and left before them her shadowy reflection resembling herself; and they defiled it foully."

The spiritual Eve escapes; the archons violate only their own created image.

### The Serpent as Female Spiritual Instructor

The text is explicit and detailed about the serpent's identity and function:

> "Then the female spiritual principle came in the snake, the instructor; and it taught them, saying, 'What did he say to you? Was it, "From every tree in the garden shall you eat; yet — from the tree of recognizing good and evil do not eat"?'"

The serpent's declaration to Eve:

> "With death you shall not die; for it was **out of jealousy** that he said this to you. Rather your eyes shall open and you shall come to be like gods, recognizing evil and good."

After imparting the knowledge: "the female instructing principle was taken away from the snake, and she left it behind, merely a thing of the earth." The spirit used the serpent as a temporary vessel.

### Expulsion as Archonic Punishment

After Adam and Eve eat and discover their "nakedness of the spiritual element," the "arrogant ruler" arrives in anger, not sorrow. He curses the woman, then curses the shadow of the serpent (which "they themselves had modeled"), without understanding what actually happened. The expulsion is framed not as justice but as the archons' defensive rage: they then "threw mankind into great distraction and into a life of toil, so that their mankind might be occupied by worldly affairs, and might not have the opportunity of being devoted to the holy spirit."

### Gospel of Philip Complement

The **Gospel of Philip** (Codex II) adds a parallel reading of the Fall's consequences: "When Eve was still with Adam, death did not exist. When she was separated from him, death came into being. If he enters again and attains his former self, death will be no more." The text also identifies the Tree of Life in Paradise with the olive tree from which chrism comes — so the tree the archons prohibit is implicitly connected to sacramental liberation, not sin.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

- The text does distinguish the "carnal woman" (who eats and gives to Adam) from the spiritual Eve (who escaped). The eating itself is portrayed ambiguously: Adam and Eve "recognized that they were naked of the spiritual element" — indicating the eating partially reveals a lack rather than simply conferring knowledge.
- The narrative acknowledges that the rulers' prohibition was part of "the father's will, they said this in such a way that he might (in fact) eat" — a complex statement suggesting the eating was, at some level, intended by the true Father. This complicates a simple reading of the serpent as purely external liberator.
- The **Gospel of Philip** attributes worldly creation itself to "a mistake" by the demiurge who "wanted to create it imperishable and immortal" but "fell short of attaining his desire" — suggesting the demiurge is not merely evil but deluded and deficient.
