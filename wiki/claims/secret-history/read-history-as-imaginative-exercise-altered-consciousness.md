## Claim Statement

Booth's stated method: the secret history cannot be grasped through ordinary materialist reasoning but must be approached as "an imaginative exercise" — the reader is asked "to imagine what it would feel like to believe the opposite of what we have been brought up to believe," because "higher forms of intelligence can be accessed in altered states."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He presents the book as a deliberate inversion: "the basic facts of history can be interpreted in a way which is almost completely the opposite of the way we normally understand them." He invokes the Western tradition's "imaginative exercises which involve cultivating and dwelling upon visual images," and aligns himself with children's authors (Lewis Carroll, the Brothers Grimm, C. S. Lewis) and satirists (Rabelais, Swift) who teach readers "to think backwards, look at everything upside down… and break free of established, fixed ways of thinking." He notes the most secret teachings are transmitted only orally or in deliberately obscure texts (Blavatsky, Gurdjieff, Steiner) "aimed at people already in the know."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

This is the source's own framing of its non-empirical status; Booth explicitly asks not to be read as making conventional factual claims but as offering a perspective-shifting "imaginative exercise," and warns the teaching "will outrage many people and fly in the face of common sense."

## Contexts Within the Source

Frames how the rest of the book's claims are to be taken; resonates with the altered-states epistemologies elsewhere in the wiki (Monroe's "Knowns," the holographic/idealist cluster).
