## Claim Statement

The opening narratives of the Hebrew Bible — Creation, the Garden of Eden, the "sons of the gods" who took human wives, and the Flood — are a **monotheistic abridgement of much older Sumerian and Babylonian texts**. Where Genesis says *Elohim* (a grammatical plural), Sitchin reads the plural literally: the Nefilim. The single biblical Deity is, on his account, a compression of the Mesopotamian pantheon.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sitchin repeatedly sets Genesis side by side with the *Enuma Elish* and Sumerian texts, claiming "the biblical parallel is clear" and "Bible and science are in full agreement." On Creation he notes the Akkadian epic, deciphered by George Smith (1876), "not only paralleled but also preceded portions of the Holy Scriptures," sparking the "BABEL UND BIBEL" debate. He reads "Let us make Man in our image, after our likeness" as a plural utterance of the gods, and treats the "single Deity — multideity difference" between the biblical and Mesopotamian Flood accounts as evidence of monotheistic editing of an older polytheistic original.

He also reads Genesis 1's sequence (grasses → seed-bearing cereals → fruit trees; waters gathered; dry land; living creatures; then man) as matching both the *Enuma Elish*'s fifth tablet and modern science — i.e. the Hebrew text preserved accurate Sumerian information.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim licenses Sitchin's whole method: reading scripture as edited history of the Nefilim rather than as theology. He nonetheless suggests (preface) the Nefilim may have been fulfilling "a vaster Master Plan," leaving room for a universal Almighty above them.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin notes the **differences** between the accounts (the biblical single Deity vs. the Mesopotamian many gods) rather than suppressing them — he treats the difference itself as the trace of editing.
