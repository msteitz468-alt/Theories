## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues that the mythological "wars of the gods" recorded in Sumerian, Egyptian, Greek, and Indian texts were not allegories or primitive fantasies but literal accounts of physical combat among the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]]—flesh-and-blood Anunnaki astronauts from Nibiru who settled on Earth. These wars revolved around control of Earth's territory, its space facilities, and the right of succession among rival divine lineages, chiefly the clans of [[Enki (Sitchin)]] and [[Enlil (Sitchin)]]. Advanced weaponry—aerial vehicles, directed-energy beams, and missiles—is described in the ancient sources with what Sitchin reads as technical specificity, and the same conflicts, he contends, recur under different divine names across cultures.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sitchin opens the book with the declaration that "long before man warred with man, the gods battled among themselves. Indeed, it was as the Wars of the Gods that the Wars of Man began" (line 101). The opening preamble frames the entire work: "And the Wars of the Gods, for control of this Earth, had begun on their own planet" (line 105).

**The Horus–Seth War (First Pyramid War context)**

The temple inscription at Edfu, which Sitchin reads as a literal battle record, describes Horus flying in a "Winged Disk" to attack enemies of Ra: "In the heights of the skies, from the Winged Disk, he saw the enemies, and came upon them from behind. From his forepart he let loose against them a Storm which they could neither see with their eyes, nor hear with their ears. It brought death to all of them in a single moment" (lines 1252–1254). The opposing force, Sitchin argues, belonged to Seth—the rival divine lineage controlling parts of Egypt and the Sinai. The aerial battles between Horus and Seth involved dedicated weapons: "Horus took to the air in a Nar (a 'Fiery Pillar'), which was depicted as an elongated, cylindrical vessel equipped with fins or short wings. Its bulkhead contained two 'eyes,' which kept changing color from blue to red and back to blue; from the rear, jetlike trails were shown; from the front, the contraption spewed out rays" (line 1721). Horus's "harpoon" weapon was, Sitchin argues, a multi-warhead missile: "the 'harpoon' was indeed an ingenious three-in-one rocket: as the first, larger missile was fired, the way was opened for the two smaller missiles to be launched. The nickname ('Weapon of Thirty') suggests that the missiles were what we nowadays call Multiple Warhead Missiles, each missile holding ten warheads" (lines 1735–1736).

The motivation for the war is traced to the rules of divine succession. Seth was denied the legal heirship because Osiris had taken Isis, the half-sister whose offspring would have been Seth's dynastic trump card. Sitchin quotes Sumerian genealogical rules to establish that "the son first born to the ruler by his own half-sister . . . superseded the Firstborn and became the Legal Heir" (lines 1583–1584), and shows that Seth's murderous campaign against Osiris and his subsequent war with Horus were driven by this succession logic, not merely lust for territory.

**The Zeus–Typhon and Indra–Vritra parallels**

Sitchin identifies the Greek Theogony's account of Zeus versus the Titans and Typhon as the same inter-divine war recorded from a different cultural vantage. When Zeus hurled his Thunder-Stone at the Titans: "The hot vapor lapped around the Titans . . . Flame unspeakable rose bright to the upper air. The Flashing glare of the Thunder-Stone, its lightning, blinded their eyes . . . Astounding heat seized Chaos . . . It seemed as if Earth and wide Heaven above had come together; A mighty crash, as though Earth was hurled to ruin" (lines 2177–2231)—which Sitchin reads as describing "nothing short of an atomic explosion" (line 2167). The same war is preserved in the Vedic texts as the conflict of Indra with Vritra: "The iron shafts with thousand points, Which blaze and hiss athwart the sky, Swift to their mark unerring fly, And lay the proudest foeman low" (lines 3021–3045). Sitchin argues that the Greek "Titans" themselves are a Sumerian term: "TI.TA.AN in Sumerian would have literally meant 'Those Who in Heaven Live'—precisely the designation of the Igigi led by Kumarbi; and their adversaries were the Anunnaki 'Who are on Earth'" (line 4079).

**The Wars of the Olden Gods (Kumarbi Cycle / Myth of Zu)**

Sitchin reads the Hittite/Hurrian Kumarbi Cycle as recording an early war between the Igigi astronauts (orbiting sky-crew) and the Anunnaki on Earth. When the god Zu stole Enlil's Tablets of Destinies from the Mission Control Center in Nippur, "Suspended were the divine formulas; The lighted brightness petered out; Silence prevailed. In space, the Igigi were confounded" (lines 4183–4207). Ninurta, Enlil's son, was sent to defeat Zu: "Ninurta arrayed 'seven whirlwinds which stir up the dust,' armed his chariot with the 'Ill Winds' weapons, and attacked Zu . . . 'the pinions of Zu were overcome.' Zu was captured" (line 4275). The text also records an early armed clash between Enlil and Enki over the Primitive Workers—the newly created humans: "Enki refused Enlil's request for the transfer of Primitive Workers to Mesopotamia. . . . Enlil took the extreme step of disconnecting the communications with the home planet. . . . Then he launched an armed attack against the Land of the Mines" (lines 4495–4496).

**The genealogical root of all wars**

Sitchin repeatedly stresses that rivalry between the Enki and Enlil lineages—rooted in Nibiru's succession rules—was the persistent source of all subsequent conflicts: "One cannot stress enough the importance of lineage and genealogy in the Wars of the Gods; the struggles for succession and supremacy, on Nibiru as on Earth later on" (line 3409). The division of Earth by lot—"Anu to heaven went up; To Enlil the Earth was made subject; That which the sea as a loop encloses, they gave to the prince Enki. To the Abzu Enki went down" (lines 3655–3685)—set the stage for the territorial wars that followed.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the overarching thesis of the entire book. It builds on the foundation established in [[The Twelfth Planet (Sitchin)]] (the Anunnaki's arrival and the creation of humanity) and applies it to the warlike episodes in world mythology. The claim is inseparable from [[Sitchin: the Pyramid Wars ended with the division of Earth among the Anunnaki]], which covers the specific military conflict over the Giza complex. The gods involved—[[Enki (Sitchin)]], [[Enlil (Sitchin)]], and their offspring Ninurta, Marduk, Nergal, Horus, Seth—are read as real commanders of real armies using real weapons. Human kings who later invoked divine mandate for their wars (Sargon, Hammurabi, Ramses II, Sennacherib) were, Sitchin argues, acting out the same pattern.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that mainstream scholars treat all of these texts as mythology, allegory, or primitive nature-worship. He cites classical scholars such as E. A. Wallis Budge and S. B. Mercer who do suggest the Horus–Seth conflict has a historical kernel ("The story of the conflict between Horus and Seth represents a historical event," Mercer, quoted at line 1709), but mainstream archaeology does not accept that the "gods" were extraterrestrial beings using advanced weaponry. The ancient texts themselves are fragmentary and damaged (Sitchin repeatedly notes broken tablets and missing sections), meaning his reconstructions involve inference. Mainstream Egyptology, Assyriology, and classical scholarship regard the divine wars as mythological dramatizations of natural phenomena, political conflicts among human factions, or theological constructs—not literal records of technology-wielding alien combatants.
