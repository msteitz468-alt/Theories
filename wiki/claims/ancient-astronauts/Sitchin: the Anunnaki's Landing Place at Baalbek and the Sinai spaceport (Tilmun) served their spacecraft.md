## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues that the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] maintained two distinct spacefaring installations on Earth: (1) the "Landing Place" on the Cedar Mountain in Lebanon — identified with modern Baalbek — which served as an atmospheric "airport" for the detachable command-module Sky Chambers (MU) of the gods; and (2) TIL.MUN ("Land of the Missiles"), the full spaceport on the Sinai peninsula, from which the large GIR rocketships ascended to orbit and eventually back to [[The Twelfth Planet (Nibiru)]]. Together these two facilities constituted the spacefaring infrastructure of the Anunnaki on post-Diluvial Earth. Both are identified as literal places — not mythology — documented by Sumerian cuneiform texts, the Epic of Gilgamesh, Egyptian Pyramid Texts, Canaanite Ugaritic tablets, and the physical remains at Baalbek itself.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

### The Two-Facility Framework

Sitchin first establishes the distinction between the two types of Nefilim craft: "the gods indeed had two types of craft: the GIRs, the Rocketships that were operated from Tilmun; and what the Sumerians called a MU, a 'Sky Chamber.' . . . the uppermost section of the GIR, the Command Module — what the Egyptians called Ben-Ben — could be detached and fly in Earth's skies as a MU" (lines 6475–6476). The consequence is that there needed to be two separate facilities: "Tilmun, then, was the location of the Spaceport. The Cedar Mountain was the location of the 'Landing Place,' the 'Crossroads of Ishtar' — the Airport of the Gods" (line 6499).

### Baalbek as the Landing Place

Sitchin identifies the "Cedar Forest" of Gilgamesh as the mountains of Lebanon: "With the exception of subsidiary growths on the island of Cyprus, there is only one such location throughout the Near East: the mountains of Lebanon" (line 6503). The Cedar Mountain of the epic — the "Abode of the Gods, / the Crossroads of Ishtar" (lines 5045–5051), guarded by the mechanical monster Huwawa — is the same place Gilgamesh addresses when he prays: "Let me go, O Shamash! . . . to the Landing Place, give command" (line 4917). The populace confirm he is headed for "the Landing Place" (line 4975).

Sitchin arrives at Baalbek through archaeological and textual convergence. He describes the platform's extraordinary scale: "the vastest-ever temple to Jupiter stood high, with some of the tallest (65 feet) and largest (7.5 feet in diameter) columns known in antiquity" (line 7521). The Trilithon — three individual stone blocks each measuring "over sixty feet in length, with sides of fourteen and twelve feet . . . weighing well over 1,000 tons" (line 7677) — surpasses any known ancient construction capability. A fourth slab still in the quarry is "sixty-nine feet long and has a girth of sixteen by fourteen feet" and "weighs, by conservative estimates, more than 1,200 tons" (line 7685). Sitchin notes: "even nowadays there exists no crane, vehicle or mechanism that can lift such a weight of 1,000–1,200 tons — to say nothing of carrying such an immense object over valley and mountainside" (line 7699). He concludes this was a landing pad built to sustain the weight of spacecraft.

The identification is confirmed by the name: "the local people abandoned the Hellenistic name Heliopolis and resumed calling the place by its Semitic name . . . Baalbek" (line 7731). Through the Ugaritic texts from Ras Shamra, Sitchin equates the site with Ba'al's "Crest of Zaphon" — the very "Landing Place" sought by Gilgamesh: "there is no doubt left in our mind that in Baalbek we have found Ba'al's Crest of Zaphon, the target of the first journey of Gilgamesh" (line 7835). The designation "Crossroads of Ishtar" implies that the goddess "could come and go from that 'Landing Place' to other landing places upon Earth" (line 7839). Local traditions preserved by a seventeenth-century traveler record that "After the Flood, when Nimrod reigned over Lebanon, he sent giants to rebuild the Fortress of Baalbek, which is so named in honor of Ba'al, the god of the Moabites, worshippers of the Sun-god" (line 7727).

### Tilmun as the Sinai Spaceport

The Sumerian epithet TIL.MUN literally means "Land of the Missiles" (line 4387). After the Deluge, Sitchin writes, the pre-Diluvial Spaceport at Sippar was destroyed: "The low-lying Mesopotamian plain was still too muddy for millennia to enable the rebuilding there of these vital installations. Another place — more elevated yet suitable, secluded but accessible — had to be found for the Spaceport and its auxiliary installations. It was to be a 'sacred zone' — a restricted area, accessible only by permission. It was called in Sumerian TIL.MUN — literally, 'Land of the Missiles'" (line 4387). Utu/Shamash — "chief of the Spacemen based on Earth, the 'Eagles'" — was placed in charge (line 4391). The hero of the Deluge, Ziusudra, was taken there: "In the Land of the Crossing, / The Land Tilmun, / The place where Utu rises, / They caused him to dwell" (lines 4155–4171).

When Gilgamesh finally crosses the Dead Sea on his overland journey, he arrives: "He arrived at TIL.MUN — 'The Land of the Living'" (line 5787). At its central peak, Mount Mashu, Sitchin quotes the text directly: "The name of the Mountain is Mashu. / At the mountain of Mashu he arrived; / Where daily the Shems he watched / As they depart and come in" (lines 5807–5823). The mountain's dual function is explicit: "On high, to the Celestial Band / it is connected; / Below, / to the Lower World it is bound" (lines 5837–5851). Its gate is guarded by "Rocket-men" whose "dreaded spotlight sweeps the mountains. / They watch over Shamash / as he ascends and descends" (lines 5861–5883).

The geographic identification with Sinai is argued from multiple directions. Sitchin states: "we can confidently conclude that the 'Land Tilmun' was the Sinai peninsula" (line 8265). The Mesopotamian texts place Tilmun "at the 'mouth' of two bodies of water" — matching the Sinai peninsula where "the Red Sea separates into two arms — the Gulf of Suez on the west, and the Gulf of Elat (Gulf of Aqaba) on the east" (line 8273). Assyrian king Esarhaddon boasted: "Upon Qanayah, king of Tilmun, I imposed tribute" after describing conquests of Ashdod — placing Tilmun adjacent to the Brook of Egypt (Wadi El-Arish), the Sinai's northern boundary (line 8287). Ashurbanipal claimed overlordship "from Tyre, which is in the Upper Sea (Mediterranean) as far as Tilmun which is in the Lower Sea" (the Red Sea) — a span matching the Sinai (line 8287). The Sumerian name Sinai itself, Sitchin notes, likely means "Belonged to Sin" (the Moon god, who also oversaw the region around Baalbek and Jericho, line 8257).

The Sumerian text about water shortage in Tilmun corroborates the Sinai identification through the discovery of a massive underground aquifer beneath the Sinai's central plain, found by Israeli hydrologists — matching a Sumerian tale in which Enki solves Tilmun's water shortage by directing Utu/Shamash to fire a missile from the sky to pierce the rock and release subterranean water: "Through the crystal stones he brought up water" (line 8415). Sitchin reads the existence of this real aquifer, 3,000 feet below the surface yet sub-artesian, as confirming that the Nefilim possessed the technology to locate and tap it.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the geographic conclusion of the companion claim [[Sitchin: humanity's quests for immortality were attempts to reach the Anunnaki's abode]]. The Landing Place at Baalbek was Gilgamesh's first destination — the atmospheric airport where the Sky Chambers landed and where mortals (including a Phoenician king of Tyre, per Ezekiel 28) occasionally gained admission. Tilmun/Sinai was the deep-space spaceport — the Duat of the Pyramid Texts, the "Stairway to Heaven" — where the full GIR rocketships were raised. Both sites fit into Sitchin's larger framework from [[The Twelfth Planet (Sitchin)]]: the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] arrived from [[The Twelfth Planet (Nibiru)]] 450,000 years ago, established mining and processing infrastructure, and built spacefaring facilities to transfer gold back to the home planet. The post-Diluvial Sinai spaceport replaced the destroyed pre-Diluvial one at Sippar (Mesopotamia). [[Enki (Sitchin)]] administered the landing on Earth, [[Enlil (Sitchin)]] commanded the mission, and [[Anu (Sitchin)]] ruled from Nibiru; Utu/Shamash commanded the Eagles at Tilmun.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that scholars have termed Sumerian statements about Shamash "rising" in the Cedar Land as "cryptic and still enigmatic" (line 6475). The identification of Baalbek with the Gilgamesh Cedar Mountain is his inference, not a statement in any ancient text that names Baalbek. The Trilithon's existence is a genuine archaeological puzzle, but no archaeologist or engineer has proposed an extraterrestrial construction explanation; the consensus is that the Romans extended an earlier, already-massive Hellenistic and Phoenician platform using sophisticated block-and-tackle methods and large labour forces. The location of Tilmun remains debated among Assyriologists, with proposals ranging from Bahrain (the dominant scholarly view) to the Sinai; Sitchin's Sinai identification is a minority position unsupported by mainstream cuneiform scholarship. The entire framework — that the Nefilim were space-faring astronauts with physical infrastructure on Earth — is rejected by mainstream archaeology, Assyriology, and Egyptology.
