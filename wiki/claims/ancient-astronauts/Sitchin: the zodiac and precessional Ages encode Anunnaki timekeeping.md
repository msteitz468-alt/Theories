## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues that the twelve-house zodiac and the entire phenomenon of the Precession of the Equinoxes — the roughly 26,000-year "Great Year" during which the vernal equinox sunrise appears to move backwards through all twelve zodiacal constellations — were not human discoveries but were deliberately invented by the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] (the Anunnaki) as a solution to a specific timekeeping problem. Having arrived from [[The Twelfth Planet (Nibiru)]], whose orbital year equals 3,600 Earth-years (one [[Shar (3,600-year orbital period)]]), the Anunnaki needed a bridge between their own Divine Time and the far shorter Earthly Time. The zodiac and its precessional cycle of 25,920 years — divided into twelve Ages of 2,160 years each — provided that bridge. The Nippur calendar, begun circa 3800 BC in the Age of Taurus, was the gift of this system to humanity; and the arrival of each new zodiacal Age represents a cosmic clock-tick originally engineered by the Anunnaki.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The argument is laid out systematically in Chapter 1. Sitchin first establishes the primacy of the [[Shar (3,600-year orbital period)]]: "The sar, 3,600 Earth-years, was the orbital period of Nibiru around the Sun; for anyone on Nibiru, it was just one Nibiru-year." The Anunnaki, he writes, "had come to Earth from Nibiru in great antiquity; and when they had come here, they counted time not in Earth terms but in terms of Nibiru's orbit. The unit of that Divine Time, a year of the gods, was the sar."

The zodiac and precession, on this reading, were the Anunnaki's computational solution to the mismatch between their divine year and humanity's earthly year: "Devising the division of the ecliptic (the plane of planetary orbits around the Sun) into twelve — to conform to the twelve-member composition of the Solar System — the Anunnaki invented the twelve houses of the zodiac; that allotted to each zodiac house 30°, in consequence of which the retardation per house added up to 2,160 years (72 × 30 = 2,160) and the complete Precessional Cycle or 'Great Year' to 25,920 years (2,160 × 12 = 25,920)." By relating 2,160 to 3,600, Sitchin further argues, "the Anunnaki arrived at the Golden Ratio of 6:10 and, more importantly, at the sexagesimal system of mathematics which multiplied 6 by 10 by 6 by 10 and so on."

The origin of the zodiac itself is attributed unambiguously to the Anunnaki: "it was not Man who had first grouped the myriads of stars into recognizable constellations, defined and named those that spanned the ecliptic, and divided them into twelve to create the twelve houses of the zodiac. It was the Anunnaki who had conceived of that for their own needs; Man adopted that as his link, his means of ascent, to the heavens from the mortality of life on Earth."

The precessional phenomenon — Celestial Time — is then described as literally flowing in reverse from earthly time, matching the direction of Nibiru's own orbit: "Celestial Time, as we have designated it, does not progress in the direction of Earthly (and all Planetary) Time, counterclockwise; rather, it moves in the opposite direction, matching the orbital (clockwise) direction of Nibiru." This makes the zodiacal Ages a kind of divine clock hand sweeping backwards: "Celestial Time does flow backward, as far as we on Earth are concerned; and therefore, in zodiacal terms, the Past is the Future."

The calendar of Nippur is the terrestrial embodiment of this system. Sitchin connects the famous mathematical and astronomical achievement of Sumerian civilization to the Anunnaki origin of the zodiac: "By a miracle that I have found no one to interpret,' the mythologist Joseph Campbell wrote … 'the arithmetic that was developed in Sumer as early as c. 3200 B.C. … so matched the celestial order as to amount in itself to a revelation.' The 'miracle,' as we have since shown, was provided by the advanced knowledge of the Anunnaki."

In Chapter 4, Sitchin links this directly to the Nippur Mission Control Center and the calendar granted at the time of Anu's state visit to Earth circa 3800 BC: "The Babylonian New Year rituals evolved from the Sumerian AKITI … festival whose roots can be traced to the state visit by Anu and his spouse Antu to Earth circa 3800 B.C., when (as the texts attest) the zodiac was ruled by the Bull of Heaven, the Age of Taurus. We have suggested that it was then that Counted Time, the calendar of Nippur, was granted to Mankind."

Chapter 7 confirms that the Sumerian zodiac was the inspiration for stone circles used as calendars: "Where did the idea of a circle as a shape appropriate to astronomical observations … come from? In our view, it came from the zodiac, the cycle of twelve constellations grouped around the Sun in the orbital plane (the Ecliptic) of the planets." Sitchin traces Sumerian knowledge of the twelve zodiacal houses back to at least 6,000 years ago: "The Sumerians were cognizant of the zodiacal ages — not only the names and images but the precessional cycle thereof — when the calendar was begun in Nippur, circa 3800 B.C., in the Age of Taurus."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the calendrical foundation for the entire book. Chapter 1 establishes the three tiers of time — Earthly Time (days, months, years), Divine Time (shars of 3,600 Earth-years), and Celestial Time (the precessional Great Year of 25,920 years) — with the Anunnaki as the originators of all three systems and the zodiac as the bridge between Divine and Earthly time. The argument that Stonehenge and the "Stonehenge on the Euphrates" were designed to track zodiacal Ages and the equinoxes flows directly from this premise (see [[Sitchin: megalithic observatories such as Stonehenge were built to Anunnaki astronomical design]]). The notion that "the Past is the Future" in precessional terms sets up the book's framing question: what can the coming Age of Aquarius teach us from the prior Age transitions already recorded in history?

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that the discovery of precession has conventionally been attributed to the Greek astronomer Hipparchus (second century BC), and that Western scholarship credits the development of the zodiac to Mesopotamian astronomers of the first millennium BC rather than to alien visitors. He concedes that the numerical correspondences he draws (e.g., 2,160 : 3,600 = 6 : 10) are his own synthesis and that "no one can really say for sure" about some of the astronomical details he invokes. Mainstream scholarship holds that the Sumerian sexagesimal system and the zodiac were human intellectual achievements arising from centuries of practical astronomical observation, not extraterrestrial downloads; and that precessional awareness in Sumer, while genuinely ancient, does not require a non-human origin.
