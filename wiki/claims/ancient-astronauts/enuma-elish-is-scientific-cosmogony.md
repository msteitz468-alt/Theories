## Claim Statement

The Babylonian *Enuma Elish* ("When in the heights"), conventionally read as a creation myth of warring deities, is in fact a **sophisticated and accurate cosmogony**: each "god" is a planet, and the epic encodes the literal formation of the solar system and of Earth.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sitchin: the text "is a true cosmogony, dealing with prior events and taking us to the beginning of time." He maps the cast of "gods" onto celestial bodies — Apsu (the Sun), Mummu (Mercury), Tiamat (the watery planet), and the young gods (the other planets) whose "great noise and commotion" he reads as orbital/gravitational disturbance. The "battle" is a planetary encounter: "their combined gravitational pull has now determined Marduk's orbital path"; the "net" is gravitation; the "winds" are satellites; the "arrow" is an electrical discharge. After Tiamat is split, the fifth tablet's description of gushing lava ("Tiamat's spittle"), the gathering of "cloud waters," the raising of continents and the release of "the Tigris and Euphrates" from her eyes are, Sitchin says, "exactly the same scientific information" as modern geology — and parallel to Genesis 1.

He notes the Akkadian text (the "Seven Tablets of Creation," *Enuma Elish*) was pieced together by George Smith (1876) and shown to predate and parallel Genesis.

## Contexts Within the Source

The epic is treated as the Nefilim's own account of cosmic history, transmitted to humans; the Babylonian elevation of Marduk as victor is read as a political overlay on an older Sumerian version.
