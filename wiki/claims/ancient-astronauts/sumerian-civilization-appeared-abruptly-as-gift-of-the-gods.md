## Claim Statement

The high civilization of Sumer appeared **suddenly, with no developmental precursor**, around 3800 B.C., displaying a sophistication (mathematics, writing, astronomy, law, medicine, metallurgy) that ordinary evolution cannot account for. Sitchin argues this knowledge was **handed to mankind by the gods** (the Nefilim), as the Sumerians themselves claimed when they said kingship and the arts of civilization were "lowered from Heaven."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sitchin opens with the "puzzle" of man's own sudden appearance — *Homo sapiens* arriving "suddenly and inexplicably ... as if from nowhere," with culture that *regressed* (Shanidar cave) and then leaped: "circa 11,000 B.C., 'thinking Man' reappeared with new vigor and on an inexplicably higher cultural level. It was as if an unseen coach ... dispatched to the field a fresh and better-trained team." He extends this to agriculture (the Near East as "some kind of genetic-botanical laboratory, guided by an unseen hand") and to Sumer itself: "How was it ... that the civilization of Sumer seemed to flower so suddenly nearly 6,000 years ago without a precursor?"

Ch. 2 catalogues Sumerian "firsts" — sexagesimal mathematics able to "calculate roots or raise numbers to powers," writing, the wheel, kilns, medicine and pharmacology, schools, law codes, banking and the silver shekel, and deep-water seafaring — to argue the suddenness and completeness imply a transmitted, not self-developed, origin.

## Contexts Within the Source

This "puzzle" is the rhetorical engine of the book: every anomaly (man, plants, civilization) is offered as evidence requiring the Nefilim as explanation.
