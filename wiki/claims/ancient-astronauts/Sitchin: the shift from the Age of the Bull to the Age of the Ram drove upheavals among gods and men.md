## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues that the precessional transition from the zodiacal Age of the Bull (Taurus) to the Age of the Ram (Aries), which he dates roughly between 2300 and 2000 BC, was not merely an astronomical event but the direct cause of a centuries-long series of wars, exiles, nuclear destruction, and religious upheavals among the Anunnaki "gods" and their human followers. The Enlilite gods — identified with the Bull and Lion — resisted ceding cosmic authority to [[Marduk (Sitchin)]] (alias Ra in Egypt), whose symbol was the Ram and who claimed the New Age as proof of his divine right to supremacy. The transition ultimately ended in the nuclear obliteration of Sumer's cities and spaceport, [[Marduk (Sitchin)]]'s triumph, and the replacement of the Sumerian civilizational order with a Babylonian one organized around the Age of the Ram.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sitchin states the thesis directly: "what was happening on Earth had been related to the heavens — to the constellations of the zodiac . . . the issue was the coming of a New Age: the impending change from the zodiac house of the bull (Taurus) to the zodiac house of the ram (Aries) as the one in which the spring equinox, and thus the calendrical moment for the New Year, would occur" (line 2932).

The contest is dramatized through the Erra Epos, in which Nergal tells Ninurta that regardless of the Enlilites' power, the Ram's coming cannot be stopped: "In that grove, / even the supermost time measurer, / the bearer of the standards, / the course cannot change. / One can blow like a wind, / roar like a storm, [yet] / on the rim of the Sun's orbit, / no matter what the struggle, / see that Ram" (lines 2952–2976). Ninurta, asserting his claim, had declared himself defined by the previous zodiacal era: "In Heaven I am a wild bull, / On Earth I am a lion" (lines 2936–2938) — which Sitchin reads as a direct invocation of Taurus and Leo as the Enlilite celestial emblems (line 2948).

[[Marduk (Sitchin)]]'s two exiles are framed as disputes over the timing of the new Age. Marduk left Babylon circa 2295 BC after Nergal persuaded him "the Time has not yet come" (line 2982). During his wandering, Marduk watched for the determining omen: "By an omen to Hatti-land I went. / In Hatti-land I asked for an oracle / [about] my throne and my Lordship. / In its midst [I asked]: 'Until when?' / 24 years in its midst I nested" (lines 2244–2252). Sitchin also identifies a key astronomical signal: a conjunction of Jupiter with the Sun in Aries, calculated by the antiquarian John Landseer to have occurred in 2142 BC, which would have announced the beginning of the new Age (line 3256).

In Egypt, the same transition is mapped onto the visual arts. Sitchin quotes coffin lid depictions from near Thebes in which "the four-headed Ram dominates the four corners of the heavens . . . the Bull of Heaven is shown pierced with a spear or lance; and the twelve zodiacal constellations . . . are arranged so that the constellation of Aries is precisely in the east, i.e., where the Sun appears on the Day of the Equinox" (line 3268). In official Memphite iconography the Sacred Bull Apis had been the supreme celestial symbol; once the Theban priests of Ra-Amen prevailed after 2040 BC, "the Bull of Heaven was depicted pierced and deflated. The Ram began to dominate celestial and monumental art and Ra was given the epithet 'Ram of the Four Winds'" (line 3064).

The conclusion is apocalyptic: "When the Age of the Ram finally arrived, it did not come as the dawn of a New Age. Rather, it was accompanied by darkness at noon — the darkness of a cloud of deadly radiation from the first-ever explosion of nuclear weapons on Earth. It came as the culmination of more than two centuries of upheavals and warfare that pitted god against god and nation against nation; and in its aftermath, the great Sumerian civilization that had lasted for nearly two millennia lay prostrate and desolate" (line 3034).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the culmination of [[Sitchin: history is governed by three "times" — Celestial, Divine, and Earthly]]: it is a concrete historical instance of Celestial Time overriding earthly political arrangements and forcing a new world order. The same logic Sitchin applies in the introduction — that the coming of the Age of Aquarius in our own era should be read against this precedent — frames the entire book. The weapon used to enforce the New Age, Sitchin argues, was nuclear: the obliteration of Sodom and Gomorrah and the Sinai spaceport circa 2024 BC, after which an "Evil Wind" (radioactive cloud) killed Sumerian cities whose lamentation texts Sitchin quotes extensively (lines 3352–3410).

The role of the calendar in legitimizing power is shown through Nabu's missionary campaign: "Nabu was crisscrossing the lands . . . seeking adherents to his father's side. His name . . . had the same meaning and came from the same verb by which the Bible called a true prophet: Nabi, one who receives the divine words and signs . . . The divine signs of which Nabu spoke were the changing Heavens; the fact that the New Year and other worship dates no longer seemed to occur when they should have. Nabu's weapon, in behalf of [[Marduk (Sitchin)]], was the calendar" (line 3084).

After [[Marduk (Sitchin)]]'s triumph, the Babylonian rewrite of the Epic of Creation assigned him authority over all three modes of time: he "determined Divine Time by his orbit, Celestial Time by defining the constellations, and Earthly Time by giving Earth its orbital position and tilt" (line 3550). The Esagil ziggurat in Babylon was oriented so that "its head pointed directly to the star Iku, the lead star of the constellation Aries" (line 3330), making architecture itself an assertion of the New Age.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin concedes that the timing of the transition was genuinely ambiguous and was disputed even among the Anunnaki: "even nowadays, no one can say for sure when one 'Age' has ended and the other begun . . . the zodiacal constellations are of varied sizes, and the Sun can linger longer or shorter periods within them. The problem is especially acute in the case of Aries, that occupies less than 30° of the celestial arc" (line 3086). This indeterminacy, he argues, is precisely why the dispute became so violent. Mainstream scholarship regards the Taurus-to-Aries transition as an astronomically real but historically inert phenomenon; the identification of Marduk or Ninurta with zodiacal ages, and the interpretation of the Erra Epos as a record of nuclear war, are rejected by Assyriologists and historians of ancient religion.
