## Claim Statement

[[Zecharia Sitchin]] argues in [[The Stairway to Heaven (Sitchin)]] that the only inscription inside the Great Pyramid bearing the name "Khufu" — red-painted hieroglyphic markings in the relieving chambers above the King's Chamber, discovered by Colonel Richard Howard Vyse in 1837 — is a modern forgery perpetrated by Vyse and his aide J. R. Hill. If those markings are removed from consideration, Sitchin contends, there is no credible evidence that Khufu or any other pharaoh built the Great Pyramid. He further argues that the "Inventory Stela," a limestone tablet found near Giza, records Khufu's own words describing the Great Pyramid and the Sphinx as already standing when he arrived — which means both monuments predate Khufu and, by extension, predate all known pharaohs. Sitchin concludes that the Sphinx too was built by the gods (the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]]) and not by any human ruler.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

### The Inventory Stela and the pre-existence of the Sphinx

Sitchin introduces the "Inventory Stela" discovered by Auguste Mariette in the 1850s in the ruins of the temple of Isis near the Great Pyramid. He describes its content:

> "According to the inscription on this stela (which is in the Cairo Museum), the Great Pyramid was already standing when Khufu arrived on the scene. Its mistress was the goddess Isis — it belonged to this goddess, and not to Khufu. Furthermore, the Sphinx too — which has been attributed to Chefra, who presumably built it together with the Second Pyramid — was also already crouching at its present location. The continuation of the inscription pinpoints the position of the Sphinx accurately, and records the fact that part of it was damaged by lightning." (lines 9893–9896)

He quotes Khufu's stela text directly:

> "For his mother Isis, the Divine Mother, / Mistress of 'The Western Mountain of Hathor,' / he made (this) writing on a stela. / He gave (her) a new sacred offering. / He built (her) a House (temple) of stone, / renewed the gods that were found in her temple." (lines 9927–9957)

And the passage he reads as confirming the Sphinx's pre-existence:

> "In the Inventory Stela, Khufu called the Sphinx 'Guardian of the Aeter, who guides the Winds with his gaze.' It was, as he clearly wrote, the image of a god: / 'This figure of the god / will exist to eternity; / Always having its face / watching towards the east.'" (lines 11225–11243)

Sitchin notes that Khufu's own claim in the Stela is only that he built a small pyramid for Princess Henutsen beside the temple of Isis — not the Great Pyramid itself:

> "The only pyramid-building claim made by Khufu is that he built the small pyramid for the princess. The Great Pyramid, he states, was already there, as was the Sphinx (and, by inference, the other two pyramids as well)." (lines 9903–9904)

### The Vyse forgery argument

Sitchin gives extended narrative to the circumstances of Vyse's operations at Giza in 1837, concluding that Vyse and Hill used red ochre paint (which Perring himself noted was "difficult to distinguish the mark of yesterday from one of three thousand years") to inscribe cartouches inside sealed relieving chambers only after blasting into them. The key evidentiary points Sitchin marshals are:

**Wrong script period.** The British Museum's hieroglyphics expert Samuel Birch noted that the inscriptions were "written in semi-hieratic or linear-hieroglyphic characters" from periods centuries later than Khufu's reign. Sitchin quotes Birch: "The only similarity that he could find to this row of symbols was that of 'a title that appears on the coffin of the queen of Amasis' of the Saitic period" — more than 2,000 years after Khufu.

**Wrong walls.** Sitchin observes that no inscriptions were found on the eastern walls of the chambers — precisely the walls Vyse had blasted through — whereas inscriptions covered the intact north, south and west walls. He asks: "cannot all the puzzles be solved, if we assume that the inscriptions were not made in antiquity, when the pyramid was being built, but only after Vyse had blasted his way into the compartments?" (lines 10209–10210)

**Two irreconcilable royal names in impossible stratigraphic sequence.** The cartouche now read as Khufu appeared only in the topmost (Campbell's) Chamber, while the cartouche of Khnem-Khufu appeared in the lower Wellington's and Lady Arbuthnot's chambers. Since pyramids are built from the base upward, the lower chambers must have been sealed before the upper ones — meaning the supposedly earlier Pharaoh Khufu would have inscribed the uppermost chamber while his alleged successor Khnem-Khufu sealed the lower ones, which is chronologically impossible.

**The forger's clinching blunder.** Sitchin identifies the decisive error: the hieroglyph for "Kh" (a sieve symbol) was consistently written instead as the Solar Disk, the symbol for the god Ra. He writes:

> "He had unwittingly spelled out not Khnem-Khuf, but KhnemRauf; not Khufu, but Raufu. He had used the name of the great god incorrectly and in vain; it was blasphemy in ancient Egypt. / . . . Only a stranger to hieroglyphics, a stranger to Khufu, and a stranger to the overpowering worship of Ra, could have committed such a grave error." (lines 10323–10331)

He traces this error back to Wilkinson's 1828 reference book Materia Hieroglyphica and de Laborde's 1832 Voyage de l'Arabie Pétrée, both of which Vyse's party demonstrably used and which contained identical rendering errors.

**Suspicious timeline.** Sitchin points out a date manipulation in Vyse's published chronicles: Vyse records finding a stone fragment with a partial Khufu cartouche on June 2, but his journal entry describing it is dated May 9 — eighteen days before the crucial cartouche was supposedly first found inside Campbell's Chamber on May 27. This suggests Vyse already knew by May 9 what the cartouche had to look like, implying it was planned.

### The Sphinx's divine authorship

Sitchin also cites ancient inscriptions calling the Sphinx the work of the gods themselves, quoting a Roman inscription:

> "'Thy formidable form is the work of the Immortal Gods.'" And a Greek poem: 'Thy formidable form, / Here the Immortal Gods have shaped . . . / As a neighbor to the Pyramids they placed thee . . . / Sacred Guide in the Land of Egypt.'" (lines 11193–11219)

He notes that E. A. Wallis Budge concluded as early as 1904 that the Sphinx "was in existence in the days of Kha-f-ra, or Khephren; and it is probable that it is a very great deal older than his reign."

Sitchin's reading of the Sphinx's function connects to his spaceport thesis:

> "The questions have been many, the answers very few. But one thing is certain: it gazes precisely eastward, along the Thirtieth Parallel." (lines 11127–11127)

## Contexts Within the Source

These two arguments — the Vyse forgery and the Inventory Stela — work together to eliminate human-pharaonic authorship of the Giza complex, clearing the way for Sitchin's attribution of the pyramids to the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]]. The Sphinx's eastward gaze along the Thirtieth Parallel directly into the Sinai peninsula links it to the Landing Grid and Spaceport thesis developed in Chapter XIV (see [[Sitchin: the Giza pyramids were Anunnaki navigational beacons, not pharaonic tombs]]). Sitchin also presents the Sphinx as a communications node: an Eighteenth Dynasty hymn describes the god entering "the two caverns which are under his [the Sphinx's] feet" and then transmitting messages heard in Heliopolis and Memphis. The connection to [[Sitchin: the Anunnaki's Landing Place at Baalbek and the Sinai spaceport (Tilmun) served their spacecraft]] is explicit: Baalbek, the Giza beacons, and the Sinai Spaceport form a unified post-Diluvial guidance system conceived and built by the Anunnaki.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin himself acknowledges that his accusations against Vyse are circumstantial — he states "How sure can we be of our accusations, a century and a half after the event? / Sure enough." The mainstream Egyptological position accepts the Vyse chambers' inscriptions as genuine quarry marks from Khufu's construction project, and attributes the orthographic anomalies to the informal nature of working marks rather than formal inscriptions. The Inventory Stela is widely treated by Egyptologists as a Late Period document composed by priests invoking Khufu's name to bolster local temple claims, not as a contemporaneous record of Khufu's own words. The attribution of the Sphinx to Chefra is supported by the similarity of the face to known Chefra statues and by its geological proximity to the Second Pyramid causeway. Sitchin's identification of the Sphinx as a pre-pharaonic divine monument built by extraterrestrials has no support in mainstream scholarship.
