## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues that interbreeding between the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] (Anunnaki astronauts from [[The Twelfth Planet (Nibiru)]]) and human women produced two categories of extraordinary beings: (1) demigods, individuals who were partly divine and partly human, inheriting enhanced intelligence, physical stature, and extraordinary longevity from their Anunnaki parentage; and (2) the antediluvian and immediate post-Diluvial biblical patriarchs, who are identified as demigods or demigod-lineage humans whose unnaturally long lifespans — Adam 930 years, Methuselah 969 years, Noah 950 years — are explained by their partial Anunnaki genome. The most famous demigod example in this book is the Epic of Gilgamesh hero, described in the ancient tablets as "two-thirds divine," whose quest for immortality Sitchin treats as a historical demigod's search for the Anunnaki's biological secret of longevity.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The interbreeding event is anchored in Genesis 6, which Sitchin quotes directly: "When The Adam began to multiply on the face of the Earth and daughters were born unto them, that the sons of the Elohim saw the daughters of men that they were suitable, and they took them as wives of all which they chose" (lines 3783–3797). The offspring are identified as the Nefilim: "The Nefilim were upon the Earth in those days and also after that, when the sons of the Elohim came unto the daughters of The Adam, and they bore children to them" (lines 3813–3821).

Sitchin identifies the Anunnaki leader who first broke the interspecies taboo: "He was angered by the fact that it was none other than Enki who was first to break the taboo by having sex with female Earthlings; and he was especially infuriated by the fact that Enki's son Marduk went ahead and actually took as a wife one such Earthling — setting (in Enlil's opinion) a perverted example to the rank and file Anunnaki" (lines 3805–3806). Enlil regarded the intermarriage as "an absolute taboo" between "two different planetary species" and it was this "Wickedness" that caused him to desire to destroy humanity in the Flood (lines 3804–3827).

On the demigod status of Gilgamesh: Sitchin cites the Epic of Gilgamesh directly, noting that at the time of George Smith's 19th-century decipherment, scholars found Gilgamesh "identified in the very opening lines on Tablet I as king of Uruk" and described as "being 'two-thirds of him divine,' his adventures were in search of immortality" (lines 1187–1188). This two-thirds divine, one-third human ratio is Sitchin's model for the demigod: the result of one Anunnaki parent and one human parent, with the divine fraction varying by generational distance.

For the patriarchs, Sitchin presents the list of ten pre-Diluvial biblical patriarchs (Adam through Noah) and argues they were demigods whose long lifespans are explained by partial Anunnaki DNA. He poses the question explicitly: "If they were the Shar-span rulers of the Sumerian King List, why the repeated biblical statement that each one of them died? Or were they perhaps a combination of the two: partly mortal men, partly gods — in other words, Demigods — with all the genetic consequences thereof? Could the biblical Patriarchs themselves, including Noah, have been the very 'Men of Renown' of Genesis chapter 6 who were fathered by the Nefilim who had mated with the 'Daughters of Man'?" (lines 4439–4441).

The demigod status of Noah is treated at length via the Dead Sea Scroll fragment "Book of Noah," in which Lamech's wife Bath-Enosh gives birth to a baby so unusual "his eyes shone, and he could speak" and right away "Lamech 'thought in his heart that the conception was from one of the Watchers'" (lines 4601–4602). Lamech's lament — "I have begotten a strange son, different from and unlike Man, and resembling the sons of God of Heaven. His nature is different, and he is not like us. And it seems to me that he is not sprung from me, but from the angels" (lines 4607–4617) — is presented as ancient corroboration that Noah was a demigod. Sitchin reads the biblical verse "Noah was a righteous man, Perfect in his genealogy he was; With the Elohim did Noah walk" as potentially meaning Noah was genetically "perfect" (unmixed), yet possibly a demigod from an Anunnaki liaison (lines 4635–4641).

Enoch (the seventh patriarch) is another central case: "Enoch walked with the Elohim and was no more, for Elohim had taken him" (Genesis 5:23–24, quoted lines 4513–4525). The extra-biblical Book of Enoch, summarized extensively in Chapter X, describes two very tall, shining divine emissaries — "two men, exceedingly big" (line 4539) — taking Enoch on a celestial journey. Sitchin draws the parallel to the Sumerian Enmeduranki, who was also taken to the divine Assembly to receive secret knowledge, and argues that "if the eight who reigned after Alulim and Alalgar were not full-fledged gods, they must be considered to have been demigods" (line 4759), with Enmeduranki's 21,600-year reign as evidence of superhuman but not fully divine longevity.

The Book of Jubilees and Book of Enoch are cited to show that two hundred "Watchers" (equated with Sitchin's Igigi) organized in groups of ten, swore an oath, and each chose a human wife: "each one of them chose for himself one, and they began to go in unto them and defile themselves with them ... And the women bore giants" (lines 4493–4494). The leader who "led astray the sons of God and brought them down to Earth and led them astray through the Daughters of Man" is identified by these texts as the angel Azazel — paralleled by Sitchin to the Anunnaki [[Marduk (Sitchin)]], the first to formally marry a human woman.

The Adapa / Enki demigod lineage is explored in Chapter X: the pre-Diluvial demigod Adapa, "Man of Eridu," was clearly identified as "a son of Enki" (line 4927), granted intelligence and wide understanding but deliberately denied everlasting life by his own father (lines 4953–4960). Sitchin reads this episode as the historical foundation for the Garden of Eden's Tree of Life episode: "Wide understanding he perfected for him; Wisdom he had given him; To him he had given Knowledge — Everlasting life he had not given him" (lines 4953–4959).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is inseparable from [[Sitchin: the Anunnaki were flesh-and-blood giants — the biblical Nephilim]], since demigods are the direct product of the Anunnaki physical presence on Earth. The interbreeding motif also sets up the Flood narrative: Enlil's anger at the god-human intermarriage is the proximate cause of his decision to use the approaching natural deluge to "wipe The Adam ... off the face of the Earth" (line 3803). The patriarchal long-life data — which Sitchin systematically tabulates against the Sumerian King List reigns — is treated as documentary evidence that these figures were closer to the Anunnaki genome than ordinary humans.

The demigod theme extends beyond the biblical world: Alexander the Great's conviction that he was a demigod fathered by the god Amon/Marduk opens Chapter I (lines 263–267, 311–313), the Egyptian Pharaoh Hatshepsut's claim that Amon disguised as her father fathered her (lines 4783–4818), and Ramses II's claim that Ptah/Enki was his actual father (lines 4883–4899). All are placed by Sitchin in the same category: genuine, if unprovable, memories of the era when Anunnaki-human unions produced a class of partly divine rulers.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that the evidence is indirect and textual, not biological. The Book of Noah's reassurance to Lamech — transmitted through Enoch and Metushelah — actually concludes that Noah was Lamech's legitimate son, not a divine child: "you can reassure Lamech 'that he who has been born is in truth his son'" (line 4625). Sitchin reads this as ambiguous rather than definitive, noting that Noah's unusual features "are due to his having been chosen by God for a special destiny." The mainstream scholarly and scientific community considers the Anunnaki demigod narrative to be mythology and rejects any genetic-literal interpretation of biblical patriarchal longevity. No biological or archaeological evidence of hybrid Anunnaki-human beings has been confirmed.
