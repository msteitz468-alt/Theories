## Claim Statement

[[Zecharia Sitchin]] argues that around 2024 BC the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] Anunnaki gods Ninurta and Nergal deployed "awesome weapons" — which Sitchin explicitly equates with nuclear devices — to obliterate two distinct targets: (1) the Anunnaki spaceport located in the central plain of the Sinai peninsula, and (2) the "cities of the plain" (Sodom, Gomorrah, and three allied Canaanite cities) in the Jordan valley. The blast created a radioactive cloud — the "Evil Wind" described in Sumerian Lamentation Texts — that drifted northeast and within hours or days depopulated all of southern Sumer, effectively ending Sumerian civilization. Sitchin reads this event simultaneously in the Sumerian Erra Epic and Khedorlaomer Texts and in the biblical account of Abraham, Lot, and the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah in Genesis 18–19, arguing that both literatures draw on a common, earlier Sumerian chronicle of the same catastrophe.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The Decision to Use Nuclear Weapons**

Sitchin reconstructs the council of war that preceded the attack from the Erra Epic. With [[Marduk (Sitchin)]] returned to Babylon and his son Nabu rallying Canaanite cities to the Mardukite cause, Nergal argued for destroying the Spaceport to deny it to Marduk permanently. Ninurta initially resisted, employing language Sitchin notes is almost identical to Abraham's bargaining in Genesis 18: "Valiant Erra, / Will you the righteous destroy with the unrighteous? / Will you destroy those who have against you sinned / together with those who against you have not sinned?" (lines ~13111–13129). Nergal's modified plan, which gained Enlil's and ultimately Anu's approval, was selective: destroy the Spaceport and the cities sheltering Nabu, while warning the Anunnaki and Igigi manning the facilities to evacuate: "To the place of the gods, access he shall not have: / The place from where the Great Ones ascend / I shall upheaval" (lines ~13157–13169).

**The Obliteration of the Spaceport**

The Erra Epic describes Ninurta (called Ishum, "The Scorcher") striking first: "Ishum to Mount Most Supreme set his course; / The Awesome Seven, [weapons] without parallel, / trailed behind him. / At the Mount Most Supreme the hero arrived; / He raised his hand— / the mount was smashed; / The plain by the Mount Most Supreme / he then obliterated; / in its forests not a tree-stem was left standing" (lines ~13207–13255). Sitchin comments: "with one nuclear blow, the Spaceport was obliterated, the mount within which its controls were hidden smashed, the plain that served its runways obliterated" (line ~13261). The Khedorlaomer Texts add: "That which was raised towards Anu to launch / they caused to wither; / Its face they made fade away, / its place they made desolate" (lines ~13515–13533). Sitchin points to a vast scar visible in NASA satellite photographs of the Sinai, and to the anomalous blackened bits of rock strewn across the central Sinai plain, as physical traces of the event (lines ~13543–13575).

**The Destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah**

Nergal (Erra) followed immediately: "Erra the King's Highway followed. / The cities he finished off, / to desolation he overturned them" (lines ~13274–13287). The Erra Epic verses are, Sitchin demonstrates, nearly word-for-word parallels to Genesis 19:24–25: "And the Lord rained upon Sodom and Gomorrah, from the skies, brimstone and fire that had come from Yahweh. / And He upheavaled those cities and the whole plain" (lines ~12517–12541). The text also describes the breach of the Dead Sea's southern shore: "He dug through the sea, / its wholeness he divided. / That which lives in it, even the crocodiles / he made wither" (lines ~13309–13333). Sitchin dates the event precisely: "He was witnessing a 'Hiroshima' and a 'Nagasaki' — the destruction of a fertile and populated plain by atomic weapons. The year was 2024 B.C." (line ~12589).

**The Biblical-Sumerian Connection: Abraham and Lot**

In Chapter 13, Sitchin synchronizes Sumerian and biblical chronologies to place Abraham in the thick of these events. Working backward from Solomon's Temple dedication and the known length of Israelite sojourn in Egypt, he calculates Abraham's birth in 2123 BC (line ~11699). Abraham's departure from Harran at age 75 — in 2048 BC — coincides exactly with Shulgi's death, which Sitchin calls "the very year of Shulgi's downfall!" (line ~11783). Abraham's mission, in Sitchin's reconstruction, was military: to guard the Spaceport in the Sinai and the Mission Control Center at Jerusalem against the advancing eastern alliance. Sitchin identifies the Babylonian cuneiform year-formula for Amar-Sin's seventh year — MU NE IB.RU.UM BA.HUL — as "Year [in which] the Shepherding-abode of IB.RU.UM was attacked," concluding: "Can this reference, in the exact crucial year, be other than to Abraham and his shepherding abode?" (lines ~12279–12289).

Lot's wife turning to "a pillar of vapor" (Sitchin revises the traditional "pillar of salt" via a Sumerian linguistic argument: the Sumerian NIMUR meant both salt and vapor) is read as radiation death: the Erra Epic predicts that "The people I will make vanish, / their souls shall turn to vapor" (lines ~14325–14329).

**The Evil Wind and the End of Sumer**

The nuclear explosions produced, Sitchin argues, an unintended radioactive fallout that the Sumerian Lamentation Texts call the "Evil Wind": "A storm, the Evil Wind, went around in the skies. / And then the radioactive whirlwind began to spread and move westward with the prevailing winds blowing from the Mediterranean" (lines ~13597–13609). The Lamentation Texts describe a calamity "unknown to man, / One that had never been seen before, / one which could not be withstood" (lines ~13699–13717), with an invisible, unstoppable death that passed through walls "like a snake" and felled people indoors and outdoors alike: "Cough and phlegm weakened the chest, the mouth was filled with spittle and foam . . . dumbness and daze have come upon them" (line ~13723). "Its rivers flow with water that is bitter, / its cultivated fields grow weeds, / its steppes grow withering plants" (lines ~13669–13681). Springs near the Dead Sea, Sitchin notes, retain radioactivity "enough to induce sterility and allied afflictions" to this day, citing I. M. Blake in The Palestine Exploration Quarterly (line ~12597). Modern water-spring contamination around the Dead Sea and the abrupt abandonment of settlements in the area in the 21st century BC, confirmed by archaeologists W. F. Albright and P. Harland, are offered as corroborating physical evidence (lines ~12593–12597).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the climactic event of [[The Wars of Gods and Men (Sitchin)]] and the endpoint to which [[Sitchin: Marduk's bid for supremacy drove the conflicts of gods and men]] leads. The nuclear holocaust is presented as the final, catastrophic consequence of the multi-century Enlilite-Enkiite divine war. It also serves Sitchin's broader [[Sumerians (Sitchin account)]] thesis: the sudden and otherwise inexplicable simultaneous collapse of all Sumerian cities — rather than a progressive invasion — is adduced as evidence that the cause was atmospheric and radiological rather than military. Sitchin explicitly invokes Hiroshima and Nagasaki as analogues (line ~12589), arguing that only post-1945 readers can fully appreciate what the Lamentation Texts describe.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Even within Sitchin's narrative, the destruction of the Spaceport was strenuously resisted by [[Enki (Sitchin)]]: "Enki spoke out strongly against the idea, urging steps to stop Nergal, for the use of the weapons, he pointed out, 'the lands would make desolate, the people will make perish'" (line ~13099). Nergal's own stated intention — "None shall be spared" (line ~13055) — was modified only by Ninurta's insistent diplomacy, yet the Evil Wind that doomed Sumer was an unintended consequence even of the "modified" plan. Mainstream scholarship rejects the nuclear-weapons reading entirely: Assyriologists and biblical scholars interpret the Sumerian Lamentation Texts as responses to actual military conquest (Elamite and Amorite invasions) and the biblical narrative of Sodom and Gomorrah as either theological legend or the memory of a natural disaster such as a seismic or volcanic event. The satellite scar in the Sinai has not been identified by geologists or archaeologists as an explosion crater. The radioactivity of Dead Sea springs has conventional geological explanations. Sitchin's chronological synchronization of Sumerian kings with biblical patriarchs is disputed; most scholars place Hammurabi — and therefore the period in question — in different centuries, or regard the Genesis 14 narrative as non-historical.
