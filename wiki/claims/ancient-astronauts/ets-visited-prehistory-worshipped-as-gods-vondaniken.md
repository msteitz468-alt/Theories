## Claim Statement

Von Däniken's master thesis: "our forefathers received visits from the universe in the remote past." Extraterrestrials arrived in spacecraft, were remembered and worshipped by primitive humanity as "gods," and influenced early civilization and religion — so that the "gods" of myth and scripture were really visiting astronauts.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He writes that "the past teemed with unknown gods who visited the primeval earth in manned spaceships," and asks why religion's "primitive gods" used "ultra-modern weapons on primitive peoples." He proclaims: "I claim that our forefathers received visits from the universe in the remote past, even though I do not yet know who these extraterrestrial intelligences were or from which planet they came." Chapter 4 poses the program directly — "Was God an Astronaut?"

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Von Däniken admits he cannot identify the visitors or their origin and frames the thesis as a courageous hypothesis he aims "to try to provide proof of," challenging orthodox archaeology rather than claiming settled fact.

## Contexts Within the Source

The umbrella claim of the book and the founding statement of the ancient-astronaut genre. It is the popular precursor to Sitchin's Sumerian specifics ([[Man was created as a 'primitive worker' to relieve the mutinying Anunnaki of gold mining]]), Bramley's custodial ETs, and Wallis's paleocontact — and the direct foil to Graham Hancock's *human* [[A lost advanced human civilization seeded knowledge into later cultures (Hancock)|lost-civilization thesis]].
