## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues that all ancient quests for immortality — from the Spanish search for the Fountain of Youth to the Epic of Gilgamesh to the Egyptian pharaoh's elaborate "Journey to the Afterlife" — were not spiritual allegories but literal attempts to reach the physical abode of the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]], the flesh-and-blood astronauts from [[The Twelfth Planet (Nibiru)]]. The gods possessed longevity because they consumed a "Food of Life" and "Water of Life" produced on their home planet; mortals who reached the gods' abode could partake of the same sustenance and share their life spans. The "paradise," "Land of the Living," and "Imperishable Star" of ancient texts all designated the same real destination: the spaceport and abode where the Anunnaki resided and from which their rocketships ascended.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sitchin opens his book by tracing universal legends of the Fountain of Youth — Ponce de León, Alexander the Great's cook who drank of the Waters of Life, Moses and the fish that swam through a tunnel — and argues they all share a common root in actual pre-Diluvial memory. He frames the whole inquiry with the Genesis verse in which "the Lord God" expels Adam lest he also "partake of the Tree of Life, and eat, and live forever" (line 135).

The Sumerian evidence is central. Sitchin quotes the Epic of Gilgamesh (ca. 2900 BC, Uruk) at length. Gilgamesh, "two-thirds god, one-third human," laments: "In my city man dies; oppressed is my heart. / Man perishes; heavy is my heart . . . / Man, the tallest, cannot stretch to heaven" (lines 4547–4563). When Shamash counsels him to enjoy mortal pleasures, Gilgamesh refuses; he determines instead to reach "the Land of the Living" — "the abode of the forefathers who by the great gods with the Purifying Waters were anointed" (lines 5399–5417). His quest is explicitly for the Anunnaki's place: "Pressing on into the forest, / the secret abode of the Anunnaki / he opened up" (lines 5227–5231). Upon arriving at Mount Mashu, Gilgamesh tells the Rocketmen guarding the gate: "On account of Utnapishtim, my forefather, have I come — / He who the congregation of the gods had joined. / About Death and Life I wish to ask him" (lines 5907–5921). Sitchin interprets this as an undisguised physical journey to reach an ancestor who was made immortal by the gods by being relocated to "the Land of the Missiles," Tilmun.

The same thesis is applied to ancient Egypt. Sitchin reads the Pyramid Texts not as myth but as operational instructions for a simulated journey to an actual location. The incantations appeal to the gods to "take this king with you, that he may eat of that which ye eat, that he may drink of which ye drink, that he may live on that whereupon ye live" (lines 1463–1465). The departed pharaoh sought the "Plant of Life" in "the Field of Life, the birthplace of Ra in the heavens" — and the texts promise him: "Give thou sustenance to this King Pepi / From thy eternal sustenance; / Thy everlasting beverage" (lines 1467–1477). Sitchin quotes a pyramid text of Pepi I: "Ho, this Pepi! / All satisfying life is given to thee; / 'Eternity is thine,' says Ra . . . / Thou perishest not, thou passest not away / for ever and ever" (lines 3093–3115). In Sitchin's reading, "Ra" is the Anunnaki commander, and his "Imperishable Star" — the "Planet of Millions of Years" — is the home planet [[The Twelfth Planet (Nibiru)]].

The Adapa episode clinches the theological framework. The Sumerian text says that when Ea created the improved human Adapa: "With wide understanding / he had perfected him . . . / To him he had given Knowing; / Lasting Life him he did not give" (lines 3933–3947). Adapa was summoned to Anu's celestial court — travelling by spacecraft — and offered the Bread of Life and Water of Life; deceived by Ea's warning that they were poisonous, he refused them and returned to Earth as a mortal. Sitchin concludes: "From then on, Mankind's ultimate goal — everlasting life — was no longer his" (line 4049). Every subsequent quest, from Gilgamesh to Alexander to Ponce de León, re-enacts Adapa's failed attempt.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the interpretive spine running through the book's first eight chapters. It links to the companion claim [[Sitchin: the Anunnaki's Landing Place at Baalbek and the Sinai spaceport (Tilmun) served their spacecraft]], since the specific destinations sought by Gilgamesh (the Cedar Mountain Landing Place, then Tilmun) are the same physical installations. The theme also connects to the core thesis of [[The Twelfth Planet (Sitchin)]]: the Anunnaki came from Nibiru for gold, engineered humanity, and retained "Life" as their exclusive possession. All of these narratives are, for Sitchin, variations on the same primal event — the gods' monopoly on longevity and the human desire to breach it.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that mainstream scholars treat the Pyramid Texts, the Epic of Gilgamesh, and the legends of Alexander as mythology or religious allegory. He notes that scholars have termed certain Sumerian statements "cryptic and still enigmatic" (line 6475). The book offers no peer-reviewed archaeological confirmation that any mortal ever reached the Anunnaki's abode. The immortality quests in the text — Gilgamesh, Adapa, Etana — all end in failure, which Sitchin attributes to divine policy rather than to the non-existence of the destination. Mainstream Assyriology, Egyptology, and classical scholarship reject the literalist reading of these texts, treating them as cosmological poetry and funerary ritual, not as accounts of actual space travel.
