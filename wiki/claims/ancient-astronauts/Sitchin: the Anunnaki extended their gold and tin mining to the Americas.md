## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues in *The Lost Realms* that the New World was not an isolated human development but a later theater of the same Anunnaki enterprise that had already driven gold-mining operations in the Old World. The Spanish obsession with "El Dorado" and the extraordinary gold hoards of the Aztecs and Incas are, in his reading, the residue of a deep prehistory in which the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] — flesh-and-blood visitors from Nibiru — directed or inspired organized extraction of precious metals in the Americas. He traces this mining heritage from tin and gold artefacts in the Yucatan well to the cyclopean engineering of Cuzco and Tiahuanacu, arguing that neither the Incas nor the Aztecs possessed the technological capacity to account for what the Spaniards found; only predecessors acting under divine (Anunnaki) guidance could explain it.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The book opens by documenting the Spanish experience: every native group questioned about gold's origin pointed not to human industry but to the gods. Aztec informants told Cortes that gold was "teocuitlatl, literally meaning 'the gods' excretion,' their perspiration and their tears" (Chapter 1). The Aztecs attributed goldsmithing knowledge first to the Toltecs, and the Toltecs to Quetzalcoatl. Cortes's own written report is cited: gold was found "in the form of dust in small cane tubes or quilts, or was melted in small pots and cast in bars... returned to the gods to whom the gold had always belonged."

On Peru, Sitchin quotes the chroniclers Ribero and von Tschudi (*Peruvian Antiquities*): "the Indians had a knowledge of veins of this precious material which the conquerors and their descendants never succeeded in discovering," while modern mines are "located at sites of aboriginal operations" with "ancient shafts... frequently reported and also finds of primitive tools, even the bodies of entombed miners" — citing S. K. Lothrop, *Inca Treasure As Depicted by Spanish Historians* (Chapter 1).

Sitchin identifies the Olmecs — whom he characterizes as African in origin and surprisingly advanced — as the probable mining agents. He concludes: "the capabilities, the scenes, the tools appear to us to lead to one conclusion: the Olmecs were miners, come to the New World to extract some precious metals—probably gold, perhaps other rare minerals too" (Chapter 5). He links this to the legend of Votan, which "speak[s] of tunneling through mountains," and to the Nahuatl god Tepeyolloti, "Heart of the Mountain," depicted as a Bearded God of Caves "holding as his tool a flamethrower."

The tin artefacts recovered from Chichen Itza's Sacred Cenote are a key piece of evidence: "Most puzzling of all was the discovery of disks of pure tin, a metal that is not found in its native state and that can be obtained only by a complex refining of ores—ores that are altogether absent in Mesoamerica" (Chapter 5). This, for Sitchin, points to organised extraction and trade networks linking the Americas to the same metallurgical tradition the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] had established in the Old World.

At Teotihuacan (Chapter 3), Sitchin interprets the subterranean tunnel under the Pyramid of the Sun, the mica-lined chambers (mica from Brazil), the diverted San Juan river, and the sluice-fitted avenue sections as "all components of a scientifically conceived plant for the separation, refining or purification of mineral substances," adding: "Was this someone in search of gold, as the pendant of the Lady of Water would suggest, or of some other, even rarer mineral?" He ties this to the Anunnaki by noting that the Sumerians described the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] as having come to Earth expressly for gold: "The Anunnaki... had come to Earth long before the Deluge... in 432,000 years before the Deluge" (Chapter 8).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the foundation of the book's argument. Sitchin uses the Spanish conquest records to establish the paradox — enormous gold hoards, veneration of gold as divine property, absence of active mining — and then works backward through Toltec, Olmec, and pre-Olmec layers to reach a conclusion that only the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] can resolve the paradox. It connects directly to [[Sitchin: Mesoamerican gods were the same Anunnaki as the Old World (Quetzalcoatl = Thoth-Ningishzidda)]], since in Sitchin's schema Quetzalcoatl / Thoth / Ningishzidda was the specific Anunnaki deity who brought metallurgical and mining knowledge to the New World.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin himself acknowledges the mainstream position: scholars such as Alexander Del Mar (*A History of the Precious Metals*) concluded that "the Aztecs had no knowledge of iron, and therefore, that subterranean mining... is practically out of the question," dismissing reported prehistoric shafts as either Spanish workings or volcanic formations (Chapter 1). The consensus among modern Mesoamerican archaeologists is that Aztec and Inca gold came primarily from placer mining (collection of surface nuggets and river sediment), not shaft mining. The identification of the Olmecs as African immigrants, and the attribution of the Teotihuacan waterworks to mineral processing, are not accepted in mainstream scholarship. Sitchin's reading of the tin disks and mica sheets as evidence of Anunnaki metallurgy has no support in the archaeological literature.
