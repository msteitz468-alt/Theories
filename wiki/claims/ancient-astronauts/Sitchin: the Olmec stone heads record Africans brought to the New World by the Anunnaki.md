## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues that the sixteen colossal basalt heads left behind by the Olmec civilization are portrait sculptures of African people — specifically West Africans of the Mande linguistic group — who were brought to Mesoamerica by the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] deity Thoth (known as Quetzalcoatl in Mesoamerica) to serve as expert miners and metallurgists in the service of the Anunnaki's New World gold-extraction programme. He holds that these individuals were not casual castaways but deliberate transplants, arriving circa 2000 BC when instability in the Old World prompted the Anunnaki to develop new sources of gold and tin in the Americas. Their African origin is attested, in his reading, by the unmistakably negroid physiognomy of the heads and by surviving Olmec art throughout Mesoamerica.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The first scholarly notice of the heads already pointed toward Africa: J. M. Melgar y Serrano, describing the head at Tres Zapotes in 1869, called it "a magnificent sculpture that most amazingly represents an Ethiopian." When a second colossal head was unearthed at La Venta it "measured about eight feet in height, twenty-one in circumference, and weighed about twenty-four tons. It depicts without question a negroid African wearing a distinct helmet." By the time of Sitchin's writing, sixteen such heads had been found; he summarises: "That these were individuals, all of the same African negroid stock but with their own personalities and diverse headgear, can be readily seen from a portrait gallery of some of these heads."

Sitchin calls on Leo Wiener's linguistic work (Africa and the Discovery of America) as independent corroboration, accepting Wiener's finding that "the Olmec tongue belonged to the Mande group of languages that originated in West Africa, between the Niger and Congo river." He notes that Nigeria, precisely in that region, has bronze figurines "cast in the telltale Lost Wax process" carbon-dated to about 2100 BC — contemporaneous with early Olmec beginnings and with the metallurgical know-how the Anunnaki needed transplanted.

The divine director of the operation, in Sitchin's scheme, was Thoth/Ningishzidda — the Anunnaki deity displaced from Egypt by Marduk — who "undertook the task of bringing his expert followers over: to start a new life, a new civilization, and new mining operations." West Africa is singled out as the sourcing region because "it is there that gold and tin, and copper to alloy bronze with, have been abundant." The Olmecs' role was not ornamental: encounters carved on stone stelae "clearly depict the Olmecs as tall, heavily built with muscular bodies — 'giants' in stature, no doubt, in the eyes of the indigenous Indian population," and finds at Chavin de Huantar in the Andes show African-looking figures "shown holding some kind of tool," many associated with waterworks symbols consistent with placer gold-panning.

The transit of the Izapa Stela 5 carving reinforces the identification: "The one on the left is shown in association with a helmeted Olmec man, which reinforces our contention that the colossal stone heads and the Olmecs they portrayed were Africans." Sitchin's broader Anunnaki narrative ties the timing to a divine political crisis: "It was then, we believe, that the decision was made to grant the African followers of Thoth/Quetzalcoatl civilization in his Mesoamerican realm" — following the nuclear destruction of the Sinai spaceport circa 2024 BC and Marduk's seizure of Babylon.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the principal thread running through chapters 7–12 of [[The Lost Realms (Sitchin)]]. It anchors Sitchin's broader argument, also developed in [[Sitchin: Andean and Mesoamerican monuments bear Old-World Anunnaki signatures]], that Old World peoples — African Olmecs paired with bearded Mediterranean Semites — were deliberately transported to the New World to staff an Anunnaki mining and metallurgical operation. The Olmecs are positioned as the operational labour force under the divine authority of [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] acting through Thoth: "It was the knowledge imparted by this pantheon, the needs of the gods who had come to Earth, and the leadership of Thoth, that directed the African Olmecs and the bearded Near Easterners to the other side of the world."

Their eventual fate — mutilated bodies depicted at Monte Alban, grotesque stone heads at Chavin de Huantar used as wall trophies, and "depictions of mutilated negroid bodies... found in the whole area, and especially at Sechin" — is read as the violent end of a divinely sponsored civilisation once the Anunnaki departed.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that mainstream scholarship rejects any pre-Columbian African presence in the Americas as a matter of settled consensus. The academic studies he cites (Wiener 1920, von Wuthenau, van Sertima) are minority positions dismissed or contested by professional archaeologists. Even these authors explained the African presence through mundane mechanisms — Arab slave-trading ships, Kushite shipwrecks, or Phoenician voyages — not divine engineering. Sitchin himself admits these earlier theories misread the date: "now we know that Olmec beginnings go back to circa 2000 B.C.," which undermines explanations anchored to later maritime contact. No mainstream archaeologist accepts the Olmec heads as portraits of Africans; the standard interpretation is that they represent rulers of Mesoamerican origin whose facial features reflect local physiognomy.
