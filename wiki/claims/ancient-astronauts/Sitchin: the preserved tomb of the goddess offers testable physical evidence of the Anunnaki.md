## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues that the "Royal Tombs of Ur," excavated by Sir Leonard Woolley beginning in 1922, are not ordinary royal burials but the actual physical interments of Anunnaki gods and demigods. The tomb designated PG-800 — whose occupant bears a cylinder seal inscribed "Nin.Puabi" (a divine title, not a queen's name) — is identified by Sitchin as the burial of a real Nibiruan goddess: a granddaughter of the goddess Ninsun and great-granddaughter of the goddess Inanna, who possessed Anunnaki mitochondrial DNA traceable back to Nibiru. The extraordinary opulence of the tomb (gold utensils embossed with Anu's rosette emblem, an oversize skull confirmed by physical anthropology, the burial of a king among her attendants) is presented as material proof that the Anunnaki were flesh-and-blood beings whose remains still exist.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sitchin devotes Chapters XV and XVI of the book to this argument, building from the archaeological record to his identification of "Nin.Puabi" as an Anunnaki goddess. Describing the abundance of gold in PG-800, he observes that "remarkably, the Anu and Antu list of eating and drinking vessels that had to be made of gold, and in one specific case (for milk) of alabaster stone, reads almost as an inventory of objects discovered in the 'royal' tombs of Ur" (lines 7221–7225). The golden utensils in PG-800 bear "the same emblem — a 'rosette' — with which the Anu-visit utensils were embossed," suggesting the buried female was "of the House of Anu — a direct linear descendant of Anu" (lines 7479–7480).

Physical anthropologist Sir Arthur Keith's 1934 report is quoted at length. Keith found that the skull of "Queen Shubad" (Puabi) had "a large and long head" with "a cranial capacity [that] could not have been less than 1600 cubic cm. — 250 c.cm. above the mean for European women," concluding she was "a member of a highly dolichocephalic race" unlike any known Sumerians (lines 7687–7699). Sitchin links this disproportionate cranium directly to Puabi's claimed Anunnaki heritage: "She had the head of a Gal and the body of a Banda" (line 7701), connecting her large skull to her grandmother Bau/Gula — literally "The Big One" — one of the female Anunnaki deities depicted in ancient art as oversized.

Sitchin reconstructs Puabi's genealogy across two converging dynastic lines leading from Anu through Enlil and Ninurta to the goddess Ninsun, then to the deified demigod Lugalbanda, establishing her as "a younger sister of Gilgamesh — both children of the unique couple: the deified demigod Lugalbanda and the mighty goddess Ninsun" (line 7857). Her title "Nin.Puabi" (lines 7627–7629) is explicitly treated not as a queenly honorific but as a divine designation: "It is important to clearly and emphatically put an end to the continued scholarly reference to Ninbanda as 'queen'. Nin, as in Ninharsag, Ninmah, Ninti, Ninki, Ninlil, Ningal, Ninsun, and so on, was always a divine prefix" (lines 7398–7399).

The burial of a named king (cylinder seal inscribed "Lugal Shu.pa.da," found among PG-800's attendants) among the retainers leads Sitchin to conclude that "whoever was buried in such a 'with pit' tomb with attendants who were themselves VIP's — even a king — had therefore to be more important than a mere royal princess or a king; it had to be at least a demigod — or even a fully qualified god or goddess" (lines 7452–7453).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the book's centerpiece archaeological argument. It links directly to [[Sitchin: the Anunnaki were flesh-and-blood giants — the biblical Nephilim]] — Sitchin's foundational contention that the gods had physical bodies and physical deaths — by showing that a goddess body reportedly still exists. It also extends the book's running theme of [[Sitchin: DNA testing of the gods' remains could prove mankind's alien origins]], since identifying the Ur tomb occupant as an Anunnaki goddess is the prerequisite for proposing DNA testing of her remains.

The goddess's elaborate burial with gold utensils matching the specifications for the supreme Anunnaki god [[Anu (Sitchin)]]'s state visit to Earth (c. 4000 BC) is presented as confirmation that the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] were a physically present civilization whose material culture survives archaeologically. The tomb's connection to the [[Sumer (Sitchin account)]] dynastic sequence — specifically the First Dynasty of Ur — situates the evidence within Sitchin's broader historical reconstruction in [[There Were Giants Upon the Earth (Sitchin)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that Woolley and all subsequent mainstream scholars assumed the sixteen extraordinary tombs belonged to mortal royalty, "because as he and everyone else knew, gods and goddesses existed only mythically and had no physical body to be buried" (line 7628). He concedes that the cylinder seal bearing the name "Nin.Puabi" could represent an otherwise unattested epithet or local nickname. His genealogical reconstruction is presented as a logical inference, not a documented fact: he prefaces much of it with phrases such as "our guess is," "I suggest," and "in this imagined scenario" (lines 7397, 7843, 7907). Mainstream archaeology and Assyriology uniformly identify PG-800 as the tomb of a high-status Sumerian queen and do not recognize Sitchin's identification of the occupant as a divine being of extraterrestrial origin.
