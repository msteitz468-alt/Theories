## Claim Statement

According to [[Zecharia Sitchin]] in [[The Stairway to Heaven (Sitchin)]], the three great pyramids of Giza were not built by Egyptian pharaohs as royal tombs but were constructed by the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] — the Anunnaki, flesh-and-blood astronauts from the planet Nibiru — as navigational beacons and guidance markers for their Spaceport in the Sinai peninsula. Sitchin argues that the pyramids formed part of a precise geometric "Landing Grid" or "Landing Corridor" anchored on Mount Ararat in the north and on the granite peaks of southern Sinai in the south, with the Great Pyramid positioned exactly on the line running from Ararat through Baalbek and onward. He further contends that the pharaohs merely appropriated the already-standing structures — attaching funerary temples and satellite buildings — and never built the pyramids themselves.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sitchin's navigational-beacon thesis is introduced when he describes how, after the Deluge destroyed the pre-Diluvial Spaceport in Mesopotamia, the Anunnaki needed a replacement infrastructure. He dramatises the process of surveying the new Landing Corridor:

> "He took his compass. With Baalbek as the focal point, he drew an arc through the highest peak of the Sinai peninsula. Where it intersected the Ararat-Baalbek line, he made a cross within a circle. Then he drew two lines of equal length, one connecting Baalbek with the peak in Sinai, the other with the site marked by the cross. / This, he said, will be our triangular Landing Corridor, to lead us straight to Baalbek. / But sir, one of those aboard said, there is nothing there, where you have made the cross — nothing that can serve as a guiding beacon! / We will have to erect there a pyramid, the commander said." (lines 9415–9427)

Sitchin immediately states that while he "merely dramatised" the conversation, certain facts are "astounding but undeniable":

> "The Great Pyramid of Giza, with its two companions and the unique Sphinx, is situated precisely on the extended Ararat-Baalbek line; and the distance from Baalbek to Mount St. Katherine and to the Great Pyramid of Giza is exactly the same." (lines 9459–9465)

In Chapter XIV he elaborates the guidance function of the pyramid's shape: by casing the stepped core in white limestone the builders achieved "a perfect play of light and shadow," and he cites Robert Ballard's 1882 observation that "one could determine his location and direction by the ever-changing alignment between the pyramids." Sitchin adds:

> "Even more important, however, was how the silhouettes and shadows of the pyramids appeared to an observer from the skies. As this aerial photograph shows (Fig. 155), the true shape of the pyramids casts arrow-like shadows, which serve as unmistakable direction pointers." (lines 10501–10503)

On the tomb theory he is emphatic:

> "The facts bespeak the opposite. The Old Kingdom pyramids never held a Pharaoh's body because they were never meant to hold a king's body. In the Pharaoh's simulated Journey to the Horizon, they were built as beacons to guide his ka to the Stairway to Heaven — just as the pyramids originally raised by gods had served as beacons for the gods when they 'sailed across the sky.'" (lines 9769–9770)

He argues that the pharaohs, observing the already-standing Giza monuments, tried to emulate them:

> "Pharaoh after Pharaoh, we suggest, attempted to emulate not the pyramid of Zoser, but the Pyramids of the Gods: the pyramids of Giza." (lines 9773–9773)

Regarding the pyramids' interior, Sitchin speculates that chambers within the Great Pyramid housed navigation and communications equipment:

> "Even nowadays, landing guidance equipment at airports emits electronic signals which instruments in an approaching aircraft translate into a pleasant buzz if on course; it changes into an alarming beep if the plane veers off course. We can safely assume that, as soon as possible after the Deluge, new guidance equipment was brought down to Earth. . . . our guess is that the purpose of the various chambers within the pyramid was to house such guidance and communications equipment." (lines 10597–10598)

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the architectural capstone of Sitchin's larger spaceport thesis, which began earlier in the book with his identification of Tilmun/Sinai as the Anunnaki's post-Diluvial Spaceport (see [[Sitchin: the Anunnaki's Landing Place at Baalbek and the Sinai spaceport (Tilmun) served their spacecraft]]). The Landing Grid system ties together Baalbek (the Landing Platform), the Giza pyramids (western guidance beacons), Mount Umm Shumar / Mount St. Katherine (southeastern anchor), and Jerusalem (Mission Control Center), all oriented along the Thirtieth Parallel and around the central "Line of Jerusalem" bisecting the corridor focused on Ararat. The [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] are portrayed as expert surveyors and engineers who used the same geometric methods of 45° lines and equidistance that they had applied in pre-Diluvial Mesopotamia. The complete absence of royal inscriptions or mummies inside any Third or Fourth Dynasty pyramid is presented as confirmatory evidence that the structures were not intended as tombs.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that his narrative reconstruction of the Anunnaki's airborne survey "we, of course, shall never know (unless a tablet is someday found recording the event); we have merely dramatised some astounding but undeniable facts." He also notes that the alignment claims rest partly on geometric coincidences that could have alternative explanations. Mainstream Egyptology uniformly rejects the navigation-beacon hypothesis: the textbook position holds that pyramids evolved from mastabas and step-pyramids as royal funerary monuments, and points to extensive Egyptian textual, artistic, and archaeological evidence linking each pyramid to the reign of a specific pharaoh. The empty chambers are explained by ancient tomb-robbing and the possibility that some pharaohs were actually buried elsewhere.
