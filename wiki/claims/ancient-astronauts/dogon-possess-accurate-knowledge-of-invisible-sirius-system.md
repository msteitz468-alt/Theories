**Claim Statement**  
The Dogon tribe of Mali possess precise, traditional astronomical information regarding the star Sirius and its companions—including the orbital period, density, and existence of the invisible white dwarf star Sirius B (*Po Tolo*) and a third star Sirius C (*Emme Ya*)—which could not have been observed without modern telescopes and calculations.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**  
According to *The Sirius Mystery* (Chapter One and the translated Griaule/Dieterlen article "A Sudanese Sirius System"):
* **Sirius B (Po Tolo / Digitaria):** The Dogon identify *Po Tolo* as revolving around Sirius A in an elliptical orbit every 50 years. They describe it as invisible to the naked eye, "the smallest of all stars," but also "the heaviest," made of a dense metal called *sagala*. Astronomically, Sirius B is indeed a white dwarf with a 50-year orbit and extreme density (degenerate matter).
* **Moons and Rings:** The Dogon possess drawings showing the four Galilean moons of Jupiter and the rings of Saturn.
* **Sirius C (Emme Ya):** The Dogon describe a third star, *Emme Ya* (sorgho star), which is larger but four times lighter than *Po Tolo*, and orbits Sirius A in a wider trajectory. They assert that it has a single planet orbiting it, which is the home world of the Nommos.

**Contexts Within the Source**  
Temple frames the Dogon's astronomical knowledge as an inexplicable mystery under conventional historical models. He notes that Sirius B is totally invisible to the naked eye (magnitude 8, completely drowned out by Sirius A's light) and was only mathematically inferred in 1844 and visually confirmed in 1862. He argues that this detailed lore could not have arisen independently from native observations or simple myths, but represents an ancient contact event.
