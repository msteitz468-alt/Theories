## Claim Statement

According to [[Zecharia Sitchin]], the single engine driving the most destructive conflicts of the ancient world — from the Tower of Babel incident to the imprisonment of a god inside the Great Pyramid, from the rise and fall of Sargon's empire to the final nuclear catastrophe — was [[Marduk (Sitchin)]]'s relentless drive to seize supremacy over the other [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] and to make Babylon the "Gateway of the Gods" and rightful capital of Earth. Every major confrontation described in [[The Wars of Gods and Men (Sitchin)]] is, in Sitchin's reading, a chapter in this long, unfinished struggle. Marduk's repeated provocations forced the Enlilite gods into a series of increasingly extreme countermeasures, culminating in the nuclear destruction of the Sinai spaceport and the cities of the Jordan plain in 2024 BC, and the unintended annihilation of Sumer itself by the radioactive "Evil Wind" that followed.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sitchin traces the chain of disasters explicitly to Marduk from the outset of Chapter 10: "By this attempt [the Tower of Babel], Marduk started a chain of events replete with tragedies" (line ~8469). The Tower of Babel incident itself, Sitchin argues from an Akkadian cuneiform text, was instigated by a god "whose thoughts were evil; against the Father of the Gods [Enlil] he was wicked" and who "the people of Babylon he corrupted to sin" — pointing unmistakably to Marduk, whose mother Damkina was also named in the text as standing by him, saying "With my son I rise" (line ~8409).

Expelled from Babylon after Enlil destroyed the tower, Marduk relocated to Egypt, where his renewed bid for Egyptian supremacy brought him into collision with Inanna's betrothed Dumuzi. Sitchin reads the Sumerian texts to mean that Marduk ordered the seizure and ultimately caused the death of Dumuzi, with Akkadian texts identifying the god "Bilulu" — "the god Marduk who had sinned" — as the one who "directed the punitive action against Dumuzi" (line ~8941). The consequence was Inanna's fury and her campaign to have Marduk sentenced to death.

The sentence, as Sitchin reconstructs it from the Ashur mystery-play tablets, was entombment alive inside the Great Pyramid — "In a great envelope that is sealed, / With no one to offer him nourishment; / Alone to suffer, / The potable watersource to be cut off" (lines ~9131–9149). Rescued via an emergency shaft bored through the masonry (the origin of the irregular segment C of the Well Shaft, in Sitchin's architectural analysis), Marduk went into exile, acquiring the epithet "Amen — The Hidden One" (line ~9385). Sitchin notes: "Circa 2000 B.C., he reappeared to claim again supremacy; for that, mankind ended up paying a most bitter price" (line ~9389).

That reappearance is mapped by Sitchin through the Akkadian Erra Epic and the Khedorlaomer Texts. When Sargon's grandson Naram-Sin — acting under Inanna's encouragement — removed sacred soil from Babylon and invaded the Sinai spaceport's region, Marduk counter-attacked: "On account of the sacrilege Sargon thus committed, the great lord Marduk became enraged and destroyed his people by hunger; from the east to the west he alienated them from Sargon" (lines ~10401–10419). After Marduk was again persuaded to leave Babylon by his brother Nergal (who then broke his promise and "smashed Marduk's abode, set fire to its gates," line ~10595), a new sequence of destabilization began. The Erra Epic in Sitchin's reading makes Nergal's betrayal the proximate trigger for the chaos that ultimately goaded Enlil into sanctioning nuclear weapons.

By the fateful year 2024 BC, Marduk marched from Hittite exile back to Babylon at the head of Amorite supporters — fulfilling the oracle "he who calls himself Supreme . . . shall come from the west" (line ~12727) — and the other gods, having exhausted diplomatic options, authorized the destruction of both the Sinai spaceport and Marduk's Canaanite supporters.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the connective spine of [[The Wars of Gods and Men (Sitchin)]]. It bridges the architectural mystery of the Great Pyramid (Chapter 10), the political turbulence of the Sargonic and post-Sargonic era (Chapter 12), and the biblical narratives of Abraham and the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah (Chapters 13–14). It intersects directly with [[Sitchin: nuclear weapons destroyed the Sinai spaceport and Sodom and Gomorrah in 2024 BC]], which represents the culminating act to which Marduk's long career of provocation led.

Sitchin situates Marduk's drive within the broader [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] dynastic struggle between the Enlilite and Enkiite clans. As the firstborn son of [[Enki (Sitchin)]], Marduk claimed seniority over all the Anunnaki on Earth. His numerical rank and the divine legitimacy of Babylon as "Bab-Ili" (Gateway of the Gods) were, in Sitchin's reconstruction, the theological justification Marduk repeatedly invoked. The tablet in which an aging [[Marduk (Sitchin)]] recalls his wanderings captures the personal dimension: "I am the divine Marduk, a great god. / I was cast off for my sins, / to the mountains I have gone. / In many lands I have been a wanderer . . . / 24 years, in its midst, I nested" (lines ~11929–11983).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin presents Marduk's own statements sympathetically — in the Erra Epic exchanges, Marduk articulates legitimate grievances: "In the aftermath of the Deluge, / the decrees of Heaven and Earth had gone astray. . . / Rebuild I must my residence / which in the Deluge was wiped away" (lines ~10483–10537). The Babylonian pro-Marduk chronicles that Sitchin draws on (the Khedorlaomer Texts, the Erra Epic) attribute many of the atrocities Marduk was blamed for — including the defilement of Enlil's Ekur — to Nergal acting under Ninurta's direction. Mainstream scholarship rejects Sitchin's entire interpretive framework: ancient-astronaut readings of Sumerian and Akkadian mythology are not accepted by professional Assyriologists, who treat these texts as religious literature, political propaganda, and literary myth rather than literal historical records of extraterrestrial events.
