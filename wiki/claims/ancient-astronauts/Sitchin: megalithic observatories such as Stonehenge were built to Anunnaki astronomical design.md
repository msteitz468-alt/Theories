## Claim Statement

Sitchin argues that Stonehenge — the "computer made of stone" on Salisbury Plain — was not the independent achievement of Neolithic or Bronze Age Britons but was designed according to astronomical knowledge that could only have originated with the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] (the Anunnaki) or their human priest-intermediaries. He extends this argument to a cluster of "temples that faced heaven" across the ancient world, including the solstitially oriented temples of Karnak in Egypt and, most strikingly, a "Stonehenge on the Euphrates": two stone circles of upright megaliths erected inside the Eninnu sacred precinct at Lagash by Gudea on the explicit instructions of the god Ninurta, at almost exactly the same time as Stonehenge II–III. The circular plan of all these structures, Sitchin contends, reflects not merely astronomical skill but the deliberate Anunnaki invention of a solar-lunar calendar computer whose architectural form was passed from the divine architects to human builders around the globe.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The core designation appears early in Chapter 2: "This proposition that Stonehenge was not only a 'Sun temple' but also a lunar observatory … leading scholars to allude to ancient gods, as the only possible explanations for the enigma." Sitchin endorses the verdict of astronomer Fred Hoyle that "Neolithic Man in Britain was simply incapable of such achievements" and that the builders "might have come to the British Isles from the outside, purposely looking for this rectangular alignment — just as the modern astronomer often searches far from home for places to build his telescopes." Hoyle's own musing — "'A veritable Newton or Einstein must have been at work at Stonehenge'" — is used by Sitchin as an opening for his own answer: whoever it was, "the search for answers to the Stonehenge enigma should thus be sought in places far away from it."

The answer comes from Sumer. Sitchin quotes Irish pre-Celtic legend referring to the site at Newgrange as the "House of Oengus, son of the chief god of the pre-Celtic pantheon who had come to Ireland from 'the Otherworld.' That chief god was known as An Dagda, 'An, the good god' … It is indeed amazing to find the name of the principal deity of the ancient world in all these diverse places — in Sumer and his E.ANNA ziggurat of Uruk; in the Egyptian Heliopolis, whose true name was Annu; and in far-removed Ireland." He then asks: "Was it, then, not the ancient peoples, but the ancient gods who created Stonehenge?"

The "Stonehenge on the Euphrates" argument is developed in Chapter 7. The Sumerian king Gudea, executing Ninurta's explicit divine instructions for the new Eninnu temple at Lagash, erected seven stone uprights in a precisely positioned pattern around 2200–2100 BC: "six in a circle and the seventh in alignment with the Sun." Sitchin writes: "Did Gudea use the stelae to form a stone henge — in ancient Sumer, more than five thousand years ago?" He then describes a second, larger stone circle: "a 'crownlike circle for the new Moon' … consisting of thirteen stones erected 'like heroes in a network' — a most figurative way, it seems to us, to describe a circle of upright stones connected at the top by lintels to form a 'network' akin to the Trilithons at Stonehenge!" The parallel in timing is made explicit: "As at Stonehenge I, seven pillars played a key role; as in all the phases of Stonehenge in Lagash, too, a large megalith created the desired sightline toward the principal solar orientation. In both places a stone 'computer' was created to serve as a solar-lunar observatory."

Sitchin also identifies the divine architect responsible for both. At the end of Chapter 7, after showing that Ningishzidda laid the Eninnu's cornerstone and determined its astronomical orientation, he states: "the Divine Architect, the Keeper of the Secrets of the Pyramids, the god called by Gudea Ningishzidda; for he was none other than the Egyptian god Tehuti whom we call THOTH." Thoth was, according to Sitchin, "He who reckons the heavens, the counter of the stars and the measurer of the Earth" — the supreme Anunnaki Divine Architect whose hand shaped temples from Stonehenge to Karnak to the Girsu.

The Egyptian-Stonehenge parallel runs through Chapter 3 as well. Lockyer's archaeoastronomical studies are cited to show that solstitially oriented Egyptian temples changed their axis as Earth's tilt shifted, exactly as Stonehenge II shifted its axis and widened its avenue — evidence that both were managed by the same ongoing knowledge tradition: "The When holds the key … the hurried remaking of Stonehenge in 2100–2000 B.C. had to do with the coming of a New Age — Mankind's first historically recorded New Age."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the structural spine of *When Time Began*. The book opens by asking what explains Stonehenge and then spends its first eight chapters assembling the answer: the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] invented the zodiac as a timekeeping device (see [[Sitchin: the zodiac and precessional Ages encode Anunnaki timekeeping]]), created the Nippur calendar, instructed human priest-kings in astronomical construction, and provided the "Divine Architects" — above all Thoth/Ningishzidda — who oversaw temples worldwide. The [[Shar (3,600-year orbital period)]] of [[The Twelfth Planet (Nibiru)]] is the underlying unit of Divine Time from which all these observatories ultimately derive their raison d'être: the Anunnaki needed to correlate their own vast orbital year with Earthly Time, and stone circles were one instrument for doing so.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that mainstream scholarship and leading archaeologists (including Richard Atkinson of Cardiff, who once ridiculed lunar-observatory theories as "Moonshine on Stonehenge") long resisted any interpretation of Stonehenge as a sophisticated astronomical predictor. He notes the consistent scientific objection that "Neolithic Man in Britain simply did not possess such knowledge" and that the stone circles arrived too early to be credited to Mycenaean Greeks, Phoenicians, or Egyptians, leaving the origin question open. Mainstream archaeology today attributes Stonehenge to the organised labour of Neolithic and early Bronze Age peoples without invoking supernatural or extraterrestrial architects; the claimed "Stonehenge on the Euphrates" parallel rests on a reading of Gudea's inscriptions that has not been accepted by Assyriology; and the identification of Ningishzidda with Thoth is Sitchin's own synthesis, not attested in the ancient texts themselves.
