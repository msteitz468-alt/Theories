## Claim Statement

In the book's Postscript, Sitchin argues that the question of humanity's alien genetic origins is an empirical, scientifically testable claim — not merely a textual or mythological one. The skeletal remains of "Nin.Puabi," the goddess he identifies as interred in Tomb PG-800 of the Royal Tombs of Ur and currently held by the Natural History Museum in London, contain mitochondrial DNA that Sitchin asserts is traceable to the planet Nibiru through an unbroken matrilineal Anunnaki lineage. He calls on the Natural History Museum to conduct genome sequencing of her bones, arguing this would reveal the specific genetic differences — the "alien genes" — that separate modern Homo sapiens from its evolutionary predecessors and that were, in his model, deliberately introduced by the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] approximately 300,000 years ago.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The Postscript opens by framing the problem that has persisted since the publication of Sitchin's first book: standard evolutionary theory cannot account for the sudden emergence of Homo sapiens, a gap he calls "the Missing Link." He writes: "The 'Link' is missing, I said, because someone jumped the gun on Evolution and used sophisticated genetic engineering to upgrade a Homo erectus or Homo ergaster by mixing his genes with their advanced genes. That 'someone' were the biblical Elohim (whom the Sumerians called Anunnaki) who came to Earth from their planet, Nibiru, fashioned The Adam, then took the Daughters of Man as wives" (lines 7963–7964).

He then announces the existence of a physical specimen that can, in principle, resolve the question: "But, through the grace of Fate and the professionalism of dedicated archaeologists, such evidence — a physical body of an Anunnaki — does exist. It is the skeletal remains of Nin.Puabi" (line 7971). He documents his correspondence with the British Museum following a 2002 announcement that unopened boxes from Woolley's excavation contained skulls from the Royal Tombs of Ur. After inquiring "whether there are plans to examine the DNA in these skulls," he was told "at present there are no plans to attempt DNA analysis" (lines 7973–7974). In 2005 the British Museum confirmed that "the skeleton of Puabi is held in the Natural History Museum, alongside others from Leonard Woolley's excavations at Ur" (line 7979).

Sitchin calls this confirmation "a bombshell discovery": "The skeletal remains of a Nibiruan goddess (and of a demigod king) who was buried some 4,500 years ago — were unexpectedly available, intact!" (line 7981). He then makes the epistemological stakes explicit: "One can debate who really built the Great Pyramids, disagree about the meaning of a Sumerian text, or dismiss an embarrassing find as a forgery; but here is irrefutable physical evidence whose provenance, date and place of discovery, etc., are beyond doubt. So, if my identification of Puabi as an Anunnaki goddess and not a 'queen', and of Mes.kalam.dug as a demigod and not a Sumerian 'prince' — we have at our disposal two genomes of people fully or partly from another planet!" (lines 7983–7984).

The key genetic argument turns on mitochondrial DNA specifically. Sitchin explains: "Though Nin.Puabi's DNA is not purely Anunnaki because her father, Lugalbanda, was only a demigod, her mitochondrial DNA that comes only from the mother is pure Anunnaki — leading through Ninsun and Bau to the Olden Mothers on Nibiru. If tested, her bones could reveal the DNA and mtDNA differences that represent our genetic Missing Link — that small but crucial group of 'alien genes' (223 of them?) that upgraded us from wild hominids to Modern Man some 300,000 years ago" (lines 7989–7990).

The urgency is given scientific grounding by the parallel with ancient-DNA research: "recent news, that biologists were able to decipher and compare Neanderthal DNA from 38,000 years ago with that of modern man, struck as lightning: If so — why not decipher and compare the DNA of an Anunnaki female who died a mere 4,500 years ago?" (line 7985). In a February 2009 letter to the Natural History Museum, confirmed by Dr. Margaret Clegg, Head of the Museum's Human Remains Unit, he learned that "No DNA analysis has ever been conducted on these remains," and that "the Museum does not routinely conduct DNA analysis on remains in the collection, and there are no plans to do so in the near future" — a refusal restated in March 2010 (lines 7987–7988).

The Postscript closes with Sitchin's fervent appeal: "It is my fervent hope that by showing that the remains of NIN-Puabi are no 'routine' matter, this book will convince the museum to do the unusual and conduct the tests. They could provide vital explanation of the answer given to Gilgamesh" — followed by the ancient Sumerian verse stating the gods gave Man knowledge and wisdom but "Everlasting life they had not given him" (lines 7991–8005). His final question: "What was it, genetically, that the 'gods' deliberately held back from us?" (lines 8011–8013).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the culmination of the book's entire argument. It depends on [[Sitchin: the preserved tomb of the goddess offers testable physical evidence of the Anunnaki]], which establishes the identity and provenance of the physical specimen. It also recapitulates the book's central thesis connecting the [[Nefilim (Sitchin)]] — equated throughout with the biblical Nephilim — to the genetic engineering of humanity, a claim introduced in [[The Twelfth Planet (Sitchin)]].

The specific figure of "223 alien genes" alludes to research published around 2001 (cited in Sitchin's earlier writings) claiming that approximately 223 human genes have no known homologue in non-human organisms, which Sitchin interprets as the Anunnaki genetic contribution. The matrilineal mtDNA argument is significant: Sitchin claims Puabi's maternal line runs through the Anunnaki goddesses Ninsun and Bau all the way to Nibiru, making her mitochondrial genome a direct record of Nibiruan biology — untainted by the paternal demigod lineage. This is the mechanism by which a 4,500-year-old female buried in [[Sumer (Sitchin account)]] could, in Sitchin's view, yield evidence about events 300,000 years prior.

Sitchin links the deliberate withholding of "Everlasting life" from Man (a recurring theme in Chapter XII, "Immortality: The Grand Illusion," lines 5589–6197) to a specific genetic act by [[Enki (Sitchin)]]: the exclusion of a longevity gene or genes from the human genome at the time of creation. The DNA test he proposes would therefore answer not only the question of alien origins but also the question of why humans are mortal while the Anunnaki were (extremely) long-lived.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sitchin acknowledges that his identification of Puabi as an Anunnaki goddess — the premise on which the DNA-testing argument depends — is his own inference, not a documented fact. He presents the genealogical reconstruction with hedges ("our guess," "I suggest," "in this imagined scenario"). The Natural History Museum explicitly declined DNA testing in 2009 and 2010, citing routine policy rather than rebutting Sitchin's interpretation. Mainstream paleoanthropology does not support the premise that humans were genetically engineered by extraterrestrials, and the "223 alien genes" claim has been disputed by geneticists who attribute those sequences to horizontal gene transfer or incomplete data. Mainstream Assyriology treats the Royal Tombs of Ur as elite human burials, not divine interments, and the occupant of PG-800 is identified as a high-status Sumerian woman, not a Nibiruan goddess.
