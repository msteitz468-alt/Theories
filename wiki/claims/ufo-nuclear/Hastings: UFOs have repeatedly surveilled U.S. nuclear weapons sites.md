## Claim Statement

Hastings argues, on the basis of declassified U.S. Air Force, FBI, and CIA documents supplemented by the testimony of nearly 100 former and retired military personnel, that UFOs have persistently and repeatedly maneuvered over, hovered above, and conducted apparent surveillance of American nuclear weapons sites since at least December 1948. The pattern spans atomic-bomb research laboratories, fissile-material production plants, weapons-storage and assembly areas, atmospheric test sites, naval vessels conducting thermonuclear tests, and — starting in the early 1960s — Intercontinental Ballistic Missile (ICBM) launch facilities controlled by multiple Strategic Air Command bases. Hastings treats this pattern not as anecdotal noise but as a documentable, four-decade-long phenomenon that U.S. intelligence agencies themselves classified at the highest levels.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hastings frames the entire book around the persistence of this surveillance pattern. In the Introduction he states: "periodic UFO activity at nuclear weapons-related facilities has stubbornly persisted over the years and continues to occur," and notes that "declassified U.S. Air Force, FBI, and CIA files establish a convincing, ongoing pattern of UFO activity at U.S. nuclear weapons sites, decade after decade. Hundreds of such sightings—many of them verified by radar—have occurred at research laboratories, fissile material production plants, test sites, missile launch facilities, bomber alert pads, and nukes storage areas."

**Late 1940s — Los Alamos, Sandia, Kirtland, Hanford**
The earliest documented incidents appear in an FBI memorandum dated January 31, 1949, which reports the "repeated observation of 'Flying Discs, Flying Saucers, and Balls of Fire'" near Los Alamos "as early as December 1948," and underlines that "This matter is considered top secret by Intelligence Officers of both the Army and the Air Forces." A follow-up FBI memo of August 23, 1950 — titled "SUMMARY OF AERIAL PHENOMENA IN NEW MEXICO" — records "approximately 150 observations of aerial phenomena" near installations in that state since late 1948, categorizing them as green fireballs, fast-moving discs, and meteor-like objects, and concluding that their occurrence "was a cause [for] concern." Security personnel at Sandia Base and Kirtland AFB filed a stream of declassified OSI Spot Intelligence Reports from late 1948 through 1950, describing round, silver-colored objects performing "zigzag maneuvers, right-angle turns, and instantaneous, 180-degree reversals of course" directly over the nuclear weapons assembly complex. At the Hanford plutonium plant in Washington state, a classified Air Force intelligence report confirms a "silvery, disc-shaped" object hovering over the plant on May 21, 1949, tracked simultaneously on radar at Moses Lake AFB; jet interception attempts failed as the object "left the vicinity at a high rate of speed — faster than any aircraft."

**CIA-level alarm**
On December 2, 1952, CIA Assistant Director H. Marshall Chadwell wrote a Secret memorandum to CIA Director Walter B. Smith stating: "Sightings of unexplained objects at great altitudes and traveling at high speeds in the vicinity of major U.S. defense installations are of such nature that they are not attributable to natural phenomena or known types of aerial vehicles," and urged that the matter be brought before the National Security Council as a priority project. Captain Edward Ruppelt, chief of Project Blue Book, confirmed in his 1956 book that "UFOs were seen more frequently around areas vital to the defense of the United States. The Los Alamos-Albuquerque area, Oak Ridge, and White Sands Proving Ground rated high."

**Killeen Base, Texas (1949)**
Declassified Army documents describe a sustained two-month incursion at Killeen Base — America's first nuclear bomb stockpile site — beginning March 1949. On the evenings of April 27–28, 1949, security guards and other personnel observed a series of small glowing objects, some the "size of a ping-pong ball," flying within six feet of observers, performing zigzag paths, and displaying what appeared to be metallic conical appendages. A May 5, 1949 inter-agency conference — attended by Army, Navy, FBI, AFOSI, and Armed Forces Special Weapons Project representatives — concluded that the sightings warranted "grave concern."

**Nevada Proving Ground and Pacific thermonuclear tests (1950s)**
Researcher Walter Webb's 1981 investigation of a witness who served at Yucca Flat found that on October 30, 1951 — just before the Buster Charlie atomic shot — 18 silver, disc-shaped objects with domes descended over the test site, hovered for "30 seconds to a minute," then "abruptly departed upward at an angle and vanished in seconds." An identical count of 18 discs was independently reported by an Air Force technical sergeant and four civilian workers at Nellis AFB on April 16, 1952, flying a course "over or very close to the test site." Aboard the U.S.S. Curtiss AV-4 during Operation Castle, the ship's deck log for April 7, 1954 formally records: "at 2305 an unidentified luminous object passed over ship from bow to stern, yellowish-orange in color, traveling at a high rate of speed and a low altitude" — just hours after a hydrogen bomb test. Retired Sandia Laboratory employee Chester "Chet" Lytle Sr., who held Top Secret clearances with the AEC, CIA, and DOD, reported that UFOs were tracked at White Sands at speeds of "Mach 3, 4, 5. Then they would just suddenly stop and hang there," and that those incidents were "very hush-hush."

**Atlas ICBM sites, Walker AFB (1963–1964)**
Multiple former members of the 579th Strategic Missile Squadron independently confirm a months-long series of UFO incidents at Atlas silos southeast of Roswell, New Mexico. Former Deputy Missile Combat Crew Commander Jerry Nelson stated that on "probably more than three but fewer than ten" occasions, "These objects would hover over the silo and shine lights down on [the guards] without making any noise." Security guard Roger Goss described reporting fast-moving, hovering lights to the launch commander, receiving the dismissive reply that they were "military aircraft" — an explanation traced to Strategic Air Command headquarters in Omaha. Airman 2nd Class Barry Krause wrote to NICAP in December 1964, while still assigned to the 579th, that UFO sightings "at most of our missile sites" had become so frequent that "It got so bad the guards were afraid to go on guard duty," and that attempts to read the incident reports were rebuffed with "top secret" classifications. A UFO was also tracked on radar at Walker AFB and jet fighters scrambled, but the official record subsequently denied that any fighters had been sent.

**F.E. Warren AFB Atlas sites (early 1960s)**
Former Air Policeman Arthur McEnaney reported that around midnight in August 1964, he and his fellow guards observed "an object hovering over the site. It was round and shone brightly." They were later "told to keep quiet about it as it was national security."

Hastings emphasizes the span and consistency of this record: "Based on these documents, and other evidence, it is the contention of the author that there exists a credible connection between the appearance of nuclear weapons in the mid-1940s, and the overall increase in UFO sightings worldwide since that time." He also notes that personnel involved "were subjected to, and passed, rigorous background checks and personality tests designed to determine with a reasonable degree of certainty their psychological stability and reliability" — the same PRP screening applied to all who worked with nuclear weapons.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim constitutes the empirical spine of [[UFOs and Nukes (Hastings)]]. Every chapter in the assigned range adds a new category of site or era to the surveillance record: atomic-bomb laboratories (Ch. 2), test sites (Ch. 3), naval vessels at thermonuclear tests (Ch. 4), air-sampling flights (Ch. 4), a possible Cuban Missile Crisis incident at Loring AFB (Ch. 6), and ICBM fields (Ch. 7). The accumulation of incidents across these distinct environments — and across the full span of the nuclear era — is Hastings's core strategy for establishing the pattern as systematic rather than coincidental. The interpretive claim that this surveillance implies non-human concern about nuclear weapons is developed separately in [[Hastings: the UFO–nuclear connection signals non-human concern about humanity's nuclear weapons]]. Both claims rest on the same documentary and testimonial base and are linked through [[The nuclear-UFO connection (Hastings)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hastings acknowledges several important caveats. First, most of his sources are anecdotal rather than scientific, and he concedes that "the great majority of these persons' revelations, although entirely credible in my view, ultimately proved to be unverifiable, simply because the events being described were usually still classified." Second, he notes that many witnesses have incomplete or imprecise memories of specific dates, designations, and colleagues' names. Third, the Air Force provided alternative explanations for many incidents: Project Blue Book dismissed the November 4, 1957 Kirtland AFB UFO as "an off-course private aircraft"; the UFO spotted hovering over the Hanford plant was publicly "dismissed by the Air Force as the sighting of a conventional aircraft"; and Atlas guards at Walker AFB were told by SAC HQ that the lights they observed were "military aircraft" or "a KC-135." Researcher and expert Lincoln La Paz, studying the green fireballs, privately suggested they might be Soviet guided missiles launched from the Ural region. Hastings also notes that the Freedom of Information Act has yielded "only limited data" on these incidents, and that "the Air Force was failing to comply with my request to release such documents to the public."
