## Claim Statement

Hastings argues that UFO activity at nuclear weapons sites was not confined to the United States but occurred in parallel at Soviet and later Russian military installations. He documents at least three distinct incidents — at Byelokoroviche, Ukraine (1982); Katta-Kurgan, Uzbekistan (1984); and Kapustin Yar, Russia (1989) — in which unidentified aerial objects were observed by Soviet military personnel over or near nuclear missile bases and weapons depots. Most strikingly, the 1982 Byelokoroviche incident involved nuclear missiles briefly and autonomously entering a launch-readiness sequence while a UFO was present, mirroring the comparable "Launch in Progress" incident Hastings documented at Minot AFB in the 1960s. Hastings presents this parallel as strong evidence that the UFO phenomenon operates across national boundaries and is specifically interested in nuclear weapons.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The centerpiece of Hastings's Soviet evidence is the October 1982 incident near Byelokoroviche, in Soviet Ukraine. Drawing on an ABC News *Prime Time Live* broadcast from October 1994, Hastings quotes retired Army Lt. Col. Vladimir Plantonev: "It looked just like a flying saucer. The way they show them in the movies. No portholes, nothing. The surface was absolutely even. The disk made a beautiful turn, like this, on the edge, just like a plane. There was no sound. I had never seen anything like that before." While the UFO was still present, an unauthorized launch sequence was initiated across multiple missiles. Retired Colonel Igor Chernovshev told ABC: "During this period, for a short time, signal lights on both the control panels suddenly turned on. The lights showing that missiles were preparing for launch. This could normally only happen if an order were transmitted from Moscow." The automated sequence counted down for fifteen terrifying seconds before aborting and returning to stand-by status. Hastings notes that ABC reporter David Ensor described the investigation as turning up "forty major incidents, including one that prompted fears of starting an accidental nuclear war."

Hastings also cites an October 1984 sighting near the Katta-Kurgan tactical nuclear missile base in what is now Uzbekistan, investigated by Dr. S. P. Kuzionov of Leningrad's Geographical Society. Missile technician Shamil Yuaihmetov and four other witnesses observed a metallic, cigar-shaped object descending at a 45-degree angle with a hissing sound. The following day, three landing-gear depressions in an equilateral triangle pattern were discovered in a nearby vineyard, each half a meter deep; damaged vegetation covered an area of 30 by 80 meters. Hastings notes that the Soviet military investigated but released no findings to researchers.

The third major case is the July 1989 incident at Kapustin Yar, a Soviet missile test complex south of Volgograd. Drawing on declassified KGB documents secured by researcher Don Berliner of the Fund for UFO Research, Hastings quotes the case summary written by a KGB officer: "Military personnel of the signal center observed UFOs in the period from 22:12 hrs. to 23:55 hrs. on 28 July 1989… disc 4-5 m. diameter, with a half-sphere on top, which is lit brightly. It moved sometimes abruptly, but noiselessly, at times coming down and hovering over ground at an altitude of 20-60 m. The command of [censored] called for a fighter… but it was not able to see it in detail, because the UFO did not let the aircraft come near it, evading it." Ensign Valery Voloshin's deposition, one of seven filed by military witnesses, records that the disc moved directly toward the complex's nuclear warhead depot and hovered over it at twenty meters: "While the object was hovering over the depot, a bright beam appeared from the bottom of the disc… and made two or three circles, lighting the corner of one of the buildings." The UFO returned to the depot a second time and remained under observation for roughly two hours before departing. Hastings observes that Col. Halt of Bentwaters, upon reviewing Berliner's report, responded simply: "This all sounds very familiar" — drawing a direct parallel with the beam-of-light incident at the RAF Bentwaters Weapons Storage Area in December 1980.

Hastings also notes that in 1994 ABC's five-month investigation obtained "over a thousand pages of documents collected by the old KGB" and spoke to "dozens of Russian scientists, government officials, and military men," indicating the phenomenon was documented at an institutional scale within the Soviet state.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is developed in Chapter 23 ("Back in the U.S.S.R.") and provides the explicit international dimension of Hastings's central thesis, captured by [[The nuclear-UFO connection (Hastings)]]. Throughout the book, Hastings notes that he had publicly speculated from at least 1985 — nine years before the ABC broadcast — that if UFOs monitored American ICBM sites, "it seemed likely, even logical to me that if these events had in fact taken place at U.S. ICBM sites, those piloting the UFOs would act even-handedly, so to speak, and monitor Soviet nuclear missile installations as well." The Soviet incidents thus function as independent corroboration of the pattern Hastings had established through U.S. testimony, and he draws an explicit structural parallel between the Byelokoroviche launch-activation and the Minot AFB "Launch in Progress" incident reported by David Schuur. The Kapustin Yar case also mirrors the Bentwaters WSA beam-of-light incident, suggesting to Hastings a globally consistent pattern of UFO interest in nuclear warhead storage. See also [[UFOs and Nukes (Hastings)]] for the full source context.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hastings acknowledges an official Soviet counter-explanation for the 1982 Byelokoroviche incident. A History Channel program (*Soviet UFO Secrets Revealed*, 2004) quoted Dr. Yulii Platov of the Russian Academy of Sciences, who stated that an official investigation by Institute 22 had determined within "literally half-a-day" that the object was a military flare dropped from aircraft during a nearby training exercise. Hastings disputes this explanation, pointing out that Plantonev's description of the object as having a perfectly geometric, disc-shaped form is inconsistent with the irregular, constantly-changing appearance of a burning magnesium flare. He also notes the History Channel program selectively omitted Plantonev's testimony and ABC's report of collective civilian eyewitness accounts describing the object as "huge, about 900 feet in diameter" and hovering for hours over the missile base. Hastings concludes that the Institute 22 investigators "had probably been misled by military commanders attempting to throw a security blanket around the alarming event." No official Soviet or Russian government documents have been declassified confirming UFO involvement.
