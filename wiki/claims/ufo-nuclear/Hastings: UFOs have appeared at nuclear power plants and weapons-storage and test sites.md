## Claim Statement

Hastings argues that UFO activity documented throughout his research is not confined to ICBM missile fields: it extends to commercial nuclear power plants, nuclear weapons-storage complexes, and nuclear research and test facilities. Assembling case reports spanning 1967 to 2008, he presents this broader pattern as "obviously integral to the UFO-Nukes Connection," broadening his core thesis from Minuteman missile sites to the entire nuclear infrastructure. In Hastings' view, wherever nuclear technology is concentrated — weapons, reactors, or research — UFO presence has been observed and reported by credible civilian and military witnesses.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

### Commercial Nuclear Power Plants (Appendix B)

Hastings compiles a dedicated appendix cataloguing sightings at commercial reactors on multiple continents. He introduces the section by stating that "UFO sightings at commercial nuclear power plants have been reported worldwide for over four decades" and that "these incidents are obviously integral to the UFO-Nukes Connection" (line 7695).

Key incidents he documents:

- **Yankee Atomic Power Plant, Haddam Neck, Connecticut (September 13, 1967):** Physicist and UFO researcher Dr. James E. McDonald interviewed three witnesses who reported "6-7 very bright lights" hovering over the plant, blinking on and off, just after 11:00 p.m. (line 7706).

- **Indian Point Nuclear Power Plant, New York (June 24, 1984):** Twelve security guards at the plant reported a diamond-shaped object estimated at 450 feet in length hovering directly above the exhaust funnel of one of the plant's three nuclear reactors. UFO investigator Philip J. Imbrogno subsequently interviewed six of the twelve guards; they described the object changing colors "from white to blue to red to green to amber." Local police in Peekskill received numerous corroborating UFO reports the same night (line 7715).

- **Chernobyl Nuclear Power Station, Soviet Ukraine (April 26, 1986):** Dr. Vladimir V. Rubtsov, Director of the Research Institute on Anomalous Phenomena, reported that a rising number of UFO observations in the area of Chernobyl had been noted by pilots in the weeks before the disaster. On the night of the fire — some three hours after the explosion — a team of nuclear specialists observed "a fiery ball of the color of brass" over the station. Radiation levels at their position measured 3,000 milliroentgens per hour before the observation and only 800 milliroentgens per hour after the UFO departed (line 7726–7727). A September 2002 Pravda article titled "UFO Prevents Blast at Chernobyl Nuclear Plant" described the same event with witness Mikhail Varitsky stating: "We saw a ball of fire, and it was slowly flying in the sky…two rays of crimson light stretching towards the fourth unit. The object was some 300 meters from the reactor. The event lasted for about three minutes" (line 7728).

- **Palo Verde Nuclear Power Station, Arizona (July 20, 2004):** A truck driver reported two bright orange balls of light near the Palo Verde plant west of Phoenix, visible to a second driver as well. The lights faded, reappeared further west, and were apparently tracked by military jets circling the area (lines 7739–7741).

- **Surry Nuclear Power Station, Virginia (May 19, 2008):** A retired scientist observed a large metallic disk hovering near the station's twin domes for 15–20 minutes, moving slowly against strong winds and completing a full circuit of the plant. NUFORC Director Peter Davenport noted in posting the report that "the witness is a skilled scientist (retired), and he was able to observe the object for a considerable length of time" (lines 7752–7758).

### Nuclear Weapons-Storage Complexes

Hastings documents UFO activity in direct proximity to major nuclear weapons-storage sites:

- **Kirtland Underground Munitions Storage Complex, New Mexico (January 29, 1998):** A UFO was observed near the southern perimeter of what Hastings describes as "one of the most restricted pieces of real estate in America," where "the Air Force, the Department of Energy, the Defense Nuclear Agency and other government groups operate a multitude of research, test, and storage facilities." The centerpiece is "the 300,000 square-foot Kirtland Underground Munitions Storage Complex, which houses one of the largest nuclear weapons stockpiles in the U.S." An F-15 (or F-16) scrambled from Kirtland AFB to intercept the UFO, which accelerated to roughly four times the jet's speed before disappearing. Hastings concludes: "While it can not be proven, it is all but certain that the base's radar tracked the UFO…and someone, undoubtedly concerned about its proximity to so many highly sensitive facilities, scrambled the jet to intercept it" (lines 6110–6112).

- **Savannah River Plant, South Carolina (September 16, 1986):** A witness driving alongside the Savannah River Nuclear Plant reported a low-flying, wingless, oblong object that made no sound and "slid" course rather than banking — behavior inconsistent with any known conventional aircraft. The event lasted approximately 90 seconds (lines 6127–6141).

- **Weapons Storage Area context:** Hastings also calls on veterans who "served at the Nevada Test Site, or at sea, during the era of atmospheric nuclear testing" to report their sightings, arguing the UFO-Nukes pattern extends to these arenas as well (line 7604).

### Nuclear Research and Test Facilities

The broadening of the pattern to research facilities is made explicit through several accounts:

- **Los Alamos National Laboratory vicinity (1983, 1996, 2001):** Hastings documents multiple UFO sightings in the vicinity of Los Alamos — the primary nuclear weapons design laboratory — spanning nearly two decades. One 1983 witness near the lab reported a disk-shaped object that hovered, expanded suddenly "like a controlled explosion," and disabled her car's ignition. Hastings himself witnessed a related phenomenon in 2001 near Placitas, New Mexico, roughly 30 miles from the lab (lines 6074–6099).

- **Idaho National Engineering Laboratory (1967):** An ecologist working at the Department of Energy's Idaho National Engineering Laboratory — a facility that "has for decades designed and tested nuclear reactors, including those used by U.S. submarines" — and several security guards watched "a luminous round object hover high above the lab for several minutes before moving away at high speed" (line 6114).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim represents the outward extension of Hastings' central argument, first established through ICBM base testimonies, into the civilian and broader military nuclear sphere. Hastings frames the pattern explicitly: "As long as nuclear weapons exist…whatever their numbers, those piloting the UFOs will be snooping around them" (line 6049). The Appendix B compilation and the civilian-sighting chapter (Chapter 25) systematically demonstrate that the phenomenon is not an artifact of Cold War ICBM deployments alone but recurs at any site associated with nuclear technology — reactors, weapons storage, research laboratories, and test ranges.

The breadth of the pattern also supports Hastings' thesis as elaborated in [[The nuclear-UFO connection (Hastings)]]: that an unknown intelligence exhibits a persistent, specific interest in humanity's nuclear capabilities across all their institutional forms.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hastings acknowledges significant caveats throughout:

- The Appendix B compilation is explicitly described as "a short, far from complete" list; Hastings makes no claim to comprehensiveness (line 7695).
- The Chernobyl observation reduces to secondhand reporting via Pravda and a Ukrainian researcher; the radiation reading comparison — while striking — cannot be independently verified.
- Several sightings, including Palo Verde and the Los Alamos civilian accounts, rest on single-witness NUFORC reports without corroboration from the facilities themselves.
- Hastings explicitly separates the green fireballs observed near Malmstrom's India Flight missile sites (January 1995) from the UFO seen the same night, accepting astronomer Walter Webb's conclusion that the fireballs were natural meteors while maintaining that the structured object observed by both the security team and the missile maintenance team was anomalous (lines 6888–6890).
- At no point do Hastings' sources include official confirmation from any nuclear plant operator or commercial facility; all accounts are civilian or retired-military in origin.
