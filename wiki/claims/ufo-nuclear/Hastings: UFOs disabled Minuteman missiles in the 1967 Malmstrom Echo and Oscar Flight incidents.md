## Claim Statement

Hastings reports that in March 1967, two entire flights of Minuteman I ICBMs at Malmstrom AFB, Montana — Echo Flight on or around March 16th and Oscar Flight approximately a week later — went simultaneously off-alert ("No-Go") while UFOs were reported by security personnel in the vicinity of their Launch Control Facilities. According to at least six former or retired U.S. Air Force officers interviewed by Hastings, UFOs apparently caused the malfunctions by inducing an unidentified electronic "noise" into the missiles' guidance-and-control systems. The U.S. Air Force's official position attributes the Echo Flight shutdown to an internal electrical fault of undetermined origin and denies any UFO involvement; it maintains complete silence on the Oscar Flight incident. Hastings treats both incidents as the strongest documented examples of apparent deliberate UFO interference with American nuclear weapons systems.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Chapter 11, "Taking Down Echo and Oscar," opens by establishing the scale of the event: "According to at least six former or retired U.S. Air Force officers, UFOs apparently disrupted the functioning of all 10 Minuteman I missiles at Echo Flight, on March 16th, and essentially repeated the feat at Oscar Flight — probably on March 24th — when at least six to eight missiles were simultaneously shut down. These large-scale disruptions, each lasting a day or more, understandably resulted in great concern, extending from the local air force commanders to the highest levels of the Strategic Air Command (SAC)." (lines 3151–3153)

**The Oscar Flight shutdown and UFO sighting (Salas testimony).** Former 1st Lieutenant Robert Salas, the Deputy Missile Combat Crew Commander on duty at Oscar Flight, describes receiving an urgent call from the site's Flight Security Controller, who reported security guards seeing "strange lights maneuvering in the sky near the LCF." The controller then called back "obviously frightened and shouting into phone": "'Sir, there's one hovering outside the front gate!' … 'A UFO! It's just [hovering] there. We're all looking at it. What do you want us to do?' … 'I can't really describe it. It's glowing red.'" Moments later — and before Salas could respond — the capsule's alarm klaxons fired: "A 'No-Go' light and two red security lights were lit, indicating problems at one of our missile sites … Before he could do so, another alarm went off at another site, then another and another simultaneously. Within the next few seconds, we had lost six to eight missiles to a 'No-Go' (inoperable) condition." After returning topside, Salas was told the UFO "had a red glow and appeared to be saucer-shaped … It had been immediately outside the front gate, hovering silently." A security patrol dispatched afterward "reported sighting another UFO during that patrol" and "lost radio contact with our site immediately after reporting the UFO." (lines 3163–3213)

Salas notes that it was "very rare" for even a single Minuteman missile to malfunction, and that "most of the failures were related to the guidance and control system." On return to Malmstrom, both officers were ordered to an OSI agent who "asked them to sign non-disclosure agreements — telling them that the incident was highly-classified and not to be discussed with anyone, even amongst themselves. The OSI agent also confiscated the launch officers' alert duty logs." (lines 3217–3219)

**The Echo Flight shutdown (Figel / Crawford testimony).** Retired Colonel Don Crawford, who relieved Echo Flight's crew after the malfunctions, reports that outgoing crew members Captain Eric Carlson and 1st Lieutenant Walt Figel told him that "missile maintenance teams working at two of the flight's widely-separated launch facilities reported seeing strange aerial lights maneuvering near their positions." At about 8:30 a.m., "Echo Flight's missiles suddenly began dropping off-line, one after the other. Within seconds, they were all inoperable, due to malfunctions in the missiles' guidance and control — G&C — systems." Crawford recalls both officers "visibly shaken" and notes that maintenance teams "worked all day and into the night to bring the flight's missiles back to alert status." (lines 3231–3237)

**The Boeing engineers' findings.** Following the Echo incident, "Engineers from the Boeing Company — which had designed the Minuteman ICBM — investigated several possible causes, including weather conditions and commercial power surges, but ruled out all of them. However, they were able to duplicate the exact faults a high percentage of the time by directly injecting a certain signal, a 'noise', into the logic coupler. Nevertheless, they were unable to determine the source of such a signal." Researcher Jim Klotz summarizes: "It appears that an unidentified 'noise' somehow induced into the inter-site cable system could have, and probably did, propagate throughout the system affecting multiple LFs, causing the No-Go conditions … It is the source of this noise and how it got into the shielded, deeply buried cable system that was and is the mystery." (lines 3241–3243)

**A maintenance witness observes the object directly.** An anonymous former EMT team member, referred to by Salas as "Mr. X," was working at Launch Facility E-7 on March 16 when the guard called him topside and he "saw a round orange glowing object hovering 'not far out' at about 30 degrees from the horizon." Each time X attempted to restart the missile, the process "would 'crap out'" at the same point — guidance-and-control coupler alignment. X reported that "the object would change to a 'pulsation'" and that he "had the sense that the object was literally directing these pulses to the particular part of the missile system that was failing — the Guidance and Control System Coupler. X stated that he had no doubt whomever was in the object knew the most vulnerable part of the system and were demonstrating that they could indeed disable the missiles." (lines 3440–3448)

**Targeting officer Jamison and the official pre-dispatch briefing.** Former 1st Lieutenant Robert Jamison, a Combat Targeting Team Commander who responded to Oscar Flight, states that before any teams were dispatched, a Non-Commissioned Officer confirmed that "Air Police guards at the stricken flight had indeed reported a UFO just prior to the missiles malfunctioning," and that all teams were directed to remain at the hangar until UFO reports from the field had ceased. At the subsequent briefing: "'They then told us what was happening,' he recalled, 'There had been some UFO activity that had been messing things up.'" Teams were specifically instructed: "If we saw a UFO while on the road, [we were to] report it. If we were at the site, [and saw a UFO] we were supposed to get into the silo and close the personnel hatch." (lines 3394–3406)

**The wing historian's account of document alteration.** Klotz located and interviewed David Gamble, the 341st Missile Wing historian who compiled the official unit history. Gamble confirmed that he had learned of UFO reports during the shutdowns but "received 'no cooperation' from those in-the-know" and stated that "written changes regarding 'the UFO aspect of the missile shutdown incident' had later been made by his superiors." The official history consequently states: "Rumors of Unidentified Flying Objects (UFO) around the area of Echo Flight during the time of the fault were disproven." Hastings notes that the engineering report ostensibly behind this finding "was never made available" to researchers. (lines 3227–3228)

## Contexts Within the Source

The 1967 Malmstrom incidents are presented in [[UFOs and Nukes (Hastings)]] as the capstone of a chapter-long escalation of UFO-nuclear encounters through the 1960s, progressing from sightings near missile silos at F.E. Warren AFB (August 1965), to a landed UFO at Ellsworth AFB (1966), to a pre-Malmstrom single-flight shutdown also at Malmstrom (early 1966, per researcher Ray Fowler). Hastings frames the Echo and Oscar shutdowns as the most operationally consequential instances yet documented of the pattern he describes throughout [[The nuclear-UFO connection (Hastings)]]: "If the President of the United States had ordered a nuclear strike against America's adversaries on March 16, 1967, the missiles of Echo Flight would have remained in their underground launch facilities." (line 3239)

Hastings also presents the incidents as revealing deliberate intent: "the missile shutdowns are important because, in addition to their impact on U.S. national security, they conceivably provide insight into the motivations of those operating the UFOs … these incidents may well represent an intentional effort to interfere with our ability to launch nuclear missiles." He and most of his sources "believe that the malfunctions were intentional, and intended to be demonstrations of some sort." (line 3482)

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hastings directly quotes the official Air Force position: the declassified 341st Strategic Missile Wing unit history states that "Rumors of Unidentified Flying Objects (UFO) around the area of Echo Flight during the time of the fault were disproven," attributing the failure to an undetermined internal electrical fault. The Air Force "remains entirely silent on the Oscar Flight shutdown … Officially, it never happened." (lines 3155–3157)

The Boeing engineers did succeed in reproducing the fault mechanism — injection of "noise" into the logic coupler — which constitutes a technically plausible terrestrial explanation for the malfunction pattern, though neither Boeing nor the Air Force were able to identify the source of such noise in the actual incident. Hastings acknowledges this finding while arguing that it does not eliminate the UFO-as-source hypothesis.

Former launch officer Salas acknowledges that he himself cannot recall the exact number of missiles that failed at Oscar Flight ("six to eight" in his published account; Jamison recalls a full ten-missile shutdown), and that the date of the Oscar Flight incident remains disputed between primary sources (March 16 vs. March 24). Researcher Jim Klotz remains cautious about conflating multiple possible events into one.
