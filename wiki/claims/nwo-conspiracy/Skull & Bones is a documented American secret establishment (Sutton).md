## Claim Statement

Sutton argues that Skull & Bones ("The Order," "Chapter 322"), founded at Yale in 1832, is a genuine secret society — not a mere club — whose members form an inter-generational "secret establishment" placed in commanding positions across American education, finance, law, foreign policy, and media.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

His case rests on an anonymous package containing "the membership list and supporting documents" of the society. He stresses that, unlike the CFR or Trilateral Commission, "Chapter 322 of The Order… is truly a secret society. Its purposes and membership are not disclosed. It is paranoid about secrecy." He documents the recurring elite families and individuals (Bush, Harriman, Stimson, Lovett, Taft, and the education "troika" of Gilman, White, and Dwight) and traces their placement in pivotal institutions.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sutton presents this as documentable from rosters and career records rather than speculation, and—unlike Webster—frames the bond as shared Hegelian-statist philosophy and class power, explicitly not ethnicity.
