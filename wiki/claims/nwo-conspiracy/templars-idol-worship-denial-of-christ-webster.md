## Claim Statement

Webster argues that the Knights Templar had secretly absorbed Eastern/Assassin heresy, and that the
charges at their 1307 suppression — denial of Christ, spitting on the cross, worship of a bearded
idol ("Baphomet"), and unnatural vice — were substantially true, transmitting a subversive current
into later secret societies.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

She lists the Inquisitor's five charges (initiation "accompanied by insults to the Cross, the denial
of Christ, and gross obscenities"; "the adoration of an idol… said to be the image of the true God";
omission of the words of consecration; lay absolution; "the authorization of unnatural vice"). She
stresses that the confessions of Jacques du Molay and many knights were echoed at the Pope's own
examination at Poitiers "conducted without torture or pressure of any kind," where the witnesses
"expressly and willingly approved" their statements — one describing the idol as "of such a terrible
aspect that it seemed to him to be the face of some devil, called in French un maufé." Citing Dr.
Bussell, she argues the Order had "long and important dealings" with the Assassins and was therefore
"suspected (not unfairly) of imbibing their precepts."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Webster acknowledges that many confessions "were made under torture" (which is "why it is more
important to consider" the untortured Poitiers testimony), that King Philippe le Bel had his own
motives, and that Pope Clement V long resisted believing the charges. She concedes the relations
between Templars and Assassins were "at first far from amicable."
