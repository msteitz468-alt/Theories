## Claim Statement

Webster argues that the visible 18th-century masonic orders were openly led by figureheads but
*secretly directed* by concealed chiefs — the "**Unknown Superiors**" — whose identity initiates
were sworn never to reveal. She presents this as the template for hidden direction running through
the whole secret-society tradition: "It is the unvarying rule of secret societies that the real
authors never show themselves."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the Baron von Hundt's *Stricte Observance* (founded 1751), she writes, "the official leaders…
were simply figureheads; the real leaders, known as the 'Unknown Superiors,' remained in the
background… exercising supreme jurisdiction over the Order." Von Hundt himself "admitted later that
he did not know the name of the lodge or chapter in which he was received, but that he was directed
from 'a hidden centre' and by Unknown Superiors." She quotes Mirabeau on the 1756 appearance of "men
sent, they said, by unknown superiors, and armed with powers to reform the order," and notes the
device "had been foreshadowed by the 'Invisibles' of seventeenth-century Rosicrucianism." She
applies the same logic to Weishaupt, suggesting he "was not in reality the sole author of
Illuminism, but one of a group."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Webster is cautious about identifying specific Unknown Superiors — she rejects Mirabeau's candidate
"Johnson" (an exposed "adventurer" who died imprisoned) as too lowly to have been one, and admits
"it is impossible to know" whether Illuminism's ideas arose in the inner lodge independently of
Weishaupt or were imparted by him.
