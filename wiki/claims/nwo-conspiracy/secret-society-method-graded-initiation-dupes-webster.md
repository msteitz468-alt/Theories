## Claim Statement

Webster argues that all subversive secret societies share one **method**: a system of *graded
initiation* in which low-degree members are taught one set of doctrines and high-degree initiates
the reverse, so that the mass of members work toward an aim known only to a hidden few. She traces
it to the Ismaili intriguer Abdullah ibn Maymun (c. A.D. 870) and says it became the model
Weishaupt copied for the Illuminati's "Insinuating Brothers."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quoting Dozy on Abdullah ibn Maymun's design — "To make tools of believers in order to give power
to skeptics… to build up a party, numerous, compact, and disciplined" — Webster writes: "I quote
this passage at length because it is of immense importance in throwing a light on the organization
of modern secret societies… the system remains the same — the setting in motion of a vast number of
people and making them work in a cause unknown to them." She reproduces the Fatimite Grand Lodge of
Cairo's nine-degree scheme, in which "in the last degrees the whole teaching of the first five was
reversed," and notes Claudio Jannet's claim that these methods "are absolutely those which
Weishaupt, the founder of the Illuminati, prescribed to the 'Insinuating Brothers.'" In Ch. IX she
matches de Sacy's description of the Ismaili graded recruitment to "the methods laid down by
Weishaupt… speaking sometimes in one way, sometimes in another, so that one's real purpose should
remain impenetrable."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Webster distinguishes "honest" from "dishonest" secret societies on exactly this point — she
concedes that obedience in religious or military orders (e.g. the Jesuits) is given by members who
*know* the goal, and reserves the charge of deception for orders that conceal the goal from their
own members.
