## Claim Statement

Webster presents Hasan Saba's order of **Assassins** (Hashishiyn, founded 1090 at Alamut) as the
prototype of organized political murder carried out through fanatical secret-society obedience —
"the supreme model on which all systems of organized murder working through fanaticism, such as
the Carbonari and the Irish Republican Brotherhood, were based," and a forerunner of the Jacobin
Terror.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

She describes the seven graded degrees (the "Old Man of the Mountain" at the top down to the
*Fadais* who "undertook to deliver the secret blow") and the hashish-and-garden deception used to
convince the *Fadai* he had glimpsed Paradise, so that he "devoted his life to murder." Quoting von
Hammer, she gives the order's secret maxim — "Nothing is true and all is allowed" — and draws the
explicit parallel: "as in the case of the French Revolution, 'whose first movers,' von Hammer also
observes, 'were the tools or leaders of secret societies,' it was… the method of enlisting numerous
dupes and placing weapons in their hands that brought about the 'Terror' of the Assassins six
centuries before that of their spiritual descendants, the Jacobins of 1793."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Webster notes the Assassins outwardly "proclaimed fidelity to Islam" and that their inner doctrine
was only confirmed when later Grand Masters (Hasan II, Jalal-ud-din) revealed it themselves — she
presents this as removing "all possible doubt," unlike the contested cases of the Templars and
Illuminati.
