## Claim Statement

Coleman asserts that the Club of Rome regards human population growth itself as the enemy, and adopts **deliberate population reduction "by whatever means"** — including euthanasia — as policy, viewing ordinary people as expendable "surplus population."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Coleman quotes [[Aurelio Peccei]], the COR founder, at a March 1982 Paris meeting: "Men are like insects. They proliferate too much." He summarizes the policy: "The COR's policy is fewer and fewer people, consuming less and less and requiring less services, by whatever means" — "a complete reversal of our society where more and more people demand better goods, services and life style."

He reports that a COR delegate referred to "the surplus population" and that the December 1980 conference "adopted the policy of euthanasia ... for getting rid of the growing population of elderly." He repeatedly invokes the French Revolution's Jacobin "murder of millions of 'surplus to requirements'" as the template, warning that "the equivalent of the Jacobin terror mobs will be unleashed upon" America.

## Contexts Within the Source

This "men are like insects" attitude is, for Coleman, the moral core of the COR — the contempt for ordinary humanity that justifies genocide and deindustrialization.

## Primary Source for Comparison

The Club of Rome's own report — [[The First Global Revolution (King & Schneider)]] — names "the population explosion in the South" a central concern (see [[Population growth and human environmental impact are the central concerns]]) and famously declares "the real enemy… is humanity itself." But in that primary text the authors call casting these dangers as "the enemy" a "trap" of "mistaking symptoms for causes," and explicitly deny advocating zero growth — a markedly more qualified statement than Coleman's "population reduction by whatever means."
