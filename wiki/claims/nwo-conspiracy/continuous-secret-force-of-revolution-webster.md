## Claim Statement

Webster's master thesis: a single, continuous "secret force of revolution" has operated through secret societies from the earliest times to the present, deliberately engineering subversion behind the visible events of history.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Following Robison and Barruel — and answering the charge that she is raising a "bogey" or "obsession" — Webster says her object is "tracing the course of revolutionary ideas through secret societies from the earliest times," relying "as far as possible on the documents and admissions of their members." Her key structural claim is the secret-society *method*: "It does not matter what the end may be, whether political, social, or religious, the system remains the same — the setting in motion of a vast number of people and making them work in a cause unknown to them." She maintains that without this hidden coordination the revolutionary "theory" would never have become "active revolution."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Webster frames the thesis as evidence-based ("it will not be for want of evidence") and concedes a generic "revolutionary spirit has always existed" apart from any one group — but treats the organized secret force as demonstrable.
