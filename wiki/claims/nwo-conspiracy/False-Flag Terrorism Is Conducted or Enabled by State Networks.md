## Claim Statement

David Icke asserts that modern terrorism — including attacks attributed to ISIS and al-Qaeda — is conducted by a combination of (a) manipulated or ignorant individuals with no knowledge of their real controllers, (b) persons under active mind control (MKUltra-type programming), and (c) direct covert operations by intelligence agencies that are then attributed to designated groups. Operation Gladio — a documented NATO/CIA post-WWII terrorist network — is cited as the proven precedent, with the testimony of convicted operative Vincenzo Vinciguerra establishing that civilian massacres were deliberately planned by intelligence services to generate public demand for greater state power. Dr Richard Day's 1969 prediction of engineered Western terrorism is cited as advance disclosure of the plan.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 14** opens: "Terrorism perpetrated by, or blamed upon, West-created psychopaths under names such as ISIS and al-Qaeda has been increasingly employed to terrify the public, generate ongoing fear and anxiety ... and justify the deletion of basic freedoms and privacy to 'protect the country from terrorism'."

Three categories of perpetrators: "carried out by a combination of mindless idiots with no idea who their real masters are; those under alter-compartment mind control; and covertly by psychopathic military intelligence networks which then blame ISIS and other groups and individuals."

Aurora, Colorado shooting (2012): Holmes described as "obviously under drug and mind control" based on his courtroom appearance. Witnesses reportedly described two shooting locations; a man in the front row opened the emergency exit from inside. "The authorities then named Holmes, a mind-controlled stooge."

British soldiers as Arab terrorists: "British soldiers dressed as Arabs were arrested by Iraqi authorities while driving a car carrying weapons and explosives. They were busted out of jail before they could be questioned when the moral British army sent six tanks and an elite SAS unit, held Iraqi police at gunpoint, demolished much of the jail and freed their own terrorists."

Operation Northwoods: described as US government plan to "embark on real or simulated actions against various US military and civilian targets."

Operation Gladio: "NATO and the CIA created a terrorist network after World War II based in Italy and expanding across Europe under the code name Operation Gladio." Vincenzo Vinciguerra (serving life sentence for Peteano car bomb, 1972) is quoted from trial testimony: "You had to attack civilians, the people, women, children, innocent people ... The reason was quite simple. They were supposed to force these people, the Italian public, to turn to the state to ask for greater security."

Vinciguerra further testified: organisations including "right wing terrorist groups Avanguardia Nazionale ... and Ordine Nuovo ... mobilised into the battle as part of an anti-communist strategy originating not with organisations deviant from the institutions of power, but from within the state itself."

Dr Richard Day (1969, Pittsburgh paediatricians): Terrorism would be used "widely in Europe and in other parts of the world ... a little bit of terrorism would help convince Americans that the world is indeed a dangerous place ... or can be if we don't relinquish control to the proper authorities."

Nice truck attack (Bastille Day 2016): French government's anti-terrorist executive (SDAT) told Nice urban surveillance authorities to "illegally destroy all 140 videos of CCTV footage." The stated reason was to prevent "uncontrolled circulation" and protect victims' families.

## Contexts Within the Source

Icke frames false-flag terrorism as a Problem-Reaction-Solution operation: state creates or enables the problem (terrorism), ensures the public reacts with fear and a demand for safety, then offers the pre-planned solution (expanded police state, deleted freedoms). He notes that post-terror attack responses always follow the same pattern: calls for more surveillance cameras, more troops on streets, more powers for police and intelligence agencies. France's ten thousand troops deployed after the 2015 attacks and UK's post-Manchester deployment in 2017 are cited as current examples.
