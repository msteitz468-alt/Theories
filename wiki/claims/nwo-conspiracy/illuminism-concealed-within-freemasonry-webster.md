## Claim Statement

Webster argues that after Weishaupt was initiated into Freemasonry (1777) he deliberately used the
masonic lodges as cover — an outer body within which the Illuminati operated as a concealed inner
circle, most members never knowing the inner system existed. She says "this plan of an inner secret
circle concealed within Freemasonry [persists] up to our own day."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

She quotes Weishaupt's own correspondence: that the Illuminati would have "a masonic lodge of our
own… we shall regard this as our nursery garden," that "to some of these Masons we shall not at once
reveal that we have something more than the Masons have," and that "all those who are not suited to
the work shall remain in the masonic Lodge and advance in that without knowing anything of the
further system." She cites Knigge ("Philo") boasting of capturing whole lodges, and argues Weishaupt
copied the Eastern graded-deception method (via "Kölmer," founded on Manichæism), modelling the
"Insinuating Brothers" on Abdullah ibn Maymun. Per Henri Martin, the Order's true end was "the
abolition of property, social authority, of nationality," masked as social reform.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Webster notes that the authenticity of the seized Illuminati papers "has never been denied even by
the Illuminati themselves" (Weishaupt only "endeavoured… to explain away" passages), but also
reports the contrary masonic view (Gould, the Masonic Cyclopædia) that the Illuminati "were, as a
rule, men of the strictest morality" whose ideas "have found universal acceptance" — a position she
rejects. She concedes it is "impossible to know" whether the ideas originated with Weishaupt or the
inner Lodge Theodore.
