## Claim Statement

Marrs presents the *Report from Iron Mountain* — a secret 1963–1966 study of the "problems of peace" reportedly commissioned by Kennedy-administration officials — as evidence that the elite regards war as a deliberate instrument of social and economic control. The report, he argues, treats war as "the basic social system" and "the principal organizing force" and "essential economic stabilizer" of modern society, and proposes engineered substitutes for war's social functions, reflecting "the elitist views of those who are said to have solicited the study."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Part II Marrs traces the study to a 1963 special group of fifteen unnamed historians, economists, sociologists and others, whose "principal meetings were at Iron Mountain, a huge underground corporate 'nuclear hideout' near Hudson, New York," tied to the CFR-linked Hudson Institute and to Rockefeller/Morgan corporate offices. He says a copy was leaked by "John Doe," a "Midwestern university professor who claimed to have been a participant," and published by Dial Press in 1967.

He quotes the report's conclusions: "War itself is the basic social system, within which other secondary modes of social organization conflict or conspire"; war as "the principal organizing force" and "the essential economic stabilizer of modern societies"; "The elimination of war implies the inevitable elimination of national sovereignty and the traditional nation-state"; and "The basic authority of a modern state over its people resides in its war powers." He reproduces the report's list of proposed "substitutes for the functions of war" (a comprehensive social-welfare program, an open-ended space program "aimed at unreachable targets," a permanent disarmament-inspection regime, an "omnipresent… international police force," "an established and recognized extraterrestrial menace," "fictitious alternate enemies," "a modern, sophisticated form of slavery," "new religions or other mythologies," "socially oriented blood games," and "a comprehensive program of applied eugenics"). He highlights the report's admission that an "alternative enemy… will have to be invented," and its proposal for a top-secret "War/Peace Research Agency" operating "outside the purview of Congress, the media and the public."

## Contexts Within the Source

Marrs frames the document as a "fingerprint" of conspiracy — an internal articulation of the elite mindset that, in his reading, explains otherwise "incomprehensible" wars (he immediately applies the same mindset to Vietnam and the Persian Gulf). He invites the reader to "compare the recommendations of this report with life in the United States today," matching each proposed substitute to a real present-day institution (UN peacekeeping, the welfare state, the space program, UFO/alien-abduction lore, environmental and eugenic programs).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Marrs notes directly that the report's authenticity is contested: "certain members of the government and media have attempted to brush it off as a joke or satire." He answers this within the text — arguing Dial Press published it "with no such disclaimers" and that its "serious and erudite tone" and "footnoted… macro-analytical approach belies the charge of fiction" — and ultimately hedges: "The Report from Iron Mountain, whether provable as historic fact or not, accurately reflects the thinking of secret society members." He also records that Leonard C. Lewin "arranged for the report's publication" and called it "an outrageous document."
