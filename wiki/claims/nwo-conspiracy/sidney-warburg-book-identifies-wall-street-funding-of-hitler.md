**Claim Statement**  
A suppressed 1933 book titled *De Geldbronnen van het Nationaal-Socialisme: Drie Gesprekken met Adolf Hitler* (Financial Sources of National Socialism: Three Conversations with Adolf Hitler), published under the pseudonym "Sidney Warburg," detailed a series of secret meetings in which New York international bankers (including representatives of Guaranty Trust) arranged over $30 million in funding for Adolf Hitler's Nazi Party between 1929 and 1933.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**  
According to *Wall Street and the Rise of Hitler* (Chapter Ten):
* **The Sidney Warburg Narrative:** Sutton summarizes the book's narrative, in which "Sidney Warburg" acts as a courier for New York bankers. He meets with Hitler three times: in 1929 (arranging $10 million in financing), in 1931 (arranging $15 million after the German banking collapse), and in 1933 (arranging a final $7 million wired via Rome and Düsseldorf).
* **The Reichstag Fire Connection:** During the final meeting on the night of the Reichstag fire in February 1933, Hitler requested 100 million marks. Warburg relayed the request, and Guaranty Trust offered $7 million.
* **Affidavit of James Paul Warburg:** Sutton reviews James Paul Warburg's 1949 notarized affidavit denying any connection to the pamphlet or the meetings. He notes that the book was quickly suppressed and almost all copies destroyed soon after publication.
* **Sutton's Analysis:** Sutton concludes that while the pamphlet contains numerous historical inaccuracies and is likely a political tract utilizing a pseudonym, the underlying claim—that New York bankers and their corporate cartels (like American I.G.) funded Hitler—is historically correct and verified by Nuremberg documents.

**Contexts Within the Source**  
Sutton examines the "Sidney Warburg" book to determine what is fact and what is myth. He argues that the book's suppression and the extreme steps taken to discredit it reflect the elite's desire to conceal the true financial origins of National Socialism, and that the story served as a garbled but essentially truthful leak of corporate-socialist backing of the Nazi Party.
