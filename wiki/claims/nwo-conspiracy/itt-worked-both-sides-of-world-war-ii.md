**Claim Statement**  
International Telephone and Telegraph (I.T.T.), directed by Sosthenes Behn, collaborated with the Nazi regime during World War II, funneling corporate funds to Heinrich Himmler's S.S. and manufacturing Focke-Wulf combat aircraft that were used to attack Allied forces.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**  
According to *Wall Street and the Rise of Hitler* (Chapters Five and Nine):
* **S.S. Funding via Schröder:** Kurt von Schröder (a partner in J. Henry Schroder bank and an S.S. Senior Group Leader) served as a director and financial conduit for I.T.T.'s German subsidiaries. Through Schröder, I.T.T. subsidiaries (Standard Elektrizitäts-Gesellschaft and Lorenz A.G.) deposited annual payments into Stein Bank's special "S" account, placing them at Heinrich Himmler's personal disposal to fund the S.S.
* **Focke-Wulf Aircraft Production:** Through its German subsidiary Lorenz A.G., I.T.T. acquired a 28 percent interest in Focke-Wulf Flugzeugbau A.G. in 1938. Consequently, an American-controlled multinational was direct owner of a major German military aircraft manufacturer during World War II.
* **Behn's Wartime Travel and Communication:** Sosthenes Behn was allowed by Allied authorities to travel to Europe during the war. He communicated with his German subsidiaries via Switzerland, coordinating corporate policy across enemy lines.
* **Gerhard Westrick's Role:** I.T.T.'s legal interests in Germany were managed by Dr. Gerhard Westrick, a partner in Schröder's law firm Albert & Westrick, who specialized in handling Wall Street reparations loans.

**Contexts Within the Source**  
Sutton presents this claim to demonstrate that multinational corporations operate on a level above nation-states. He highlights that I.T.T. was able to preserve its German assets, profit from German armaments, and continue its international operations while American soldiers were fighting the German military, exemplifying corporate-socialist collaboration.
