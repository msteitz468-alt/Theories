**Claim Statement**  
American multinational corporations operating through German affiliates, particularly German General Electric (A.E.G.), directly financed Adolf Hitler's final election campaign and consolidation of dictatorial power in February 1933.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**  
According to *Wall Street and the Rise of Hitler* (Chapters Three and Seven):
* **AEG Funding Transfer:** Sutton presents photographic evidence of the bank transfer slip showing that Allgemeine Elektrizitäts-Gesellschaft (A.E.G. or German General Electric) transferred 60,000 Reichsmarks on February 27, 1933, to Hjalmar Schacht's "National Trusteeship" account (*Nationale Treuhand*) at the Delbrück, Schickler Bank, which was utilized to finance Rudolf Hess and the Nazi Party's election expenses.
* **General Electric Interlock:** U.S. General Electric held a 25–30 percent interest in A.E.G. during this period. Four directors of A.E.G. were American corporate leaders, including Owen D. Young (chairman of U.S. General Electric and author of the Young Plan) and Gerard Swope (president of U.S. General Electric).
* **Other Corporate Contributors:** Sutton lists other prominent corporate contributors to the trusteeship account, including I.G. Farben (which contributed 400,000 RM, the largest single donation) and other cartels associated with Wall Street financial interlocks.

**Contexts Within the Source**  
Sutton presents this claim to refute the idea that Hitler's rise was a popular, underfunded, and purely German national phenomenon. He argues that the financial backing of Wall Street-connected corporate giants was decisive in giving the Nazi Party the resources needed to win the March 1933 election and establish a total dictatorship.
