## Claim Statement

Coleman asserts the Club of Rome's primary objective is **"the dissolution of the United States as an industrial and agricultural power"** — the deliberate dismantling of industry and technology via a **"Post-Industrial Zero-Growth"** policy, returning society to "a New Dark Age" under one-world government.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The COR objective is "the dissolution of the United States as an industrial and agricultural power" (Ch. 3); its meeting agenda was "how best to dismember the industrial heart of the United States." Coleman quotes Mitterand at the December 1980 COR meeting: "Industrial capitalist development is the enemy and the opposite of freedom" — which Coleman glosses as: industry gives people better lives and larger families, consuming more resources, so industry must be destroyed.

He builds Chapter 9 on [[Willis Harmon]]'s paper: "We must change the industrial technological image of man — fast ... the images of man that dominated the last two centuries will be inadequate for the postindustrial era." Coleman reads this as a plan for Americans, "enjoying too much freedom," to be "corralled and culled, so that the COR can curb industrial growth, and therefore population growth" — "a return to the Dark Ages." He ties the policy to [[Post-Industrial Zero-Growth]] and the doctrines of Malthus and Adam Smith.

## Contexts Within the Source

Deindustrialization is presented as inseparable from depopulation: shrinking industry is the mechanism for shrinking population.

## Primary Source for Comparison

The "zero growth" idea Coleman attributes to a deindustrialization plot traces to the Club's own commissioned report, [[The Limits to Growth (Meadows et al.)]]. That primary text frames the issue as ecological **overshoot** and explicitly disclaims the conspiratorial reading: the authors say they never advocated "zero growth," that a sustainable society "need not, and probably could not, be centrally controlled or authoritarian," and that the transition "is probably possible without reductions in either population or industrial output." See [[A deliberate transition to sustainability is possible — and need not be authoritarian or impoverishing]].
