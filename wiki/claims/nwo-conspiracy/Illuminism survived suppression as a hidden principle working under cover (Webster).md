## Claim Statement

Webster argues that the official suppression of the Bavarian Illuminati in 1786 did **not** end it:
"Illuminism in reality is less an Order than a principle," and a principle "can work better under
cover of something else." She claims the Order continued — through the German Union and the French
lodges — and that "twenty-five years later not only the Illuminati but Weishaupt himself were still
as active as ever behind the scenes in Freemasonry."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

She quotes Weishaupt's own precept that the work "could best be conducted 'under other names and
other occupations,'" and traces the cover forward to the Paris lodge of the **Amis Réunis**, which
"collected together the subversives from all other lodges" so that "the way was soon paved there for
Illuminism." She argues "illuminized Freemasonry made the French Revolution" (per Cadet de
Gassicourt, only ~27 real initiates among the lodges, "the rest… largely dupes"), and that the
Mirabeau "Projet de Révolution" was the first of a recurring series of "Protocols" — a Machiavellian
plan of "engineering revolution and using the people as a lever" — repeated, she says, through "the
Protocols of the Elders of Zion, and… the Russian Bolsheviks to-day."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Webster reports the opposing position squarely — "the opponents of Barruel and Robison therefore
declare that Illuminism came finally to an end" in 1786 — and stakes her counter-claim on
"documentary evidence" to follow. She also distinguishes loyal, non-illuminized Masons (who "rallied
to the royal cause") from the "illuminized" inner element she blames.
