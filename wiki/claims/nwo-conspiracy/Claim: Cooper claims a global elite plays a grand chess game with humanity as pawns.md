**Claim Statement**

Cooper asserts that a hidden global power elite deliberately orchestrates world events — wars, economies, politics — according to a long-range plan. Humanity collectively functions as pawns in this contest. The outcome is not accidental: "I cannot and will not accept the theory that long sequences of unrelated accidents determine world events."

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

From the Preface:

> "I believe, therefore, that a grand game of chess is being played on a level that we can barely imagine, and we are the pawns. Pawns are valuable only under certain circumstances and are frequently sacrificed to gain an advantage."

Cooper argues that the existence of such a conspiracy is not only plausible but practically inevitable given the nature of power:

> "It is inconceivable that those with power and wealth would not band together with a common bond, a common interest, and a long-range plan to decide and direct the future of the world. For those with the resources, to do otherwise would be totally irresponsible."

He frames the conspirators' purpose as winning "the game" above all else: "The power elite intend to win." He explicitly counsels readers to study chess strategy — specifically, to understand sacrifice — to comprehend how the elite think.

Cooper states his research shows "the future laid out for us may be just about impossible to change" without mass awakening, and that "nothing short of civil war will stop the planned outcome" if the people remain apathetic.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The chess metaphor functions as the book's governing analogy. Cooper uses it to explain why "it's not fair" is an irrelevant objection — the rules of the elite game do not accommodate fairness. This framing appears in the Preface before any specific claims are made, establishing the reader's interpretive frame for all subsequent material.

Cooper positions himself not as a theorist but as a former insider who encountered institutional evidence of cover-up and suppression across his military career.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**

Cooper acknowledges: "It is possible that one or more conclusions may be wrong." He does not, however, qualify the core thesis.
