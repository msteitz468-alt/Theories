## Claim Statement

Marrs argues that the Bolshevik Revolution was not "a popular uprising of the downtrodden masses" but a movement planned and funded by Western financiers in Germany, Britain, and the United States — part of the same "Trading with the Enemy"/finance-both-sides pattern by which, he contends, the secret-society network profited from and controlled the upheavals of the twentieth century.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the "Russian Revolution" section of Part II, Marrs writes there is "a wealth of documentation indicating that the Russian Revolution — indeed the very creation of Communism — sprang from Western conspiracies beginning even before World War I." Drawing on Griffin, Still, Icke, Gary Allen and others, he asserts:

- Leon Trotsky was in New York in January 1917 and "soon discovered that there were wealthy Wall Street bankers who were willing to finance a revolution in Russia"; he left in March 1917 "along with nearly three hundred revolutionaries and funds provided by Wall Street," and after being detained in Halifax was released on an American passport at the urging of Wilson's aide Colonel House.
- Jacob Schiff (Kuhn, Loeb) reportedly "sank about $20 million for the final triumph of Bolshevism," and CFR member Elihu Root "contributed yet another $20 million" (citing the Congressional Record of Sept. 2, 1919); Arsène de Goulévitch claimed "over 21 million roubles were spent by Lord Milner in financing the Russian Revolution."
- The 1915 American International Corporation was "formed to fund the Russian Revolution," with directors representing Rockefeller, Rothschild, Du Pont, Kuhn-Loeb, Harriman and Federal Reserve interests (Frank Vanderlip; George Herbert Walker).
- Lenin was sent through Germany on the "sealed train… along with at least $5 million," arranged by Max Warburg and the German High Command.

He quotes Gary Allen's logic that if "the world's richest and most powerful men" financed a movement and "do not fear it, it must be because they control it," and cites Winston Churchill's 1920 article on a "worldwide conspiracy for the overthrow of civilization." Marrs reads the episode through the Hegelian dialectic: capitalists created Communism as a "thesis" enemy to generate markets, arms, and a managed "synthesis."

## Contexts Within the Source

This is one of the Part II "fingerprints" demonstrating his thesis that the hidden network engineers both sides of conflicts for profit and control. He explicitly links it to "Trading with the Enemy" — US and Allied financing of opposing regimes — and to the same banking families (Morgan, Warburg, Schiff, Rockefeller, Harriman, Milner) he tracks elsewhere in the book.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Marrs records that the financiers' backing of an avowedly anti-capitalist movement "mystified conspiracy researchers for years," and that Icke saw a "multidimensional" purpose (getting Russia out of WWI to Germany's benefit while manufacturing the "bogeyman" of Communism). He notes the claim rests on relayed sources (Griffin, Still, Icke, Allen, de Goulévitch) and on quoted figures rather than primary records he reproduces himself.
