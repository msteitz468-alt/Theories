## Claim Statement

Coleman claims the **1973 oil embargo / energy crisis was an artificially created event** — a "phony oil shortage" tied to the 1973 Arab–Israeli war — used by the Club of Rome to convert the public to its anti-industrial, "limits to growth" thinking and to expand COR influence over governments.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 12 Coleman writes that Aurelio Peccei "used the artificially-created oil embargo of the 1973 Arab-Israeli war, as a warning. He said this brought 'many people into alignment with Club of Rome thinking'" — an "entry point for many people to break with former ways of thinking and to take the Club of Rome's advice much more seriously." Coleman reads Peccei's remark as a confession: "Here you have a man openly admitting that the 1973 Arab-Israeli war was a contrived situation of a phony oil shortage in the world and by so doing, convinced more people that smaller is better ... and that industrial progress had to be curbed."

He adds that the embargo seemed to validate "the Forrester-Meadows reports" (the *Limits to Growth* studies), and that "During the period 1973-74, the Club of Rome's influence over the policies of many governments expanded dramatically" — citing Queen Juliana of the Netherlands ordering "an exhibition of the ideas of the Club of Rome" in Rotterdam.

## Contexts Within the Source

The episode exemplifies Coleman's "nothing happens by accident" framing: a manufactured scarcity crisis is used as propaganda to advance the COR's deliberate de-industrialization and zero-growth agenda.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The "admission" is Coleman's interpretation of Peccei's claim that the embargo "brought many people into alignment with Club of Rome thinking"; Coleman supplies no independent evidence that the embargo was contrived rather than a consequence of the 1973 war.
