## Claim Statement

The legislation that became the Federal Reserve Act was secretly drafted in November 1910 by a small group of New York and international bankers, together with Senator Nelson Aldrich, at the J. P. Morgan-owned Jekyl Island Hunt Club in Georgia — not, as publicized, written in Congress by Carter Glass and Woodrow Wilson.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins opens the book with the November 22, 1910 departure in a sealed railway car from Hoboken, the use of first names only, and nine days' drafting work. He treats participants' own accounts as confirmation:
- Vanderlip (*From Farmboy to Financier*): "Our secret expedition to Jekyl Island was the occasion of the actual conception of what eventually became the Federal Reserve System. The essential points of the Aldrich Plan were all contained in the Federal Reserve Act as it was passed."
- Warburg's memorandum: "The matter of a uniform discount rate was discussed and settled at Jekyl Island."
- J. Laurence Laughlin's history names the attendees (Aldrich, Davison, Warburg, Vanderlip, Norton) and the two-week secret meeting.

The Preface notes that the birthplace, Jekyl Island, bears no commemorative plaque — "a triumph for the ... publicists of the Federal Reserve Board, who have perpetuated the comfortable fiction that the Act was born in the halls of Congress."

## Contexts Within the Source

The claim is the narrative spine of the book; the secrecy is presented as proof that the Act served the bankers rather than the public.
