## Claim Statement

The Commission for Relief in Belgium, run by Herbert Hoover and Emile Francqui, was in reality a mechanism to keep Germany supplied with food during World War I — prolonging the war — and the nurse Edith Cavell was executed because she discovered and reported it.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins (Ch. 7), citing John Hamill's *The Strange Career of Mr. Hoover*: "That is what the Belgian Relief Committee was organized for — to keep Germany in food." He cites Schmoller's Yearbook (1916) for large quantities of meat, potatoes, bread, and butter "shipped from Belgium to Germany," and a German newspaper crediting the American Relief Committee's "continuous relations" for solving Germany's provisioning problem.

Per the source, nurse Edith Cavell wrote to the *Nursing Mirror* (April 1915) complaining the relief supplies were feeding the German army; Sir William Wiseman (head of British Intelligence and a Kuhn Loeb partner) "secretly notified the Germans that Miss Cavell must be executed," and the Germans "bowed to Sir William Wiseman's demands," shooting her and creating "one of the principal martyrs of the First World War." Mullins notes Hamill's book "was systematically located and destroyed by government agents" before Hoover's re-election campaign.
