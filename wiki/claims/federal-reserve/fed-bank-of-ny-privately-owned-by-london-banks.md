## Claim Statement

The Federal Reserve Bank of New York — which Mullins argues actually sets U.S. monetary policy — was, from 1914, majority-owned by three private New York banks (National City Bank, First National Bank, National Bank of Commerce) whose principal stockholders all had direct ties to London banking houses.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins (Ch. 6) gives 1914 shareholding detail: National City Bank (James Stillman owning 47,498 of 250,000 shares; Rockefeller-linked) bought ~10% of the new Federal Reserve Bank of New York; First National Bank ("the Morgan Bank," George F. Baker holding 20,000 of 100,000 shares); and National Bank of Commerce (controlled by J. P. Morgan via Equitable Life and Mutual Life, with Paul Warburg and Jacob Schiff also holding shares).

He traces each to London: National City via Stillman (whose father was "a British agent and blockade runner"), First National via Baker (whose family intermarried with London's St. George and with the Schiffs), and National Bank of Commerce as "clearly controlled by Morgan, who was really a subsidiary of Junius S. Morgan Company in London and the N.M. Rothschild Company of London." He quotes Congressman McFadden (1930): "The Morgan control of the Federal Reserve System is exercised through control of the management of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York."
