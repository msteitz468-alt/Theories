## Claim Statement

Paul Warburg coined the designation "Federal Reserve System" specifically to avoid the term "central bank" and thereby evade the long American tradition of opposition to central banking, even though the institution would function as a central bank in all essential respects.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins (Ch. 1): "The main problem, so Paul Warburg informed his colleagues, was to avoid the name of 'Central Bank', and for that reason he had come upon the designation of 'Federal Reserve System.' This would allay suspicion in the popular mind that the bill was a central bank plan. However, it would still function as a central bank, fulfilling the three main functions in that tradition."

The source enumerates those three functions: private ownership with profit from the money issue; command of the nation's financial resources; and the ability to "mobilize credit and mortgage the United States by involving us in major foreign wars."

The source places the anti-central-bank tradition in a lineage from Jefferson vs. Hamilton (First Bank) through Andrew Jackson vs. Nicholas Biddle (Second Bank), arguing the disguise was needed to overcome that history.

## Contexts Within the Source

Presented as one of several deliberate deceptions (the others being the regional structure and the Aldrich/Wilson public framing).
