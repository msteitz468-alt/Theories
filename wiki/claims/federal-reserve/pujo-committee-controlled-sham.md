## Claim Statement

The 1912 Pujo Committee "Money Trust" investigation was a controlled performance designed not to expose the bankers but to convince the public a money monopoly existed — so that the Federal Reserve Act could then be sold as the remedy. Its Special Counsel, Samuel Untermyer, protected his own class.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins (Ch. 3): the hearings "dragged on for five months" and filled "four volumes of some six thousand pages," yet "the bankers were asked few questions which were embarrassing, and nothing was brought out which the public might have been excited by." The eleven congressmen "did not ask a dozen questions apiece." LaFollette and Lindbergh, who forced the inquiry, "were not asked to testify or aid."

The source notes Untermyer was "one of the main contributors to Woodrow Wilson's campaign fund" and let Jacob Schiff and J. P. Morgan testify without disclosing anything damaging (Morgan: "Money is a commodity"). It concludes the public was convinced a monopoly existed, "but the bankers and their subsidized newspapers claimed that the only way to break that monopoly was to enact the ... Federal Reserve Act."

## Contexts Within the Source

Presents the hearings as the publicity that prepared the ground for the Act, with Untermyer as a co-author of the bill it enabled.
