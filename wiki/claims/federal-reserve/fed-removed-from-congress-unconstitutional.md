## Claim Statement

Because the Federal Reserve is a bank of monetary issue but was deliberately placed beyond Congressional control through appointive offices, it is unconstitutional: the Constitution (Article 1, Section 8, Paragraph 5) vests the power to coin money and regulate its value in Congress, which cannot validly be delegated to a private, unelected body.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins (Ch. 1): Aldrich insisted "the officials should be entirely appointive offices, and that Congress should not have anything whatever to do with them." The source: "Removal of the System from Congressional control and supervision made the entire Federal Reserve proposition unconstitutional from its inception, because the Federal Reserve would be a bank of monetary issue, and Congress is expressly charged in the Constitution with the issuance of money."

It quotes Article 1, Section 8, Para. 5 ("CONGRESS SHALL HAVE THE POWER TO COIN MONEY AND REGULATE THE VALUE THEREOF") and argues enactment meant the legislative department "would lose its sovereignty." A footnote notes the Supreme Court (McCulloch v. Maryland) upheld delegation as "necessary and proper" / "convenient," which Mullins derides.

The source closes the book restating it: had Congress "refused to let Woodrow Wilson and Carter Glass hand over the sovereign right of coinage ... the American people today would not stand on the brink of slavery."
