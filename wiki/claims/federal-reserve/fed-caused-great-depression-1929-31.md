## Claim Statement

The Federal Reserve deliberately produced the Great Depression of 1929–31: after a secret 1927 conference with European central bankers, it pursued cheap money and open-market purchases that fueled the stock boom and exported ~$500 million in gold to Europe; then, after Montagu Norman's February 1929 visit to Mellon, abruptly reversed to high rates to collapse the market — wiping out ~$160 billion in security values while big bankers exited in advance.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins (Ch. 11) cites the House Stabilization Hearings of 1928 and quotes Governor Adolph Miller acknowledging the 1927 easy-money policy was undertaken to help Europe and that it "stabilized the European currencies, and upset ours" — "Yes, that was what it was intended to do." The source quotes Miller again (Senate, 1931): "If we had had no Federal Reserve System, I do not think we would have had as bad a speculative situation as we had."

It states Paul Warburg gave the exit signal in his March 1929 report; that the New York Fed raised its rate to 6% on Aug. 9, 1929; and that the Crash from October wiped out "a hundred and sixty billion dollars worth of security values." A *Newsweek* item (1936) is cited describing a Board member who "in 1929 called a meeting and ordered his banks to close out all security loans by September 1," riding through "with flying colors."

## Contexts Within the Source

Second deliberate depression; tied to the [[Event: Federal Reserve–European central bank conference (1927)]] and the gold-standard/League of Nations thesis.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Mullins notes economists who blamed only the 1927 easy-money policy; he counters (via Goldenweiser) that credit had been pyramiding since 1923–24, so the cause was sustained, not a single year.
