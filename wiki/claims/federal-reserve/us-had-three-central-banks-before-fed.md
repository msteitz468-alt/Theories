## Claim Statement

The Federal Reserve was America's fourth central bank. It was preceded by three earlier fractional-reserve central banks — the Bank of North America (1781), the First Bank of the United States (1791), and the Second Bank of the United States (1816) — each modeled on the Bank of England, each created to lend money to government, and each producing inflation before being abolished.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Griffin (Chs. 16–17 summaries):
- **Bank of North America** (chartered 1781 by the Continental Congress): "Modeled after the Bank of England ... authorized to issue more paper promissory notes than it held in deposits.... riddled with fraud," its charter allowed to expire in 1783.
- **First Bank of the United States** (1791): "a replica of the first, including fraud," with "the largest investment and the most powerful influence ... from the Rothschilds in Europe"; in five years inflation "confiscated 42% of everything people had saved." Its charter was defeated by one vote in each house in 1811.
- **Second Bank of the United States** (1816): "almost an exact carbon copy," whose nationwide reach brought "the age of the boom-bust cycle" to America. Destroyed by President [[Andrew Jackson]] in the [[Event: The Jackson–Biddle Bank War (1832–1836)]] after Bank head [[Nicholas Biddle]] deliberately contracted credit to trigger a panic.

Griffin presents this history (Section IV, "A Tale of Three Banks") as the precedent the Founders' "lost treasure map" of sound money was meant to prevent.
