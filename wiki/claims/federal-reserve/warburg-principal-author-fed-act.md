## Claim Statement

Paul Warburg of Kuhn, Loeb & Co. — not Carter Glass or Woodrow Wilson — was the actual author of the Federal Reserve Act, having designed the central-bank plan at Jekyll Island and shaped its passage through his contacts.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins marshals multiple quoted authorities:
- Prof. E. R. A. Seligman (Columbia): "The Federal Reserve Act is the work of Mr. Warburg more than of any other man in the country."
- Colonel Ely Garrison: "Paul Warburg is the man who got the Federal Reserve Act together after the Aldrich Plan aroused such nationwide resentment ... The mastermind of both plans was Baron Alfred Rothschild of London."
- S. E. Harris (*Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 1931) and J. Laurence Laughlin both credit Warburg's decisive influence.
- The source notes Warburg collected his writings in a 2,500-page volume on the System that "proved conclusively that it all originated in his banker's brain," and that Untermyer privately called Carter Glass's rival authorship claim a "bald misstatement."

Mullins states the principal writers of the administration bill were H. Parker Willis, Paul Warburg, and Samuel Untermyer, with "all the important provisions" stemming from Warburg's Jekyll Island work.

## Contexts Within the Source

Used to argue the Act's true purpose was a banker's central bank, and that public credit for Wilson/Glass is propaganda.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The source acknowledges Carter Glass claimed authorship in *Adventure in Constructive Finance*; Mullins rejects this, citing Untermyer and Warburg's own record.
