## Claim Statement

The Federal Reserve functions as "lender of last resort," creating money out of nothing to bail out large failing debtors and banks; the cost is passed to the public as inflation. Congress is persuaded to guarantee loans by warnings of economic catastrophe, transferring the burden from the banks' ledgers to the taxpayer.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Griffin (Chs. 2–3) describes "the game called Bailout" with examples: Penn Central (1970, $125M guarantees), Lockheed (1970, $250M), New York City (1975, $2.3B), Chrysler (1978, $1.5B), Commonwealth Bank of Detroit (1972), First Pennsylvania Bank (1979), and Continental Illinois (1982/84 — FDIC "assumed $4.5 billion in bad loans and ... took 80% ownership," so "the bank was nationalized, but no one called it that").

He summarizes: "All of the money to accomplish these bailouts was made possible by the Federal Reserve System acting as the 'lender of last resort.' ... the money is created out of nothing, resulting in the confiscation of our nation's wealth through the hidden tax called inflation." On the FDIC: "not insurance, because the presence of 'moral hazard' makes the thing it supposedly protects against more likely to happen."

## Contexts Within the Source

Presents bailout as one of the Fed's original design purposes (cartel objective 4: shift losses to taxpayers). The international version runs "from the Federal Reserve through the IMF/World Bank."
