## Claim Statement

The American financial panics of 1837 and 1857 were deliberately engineered by the Bank of England (synonymous, per Mullins, with Nathan Rothschild) to contract U.S. credit and ruin investors — enabling London-allied firms such as George Peabody & Co. (the future House of Morgan) to buy distressed securities cheaply and profit.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins (Ch. 5), citing Henry Clews: the Panic of 1837 followed Andrew Jackson's withdrawal of government funds after the Second Bank's charter expired; the country prospered and "the government paid off the entire national debt," but "The Panic of 1837 was aggravated by the Bank of England when it in one day threw out all the paper connected with the United States" — refusing U.S. securities "to create an immediate financial panic ... [and] ruin those seeking to turn United States securities into cash."

For 1857, Mullins recounts that the Bank of England advanced George Peabody & Co. £1 million during the panic (while ~900 other firms failed), letting Peabody and Junius Morgan "purchase such bonds as possessed real value freely, and then resold them at a large advance when sanity was restored" (quoting Matthew Josephson). The source states James de Rothschild of Paris was "the principal investor" in the Second Bank of the United States, and that the Rothschild firm became U.S. State Department financial agent in 1835.
