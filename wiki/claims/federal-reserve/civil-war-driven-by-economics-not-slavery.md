## Claim Statement

The American Civil War was fought primarily over economic interests — tariffs, trade, banking monopolies, and European territorial ambitions — rather than slavery; slavery was "but a ploy" added later to turn the war into a moral crusade. The war was financed through fiat money (greenbacks and the National Banking Act in the North; printing presses in the South), confiscating wealth via inflation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Griffin (Chs. 18–19 summaries): "America's bloodiest and most devastating war was fought, not over the issue of freedom versus slavery, but because of clashing economic interests. At the heart of this conflict were questions of legalized plunder, banking monopolies, and even European territorial expansion into Latin America." Northern protectionist tariffs forced the South to buy from the North at higher prices; "it was a classic case of legalized plunder, and the South wanted out."

Per the source, European powers wanted America split (France eyeing Mexico, England poised on the Canadian border); Tsar Alexander II's dispatch of his fleets to New York and San Francisco helped deter Anglo-French intervention. War finance: in the North "a national banking system was created to convert government bonds into fiat money, and the people lost over half of their monetary assets" to inflation; in the South, "the monetary loss was total." Griffin links Lincoln's assassination (Knights of the Golden Circle) and Tsar Alexander II's assassination (People's Will) to secret societies with "rumored ties" to financiers including [[Jacob Schiff]].
