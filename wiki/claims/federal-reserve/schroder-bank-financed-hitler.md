## Claim Statement

The J. Henry Schroder Bank, through a January 4, 1933 meeting in Berlin, provided the financing that rescued a debt-ridden Hitler and enabled his assumption of power — motivated by the prospect that a Second World War "would double their profits." The Dulles brothers, whose firm represented Schroder, were present.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins (Ch. 7) quotes Otto Lehmann-Russbeldt: "Hitler was invited to a meeting at the Schroder Bank in Berlin on January 4, 1933. The leading industrialists and bankers of Germany tided Hitler over his financial difficulties ... In return, he promised to break the power of the trade unions." Present were John Foster Dulles and [[Allen Dulles]] of Sullivan & Cromwell, "which represented the Schroder Bank"; Allen Dulles "later became a director of J. Henry Schroder Company."

Mullins: "if Hitler did not become Chancellor of Germany, there was little likelihood of getting a Second World War going, the war which would double their profits." He quotes the Great Soviet Encyclopaedia: "The banking house Schroder Bros. (it was Hitler's banker)." He also ties in the Hoover Moratorium on German war debts as designed "so that Germany would have funds for rearming."

**Extraction note:** A contested historical claim recorded as the source's assertion, without endorsement. (Some elements overlap themes in Antony Sutton's separate work on Wall Street and Hitler.)
