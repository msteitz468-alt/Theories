## Claim Statement

J. P. Morgan & Co., as sales agent and purchasing agent for Allied war bonds and materials, profited from both ends of WWI financing; when German submarine success threatened Allied defeat and Morgan's bond cash flow, Morgan engineered a pro-war media campaign and (with Colonel House and Wilson) worked to bring the US into the war to protect those interests.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Griffin (Ch. 12 summary): England and France "had selected the House of Morgan as sales agent for their bonds. Morgan also acted as their U.S. purchasing agent for war materials, thus profiting from both ends of the cash flow." As the Allies neared defeat, bonds became unsellable and "the Morgan consortium would suffer gigantic losses" on default. "The only way to save the British Empire, to restore the value of the bonds, and to sustain the Morgan cash flow was for the United States government to provide the money," requiring US entry.

Griffin states "a secret agreement to that effect was made between British officials and Colonel House, with the concurrence of the President," even as Wilson campaigned on "He kept us out of war"; and that "Morgan purchased control over major segments of the news media and engineered a nation-wide editorial blitz against Germany." Within days of the declaration, Congress voted $1 billion in Allied credit, $200 million applied to the Morgan account.
