## Claim Statement

The Federal Reserve System enabled the United States to lend the Allies some $25 billion and committed the country to enter World War I — chiefly, the source argues, to protect the overextended loans of J. P. Morgan, Kuhn-Loeb, and the Rothschilds rather than for any genuine national interest.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins (Ch. 8): before 1914 the US was a debtor nation that "did not feel capable of mortgaging itself to make national loans"; the System changed this. "The System forced the American people to lend the Allies twenty-five billion dollars which was not repaid, except in interest to the New York bankers, and it also committed us to make war against the German people."

He quotes Ambassador Walter Hines Page's confidential March 5, 1917 letter to Wilson urging US credit/entry to sustain the Morgan agency for Britain and France, and argues the Lusitania carried Dodge munitions. The first Liberty Loan of $400 million, per the source, repaid a J. P. Morgan British loan. Wilson is quoted admitting "The World War was a matter of economic rivalry" and that the US would have entered regardless.
