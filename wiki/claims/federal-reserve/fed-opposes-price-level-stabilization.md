## Claim Statement

Despite promises in 1913 that it would stabilize the dollar, the Federal Reserve has consistently opposed any stabilization of the price level — because instability and fluctuation are themselves sources of profit for the speculators and bankers the System serves.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mullins quotes the Board's official memorandum (March 13, 1939): "The Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System opposes any bill which proposes a stable price level," and Eccles's 1939 letter to Senator Wagner opposing Senate Bill No. 31 "or any other legislation of this general character." The fuller 1939 memorandum is quoted opposing a stable price level on the grounds that prices "do not depend primarily on the price or cost of money."

The source recounts that Senator Robert L. Owen's stabilization provision was struck from the 1913 Act by Glass's committee, and that the Strong Bill of 1928 (to empower the System to stabilize the dollar's purchasing power) was defeated. Mullins notes Eccles's contradictory 1935 statement that the government's control of gold "thus largely regulat[es] the price structure" ("Liars should have good memories").

## Contexts Within the Source

Supports the thesis that the System profits from the very instability it was publicly chartered to prevent.
