## Claim Statement

On May 1, 1776, Adam Weishaupt, a professor of canon law at the University of Ingolstadt, founded the secret order first called "The Order of Perfectibilists," soon renamed the Order of the Bavarian Illuminati. Beginning with five members, it sought to abolish monarchical government and state religion and return society to "humanistic rational thinking." It grew rapidly — to ~2,000–3,000 members by 1784 — especially after the 1780 recruitment of [[Adolph Knigge]], who built the higher grades and fused the order with Freemasonry.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Marrs recounts Weishaupt (Jesuit-trained, converted to Protestantism) modeling the order on both the Jesuit and Masonic structures, taking the code name "**Spartacus**," with his right-hand man Franz Xaver von Zwack ("**Cato**"). Knigge created the ruling "Areopagites" council; the two later quarreled, and Knigge resigned in 1784. By 1788, Marrs says, "nearly all Masonic lodges in Europe… had been infiltrated by men who subscribed to Illuminism."
