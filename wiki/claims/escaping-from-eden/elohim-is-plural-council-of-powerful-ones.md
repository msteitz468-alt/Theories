## Claim Statement

Wallis argues that the Hebrew word *elohim*, conventionally translated "God," is grammatically a **plural** noun whose root meaning is "powers" or "powerful ones," and that in the oldest sources of Genesis it denoted a literal plurality of physical beings — a "Council" of "Powerful Ones" — not a singular deity. He contends the singular "God" reading was imposed later by a redactor ("J") who inserted the divine name Yahweh into older elohim-stories, producing the moral incoherences that read as "anomalies."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Wallis catalogs the textual "anomalies": the plural noun *elohim* (Gen 1:1); "Let us make human beings… in the image and likeness of ourselves" (1:26); "the human has become like one of us" (3:22); "let us go down and confuse their language" (11:7); plural verbs with *elohim* (Gen 20:13; 35:7). He points to the recurring "heavenly council" (Psalm 82:1; 89:6–8; 1 Kings 22:19–23; Job 1:6) and argues that in Genesis 1 *elohim* doesn't *have* a council — *elohim* **is** the council. He notes the redaction-critical scholarship (Astruc, Graf, Wellhausen) on the "J" editor, and that the very presence of the post-Moses name Yahweh shows "we are not reading the original version of Genesis."
