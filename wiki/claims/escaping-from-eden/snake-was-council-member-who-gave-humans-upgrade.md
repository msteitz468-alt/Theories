## Claim Statement

Wallis argues the "Snake" of Genesis 3 is not a serpent but an intelligent, speaking, non-human/non-divine being — one of the Powerful Ones ("elohim-kind"). Rather than a tempter, the Snake **gives humans a beneficial "upgrade"**: a higher consciousness bringing moral awareness, self-awareness, and sexuality — a collaboration in which "the female of the species takes the lead." For this unauthorized act, the Snake is exiled to Earth.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Wallis notes the Snake "arrives on the scene without any kind of introduction," is clearly intelligent and can "speak human," and that Revelation later correlates "the great dragon… the serpent from the beginning" with "the accuser," who in Job 1:6 appears among the *benei elohim* (the Council). He frames the eating of the tree of knowledge as a genuine elevation (why would a good God forbid moral awareness?), and reads the Council's internal **war** — those siding with the Snake "outnumbered two to one and exiled on Earth" — as symbolized by the Snake's banishment "to eat the other Powerful Ones' dust."

## Contexts Within the Source

A consequence of [[Genesis's "elohim" is a plural noun for a council of physical "Powerful Ones"]]. The benevolent-rebel-serpent motif resonates with Bramley's [[The Brotherhood of the Snake]] and the Custodial reading of Eden.
