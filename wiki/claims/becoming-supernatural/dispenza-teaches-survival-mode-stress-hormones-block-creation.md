## Claim Statement

Dispenza teaches that when people chronically react to stressful circumstances — real or remembered — the hormones of stress (adrenaline, cortisol) lock the brain into high-range incoherent beta brain waves, addict the body to familiar stress-chemistry, direct all available creative energy outward to the known material world, down-regulate healthy gene expression, and make it physiologically impossible to access the present moment or create a new future. He describes this as "living in the past" — the primary obstacle to becoming supernatural.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Chapter 1 establishes the mechanism through Anna's case: "When the fight-or-flight nervous system is switched on and stays on because of chronic stress, the body utilizes all its energy reserves to deal with the constant threat it perceives from the outer environment. Therefore, the body has no energy left in its inner environment for growth and repair, compromising the immune system."

Chapter 2 extends this into a full model:
- Thoughts produce biochemical reactions; repeated thoughts produce repeated feelings; feelings drive more of the same thoughts — a "thinking-feeling loop" that hardwires the brain and conditions the body into the past.
- Emotions are "the chemical residue of past experiences"; if a person can't think greater than how they feel, they are locked into thinking from the past.
- The body emits an electromagnetic field equal to the person's state of being; repeating the same state re-creates the same life.
- In survival mode, the brain fires in a "disordered, incoherent pattern" because the person narrowly shifts attention between known people, places, and things, compartmentalizing the brain and making it work inefficiently.
- The body becomes addicted to stress hormones, craving them the way an addict craves a drug. Simply changing the external environment (getting a new job, moving to Hawaii) does not break this chemical addiction.
- All of a person's creative energy is "bonded" to known elements of their outer world; with no energy remaining in the inner world, no new creation is possible.

He uses the analogy of atoms bonded by a shared field: "Just like two atoms that bond together to form a molecule — which share energy and information — when two people share the same emotions and energy, they become bonded together as well." Breaking these emotional bonds, he says, requires the energy of meditation and overcoming the body's resistance.

## Contexts Within the Source

This framework appears as the core problem statement that the rest of the book's methods are designed to address. It recurs in Chapters 3, 4, 5, and beyond.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Dispenza presents the stress-hormone model in a broadly accepted neuroscientific frame (fight-or-flight, HPA axis) but extends it into contested territory by claiming that:
(a) the "electromagnetic field" around the body can be physically measured with GDV equipment not accepted by US scientific bodies;
(b) gene expression changes follow emotional-state changes within days;
(c) bodily addiction to emotions functions identically to chemical drug addiction.
He does not cite peer-reviewed literature for these specific claims.
