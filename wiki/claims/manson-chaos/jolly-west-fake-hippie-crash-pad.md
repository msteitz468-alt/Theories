## Claim Statement

O'Neill identifies Dr. Louis Jolyon ("Jolly") West — an MKULTRA-funded psychiatrist named by the New York Times as one of seven scientists who secretly participated in MKULTRA under academic cover — as keeping an office at David Smith's Haight-Ashbury clinic in 1967, where he set up a fake "hippie crash pad" (the "Haight-Ashbury Project") staffed by grad students posing as hippies to "lure" and observe youth. O'Neill argues "What is a hippie?" was a cover for West's real interest: LSD, hypnosis, and the inducement of "abnormal states."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

O'Neill marshals West's record: deprogramming Korean War POWs (entrée to the intelligence community); papers warning of violent "LSD cults" and arguing (against prevailing science, in 1965's "Dangers of Hypnosis") that people could be hypnotized into violating their moral codes and killing, with no memory; his examination of **Jack Ruby** alone in his cell, after which Ruby appeared "psychotic" before the Warren Commission; and an Oklahoma project, "Mass Conversion," whose funds, O'Neill found, "came from Sidney J. Gottlieb, the head of the CIA's MKULTRA program." West's crash pad was bankrolled by the "Foundations Fund for Research in Psychiatry," which O'Neill concluded was a CIA front. He notes West denied MKULTRA involvement until his death (an assisted suicide) in 1999, and that O'Neill was the first to examine West's donated UCLA papers.
