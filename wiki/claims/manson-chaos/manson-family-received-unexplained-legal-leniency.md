## Claim Statement

O'Neill documents a "pattern of catch-and-release" in which Manson and his followers repeatedly escaped serious legal consequences in the period before the murders — arrests dropped, probation reinstated against probation officers' recommendations, parole violations ignored — a leniency he found "always helped along by a hand from the outside," frequently connected to Manson's parole officer, Roger Smith.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Examples: Manson (on federal parole) and four others arrested in June 1969 were released within 24 hours. Susan Atkins had her probation **terminated early** by two different judges in two states (Oregon 1968; Mendocino 1969) over the explicit objections of probation officers who warned she was a danger — decisions officials called "very strange" and unlike anything in 32 years on the job. After Manson's girls were jailed in Ukiah (1968), Roger Smith and his wife drove up, took custody of Manson's infant son, and submitted "glowing appraisals" urging leniency — while concealing that Smith's last parole client was Manson himself, the man who had sent the women there. A probation officer (David Mandel) later told O'Neill: "Of course it should have been disclosed. It's a huge conflict of interest!" O'Neill: "as long as she stayed on his side, it seemed the rules didn't apply."
