## Claim Statement

A remarkable concentration of the Wall Street interests involved in supporting the Bolsheviks operated from a single address — 120 Broadway, New York City — which housed the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, the Morgan-controlled American International Corporation, Guaranty Securities, and the Bankers Club, among others.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sutton (Ch. XI): "This activity in behalf of the Bolsheviks originated in large part from a single address: 120 Broadway, New York City." He lists: the Federal Reserve Bank of New York; American International Corporation ("the vehicle for this pro-Bolshevik activity"); Guaranty Securities Co. (with Guaranty Trust next door at 140); Weinberg & Posner (where first Soviet ambassador Ludwig Martens had been vice president); Hunt, Hill & Betts; John MacGregor Grant Co. (financed by Aschberg and Guaranty Trust, on the Military Intelligence black list); the Guggenheims; the executive heart of General Electric; and the Bankers Club "on the top floor (the thirty-fourth)."

Sutton connects this to Quigley: 120 Broadway "appears to be the foreign counterpart of Carroll Quigley's claim that J.P. Morgan infiltrated the domestic left. Morgan also infiltrated the international left."

## Contexts Within the Source

The geographic spine of Sutton's "alliance of bankers and revolution"; physically links the Federal Reserve Bank of New York to the pro-Bolshevik corporate network.
