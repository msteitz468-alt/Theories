## Claim Statement

Sutton documents that President Woodrow Wilson personally facilitated Leon Trotsky's return to
Russia in 1917 by providing him a U.S. passport — "the fairy godmother who provided Trotsky with a
passport to return to Russia to 'carry forward' the revolution" — even as State Department
bureaucrats were trying to tighten passport controls against exactly such revolutionaries. En route,
Trotsky was removed from the S.S. *Kristianiafjord* by British/Canadian naval personnel at Halifax
and briefly interned, then released.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sutton quotes Jennings C. Wise: "Historians must never forget that Woodrow Wilson, despite the
efforts of the British police, made it possible for Leon Trotsky to enter Russia with an American
passport." He notes Trotsky left New York on March 26, 1917 carrying that passport and **$10,000**
of "supposed German" origin (per the 1919 Senate Overman Committee testimony of Col. Hurban, which
Sutton observes was cut short "before the source of Trotsky's funds could be placed into the Senate
record"). Per declassified Canadian archives, Trotsky was taken off at Halifax on April 3, 1917,
listed as a German prisoner of war, and interned at Amherst, Nova Scotia, before being released to
continue to Petrograd. Sutton ties the voyage to financier **Charles R. Crane** (a Wilson backer
with a "known role in the 'first' revolution") who sailed with the American Communist Lincoln
Steffens.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sutton is careful: he says the Crane–Trotsky pattern is "puzzling" and "suggests, though without at
this point providing evidence, some link," and reports Trotsky's funds as *officially* of German
origin while promising to develop the New York financing evidence later — he does not assert a
single proven paymaster here.
