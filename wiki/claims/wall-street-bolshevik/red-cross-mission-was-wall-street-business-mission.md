## Claim Statement

Sutton argues that the August 1917 American Red Cross Mission to Russia "had only a nominal
relationship with the American Red Cross" and was in reality a **Wall Street financial and political
mission** — privately funded by financier William Boyce Thompson and staffed mostly by New York
lawyers and financiers given military titles, not medical personnel.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He records that "all expenses, including those of the uniforms… were paid out of the pocket of
William Boyce Thompson," and that the all-officer group ("about forty Colonels, Majors, Captains and
Lieutenants… no privates") was dubbed the "**Haytian Army**" by a contemporary observer who noted
it "come to fill no clearly defined mission" while Russia already had "a surplus of medical talent."
Of the 24 actual members, Sutton tabulates that **only five were doctors**; the majority were "made
up of lawyers, financiers, and their assistants, from the New York financial district" — Andrews
(Liggett & Myers Tobacco), Barr (Chase National Bank), Thacher (Simpson, Thacher & Bartlett),
Thompson himself ("Commissioner and Business Manager; Director United States Federal [Reserve] Bank
of New York"), and others. The nominal medical head, Dr. Frank Billings, "was reported to be
disgusted with the overtly political activities of the majority of the mission." It is the same
Thompson who, Sutton documents, [[Federal Reserve Bank of New York director William Boyce Thompson gave $1 million to the Bolsheviks]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sutton names the genuine physicians and researchers who did serve (Billings, Thayer, Winslow, Post,
etc.), acknowledging the mission was not *wholly* a front; his point is the *dominance* of financial
over medical personnel and purpose, documented from the mission's own roster.
