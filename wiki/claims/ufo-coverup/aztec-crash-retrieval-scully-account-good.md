## Claim Statement

In his crash-retrieval chapter Good recounts the **Aztec, New Mexico recovery** popularized by columnist **Frank Scully** in his 1950 best-seller *Behind the Flying Saucers*: the alleged 1948 landing of a 99.99-foot disc near Aztec from which sixteen (per a later source, fourteen) small charred humanoid bodies and the intact craft were recovered and taken to Wright Field. Good notes the story is "widely dismissed … as a hoax," that Scully's principal source "Dr Gee" was in fact a **composite of eight scientists**, and he presents the account — and William Steinman's 1986 elaboration of it — **with explicit qualification rather than endorsement**, since the corroborating sources remain undisclosed.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Good (Ch. 16, "Down to Earth," subsection "The Aztec Recovery, 1948") writes that "the story sounds ridiculous and is widely dismissed (with the others) as a hoax perpetrated on Scully, and has been the butt of endless jokes ever since. But there is a great deal more to this particular story than has been hitherto supposed." He clarifies that "the mysterious 'Dr Gee' … was in fact a composite character of eight scientists … a convenient literary device as well as a means of protecting his sources," and relays Scully's details: a disc of metal resembling aluminium that resisted 10,000° heat and diamond drilling, entry gained through a fractured porthole, and sixteen humanoids 36–42 inches tall found dead inside.

He then presents William Steinman's 1986 account (Aztec disc detected by radar on 25 March 1948; recovery by the Army's "Interplanetary Phenomenon Unit"; a scientific team under Vannevar Bush including Oppenheimer, von Neumann, and others; fourteen bodies). Good repeatedly flags the evidentiary weakness: Steinman, "like Scully, … is unwilling to divulge his sources, which inevitably lays him open to charges of fabrication," and he frames the whole crash-retrieval question through aerospace scientist Dr. Pierre Guérin's caution that recovered-UFO stories "have the ring of truth about them" but "the material proofs alleged to exist remain concealed by the authorities."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter is, in the source page's words, "the most contested material in the book." Good treats Aztec as one of several alleged recoveries (he cites Leonard Stringfield's collection of "at least forty such accounts" and the better-documented Roswell case) within his larger documentary argument, supporting [[Government UFO secrecy is worldwide and documented]] while keeping crash-retrieval claims at arm's length.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The qualification is the point: Good notes the Aztec story "has been discounted as a hoax" and that investigators "have never been able to authenticate it," that both Scully's and Steinman's key sources are anonymous, and (via Guérin) that no one outside the highest intelligence levels "is capable of knowing for certain whether … there do exist material, concrete (and therefore irrefutable) proofs of UFOs as such."
