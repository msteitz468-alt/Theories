## Claim Statement

[[Richard M. Dolan]] documents in [[UFOs and the National Security State Vol. 2 (Dolan)]] that from late October through November 1975, a wave of unidentified aerial objects intruded repeatedly into restricted airspace over Strategic Air Command (SAC) nuclear weapons bases across the northern United States and Canada. The bases affected included Loring AFB (Maine), Wurtsmith AFB (Michigan), Malmstrom AFB (Montana), Minot AFB (North Dakota), Offut AFB (Nebraska), and the NORAD installation at Falconbridge (Ontario). Dolan argues these intrusions constituted a genuine military crisis: jet interceptors were scrambled, Sabotage Alert Teams refused orders, missile targeting computers were apparently tampered with, and NORAD went to top alert status — yet no object was ever successfully identified or apprehended. The same wave of events is also documented by [[UFOs and Nukes (Hastings)]].

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Dolan's account rests substantially on partially declassified Defense Department memoranda obtained through FOIA, supplemented by researcher interviews and NORAD documents. He establishes context early, noting that "throughout 1975 and well into 1976, UFOs were seen over many military bases" and that "the U.S. nuclear arsenal appeared to be a target of interest, and there was little the Air Force could do about it."

**Loring AFB (October 27–31, 1975).** Dolan describes the first confirmed case: on October 27, the base control tower tracked a low-flying craft that penetrated the perimeter and came within 300 yards of the nuclear weapons area at 150 feet altitude. The base commander, Colonel Richard E. Chapman, brought the base to major alert status and requested jet interceptors from two NORAD regions — both of which denied the request. The object appeared red and orange, described by a B-52 crew chief as resembling "a stretched-out football," hovering "without movement or noise" and appearing "solid." The encounter lasted at minimum seven hours. A Defense Department memo quoted by Dolan stated: "It is our opinion that the unknown helicopter has demonstrated a clear intent in the weapons storage area, is smart and a most capable aviator. Local security has been increased. We anticipate further incidents." The object was never identified.

**Wurtsmith AFB (October 30, 1975).** A silent intruder was tracked near the weapons storage area. A KC-135 tanker crew was vectored to intercept and gave chase for 35 miles; the crew found they were in "a cat-and-mouse game." When they increased speed to close the distance, "the object simply pulled away." A crew member later told the base historian: "I know this might sound crazy, but I would estimate that the UFO sped away from us doing approximately 1,000 knots" — more than 1,000 mph. The crew was cautioned "not to discuss the incident."

**Malmstrom AFB (November 7–8, 1975).** Dolan describes what he considers the most dramatic episode of the wave. At approximately 3 p.m. on November 7, electronic sensors detected a security violation at missile site K-7. A Sabotage Alert Team (SAT) dispatched to investigate reported by radio "a brightly glowing orange disc, as large as a football field, hovering over the missile site." The men refused orders to proceed to the site. The object rose to 1,000 feet, registered on NORAD radar, and F-106 interceptors were scrambled — but the object climbed to 200,000 feet (more than double the ceiling of the U-2 spy plane) and disappeared. The missile at K-7 "showed indications that its computer targeting system had been tampered with, and it had to be removed." Six other Launch Control Centers reported UFOs the same day. The following night, UFOs returned; ground witnesses observed a cat-and-mouse pattern in which objects "turned off their lights and became invisible as the intercepting jets approached, and turned their lights back on after the jets departed."

**Minot AFB and Falconbridge (November 10–11, 1975).** At Minot, a "bright, silent object" buzzed the base at 1,000–2,000 feet; three personnel notified the National Command Center. At Falconbridge — a NORAD installation with nuclear weapons — civilians, police, military, and radar all tracked a UFO. NORAD Captain Gordon Hilchie openly confirmed: "yes, we saw this so-called UFO at the same time people outside were saying they saw it, too." The object climbed from 26,000 to 45,000 to 72,000 feet. The base commanding officer, Major Robert Oliver, stated: "I classified the UFO report going to Colorado Springs as secret."

**NORAD's response.** NORAD Commander General Daniel James issued a November 11 memo to units throughout North America about "numerous reports of suspicious objects" at Loring, Wurtsmith, Malmstrom, Minot, and Falconbridge, acknowledging that "to date efforts by Air Guard helicopters, SAC helicopters and NORAD F-106s have failed to produce a positive ID." Dolan notes the General's concern about preventing "overreaction by the public to reports by the media that may be blown out of proportion" — while UFOs penetrated nuclear weapons facilities. Dolan adds that Malmstrom officials later informed researchers that "all documentation had been destroyed in accordance with Air Force directives."

The same episode is covered in detail in [[UFOs and Nukes (Hastings)]], which draws on extensive interviews with military witnesses to this and other nuclear-site UFO events. See also [[Hastings: UFOs have repeatedly surveilled U.S. nuclear weapons sites]].

## Contexts Within the Source

This wave of events is presented as the dramatic climax of Chapters 1–2 of the book. Dolan frames it as evidence for several interlocking arguments: that UFOs exhibited deliberate interest in nuclear weapons; that the U.S. military took the incidents with the utmost gravity while publicly minimizing them; and that the news media — national wire services, television networks — failed or declined to cover a story of obvious national importance. He writes: "The American news media, particularly at the national levels, failed to report these astonishing and dramatic events. One is left only with two possibilities: news media incompetence or complicity with national security groups."

Dolan sets earlier context: back in February 1975, near Harlowton, Montana, officials had already observed a bright object hovering over the "Kilo One" ICBM base; and in September 1975, "UFOs described as huge orange or red glowing discs were being seen over Minuteman missile sites at Malmstrom." The October–November wave was thus not isolated but a spike within a sustained pattern.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Dolan acknowledges the official counter-explanations throughout. Military public affairs officers offered "temperature inversions," misidentified aircraft, and "helicopter" as explanations. NORAD General James himself suggested "many of these sightings were probably helicopters" while admitting none had been identified. The Canadian National Research Council later attributed the Ontario sightings to "layers of crystallized ice in the atmosphere." Dolan treats these explanations with open skepticism — noting, for instance, that no helicopter of 1975 was capable of silent hovering, 1,000-mph acceleration, or climbing to 200,000 feet — but he documents them. He also notes that the overall documentation is almost certainly incomplete: "we only have the tip of the probable iceberg, at least in terms of documentation."
