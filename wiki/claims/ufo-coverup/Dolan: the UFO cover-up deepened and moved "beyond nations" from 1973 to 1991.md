## Claim Statement

[[Richard M. Dolan]] argues in [[UFOs and the National Security State Vol. 2 (Dolan)]] that the UFO phenomenon is genuine and "not us" — the product of some intelligence other than humanity's official civilization — and that it has been actively covered up by elements of the human power structure since at least the 1940s. Over the period 1973–1991, Dolan contends, that cover-up deepened and evolved: the secret-keepers appear to have grown "beyond nations," with control drifting from (theoretically accountable) government and military officials toward private and transnational entities. The U.S. presidency, he argues, may no longer be the final word on matters of UFO secrecy.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Dolan states the overarching thesis in the Introduction with considerable directness. The phenomenon, he writes, is "something most assuredly 'not us,'" and is simultaneously "a reality that is actively being covered up. It is a phenomenon so significant that elements of the human power structure have made it their priority to keep information about it strictly to themselves, apparently at all cost." On the identity of those responsible, he adds: "As to the identity of these secret-keepers, many sources culled from several decades of leaks, as well as broad geopolitical trends, make a reasonable case that they are 'beyond' nations. The U.S. power structure, while still integral to the system of secrecy, may not be the final word as it once was."

Dolan grounds this transnational drift in the broader history of globalization he traces in the same Introduction. Multinational structures — the Bilderberg Group, the Council on Foreign Relations, the Trilateral Commission, and entities such as the UN, World Bank, and IMF — weakened traditional national sovereignty during the 1970s and 1980s. Regarding the UFO secret specifically, he writes: "Control over key components of the UFO secret have drifted further and further away from (theoretically responsible) government and military officials, and increasingly toward private entities. This transfer of power is reflected in a number of statements from insiders, as well as by more generalized studies of the Pentagon's system of 'Special Access Programs,' the prototypical black budget programs." He describes the result as "a silent, transnational revolution during the late 1980s and early 1990s."

On the societal consequences of the cover-up, Dolan argues: "It means that our society has lived in an 'official' reality so incomplete, so inaccurate, that we may with justice call it fictitious. It means that the history we have learned, the science we think we know, and the very core of who we think we are, need to be rediscovered."

Dolan also notes a key dynamic within his own period of study: that the Freedom of Information Act released thousands of pages of government UFO records, which "disproved the longstanding denial of interest in UFOs by the CIA, FBI, and military agencies," yet the truly sensitive material — anything classified above "Confidential" — was never released. He cites Senator Barry Goldwater's 1975 letter confirming that UFO material at Wright-Patterson AFB was "above Top Secret," and that even Goldwater was denied access by USAF Chief of Staff General Curtis LeMay.

## Contexts Within the Source

This thesis forms the explicit conceptual frame of the entire volume. Dolan introduces it in the "The Problem," "Main Themes," and "The Story" subsections of the Introduction (lines 311–403). The cover-up deepening into private and transnational hands is the interpretive lens through which he reads events of the 1970s and 1980s — from the FOIA battles and disinformation campaigns to the emergence of the "black budget." The argument connects directly to concepts explored in the companion volume [[UFOs and the National Security State (Dolan)]] and to the broader framework of [[The national security state (Dolan)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Dolan acknowledges significant epistemic limits throughout. He notes that government documents that were released under FOIA were "of mid- to low-levels of classification," meaning the most sensitive material was not available and the true inner workings of the cover-up remain unknown: "Regarding the topic of UFOs, in which national security issues come into play, accompanied by official silence, disinformation, and outright lies, any history will always be incomplete." He also distinguishes carefully between what is established and what is inferred: "At all times, we must distinguish between what we know and what things look like." His claim about transnational secret-keeping is presented as a "reasonable case" from circumstantial evidence and leaks, not as proven fact.
