## Claim Statement

Good's culminating chapter centers on the **National Security Agency**. He recounts how, through the **Citizens Against UFO Secrecy (CAUS)** group, lawyer **Peter Gersten** sued the NSA under the Freedom of Information Act and forced it to admit holding **239 UFO-related documents** — while it released almost none. **Judge Gerhard Gesell** dismissed the suit after reviewing in camera a top-secret NSA affidavit (by Eugene Yeates) classified at **"Top Secret Umbra,"** the highest classification for signals-intelligence documents. Good argues this proves the UFO subject is classified at the very highest levels of the US government.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Good (Ch. 17) reports that eighteen NSA UFO documents first surfaced during CAUS's litigation against the CIA; Gersten's FOIA requests were refused under "5 US Code, Section 552 (b)(1) … in the interests of national security." "Later that year NSA representative Eugene Yeates admitted in a court hearing that the NSA had found a total of 239 documents on UFOs that were relevant to the FOIA request." Gersten then sued the NSA on CAUS's behalf in the District Court, Washington DC, in spring 1980; "Judge Gesell studied a twenty-one-page NSA affidavit in camera and ruled that the agency was fully justified in withholding the documents in their entirety," finding "the public interest in disclosure is far outweighed by the sensitive nature of the materials and the obvious effect on national security."

Good stresses the classification level: "The in camera affidavit … was itself classified at a level above top secret – Top Secret Umbra, the highest classification for SIGINT documents at the time," from which "it may … be safely inferred that the subject matter of the NSA affidavit is of the utmost intelligence sensitivity." He notes the censored copy the NSA sent him in 1983 was almost entirely blacked out — "Of 502 lines in the affidavit, 412 are totally or partially deleted" — and that an appeals panel upheld Gesell "virtually without comment" after the judges (and the US defense attorney, but not Gersten) received special clearances to view the affidavit. He cites one document the NSA *did* release, *UFO Hypothesis and Survival Questions*, as evidence the agency engaged the subject substantively.

## Contexts Within the Source

This chapter is the book's title and thesis made concrete: that UFO information is held "above top secret." It is the strongest documentary instance of Good's organizing claim [[Government UFO secrecy is worldwide and documented]] and of [[High-level officials privately affirm a UFO cover-up while denying it publicly]]; it builds on the parallel CIA FOIA battles (Ch. 14) waged by the same litigants.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Good registers his own reservation: while professing himself "all in favour of the NSA protecting its intelligence-acquiring capability," he writes that he finds "it difficult to accept that the deleted portions relate exclusively to these matters," and asks "why did the NSA not produce any of the documents for the judge to examine, as is customary in a dispute on whether material is properly classified?" — leaving open that the redactions might protect SIGINT methods rather than UFO content.
