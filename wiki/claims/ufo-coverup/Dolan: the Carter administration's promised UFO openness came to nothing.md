## Claim Statement

Dolan argues in Chapter 3 that President Jimmy Carter arrived at the White House with the most UFO-friendly disposition of any modern U.S. president, yet every concrete initiative to open the subject — from his own inquiries to a proposed NASA study to Alfred Webre's White House-backed research project — was quietly killed by the national security bureaucracy. The Carter era ended, Dolan writes, with "the Carter UFO initiative … dead" and "the various efforts to create an institutional foundation for UFO research had all failed." The episode illustrates, in Dolan's view, the degree to which the secrecy apparatus described by [[The national security state (Dolan)]] had become autonomous: even a sitting president could not penetrate it.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Dolan reconstructs the failure of Carter-era openness through a series of interlocking episodes, each grounded in documentary evidence or named testimony.

**The CIA brush-off.** Attorney Daniel Sheehan reports that after Carter's election, CIA Director George Bush was asked about UFO information. Bush replied that "this was information that existed on a need to know basis only. Simple curiosity on the part of the President wasn't adequate." According to Sheehan, Bush further conditioned handing over CIA UFO material on Carter retaining Bush as Director of Central Intelligence — an offer Carter refused (lines 1664–1666).

**Carter's motive for replacing Bush.** Researcher Stanton Friedman later spoke briefly with Carter, who "indicated that the reason he chose his CIA director (Stansfield Turner) was that they were Annapolis classmates and therefore he thought that he could be trusted to tell him the truth … the implication was that other DCIs had not informed their presidents fully" (line 1666).

**The White House inquiry goes nowhere.** After inauguration, Carter's science advisor Frank Press and press secretary Jody Powell inquired with the CIA and Pentagon whether they were withholding UFO documents; both agencies said no. Press also learned that "military security worked on a 'need to know' basis. If military censors determined that the White House did not have a need to know, the President would not be told" (line 1682).

**The Marcia Smith/Library of Congress episode.** Sheehan also claims that in January 1977, the Congressional Research Service was asked to study extraterrestrial intelligence for the Carter administration, and that he was granted access to what appeared to be "classified sections of the Project Blue Book" held in a secured vault at the Library of Congress Madison Building. There he found "a series of 'unmistakable' photographs of a classic flying saucer embedded in snow and surrounded by Air Force personnel wearing parkas" (lines 1702–1710). Two classified reports that resulted — one on extraterrestrial intelligence, one on UFOs — were said to have gone to President Carter.

**The NASA stone-wall.** When Press formally wrote to NASA Administrator Robert Frosch on July 21, 1977, asking NASA to become the "focal point for the UFO question" and convene a small panel of scientists, Frosch and NASA resisted. Two weeks before Press's letter, Colonel Charles Senn, Chief of USAF Community Relations Division, had already written to Lt. General Duward Crow of NASA: "I sincerely hope that you are successful in preventing a reopening of UFO investigations" (line 1770). NASA ultimately "grudgingly took on additional letter-answering duties" but went no further. The White House accepted this, and on December 27, 1977, issued a press release accepting "NASA's evaluation of the situation" without pushing for an actual investigative body (line 1798).

**The Webre project cancelled.** Alfred Webre, a Senior Policy Analyst at Stanford Research Institute, developed a three-phase "extraterrestrial communication" research proposal with informal White House backing. In September 1977, the project was abruptly terminated. Webre later stated that a Pentagon liaison had warned SRI that its contracts would be cut if the study went forward, because "there are no UFOs." Dolan adds that "whether Webre's proposal was shut down by the Pentagon or by SRI, it is evident that the White House itself was never committed to it" (lines 1772–1776). Webre himself concluded that his proposal "was nipped in the bud by the U.S. National Security State" (line 1774).

**Shirley MacLaine's account of Carter's private views.** On the Larry King Show in 1995, actress Shirley MacLaine described Carter's private statements to her: "he told me many times … that it was true, that there were crafts, that he believed there were occupants … He wanted to shine the sunshine laws on intelligence, to expose it, to see how the people would react, but he didn't and wouldn't and couldn't as he explained to me" (lines 1738–1740). Dolan regards this statement as "extremely specific, especially one made by a former President," and notes that MacLaine confirmed to him personally that Carter had discussed UFOs with her "in very serious terms."

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 3 opens the period Dolan calls "Great Expectations," in which external conditions — a new president, a revitalized FOIA regime, widespread public and congressional interest — created unprecedented hope for UFO openness. Each section of the chapter documents the collapse of that hope through the actions of the agencies that collectively constitute [[The national security state (Dolan)]]: the CIA, NSA, Pentagon, NASA, and USAF all acted to contain the issue. Dolan explicitly frames this as evidence that the national security apparatus had long since become autonomous from elected political control on this specific subject. The chapter is thus a case study in the same structural argument made across [[UFOs and the National Security State Vol. 2 (Dolan)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Dolan does not discount the possibility that Carter himself lacked the will to press the matter. He notes that the UFO "pledge" never made it into Carter's 214-page official compilation of campaign pledges, and that Carter's science advisor Frank Press openly stated he was "not particularly interested in UFOs." Dolan concludes: "If the White House had truly wanted this project, one assumes it would have happened" — placing at least partial responsibility on Carter's own administration rather than on secrecy forces alone (line 1776). The Webre episode in particular is complicated by SRI administrator Peter Schwartz's denial that the Pentagon threatened funding: "There were people at SRI who were unhappy about anything controversial that might bring negative attention to the institute, but we were never threatened by DoD" (line 1774). Dolan acknowledges the uncertainty but maintains the outcome — cancellation — is not in dispute.
