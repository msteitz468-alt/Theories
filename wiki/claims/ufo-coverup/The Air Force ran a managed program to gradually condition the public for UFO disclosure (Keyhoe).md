## Claim Statement

Beyond the bare charge that the Air Force was concealing the saucers, Keyhoe advances a more specific thesis: the Air Force's seemingly contradictory public statements were not mere bungling but **part of a deliberate, top-directed program to prepare the American public — gradually and without panic — for an eventual official disclosure of the UFO reality** (a "managed leak"). On this reading, alternating denials and hints, controlled releases of case summaries, and tolerated articles (including his own) were instruments of a phased public-education policy.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Keyhoe states the thesis at the outset: the contradictory Air Force statements "are part of an intricate program to prepare America—and the world—for the secret of the disks" (Author's Note). Of Cmdr. McLaughlin's unusually candid White Sands account being allowed into print, he asks whether it was "an incredible slip-up" or "part of an elaborate program to prepare the American people for a dramatic disclosure" (Ch. 5). On finishing the *True* article he tells editor Purdy: "I'm absolutely convinced now that there's an official policy to let the thing leak out," which "explains why Forrestal announced our Earth Satellite Vehicle program, years before we could even start to build it" (Ch. 16).

His Chapter 20 summary makes the policy explicit and dated: "During the spring of 1949 this policy, which had been strictly maintained by Forrestal, underwent an abrupt change. On top-level orders, it was decided to let the facts gradually leak out, in order to prepare the American people." The *True* article "had been considered in line with the general education program," but the public's unexpected reaction "was mistaken by the Air Force for hysteria, resulting in their hasty denial that the saucers existed." He expects the pattern to persist: "we may continue for a long time to see routine denials alternating with new suggestions of interplanetary travel."

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the more charitable half of Keyhoe's secrecy argument: he reads the concealment "partly as a deliberate 'Air Force policy' of cautiously preparing the public against panic rather than pure malice," repeatedly invoking the *War of the Worlds* / Orson Welles panic as the danger to be avoided. It is the structural complement to his harder claim [[The Air Force is concealing the reality of flying saucers (Keyhoe)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Keyhoe holds the "managed leak" reading in tension with the evidence of active suppression he documents elsewhere — pressured witnesses, refused files, the burned/withheld case summaries — and concedes the public's calm response to the *True* article was *not* what a panic-fearing Air Force had predicted, which itself complicates the thesis of a smoothly managed program.
