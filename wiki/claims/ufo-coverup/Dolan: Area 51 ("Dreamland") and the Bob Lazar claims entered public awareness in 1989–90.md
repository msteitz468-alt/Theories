## Claim Statement

Dolan documents that in 1989 journalist George Knapp of KLAS-TV Las Vegas aired interviews with a physicist — later revealed to be Robert Lazar — who claimed to have worked at a classified sector of the Groom Lake (Area 51) facility called S-4, where the U.S. government was attempting to reverse-engineer nine extraterrestrial flying discs. Dolan treats this as "the dam breaker" that turned Area 51 from a virtually unknown facility into a household name and a focal point for UFO investigators through the 1990s. He presents Lazar's account evenhandedly — recording both the corroboration obtained by Knapp and Lazar's many supporters, and the significant credibility problems that investigators could not resolve.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Dolan traces the first public mention of "Area 51" to the 1988 television special *UFO Cover-Up? Live!*, on which unidentified sources (later identified as Richard Doty and Robert Collins) stated that the alien base in Nevada was "in a place called 'Area 51' or 'Dreamland.' (This was the first time Area 51 was ever mentioned on television.)"

The core of the chapter concerns Lazar. Dolan summarizes Lazar's account at length: Lazar claimed to have been recruited via Edward Teller, flown to Groom Lake, bussed to the adjacent S-4 facility near Papoose dry lake, and assigned to a project called "Galileo" dealing with "gravity and propulsion." Inside the facility he said he saw nine flying saucers: "It bore a striking resemblance to the 'perfect' UFOs allegedly photographed by Billy Meier. Lazar had an ominous feeling, 'almost … that you shouldn't be there.'" The craft he examined was described as: "almost as if it were fashioned out of wax and then slightly melted." Lazar said the fuel was the super-heavy Element 115, and that the craft used three gravity amplifiers to "distort the surrounding gravitational field" and bend space-time.

Lazar subsequently took John Lear and others to view test flights from public Bureau of Land Management land on three consecutive Wednesdays in March–April 1989. When his security clearance was revoked after one of these excursions, he went public with Knapp in May 1989. Knapp's November 10, 1989 broadcasts — the first to identify Lazar by name — became a sensation.

Dolan's assessment: "Whatever the ultimate truth behind Lazar's story, it was the dam breaker. After Lazar, throughout most of the 1990s, Area 51 drew interested people to its perimeter, armed with cameras and video equipment. The most secret place in the world suddenly became the star attraction of conspiracy hunters everywhere."

Knapp went on to speak with "perhaps twenty Area 51 insiders" who corroborated various parts of Lazar's claims. DSP satellite expert Ron Regehr, who knew Lazar throughout the 1980s, told Dolan that Lazar was "one hundred percent" authentic. Dolan also records that a Lockheed engineer told investigator James Goodall: "Absolutely, positively without a doubt, they exist" — referring to UFOs — and that a master sergeant with three Groom Lake tours said "the United States government and the military has things out there — you can't describe them as airplanes — that are literally out of this world."

## Contexts Within the Source

The Lazar story anchors Ch. 8's argument that by 1989–90 the UFO secrecy structure was under unprecedented pressure from multiple directions simultaneously: Lazar's public claims, the Bill Moore confession about the Bennewitz disinformation campaign, the Belgian Air Force's public acknowledgment of extraordinary triangular craft, and the Soviet Union's own openness about UFO encounters. Dolan links the Lazar narrative to his broader contention that a genuine reverse-engineering program existed at S-4, supported independently by the 1988 "Alien Reproduction Vehicle" demonstration described by Brad Sorensen at a Lockheed Palmdale exhibit.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Dolan devotes considerable space to problems with Lazar's account. No records of his claimed attendance at MIT or Cal Tech were ever found. Los Alamos National Laboratory initially denied he had worked there, though Knapp subsequently found his name in a LANL phone book and a 1982 newspaper article. Lazar's polygraph test was "inconclusive." His memory of "large blocks of time was also fuzzy." Stanton Friedman, Tom Mahood, Glen Campbell, and John Alexander each investigated Lazar's claims and "generally conclu[ded] they were fraudulent." Dolan also raises the possibility that Lazar may have been "sized up" and "deemed to be a useful conduit for the release of … disinformation" — specifically, that the story of Element 115 as essential fuel could be a deliberate dead end to mislead private replication attempts. Dolan leaves the question unresolved: "It could be that Lazar was a fraud, plain and simple; or that he was telling the truth as he knew it; or that he was manipulated in some way for purposes unknown."
