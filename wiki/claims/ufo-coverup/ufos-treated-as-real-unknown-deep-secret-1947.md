## Claim Statement

Dolan argues that real, unexplained aerial objects — the wartime "foo fighters," then the 1946 European and 1947 American waves — confronted military and intelligence services with a genuine unknown, and that U.S. authorities, having ruled out both Soviet and American origins, treated the matter as so serious that it "probably became a deep secret by 1946, or 1947 at the latest."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He notes the objects were "frequently tracked on radar and observed visually," violating "sensitive military installations," and performed maneuvers (right-angle turns, instant acceleration, 1,000+ mph) "as no aircraft could." Investigators studied and rejected the Soviet and U.S.-secret-craft hypotheses, narrowing options to "something real and alien, or… 'conventional' but as yet unknown." By late 1947 an Air Technical Intelligence Center (ATIC) team at Wright-Patterson favored the extraterrestrial thesis, culminating in the 1948 "Estimate of the Situation" sent to Gen. Hoyt Vandenberg. Multiple services (Air Force, FBI, and by 1948 the CIA) investigated simultaneously.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Dolan flags the limits of the record — the "Estimate of the Situation" is attested only by "insiders" with "no official papers proving this" — and leaves the alien-vs-unknown question explicitly open.
