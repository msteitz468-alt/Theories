## Claim Statement

Good documents that the Soviet Union — like the Western powers — quietly treated UFOs as a serious subject, establishing official scientific bodies to study them even while the KGB and the state managed public information, demonstrating that the cover-up pattern was genuinely worldwide and not capitalist-specific.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He reports the February 1984 founding in Moscow of the **Commission for the Investigation of Anomalous Atmospheric Phenomena** — "comprised of high-level scientists and academicians" and headed by the cosmonaut **Pavel Popovitch**, who said there had been "hundreds of reports each year." Good recounts the Commission taking seriously the 27 March 1983 **Gorky Airport** case (a wingless cigar-shaped object tracked on radar for forty minutes by "reliable and well-trained aviation experts"), and the dramatic June 1982 **Baikonur cosmodrome** incidents (UFOs allegedly disabling launch-pad structures, putting the base "out of action for at least two weeks") — adding that even skeptic James Oberg admitted the base was inexplicably out of commission then.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Good notes some Soviet reports "read like science fiction," and weighs the possibility that the USSR spread "false UFO stories as a means of covering up their space launches" — i.e., disinformation as well as genuine study.
