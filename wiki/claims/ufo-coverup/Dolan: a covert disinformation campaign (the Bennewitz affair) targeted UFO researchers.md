## Claim Statement

Dolan argues that U.S. Air Force Office of Special Investigations (AFOSI) operatives — most prominently Special Agent Richard Doty — ran an active, documented disinformation campaign against UFO researcher Paul Bennewitz during the early 1980s, deliberately feeding him fabricated and altered documents, false signals intelligence, and staged physical phenomena in order to destabilize him and steer his research away from sensitive classified technologies at Kirtland and Manzano Air Force installations. Dolan presents the Bennewitz affair as the clearest single case of intelligence agencies targeting an individual researcher, while simultaneously documenting parallel operations against other researchers and organizations that together constitute a coordinated pattern of counterintelligence manipulation directed at the UFO research community.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The altered Aquarius document (summer 1981).** Dolan recounts that "more active disinformation began when William Moore gave Bennewitz an altered version of the Project Aquarius document." Moore had seen the authentic original in November 1980 and possessed his own copy since February 1981. He gave Bennewitz the tampered version "on behalf of Air Force Intelligence, knowing it had been altered. He felt guilty about it, but did it in order to retain his access to inside information." The key alteration substituted "NASA" for "NSA" — a built-in tracer designed to let intelligence track the document's distribution. Dolan notes this was "a classic disinformation ploy": falsehoods packaged with elements of truth so as to discredit the whole. This was also the first appearance of the alleged UFO control group designation "MJ Twelve" in a public document.

**Surveillance and infiltration of Bennewitz's home.** By spring 1981, AFOSI and NSA were both paying close attention to Bennewitz, with operatives making visits "with and without his knowledge." Doty and Dr. Robert Fugate visited to study a magnetic-field detection device Bennewitz had built; afterwards Fugate's technicians built an improved version. Doty and two NSA operatives also broke into Bennewitz's home on one occasion, where all three reportedly witnessed unexplained light phenomena ("a bright orange-yellow ball of light, hovering in a corner, with a pale blue halo") that left even the intelligence personnel perplexed.

**The fabricated Dulce underground base.** Dolan reports that Doty himself later claimed AFOSI created an elaborate physical deception to mislead Bennewitz: "AFOSI improved old, out-of-the-way dirt roads, airlifted mothballed equipment to the area, and placed fake air vents on Mount Archuleta — all for Bennewitz." The stated purpose was to redirect Bennewitz toward a fictional alien underground base beneath Dulce, New Mexico, and away from actual classified programs at the Kirtland-Manzano complex. Dolan calls this "over the top" as a claim, noting that a local New Mexico state police officer "vehemently disagreed" that the vents were fake, and that the matter "remains unresolved."

**Allen Hynek's reported role.** At the July 1982 MUFON Symposium in Toronto, Moore says Hynek told him privately that the Air Force paid him annual consulting fees and had tasked him "during the previous summer — that is, 1981 — to deliver a new computer and program to Bennewitz." Hynek "did not tell Bennewitz that this was done at the request of the U.S. Air Force." Dolan treats this as plausible but unconfirmed, adding that surveillance operatives apparently monitored even this private conversation: the following day Moore's hotel phone rang and "Falcon" asked, "How did the pictures turn out?" — indicating Moore "was not as alone with Hynek as he had thought."

**The Linda Moulton Howe operation (1983).** On April 9, 1983, AFOSI Special Agent Doty met television producer Linda Moulton Howe at Kirtland AFB. He showed her a document he described as a "BRIEFING PAPER FOR THE PRESIDENT OF THE UNITED STATES OF AMERICA ABOUT THE SUBJECT OF UNIDENTIFIED AERIAL CRAFT" — she could read it but not copy it, and she was audiotaped and videotaped without her knowledge. Doty promised footage of crashed discs, alien bodies, and a live extraterrestrial for her planned HBO documentary, then strung her along for months until HBO withdrew its funding. Dolan concludes Doty was "clearly lying" when he later denied showing her any such documents, given the overwhelming detail she provided. In later correspondence with Dolan, Doty described what he showed Howe as a "scripted document — part truth and part disinformation" and acknowledged that "other intelligence operations on Howe going on at the time" had successfully prevented her from producing the documentary. Dolan identifies this as a classic psy-ops technique: "Journalists were taken into back rooms and showed 'secret documents' — some were forgeries, but others were genuine — which they could read but not copy, then hopefully write about in their newspapers. The reason is that falsehoods, properly packaged, can tarnish any truth."

**The MJ-12 documents (December 1984).** A manila envelope postmarked from Albuquerque arrived at the home of television producer Jaime Shandera containing seven pages of film that, when developed, constituted the now-famous "Eisenhower Briefing Document" for Operation Majestic-12. Dolan notes the Albuquerque postmark pointed directly to Doty's base of operations, and that these documents bore an "obvious relationship" to what Doty had shown Howe twenty months earlier, though with significant differences. Dolan argues that "if hoaxes or disinformation, the documents would most likely be the result of an intelligence operation" — and that the most probable disinformation scenario is that genuine UFO reality underlies them but they were crafted to "lead researchers into dead ends." As Barry Greenwood later put it: "MJ-12 did immense damage to document research."

**The broader intelligence pattern.** Dolan situates these individual operations within a systemic pattern. APRO, he notes, "had been watched by the intelligence community since the 1950s, was now part of an Air Force intelligence disinformation campaign through the graces of William Moore, Richard Doty, and the good people at AFOSI." He identifies a "Coalition of Concerned Ufologists" that attacked researcher Leonard Stringfield as likely being an intelligence operation, since its leader Willard McIntyre "had a longstanding connection to the Society of Photographic Scientists and Engineers (SPSE), an organization closely connected to the CIA." The National Enquirer's CIA connections via publisher Gene Pope meant that the tabloid may have functioned as a UFO management tool consistent with the 1953 Robertson Panel's recommendations to debunk and ridicule: "Good UFO reports making it into the Enquirer — such as the 1975 airspace violations — died a quick death." Dolan also raises the possibility that Philip Klass, whose publisher Aviation Week and Space Technology had "close ties to the U.S. intelligence community," was serving a deeper institutional agenda in his energetic debunking work.

**Dolan's summary assessment.** In the Chapter 5 summary, Dolan writes that the documents Doty showed Howe (and the MJ-12 documents) "seem to have been used for purposes of disinformation that specifically targeted the UFO field. Considering the areas that Howe, Bennewitz, and others were looking into at the time, it makes the most sense that this disinformation targeted the UFO field, and was not simply designed to protect conventional technologies from the Soviets."

## Contexts Within the Source

The Bennewitz affair and the affiliated disinformation operations constitute the primary analytical thread running through Chapters 5 and 6. They appear against a backdrop of simultaneously tightening state secrecy — Reagan's Executive Order 12356 (April 1982) eliminated FOIA response time limits and caused UFO document requests to slow to a trickle — and expanding covert programs (PROMIS software theft, Iran-Contra, the Advanced Theoretical Physics Group, the "Aviary"). Dolan argues that the disinformation operations against researchers were only possible within the broader architecture of [[The national security state (Dolan)]]: institutions capable of multi-year, multi-agency deception campaigns operated by people insulated from congressional oversight and public accountability.

The Bennewitz affair also directly shaped the MJ-12 document controversy that Dolan treats as perhaps the most consequential event in UFO research history: "Howe's encounter with Doty was the first significant entry of MJ-12 into UFO research." Every subsequent document controversy in the field — and Dolan identifies many — is conditioned by the poisoning of the evidentiary well that Doty and AFOSI accomplished.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Dolan includes several important caveats:

1. **Doty's own denials.** In a letter to researcher Larry Bryant, Doty denied showing Howe any presidential briefing paper or Project Aquarius document, claiming the meeting was simply about the 1980 Kirtland incident at Howe's initiative. Dolan dismisses this denial as incredible given the detail of Howe's account, but notes the contradiction cannot be definitively resolved.

2. **The Dulce claim is probably exaggerated.** Dolan calls the claim that AFOSI fabricated an entire underground-base infrastructure at Dulce "over the top," noting that a credible local witness "vehemently disagreed" the physical evidence there was fake.

3. **The Hynek story is unconfirmed.** Moore's account of Hynek admitting to being an Air Force informant is treated as plausible but lacking corroboration: "None of Hynek's former associates has supported this story." Gregory Bishop, Dolan notes, trusts Moore's account — but Gregory Bishop "received it directly from Moore."

4. **Some conventional motive exists.** Dolan acknowledges the argument, made "by some," that AFOSI's operations were "solely to hide purely terrestrial Air Force programs." He considers this "doubtful" but does not rule out a partial conventional motive alongside the broader UFO-related one.

5. **The MJ-12 documents remain contested.** Dolan himself says determining their authenticity "will undoubtedly be a matter of debate until and unless relevant authorities open the books," and acknowledges "a distinct majority" of researchers concluded they were hoaxes or disinformation.
