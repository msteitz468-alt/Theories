## Claim Statement

Good uses the British case to show the gap between public denial and private concern: UFOs were debated in the House of Lords and pressed on the Ministry of Defence by senior peers, yet the government met the questions with minimal, stonewalling answers — itself evidence (Good argues) of managed secrecy.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He cites the historic **January 1979 House of Lords debate** (in which Lord Strabolgi gave the government's "there is no cover-up and no security ban" line), and the **4 March 1982** exchange in which the **Earl of Clancarty** asked "how many reports have been received by the Ministry of Defence," was given figures by Viscount Long, and replied that he "believed the number to be far higher." Good pairs these with Admiral of the Fleet **Lord Hill-Norton**'s correspondence pressing the Defence Secretary — and his astonishment that "a serious letter to a Minister from a member of the House of Lords was allowed to remain unanswered."
