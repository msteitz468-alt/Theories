## Claim Statement

The physical universe is not composed of solid material matter, but is a computer-generated simulation constructed from information. What we perceive as physical objects, time, and space are computational renderings presented to our consciousness, similar to a massive multiplayer online role-playing game.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Virk asserts in the Foreword to the Second Edition:
> "far from living in a physical world of matter, we live inside a computer-generated reality built on information. This reality can be thought of as either a multiplayer video game or a computer simulation, depending on your orientation."

In Chapter 1, Virk writes:
> "The fundamental question raised by the simulation hypothesis is this: Are we all actually characters living inside some kind of giant, massively multiplayer online video game, a simulated reality that is so well rendered that we cannot distinguish it from 'physical reality'?"

He links this to John Wheeler's "It from Bit" concept in physics, which suggests that physical things (it) arise from binary choices of information (bit):
> "The basis for the simulation hypothesis is that the universe is not made of physical things, but rather is made of information... Wheeler coined the phrase 'it from bit' to express this idea." (Ch. 14)

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the foundational thesis that Virk explores through computer science (Part I), physics (Part II), and mysticism (Part III). He argues that viewing reality as informational and computational resolves long-standing paradoxes in quantum mechanics (such as the observer effect) and validates ancient spiritual models of the universe.
