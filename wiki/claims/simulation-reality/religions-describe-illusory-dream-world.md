## Claim Statement

Both Eastern and Western religious scriptures describe the physical world as a temporary, dream-like illusion (e.g., *Maya* in Buddhism/Hinduism, *dunya* in Islam, or a spiritual test in Judeo-Christianity). These traditions describe a universe that was designed, programmed, and monitored by a creator deity and administrative entities (angels/lords) who execute system rules.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Virk notes in Chapter 1:
> "Many of the world’s religious traditions tell us that the world around us is an illusion created for our benefit. This is particularly true in the Eastern traditions of Buddhism and Hinduism, which explicitly tell us that the world we see is maya, or illusion. This implies that there is something beyond the illusion. Even the Western religions have a similar concept of this world (the 'here') and the other, eternal world (the 'hereafter')."

In Chapter 12, he expands on Western concepts:
> "In Judeo-Christian and Islamic texts, the world is described as a transient test or a temporary platform... The angels are depicted as recording every action and word in 'books'—which, in computer terms, is simply logging player transactions and inputs in a system database. The ultimate Creator is the chief architect who programmed the environment and set up the scoring system."

He cites Gnostic texts, highlighting the demiurge and archons as the rulers of a simulated prison-world:
> "Gnostics believed that the physical world was a flawed copy, a simulation built by a lesser god, the Demiurge, to keep divine consciousness trapped... This aligns with the idea of a closed virtual reality game run by administrators who don't want the players to realize they are inside a game." (Ch. 12)

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is explored in detail in Part III, where Virk compares computational concepts (databases, logs, rendering engines, avatars, non-player characters) to ancient descriptions of judgment books, spiritual bodies, divine messengers, and the illusory nature of life.
