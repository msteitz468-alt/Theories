## Claim Statement

The observer effect and the collapse of the probability wave in quantum mechanics are evidence of conditional rendering—a computational optimization technique. The universe does not persistently render physical matter in areas that are unobserved; rather, it exists as a wave of mathematical probabilities in memory and collapses into localized physical form only when observed by a player.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Virk argues in Chapter 7:
> "Quantum indeterminacy by itself is evidence of a type of conditional rendering... this kind of unexplainable behavior seems to make sense only if the simulation hypothesis is true."

In Chapter 14, he states:
> "A number of physicists have proposed that finding evidence of conditional rendering—that is, drawing only what the player sees in our 'physical' world—is a key way to prove that we are inside a video game... the universe is somehow optimizing resources similarly to the way video games optimize computing resources by rendering only what is necessary at that point in time."

He notes that the double-slit experiment and delayed-choice experiments demonstrate that the physical properties of a particle (such as which path it took) are calculated and rendered "on-demand" when the final measurement or observation is made:
> "If we can show that someone or something is required to observe the results in order to render them, this by itself can be evidence of a video game–like simulated world." (Ch. 14)

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim links Part II (Physics) with Part IV (Verification). Virk uses Thomas Campbell's experimental proposals at Cal Poly Pomona to show that researchers are actively testing whether consciousness-mediated wave collapse is an optimized rendering process of a digital substrate.
