## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the assassination of Senator Robert F. Kennedy at the Ambassador Hotel in Los
Angeles on June 5, 1968, was not the act of lone gunman Sirhan Sirhan. Per Jeffries, physical
evidence, multiple witness accounts, and a pattern of LAPD evidence suppression and witness coercion
indicate at least one additional shooter was present. The book further argues that the official
investigation was a deliberate cover-up paralleling the handling of the JFK case.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Physical impossibility of Sirhan as sole shooter.** All witnesses placed Sirhan in front of RFK,
never closer than one foot; the autopsy showed the fatal shot entered Kennedy's head from behind
and that powder burns indicated the shots were fired from at most one and a half inches away.
Jeffries argues these facts are mutually exclusive. The LAPD's reconstruction of how eight bullets
from Sirhan's gun accounted for all wounds (including the "Evans bullet" that reportedly struck
multiple hard surfaces yet retained three-quarters of its mass) is, per the book, physically
comparable to the JFK "magic bullet."

**More bullets than Sirhan's gun could fire.** FBI agents, LAPD officers, and journalists all
documented what appeared to be bullet holes in the pantry door frame's center divider. FBI agent
William Bailey swore in a 1976 affidavit that he and several agents saw "at least two (2) small
caliber bullet holes in the center post of the two doors" and would "go to my grave knowing they
were two bullet holes." The Chicago Tribune reported on June 6, 1968, that police removed "two .22
caliber bullets that had gone wild" from the center divider molding. Detective Mike Rothmiller, who
worked in the LAPD's Organized Crime Intelligence Division, reported reading a file that accounted
for ten spent .22 caliber slugs — more than Sirhan's eight-round revolver could fire.

**Girl in the polka-dot dress.** Multiple unconnected witnesses — including Sandra Serrano, waiter
Thomas Vincent DiPierro, security guard Thane Eugene Cesar, and numerous others — described a woman
in a white dress with black polka dots apparently accompanying Sirhan before the shooting and
running from the building shouting "We shot him!" after it. Jeffries quotes Sergeant Paul Scharaga,
who filed an APB for this woman at 12:30 a.m., and was appalled to find the dispatch cancelled at
1:41 a.m. with the explanation that investigators "only have one man and don't want them to get
anything started on a big conspiracy."

**LAPD witness coercion.** Jeffries details the "ludicrous polygraph examination/mental torture
session" inflicted on Sandra Serrano by Sergeant Enrique Hernandez, reproducing transcript excerpts
in which Hernandez repeatedly harangues her, telling her "Please, in the name of Kennedy … don't
shame his death" and "one of these days, if you're woman enough, you will get a letter from Ethel
Kennedy — personal — thanking you for at least letting her rest on this aspect." After breaking her
down, Hernandez was promoted. Jeffries notes that Hernandez also forced waiter DiPierro to claim he
got the polka-dot story from Serrano, while ten days earlier having forced Serrano to "admit" she
got it from DiPierro.

**Evidence destruction.** The LAPD admitted destroying the pantry door frames containing alleged
bullet holes because they "had no room for it," then acknowledged the x-rays of the frames were
also "not in existence." Police Chief Gates said the holes "proved absolutely nothing."

**Sirhan's gun could not be matched.** A 1975 seven-member firearms panel ordered by Judge Robert
A. Wenke found that none of the bullets recovered from victims could be matched to Sirhan's gun,
and that the original test bullets allegedly fired by the LAPD on June 6, 1968, could not be
confirmed either. The panel member Lowell Bradford was forced to issue a public statement correcting
press claims of "no second gun," clarifying that "the Sirhan gun cannot be identified with the
bullets from the crime scene."

**Attorney General's premature conclusion.** Ramsey Clark declared "no evidence of conspiracy" in a
press conference the day after the assassination, before any serious investigation had taken place.

## Contexts Within the Source

Jeffries frames the RFK assassination as a conscious parallel to the JFK case: same lone-nut
designation, same witness intimidation, same evidence destruction, same media complicity. He states
explicitly: "In my view, both Kennedy assassinations were connected and were the result of powerful
conspiracies." This claim links to [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]] and [[The political "body count" pattern (Jeffries)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries acknowledges that, unlike Oswald, Sirhan "undoubtedly fired shots" in the pantry and was
caught at the scene. He leaves open whether Sirhan was "some kind of Manchurian Candidate" but
argues "logic compels the assumption that greater forces than this lone young Palestinian were
behind" the assassination. He does not identify the second gunman by name, though he mentions
security guard Thane Eugene Cesar as a figure "some researchers" have suspected.
