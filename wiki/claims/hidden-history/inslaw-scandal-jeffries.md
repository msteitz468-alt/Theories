## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the Reagan Justice Department stole PROMIS software from the privately owned
Inslaw company in 1983, that journalist Danny Casolaro was murdered while investigating the
broader conspiracy he called "the Octopus" (tying together Inslaw, Iran-Contra, and the October
Surprise), and that multiple witnesses and investigators connected to the case died suspiciously
while the government successfully suppressed accountability.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per Jeffries, two separate federal bankruptcy courts found that the Justice Department had "stolen
the PROMIS software from Inslaw," and a House Judiciary Committee investigation confirmed the theft.
The Committee recommended compensation, but Attorney General Richard Thornburgh ignored the
recommendation. Under the Clinton administration, Attorney General Janet Reno's 1995 review
concluded there was "no credible evidence of conspiracy" — illustrating what Jeffries characterizes
as bipartisan complicity.

Danny Casolaro, who called the interlocking scandal "the Octopus," was found dead on August 10,
1991, in a Martinsburg, West Virginia, hotel — officially a suicide by wrist-slashing. Jeffries
notes that Casolaro's brother, Dr. Anthony Casolaro, stated that Danny "was so scared of the sight
of blood that he wouldn't allow samples to be drawn for medical purposes" and thus would not have
chosen that method of suicide. Danny had just told friends he was going to "bring back the head of
the octopus." The medical examiner allowed the body to be embalmed before the family was even
notified. Former attorney general Elliot Richardson, serving as Inslaw's counsel, declared: "It's
hard to come up with any reason for this death, other than he was deliberately murdered because he
was so close to uncovering sinister elements in what he called 'The Octopus.'"

Other deaths Jeffries attributes to the case: Alan Standorf (found dead on the rear floor of his
car at Washington's National Airport, killed "weeks earlier" at another site by a blow to the head);
attorney Dennis Eisman (found shot to death on April 5, 1991); Ian Spiro's wife and three children
(found murdered at home, November 1, 1992) and Spiro himself (found dead in a car in the desert
days later).

Michael Riconosciuto, who claimed to have programmed a secret "back door" into PROMIS enabling
clandestine surveillance of foreign governments, alleged the software was a "reward" to Reagan aide
Earl Brian for participation in the October Surprise operation. One week after swearing out an
affidavit about Inslaw on March 21, 1991, Riconosciuto was arrested on drug charges and sentenced
to 30 years — "unheard of for a first-time drug offender," per Jeffries.

## Contexts Within the Source

Jeffries presents Inslaw as one of the "forgotten scandals of the Reagan years" that connects to
the broader pattern documented in [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]]. The deaths linked to the case are part of [[The political "body count" pattern (Jeffries)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries notes that the Clinton administration's review "concluded there was no credible evidence
of conspiracy," and that both Riconosciuto and Russbacher (linked to the October Surprise) faced
government prosecutions that muddied their credibility as witnesses. He presents the bankruptcy
court findings as the strongest objective evidence of theft but does not independently verify the
Casolaro murder allegations.
