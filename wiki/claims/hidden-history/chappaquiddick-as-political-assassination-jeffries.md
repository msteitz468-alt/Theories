## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the events at Chappaquiddick on July 19, 1969, were not merely the result of
a drunk senator's negligence but represented a deliberate "political assassination" of Senator Edward
Kennedy's presidential prospects, "orchestrated by the same forces responsible for the deaths of
John and Robert Kennedy." Jeffries argues Kennedy's own account is implausible and that physical
evidence points toward a set-up rather than a simple accident.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Jeffries dismisses the standard conservative interpretation (Kennedy directly caused Mary Jo
Kopechne's death and lied about it) and the standard liberal one (ignore the subject) in favour of
a "third alternative": the incident was engineered to neutralize the last Kennedy brother. He bases
this on the following:

- Kennedy — a big man with a bad back — could not plausibly have escaped from an upside-down car on
  a pond bottom in strong currents, yet Kopechne, younger and in better shape, could not free herself.
- Researcher R. B. Cutler's analysis of marks on the bridge showed the car had stopped and then
  "suddenly accelerated at a high speed over the edge," contradicting Kennedy's account of a wrong
  turn. Some car damage, per Cutler, appeared to have been inflicted before it entered the water.
- Alcohol was found in Kopechne's blood, though all witnesses testified she never drank; Jeffries
  raises the possibility she had been drugged. Kennedy had wounds on the back of his skull — including
  "a huge bump on top" — suggesting he may have been rendered unconscious and was not even in the car.
- Nixon White House operative Anthony Ulasewicz claimed he had been ordered to fly to Chappaquiddick
  to "dig up dirt" on Ted Kennedy — and, per researcher Richard Sprague (author of *The Taking of
  America, 1–2–3*), arrived early on the morning of the incident before it had been made public.
- CIA operative E. Howard Hunt traveled to Hyannis Port in a red wig to gather Chappaquiddick
  information for use against Kennedy in the 1972 campaign; critics wondered if he was "returning to
  the scene of the crime."
- Deputy Sheriff Christopher Look and others testified they saw Kennedy's car with three people
  inside just before the accident — contradicting Kennedy's account.

The absence of any autopsy of Mary Jo Kopechne meant the full circumstances could never be
established. The net result, per Jeffries, was that Chappaquiddick "destroyed Ted Kennedy's chances
of ever being elected president, as surely as if he'd been slain by another 'lone nut.'"

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is positioned within Jeffries's broader argument that the Kennedy family was systematically
targeted by the same deep-state forces across multiple decades. It links to [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]] and the book's pattern of
political violence and career destruction. Jeffries also catalogs numerous additional suspicious deaths
in the Kennedy extended family as circumstantial evidence of ongoing targeting (multiple separate
plane crashes, etc.).

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries's own characterization of Kennedy's official story as "highly implausible" does not rule
out a simpler explanation (drunk-driving accident with cover-up). He presents Cutler's analysis as
the "most sense" scenario but does not claim certainty. He acknowledges the framing is partly
inferential.
