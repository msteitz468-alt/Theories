## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the American judicial and law enforcement system is structurally biased in favor of the wealthy and powerful, routinely convicting innocent poor people while protecting influential criminals, and that DNA exonerations, suppressed FBI evidence, and on-camera police brutality demonstrate systemic, not incidental, corruption.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per [[Hidden History (Jeffries)]]:

**Wrongful convictions:**
- The Death Penalty Information Center documented 140 people released from death row since 1973 on newly discovered exculpatory evidence.
- The National Registry of Exonerations found more than 2,000 people falsely convicted and subsequently exonerated from 1989 to 2012; the registry excluded at least 1,170 additional cases involving police fabrication of evidence.
- Peter Limone: convicted in 1968 for a 1965 murder committed by FBI informant Joseph "The Animal" Barboza; FBI agents knew Limone was innocent since 1965 but did nothing; Limone served 33 years before exoneration in 2001; two innocent co-defendants died in prison.
- Darrell Grayson: executed in Alabama (July 27, 2007) without ever receiving DNA testing that his attorneys argued could prove his innocence; the state attorney general said testing would "delay justice."

**Class double standards:**
- Public defenders spend an average of five to ten minutes with clients; 95 percent of federal defendants plead guilty; 90 percent of federal defendants are convicted regardless of counsel.
- The wealthy and famous have a dramatically higher acquittal rate; "the one telling factor in determining possible imprisonment is class."
- Police refer lower-class juveniles to court far more frequently than upper-class counterparts (1973 Philadelphia study).

**Historical examples:**
- Stephen Dennison: served 34 years beginning in 1925 for shoplifting a $5 box of candy.
- Leonard Hankins: convicted of robbery and murder in Minneapolis, 1933; FBI obtained a full confession from the real criminal but Minneapolis police refused to release Hankins because the FBI wouldn't share its file; Hankins served another 15 years.

**Corrupt judiciary and police:**
- The Supreme Court in 2012 voted 8–1 to refuse constitutional safeguards against suggestive eyewitness identification testimony.
- In 1996, the Court upheld seizure of a woman's car for a crime committed by her husband, even though she had broken no law.
- Police bodycam and bystander videos show routine harassment of skateboarders, elderly individuals, and wheelchair users, but "no filmed examples of [police] giving this rough treatment to members of violent gangs."
- Kelly Thomas: unarmed homeless man beaten for more than 20 minutes by Fullerton, California, officers (July 5, 2011; entire incident on video); two officers found not guilty (January 2014).
- Miriam Carey: unarmed mother shot to death by federal officers near the Capitol on October 3, 2013; her one-year-old daughter witnessed the shooting from the backseat; Congress gave the Capitol police a standing ovation the following day.

**Supreme Court overreach:**
- Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes: "The Constitution is what the judges say it is."
- The Supreme Court has evolved from constitutional interpreter to lawmaking body; in 2005 it upheld law enforcement's right to physically detain obviously innocent citizens and destroy their property if it "assists in taking command of the situation."

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 16. Jeffries presents the justice system as an extension of elite power, consistent with the broader argument in [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries acknowledges that the Innocence Project and similar groups have succeeded in some exonerations, suggesting not all officials conspire; he frames these as exceptions that prove the rule.
