## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that investigative journalist Gary Webb — who exposed CIA involvement in crack cocaine trafficking to fund Nicaraguan Contras — did not kill himself but was murdered, and that the Sacramento County coroner's finding of suicide-by-two-gunshots-to-the-face was a conclusion designed to suppress further investigation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per [[Hidden History (Jeffries)]], Gary Webb's 1996 "Dark Alliance" series in the San Jose Mercury News alleged that the CIA had knowingly allowed Nicaraguan Contra drug traffickers to sell crack cocaine in Los Angeles, with profits going to the Contras. The mainstream press attacked the story aggressively; Webb was forced to resign from the Mercury News and his marriage ended.

In the period before his death, Jeffries alleges:
- Webb reported seeing strange figures outside his home and receiving death threats.
- Filmmaker A.J. Booth, working on a documentary, told Alex Jones that Webb had been followed constantly before his death and that everyone he spoke to said "there's no way this guy would have taken his own life — that he enjoyed life, he loved his kids."

On December 10, 2004, Webb was found dead from two gunshot wounds to the face. The Sacramento County coroner ruled suicide. Jeffries quotes the coroner's own acknowledgment: "It's unusual in a suicide case to have two shots."

Two typed suicide notes were found. Movers arriving at Webb's home found a note on the door reading "Please do not enter. Call 911 and ask for an ambulance."

**CIA vindication of Webb's reporting**: Jeffries notes that the CIA inspector general's 1998 report concluded there had been a 1982–1995 Memorandum of Understanding between the CIA and the Justice Department authorizing them to ignore drug trafficking by CIA "agents, assets, and non-staff employees." Webb reacted: "I now understood how the government could so easily deny the 'evidence' of CIA/Contra drug trafficking, since the CIA and the Justice Department had a gentleman's agreement to look the other way."

**Webb's own words**: "The reason I'd enjoyed such smooth sailing for so long hadn't been … because I was careful and diligent and good at my job. It turned out to have nothing to do with it. The truth was that, in all those years, I hadn't written anything important enough to suppress."

Retired FBI agent Ted Gunderson claimed Webb had been in communication with PROMIS/Cabazon whistleblower Michael Riconosciuto in the weeks before his death, working on a new book.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries acknowledges the official ruling of suicide. He relies primarily on the physical implausibility of self-inflicting two gunshot wounds to the face, and on the testimony of people who knew Webb that he showed no signs of suicidal intent.
