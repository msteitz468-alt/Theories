## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the US government knowingly left prisoners of war and soldiers missing in
action behind in Vietnam, and that a sustained cover-up — involving the Nixon and Bush
administrations, the Senate Select Committee chaired by John Kerry, and Senator John McCain —
deliberately suppressed evidence of living survivors, destroyed documents, and intimidated witnesses.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Colonel Millard Peck's resignation.** In June 1991, Army Colonel Millard Peck — the DIA's top
POW-MIA officer and one of the Army's most decorated Vietnam heroes — tacked a five-page resignation
letter to his office door, blasting government officials and the National League of Families
executive director Ann Mills-Griffiths for actively sabotaging investigations. Peck alleged
Mills-Griffiths edited or rewrote all significant DIA documents on the subject, blocked real
progress, and had access to top-secret traffic for which she was not cleared. "From what I
witnessed, it appears that any soldier left in Vietnam, even inadvertently, was, in fact, abandoned
years ago."

**The Nixon White House set-up.** A White House memo — initialed by Chief of Staff H. R. Haldeman
and discussed in a meeting between Nixon and RNC Chairman Robert Dole — recommended "a fairly major
move for cosmetic purposes" to keep POW wives "on the reservation." General Alexander Haig's staff
wrote that efforts over the next eight weeks would be devoted to "keeping the families on the
reservation in order to buy this time."

**John Kerry's Senate Committee (1991–93).** Jeffries alleges Chairman Kerry: refused to investigate
roles of Reagan or Bush; fired two staff members (Deputy Staff Director Dino Carluccio and former
Representative Billy Hendon) who charged documents showing a "clear pattern of betrayal" were
suppressed; coached Defense Department witnesses; ordered the destruction of an original intelligence
briefing report indicating POWs were alive as recently as 1989 (documented in a May 3, 1992, memo
from investigator John McCreary to Vice Chairman Robert Smith, citing staff director Frances Zwenig's
instruction to "delete all computer files" and chief counsel J. William Codinha's dismissal of the
destruction as a "mountain out of a molehill"); and during a trip to Hanoi, apologized to Communist
leaders for "inspecting" their military compound and referred to the search as "silly." Senator
Thomas Daschle explicitly confirmed the trip's purpose was to "close the book on the emotional issue
of MIAs."

**Russian confirmation.** Russian President Boris Yeltsin wrote to the Senate Committee in 1992
stating that the Soviets had held US prisoners from World War II and that Americans held in Vietnam
were interrogated by Soviet intelligence. He repeated these statements to journalists aboard a
flight to Washington. Jeffries argues the committee was "not interested in this or any other real
information."

**John McCain's obstruction.** Per Jeffries, Senator McCain — who built his career on his own
Vietnam POW experience — was "hostile to every witness" claiming evidence of living POWs, viciously
attacked activists like former POW Eugene "Red" McDaniel as "frauds," opposed the "Truth Bill"
calling for disclosure of all POW records, introduced a countermeasure permitting the Pentagon to
control what was released, and verbally attacked (and allegedly physically shoved) family members
and activists in Senate hallways when they challenged him.

**Document destruction.** In March 1993, General Thomas Needham, Navy Commander Dale Hayes, and an
unidentified CIA employee destroyed POW-MIA case files filling "20 to 30 feet" of wall cabinets at
the US embassy in Bangkok — files that had been declassified by executive order under [[George H.W. Bush]] — while the Senate Select Committee failed to pursue them.

**Soviet GRU document.** Researcher Stephen Morris of Harvard discovered a September 12, 1972,
report by North Vietnamese General Tran Van Quang stating that 1,205 American POWs were being held
and that "The Government of the USA knows this well."

## Contexts Within the Source

This is one of the most extensively documented sections of Chapter 5 and illustrates Jeffries's
argument that the cover-up transcended party lines (Nixon, Bush, Kerry, McCain all cooperated in
suppression). It links to [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]], [[The political "body count" pattern (Jeffries)]], and [[False opposition and the controlled two-party system (Jeffries)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries acknowledges that the Senate Select Committee ultimately concluded no conclusive proof of
living POWs existed. He attributes this to evidence suppression rather than absence of POWs.
Witnesses like Peck and McCreary provide documentary support cited by Jeffries but the documents
themselves were classified or destroyed, limiting external verification.
