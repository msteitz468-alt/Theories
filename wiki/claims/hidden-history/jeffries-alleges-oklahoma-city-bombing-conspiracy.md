## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the April 19, 1995, bombing of the Alfred P. Murrah Federal Building in Oklahoma City — which killed 168 people — was not the work of Timothy McVeigh and Terry Nichols alone; that government informants and possible federal foreknowledge were involved; that physical evidence shows a fertilizer bomb could not have caused all the damage; and that McVeigh received a legally indefensible trial deliberately designed to convict him.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per [[Hidden History (Jeffries)]], the key allegations include:

- **Bomb experts dispute fertilizer theory**: Brigadier General Ben Partin, "perhaps the foremost demolition expert in the country," and longtime FBI agent Ted Gunderson both disputed that a fertilizer bomb could have caused the building's collapse. During local TV coverage on April 19, federal authorities repeatedly referenced a second and third bomb found inside the building; a terrorism expert (Randall Heather) discussed a "sophisticated device" by a "sophisticated group."
- **John Doe #2 suppressed**: Witnesses described a second man ("John Doe #2") with McVeigh at the Ryder truck rental shop; three employees said "Robert Kling" (the rental alias) was not alone. One said two of the three employees described a "Kling" who clearly could not have been McVeigh. The government chose not to call any Oklahoma City eyewitness to identify McVeigh — because every such witness had also seen John Doe #2.
- **Government foreknowledge**: ATF informant Carol Howe told the FBI months before the bombing that neo-Nazis (including German national Andreas Strassmeir, later spirited out of the country) had been casing the Murrah building and naming April 19 as the target date. Judge Richard Matsch refused to allow her to testify. FBI informant Cary James Gagan had filed a civil suit claiming he warned the FBI in September 1994 of a plot to bomb a federal building, and that "they let it happen."
- **McVeigh's trial was a railroad job**: Jeffries compares it to the trials of Bruno Richard Hauptmann, James Earl Ray, and Sirhan Sirhan. Defense attorney Stephen Jones presented almost no defense (resting after three and a half days, only twenty-five witnesses). Judge Matsch barred much defense evidence, including the Carol Howe testimony and local TV footage of the unexploded bombs. Even Alan Dershowitz criticized the defense.
- **ATF agents forewarned**: Edye Smith, who lost two sons in the bombing, asked CNN on May 23, 1995, "Where was ATF? All fifteen or seventeen of their employees survived … they had an option to not go to work that day, and my kids didn't get that option…." CNN cut off her interview.
- **Evidence destroyed**: The remnants of the Murrah building were demolished only weeks after the bombing, and rubble was buried at an undisclosed location, preventing independent forensic examination.
- **Mysterious deaths after the bombing**: Oklahoma City Police Officer Terrance Yeakey — who had sent a letter saying he was told to "keep my silence" and was "ashamed to wear a badge from Oklahoma City's Police Department" — died in circumstances Jeffries says would "have made the Warren Commission blush": slashed wrists, arms, elbows, stabs to both sides of the neck, then shot in the side of the head after walking one and a half miles from his car. Dr. Howard Chumley, who refused to falsify injury reports for ATF agents, died when his plane plunged into a field. These deaths are linked to [[The political "body count" pattern (Jeffries)]].
- **Clinton exploited the bombing**: Jeffries notes Clinton used McVeigh's alleged militia connections to demonize the Third Party movement and "right wing" talk radio.

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 6 treats the Oklahoma City bombing as a parallel to the JFK assassination: a designated "lone nut" scapegoat, suppressed evidence, intimidated witnesses, and a complicit press. It is presented as part of [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries does not assert definitively that federal officials planned or executed the bombing. His strongest claim is that the government had foreknowledge and allowed it to happen, and that McVeigh did not act alone. He acknowledges McVeigh himself gave contradictory and strange interviews.
