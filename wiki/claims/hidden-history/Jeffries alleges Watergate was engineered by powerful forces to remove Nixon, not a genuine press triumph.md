## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that while the Watergate scandal resulted in Nixon's removal from office, it was
not a triumph of the free press exposing genuine wrongdoing. Rather, per Jeffries, "someone powerful
wanted him removed from office," and the press, which had otherwise ignored far more serious
scandals, acted with conspicuous selectivity. The book also documents a string of suspicious deaths
connected to Watergate that received almost no media attention.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Jeffries argues the break-in itself was "child's play compared to dozens of more serious scandals
that had been ignored and covered up by the same kept press," specifically including the Kennedy
assassinations. He poses the rhetorical question: if journalists thought a "rather pointless
break-in" warranted the first presidential impeachment in a century, why did the same journalists
"sneer and snicker at notions of conspiracy in the Kennedy assassinations?"

Researcher Mae Brussell compiled a list of thirty mysterious deaths connected to Watergate,
per Jeffries. Notable examples he cites:

- Beverly Kaye, 42, died of a "stroke" in a White House elevator in December 1973; she was involved
  with storing the White House tapes and reportedly knew what was on the missing 18-minute gap.
- Nixon friend Murray Chotiner was killed on January 23, 1974, when his car was struck by a
  government truck.
- Congressman William Mills allegedly committed suicide on May 24, 1973, days after a damaging
  campaign-contribution story; "seven suicide notes" were found.
- Dorothy Hunt (wife of CIA operative E. Howard Hunt) was killed in a plane crash on December 8,
  1972.
- Louise Boyer, Nelson Rockefeller's longtime assistant, fell to her death from a New York apartment
  building on July 3, 1974, shortly before Rockefeller became unelected vice president.
- Martha Mitchell — who "made numerous allegations against Nixon" and "claimed to have been held
  against her will at one point" — died of cancer in 1976, "abandoned by most of her family."
  Nixon himself told David Frost: "If it hadn't been for Martha Mitchell, there'd have been no
  Watergate."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is part of Jeffries's thesis in [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]] that the mainstream press is a managed tool rather than
a genuine watchdog. The suspicious-deaths catalog links to [[The political "body count" pattern (Jeffries)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries does not deny Nixon's wrongdoing, calling him "a typically ambitious, corruptible
politician." His allegation is not that Nixon was innocent but that his removal served elite interests
and that the same press standards were never applied to comparable or greater wrongdoing.
