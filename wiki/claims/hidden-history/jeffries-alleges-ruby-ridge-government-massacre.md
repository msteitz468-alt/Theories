## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the Ruby Ridge standoff of August 1992 was a shameful, murderous government overreaction in which federal agents killed Randy Weaver's teenage son Samuel and wife Vicki — while Vicki was cradling her baby — over a trivial, entrapped gun charge. He presents the incident as part of the pattern of federal government violence against its own citizens described in [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]].

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per [[Hidden History (Jeffries)]], Randy Weaver was a white racial separatist who "wanted nothing but to be left alone." His alleged crime was failing to appear in court on a charge of selling a sawed-off shotgun that was only a quarter-inch shorter than the legal limit — to an undercover federal agent who had entrapped him through persistent friendship.

Jeffries alleges:

- The cabin was surrounded by approximately one hundred ATF, FBI, and Idaho State Police officers in camouflage; eventually four hundred federal agents were at the scene.
- During the standoff, Weaver's fourteen-year-old son Samuel was shot in the back as he ran from agents.
- FBI sniper Lon Horouchi shot Vicki Weaver through the head as she stood in the cabin doorway cradling her infant.
- The agents left the family's dead dog in the road and ran over it repeatedly with government vehicles.
- Agents taunted the surviving family by calling out Vicki's name after killing her.
- Senator Larry Craig requested the Justice Department's report on the incident at least three times without success.
- A Boise jury acquitted Weaver and others of murder and conspiracy. The Weaver family later filed a wrongful death suit; without admission of wrongdoing, Randy Weaver received $100,000 and his three daughters $1 million each.

Jeffries quotes Sarah Weaver's 2010 appearance on William Shatner's show *Aftermath*, where she expressed forgiveness toward the sniper.

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 6 introduces Ruby Ridge as one of the "most shameful" incidents of the Clinton years. Jeffries pairs it with Waco as evidence of federal government violence against dissidents during the Clinton administration, and as background for the Oklahoma City bombing.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries acknowledges Weaver held white separatist views. He notes the jury found Weaver not guilty and the eventual cash settlement — but emphasizes this came without any official admission of wrongdoing.
