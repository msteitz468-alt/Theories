## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the March 30, 1981, attempt on President Reagan's life was surrounded by
suspicious circumstances, including a family connection between alleged assassin John Hinckley Jr.
and Vice President George H. W. Bush, physical improbabilities in the shooting itself, and the
inexplicable fact that Hinckley has never been interviewed on television in more than three decades.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per Jeffries, Neil Bush — son of Vice President [[George H.W. Bush]] — was scheduled to have dinner
with John Hinckley's brother Scott Hinckley the day after the shooting. Neil Bush's response to
early press reports was described as "curious": "I don't recognize any pictures of him. I just wish
I could see a better picture of him." George W. Bush told the Associated Press he was unsure if he
had met John Hinckley. The Hinckley family was "rumored to have been large financial contributors"
to [[George H.W. Bush]]'s unsuccessful 1980 presidential primary campaign.

The nearly fatal bullet supposedly ricocheted off the presidential limousine "in a most unlikely
manner, through a space of an inch or less between the open passenger door." NBC's Judy Woodruff
declared in early news accounts that at least one shot came from "somewhere above," contradicting
the lone-gunman narrative. Jeffries notes a shared "lone nut" profile: Hinckley, like Mark David
Chapman (who killed John Lennon three months earlier), was reportedly affiliated with World Vision,
an international Christian relief organization; Chapman was also obsessed with the novel *Catcher
in the Rye*, sitting down to read it calmly after shooting Lennon.

Jeffries finds it anomalous that Charles Manson and serial killers like John Wayne Gacy were
granted multiple televised interviews while Hinckley — a sitting political figure's would-be
assassin — was never permitted to speak to reporters, citing a 1989 *Los Angeles Times* story about
Hinckley's failed petitions to be allowed press access.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is part of the book's treatment of [[George H.W. Bush]] as connected to a pattern of
political violence and suspicious events. It links to [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]] and continues the book's pattern of questioning
"lone nut" designations.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries does not assert outright that Bush ordered or was informed of the shooting in advance. The
Bush-Hinckley social connection is presented as a reported fact that received "sporadic" mainstream
coverage rather than as proof of conspiracy. Hinckley's motive (impressing actress Jodie Foster) was
the official finding and Jeffries calls it an "odd motivation even for a disturbed mind" without
claiming a definitive alternative explanation.
