## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the largest American charities — including the Red Cross, United Way, American Cancer Society, and celebrity-backed foundations — systematically divert donated money to executive salaries, overhead, and reserve funds rather than their stated causes, and that the same elite figures who advocate higher taxes for others legally shelter their own fortunes through these foundations.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per [[Hidden History (Jeffries)]]:

**Red Cross:**
- After the 1989 San Francisco earthquake, the Red Cross kept $40 million of $50 million donated; San Francisco Mayor Art Agnos sued, resulting in an out-of-court settlement.
- Following 9/11, the Red Cross collected $564 million but released only $154 million; Red Cross President Bernadine Healy told a congressional committee the Liberty Fund had "evolved into a war fund."
- Oklahoma City bombing: per Insight magazine, Red Cross intercepted envelopes containing money sent directly to bereaved families; Senior VP Bill Blaul dismissed the complaints.
- In 2005, the Canadian Red Cross knowingly shipped blood infected with HIV and hepatitis C.
- 3M donated $750,000 in life-saving respirators for 9/11 first responders; rescue chief Doug Copp alleged the Red Cross stored them in a warehouse to sell later.
- Haiti earthquake (2011): ABC News reported the Red Cross was sitting on several hundred million dollars of donated funds; the organization said it was better to release money slowly.

**United Way and others:**
- United Way CEO William Aramony: paid $390,000 salary plus $76,000 bonuses; convicted of twenty-five counts of conspiracy, fraud, and sexual misconduct; sentenced to seven years.
- Ralph Dickerson (United Way CEO, 2006): caught stealing $227,000; allowed to simply repay the money and avoid prison.

**Celebrity foundations:**
- Bono's ONE antipoverty foundation: in 2010, gave only 1.2 percent of donations to those in need; over half went to salaries.
- Sting's Rainforest Foundation: 41 percent of donations to the cause; given a zero rating by Charity Navigator for four consecutive years; Mayor Bloomberg still declared "Rainforest Awareness Month" in its honor.

**Elite hypocrisy:**
- Warren Buffett advocates higher estate taxes while sheltering his fortune in the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation; refused to assist his own daughter with kitchen remodeling or his granddaughter's college tuition.
- Bill Gates advocates estate taxes while structuring his children's inheritance to bypass them via Microsoft stock appreciation.
- The Gates Foundation's endowment is larger than the GDP of more than 100 countries; Gates CEO Jeffrey Raikes was paid $990,000 in 2008.
- Al Gore gave 0.18 percent ($353) of his 1997 income to charity; Joe Biden averaged $369 per year in charitable giving from 1998 to 2008.
- Jesse Jackson's Jackson Foundation listed "none" under "Direct Charitable Activities" on its 2004 tax return while spending $84,172 on a gala to honor Jackson.
- Bill Gates (2010 Aspen Ideas Festival): implied elderly patients should be denied care rather than "wasting" resources, describing it as "the death panel" — cited by Jeffries as revealing the foundation's eugenicist orientation.
- Buffett's wife Susie was a volunteer for the Franklin Credit Union in Nebraska and was close to Larry King (per DeCamp's account).

**American Cancer Society:** Only ~16 percent of over $6 million in yearly donations goes directly to help cancer patients; the organization holds $1 billion in cash reserves; board dominated by pharmaceutical and processed-food industry figures.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries quotes the American Red Cross's own response to his inquiry, in which a spokesperson claimed 91 percent of funds go to "humanitarian services" and cited Better Business Bureau standards. Jeffries disputes this figure and argues "program expenses" do not equal direct relief.
