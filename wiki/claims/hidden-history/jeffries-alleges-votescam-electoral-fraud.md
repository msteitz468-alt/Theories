## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges, relying heavily on the work of brothers Ken and Jim Collier, that American elections have been systematically stolen for decades through a private, secret, unsupervised vote-counting infrastructure controlled by television networks, the League of Women Voters, and a shadowy private corporation (News Election Service / Voter News Service / National Election Pool) — with no public accountability, no transparent software, and no meaningful oversight.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per [[Hidden History (Jeffries)]], the Collier brothers' investigation began in 1970 when Ken ran for Congress in Dade County, Florida, against Claude Pepper. Key allegations:

- **Mathematical impossibility**: Comparing official election results across three separate Dade County elections (September primary, October run-off, November final) for the Florida Senate and governor's races, the Colliers found identical vote totals in each — a statistical impossibility.
- **Network "projections" preceded actual results**: One local TV network predicted the outcome of forty different races involving 250 candidates with near-perfect accuracy only four minutes after polls closed. Another station projected the final total at exactly 96,499 votes. The networks claimed this was derived from a single voting machine's results. When the Colliers confronted programmer Elton Davis at the University of Miami about the formula, he told them: "You'll never prove it, now get out."
- **League of Women Voters fabricating call-ins**: Networks claimed League of Women Voters members were calling in results from the field. The League's head Joyce Deiffenderfer admitted there were no members out that night, and broke down in tears saying "I don't want to get caught up in this thing."
- **Ballot tampering videotaped**: In the early 1980s, the Colliers videotaped volunteers running the same punched ballots through the tabulating computer again and again, and caught other workers "sorting" through already-punched ballots. Jim Collier obtained an armload of blank election tally sheets and confessions from precinct officials who had signed blank sheets weeks before the election.
- **Janet Reno blocked investigation**: The Colliers took their evidence to Dade County state attorney Janet Reno, who instead had Jim Collier arrested for stealing the blank tally sheets.
- **Antonin Scalia killed their lawsuit**: Then-federal appeals judge Scalia filed a "killer memo" dismissing the Colliers' lawsuit against Justice Department lawyer Craig Donsanto, who had refused to prosecute League of Women Voters members caught on tape punching ballots illegally.
- **CIA acknowledged private vote-counting**: A 1980 CIA memo acknowledged that "public officials have abdicated responsibility of aggregation of election night vote totals to a private organization, News Election Service of New York (NES) … This private organization performs without a contract: without supervision by public officials."
- **Voter News Service (VNS) / National Election Pool (NEP)**: Jeffries traces NES into its successor VNS and then NEP. VNS executive director Bill Headline refused to disclose the organization's location on election night. Its software is secret. Victoria Collier (Jim's daughter) could not gain access to their offices or information; when she identified herself as alternative press, Headline ended the call abruptly. VNS was disbanded in 2003 and replaced by the identical NEP.
- **Collier brothers both died young** in the 1990s.

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 8 opens with the 2000 Florida election controversy and immediately situates it within the Colliers' long-running documentation of election fraud. ## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries acknowledges Votescam was first published in The Spotlight, a newspaper his correspondent Victoria Collier considered anti-Semitic and refused to associate her father's work with. Jeffries defends The Spotlight's investigative record.
