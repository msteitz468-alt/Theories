## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the shooting of presidential candidate George Wallace by "lone nut" Arthur
Bremer on May 15, 1972, occurred in a suspicious context that included prior knowledge of the attack
by intelligence officials, possible evidence tampering, and a Nixon White House attempt to exploit
the event politically. Per the book, "once again bullets, not ballots, may have helped influence a
presidential election."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per Jeffries, when two FBI agents entered Bremer's apartment hours after the Wallace shooting, they
found the Secret Service already there — "having been ordered to break into the residence by James
Rowley, who'd been the chief of the Secret Service since before the JFK assassination." The Secret
Service "apparently removed some unknown documents from the apartment." Neither agency secured the
residence afterward; reporters entered freely and also took documents. A "typical 'lone' assassin's
diary" was found, allegedly written by Bremer; author Gore Vidal would later speculate that CIA
operative E. Howard Hunt was its actual author.

CIA operative Hunt maintained that Nixon aide Charles Colson had asked him to break into Bremer's
apartment to find material linking the shooter to Democratic candidate George McGovern, though Colson
subsequently called it off. Martha Mitchell, wife of Attorney General John Mitchell, told George
Wallace himself that her husband had admitted that Charles Colson had met with Bremer four days
prior to the assassination attempt.

Jeffries notes that Nixon tried to get aides H. R. Haldeman and Charles Colson to plant a fake news
story linking Bremer to Ted Kennedy, and that Nixon had earlier removed Secret Service protection
from Kennedy after the 1972 election, stating "If he gets shot, it's too damn bad." In reference to
Chappaquiddick, Nixon aide John Dean was recorded on March 3, 1973, saying "If Kennedy knew the
bear trap he was walking into…."

## Contexts Within the Source

The Wallace shooting is treated as another application of the "lone nut" pattern in a series of
political attacks during the 1960s–70s. Jeffries links it to the broader pattern in [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]] and uses it
to illustrate Nixon's paranoia about and hostility toward the Kennedy legacy and political opponents.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries does not assert outright that the shooting was definitively orchestrated by the government;
he describes the circumstances as suspicious and notes that "whether Wallace could have garnered
enough votes away from Nixon … to swing the election to huge underdog Democrat George McGovern is
highly questionable." His framing is "may have helped influence" rather than a proven conspiracy.
