## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the murder of President John F. Kennedy on November 22, 1963, in Dallas, Texas,
was not the act of a "lone nut" named Lee Harvey Oswald but was instead a conspiracy and cover-up
carried out by powerful actors within or connected to the US government and intelligence community.
Per Jeffries, Oswald was a patsy, the Warren Commission investigation was a deliberate whitewash,
and the crime was never honestly investigated by any authoritative body. Jeffries characterizes the
assassination as "the mother of all conspiracies" and the pivotal event of his book's thesis.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Oswald not the shooter.** Jeffries states that "a few days of intense study of the available record
will convince any honest person, beyond any reasonable doubt, that Lee Harvey Oswald was not
responsible for the crime." He notes: no one could place Oswald in the sixth-floor window with a gun
(per Dallas Police Chief Jesse Curry); multiple witnesses placed him on the first floor or in the
building's doorway at the time of the shooting; he was, per his own Marine colleagues, "a rather
poor shot"; and no genuine expert in official reconstructions could duplicate the shooting feat
under more favorable conditions.

**The weapon.** The rifle found on the sixth floor — a cheap, Italian Mannlicher-Carcano with a
defective scope allegedly ordered under the alias "Alex Hidell" — was initially identified by two
Dallas officers (Seymour Weitzman and Eugene Boone) in separate affidavits as a German Mauser. As
late as midnight on November 22, Dallas DA Henry Wade also called it a Mauser to the press. The
Carcano, Jeffries asserts, would not have been admissible in an honest court because no reliable
chain of custody existed for it. He also cites film showing Dallas police descending from the
roof of the Book Depository with a second weapon that lacked a sight (the Carcano had one).

**The "magic bullet."** Jeffries argues the single-bullet theory (CE399 supposedly causing seven
wounds) is physically impossible. The nearly pristine CE399 was found at Parkland Hospital under
dubious custody: the first three handlers could not identify it as the bullet later placed in
evidence, and the agent who transported it (Richard Johnsen) later said he did not recall having
possession of it and never initialed it. Autopsy photos and x-rays, per Jeffries, contradict each
other and contradict the accounts of Parkland doctors, who consistently described an entrance wound
in Kennedy's throat (implying a shot from the front) and a massive blowout at the back of Kennedy's
head (implying the same). He cites Dr. Malcolm Perry, who said three times at his initial press
conference that the throat wound was an entrance wound, and who was later pressured by Secret
Service agent Elmer Moore to change his testimony.

**The Warren Commission.** Jeffries argues the Commission was designed to produce a predetermined
conclusion. He cites the November 25, 1963, Katzenbach memo to Bill Moyers stating that "the public
must be satisfied that Oswald was the assassin" and that "speculation about Oswald's motivation
ought to be cut off." The Commission included Allen Dulles — the CIA director JFK had fired after
the Bay of Pigs — among its members. Jeffries reproduces Jack Ruby's Warren Commission testimony
to show Ruby was plainly terrified and pleading to be taken to Washington to tell something he
could not say in Dallas; Chief Justice Warren declined, offering "lame excuses." Ruby's later public
statements explicitly implicated Lyndon Johnson.

**Witness intimidation and suspicious deaths.** Jeffries details multiple witnesses who were
threatened or recanted after FBI visits: Darrell Tomlinson (the "magic bullet" finder) told a
researcher the FBI woke him and told him to "keep his mouth shut"; Warren Reynolds changed his
identification of a Tippit murder-scene witness after being shot in the head and his daughter nearly
kidnapped; Richard Carr was told by the FBI "if you didn't see Lee Harvey Oswald up in the School
Book Depository with a rifle, you didn't witness it" and later had dynamite placed in his car and
was stabbed. Journalist Dorothy Kilgallen, who privately interviewed Jack Ruby and vowed to "blow
the case sky high," was found dead on November 8, 1965. Jeffries notes that three people who had
met in Ruby's apartment the day after the Oswald shooting all died within a little over a year:
reporter Bill Hunter (accidentally shot in a police station), Jim Koethe (karate chop to throat),
and lawyer Tom Howard (heart attack at 48).

**Oswald-Ruby connection.** Jeffries cites Dallas DA Bill Alexander's admission that police received
"about 100" reports of an Oswald-Ruby connection; multiple Carousel Club employees testified to
seeing Oswald in Ruby's club, including performer Wally Weston (who punched Oswald) and stripper
Karen Bennett, who told the FBI she believed "Oswald, Ruby and other individuals… were involved in
a plot to assassinate President Kennedy." Jeffries treats the official dismissal of these connections
as evidence of the cover-up.

**Oswald as intelligence operative.** Jeffries cites Oswald's "spy" Minox camera (unavailable to
the public in 1963), his inexplicable defection to the USSR and easy repatriation at the height of
the Cold War, the FBI's 1960 concern that "an imposter is using Oswald's birth certificate," and an
FBI memo stating that CIA and FBI agents who compared Oswald's voice to a recording of the man who
contacted the Soviet Embassy in Mexico City "are of the opinion that the above-referred-to
individual was not Lee Harvey Oswald."

**The Jim Garrison investigation.** Jeffries presents New Orleans DA Jim Garrison's investigation
as the only genuine official inquiry, finding a conspiracy rooted in New Orleans involving David
Ferrie, Clay Shaw, and Guy Banister (all of whom had intelligence and/or organized-crime
connections). Garrison was vindicated, per Jeffries, by former CIA official Victor Marchetti, who
confirmed the Agency assisted Clay Shaw and that Ferrie was a CIA operative. Jeffries notes that
governors of multiple states (including Ronald Reagan of California) refused to extradite witnesses,
and that key Garrison witnesses (Ferrie, Eladio del Valle) were found dead within hours of each
other after the investigation became public.

**The HSCA.** The House Select Committee on Assassinations concluded "probably" a conspiracy,
citing acoustics evidence of a shot from the grassy knoll. Jeffries criticizes its lead counsel
G. Robert Blakey for limiting the inquiry, agreeing to a secrecy pact with the CIA, and supporting
Warren Commission conclusions. He notes that a wave of important witnesses (mobsters Giancana and
Roselli, George DeMohrenschildt, CIA officer William Harvey, former Cuban president Carlos Prio
Socarras, mob figure Charles Nicoletti) died in 1976–77 just as the HSCA was being formed, three
of them in a single month.

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 1 ("The Mother of All Conspiracies") is the longest and most detailed chapter in the book.
It grounds the entire thesis of [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]] and establishes the patterns — lone-nut narrative, cover-up machinery,
witness intimidation, media complicity, and suspicious deaths — that Jeffries then applies to every
subsequent case in the book. The chapter also draws on work by [[Jim Marrs]] (*Crossfire*) and
references the Garrison investigation.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries acknowledges he is an "obsessed researcher" who shares Jim Garrison's theory that Oswald
was assigned to infiltrate a plot he believed was a fake, and states this is "where I think the
evidence logically leads" rather than a certainty. He says the evidence is circumstantial in places
and that the true chain of command behind the assassination remains unknown. He does not claim to
identify the ultimate masterminds, only to demonstrate the physical impossibility of the lone-gunman
theory and the deliberate nature of the cover-up.
