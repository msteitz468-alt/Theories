## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that the federal government's assault on the Branch Davidians at Waco, Texas, was an unjustified, unconstitutional act of violence that killed 86 Americans — including 24 children — for unclear and illogical reasons, and that the official narrative blaming David Koresh for the deaths was a lie contradicted by physical evidence later admitted by the FBI itself.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per [[Hidden History (Jeffries)]], the initial ATF assault on February 8, 1993, was ostensibly to serve a warrant for alleged weapon stockpiling and child abuse — charges Jeffries says were used as pretexts. Key allegations:

- **Cruelty during the siege**: Attorney General Janet Reno authorized agents to crush children's bicycles with armored tanks, blast amplified sounds of rabbits being slaughtered and Tibetan monks chanting, shine blinding lights on bedroom windows at night, and cut off food, water, electricity, and plumbing — all while publicly claiming to be protecting the children.
- **A Davidian's dead body left on a fence**: A Davidian killed in the initial assault was left draped over a fence for days in plain view of those inside; vultures reportedly devoured parts of his body.
- **CS gas and shooting on the final day**: On April 19, 1993, CS gas — banned by the Geneva Convention in warfare — was pumped into the building. Jeffries alleges that autopsies found bullet holes in the heads of Koresh and other Davidians, suggesting they were shot by government agents.
- **Arson concealed**: The FBI initially denied using incendiary devices, but in 1999 admitted that flammable pyrotechnic tear-gas canisters had been used. Infrared videotapes released later showed shots were fired against the compound and incendiary devices were deployed.
- **Child abuse charges fabricated**: The Justice Department's own internal report later admitted there was "no evidence that the Branch Davidian children were mistreated in any way" and "no hint of sexual misconduct on the part of Koresh." Clinton and Reno had repeatedly called Koresh a child abuser but never apologized.
- **Evidence destroyed**: The remnants of the building were demolished and buried by authorities, preventing independent investigation.
- **Cover-up death**: Carlos Ghigliotti, hired by the House Government Reform Committee to analyze surveillance footage using FLIR technology, uncovered evidence contradicting government claims. His badly decomposed body was found in his Laurel, Maryland, lab on April 28, 2000, at age 42. Police declared it "natural causes."

Jeffries also notes that Congress's 1995 investigation was purely partisan, with Democrats defending the Clinton administration.

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 6 treats Waco as perhaps the single most shameful episode of the Clinton years. Jeffries links it to the broader pattern of government violence against citizens (Ruby Ridge) and notes it was cited by Timothy McVeigh as motivation for the Oklahoma City bombing. The Ghigliotti death is linked to [[The political "body count" pattern (Jeffries)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries acknowledges that the conventional account holds Koresh and his followers responsible for setting the fire. He presents the 1999 FBI admissions about pyrotechnic devices as vindicating the critics, but notes the Danforth investigation subsequently "completely vindicated the FBI."
