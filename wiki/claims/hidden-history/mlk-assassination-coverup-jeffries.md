## Claim Statement

Jeffries alleges that Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination on April 4, 1968, in Memphis, Tennessee,
was not the act of "lone nut" James Earl Ray but was a frame-up in which Ray was the patsy. The book
claims the FBI had a strong motive to kill King, that the principal eyewitness against Ray was a
drunk who lacked his glasses, and that an honest witness who contradicted the official account was
imprisoned for years in a mental institution.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Per Jeffries, Ray "was talked into confessing as the only way to avoid the death penalty by his
attorney Percy Foreman, but he recanted three days later and thereafter consistently maintained his
innocence until his death in 1998." Ray maintained he was guided by a mysterious "Raoul" who had
set him up. Ray was "a typical Southerner of his time, but he appeared to harbor no strong political
beliefs," had never physically harmed anyone, and there was no evidence he was a good shot.

The sole witness who identified Ray as fleeing the scene was Charles Stephens, common-law husband of
Grace Walden. However, Jeffries reports that Stephens was drunk at the time of the killing and not
wearing his glasses; a taxi driver who visited his room that evening found him "too drunk to even get
up off the floor." Grace Walden "had seen a man running from the bathroom, too, but she was positive
that it was someone other than James Earl Ray." Because Walden maintained this account, per Jeffries,
"Grace spent over ten years in a mental institution."

The "evidence" bundle found near the scene — containing a rifle, ammunition, binoculars, and other
items — Jeffries describes as "comically convenient" and "incomprehensibly left in a doorway near
the assassination scene." The single slug removed from King's body could not be matched with Ray's
alleged rifle. Witnesses at the scene differed on the origin of the shot, with some pointing to
bushes below the balcony.

Jeffries further notes that the FBI under J. Edgar Hoover had sent King a thinly veiled instruction
in 1964 to take his own life ("King, there is only one thing left for you to do. You know what it
is"), giving the Bureau "a much stronger motive to kill King than James Earl Ray did."

Jeffries also alleges that the "establishment-anointed 'black leader'" Jesse Jackson falsely claimed
to have cradled King's head as he died, appearing on NBC the next day in a shirt he claimed was
still stained with King's blood, when in fact Jackson was not even on the balcony. MLK aide Hosea
Williams is quoted as saying, "I knew Jesse was lying."

Per the book, attorney general Ramsey Clark prematurely declared there was "no conspiracy" to kill
King, mirroring his premature statement about the RFK case.

## Contexts Within the Source

The MLK assassination is treated as a parallel to the JFK and RFK cases under the same pattern:
lone-nut designation, suppression of inconvenient witnesses, and a cover-up protecting the real
perpetrators. It links to [[Suppressed conspiracies and cover-ups in modern American politics (Hidden History thesis)]] and the broader argument about targeted political killings.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Jeffries notes that the Coretta Scott King and Dexter King worked to help Ray get a new trial in
the late 1990s but Ray died before he could be vindicated, and leaves open how high in the chain of
command the conspiracy reached. He does not speculate beyond the FBI motive.
