## Claim Statement

The authors identify the two "most dominant elements" of the contemporary problematique as "the population explosion in the South" and "the only recently recognized macro-effects of man on his environment" — the same two preoccupations as the Club's 1972 *Limits to Growth*. They treat global warming, water shortage, famine, and resource depletion as interacting environmental threats demanding coordinated action.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

King and Schneider state these "were exactly the two central preoccupations in *The Limits to Growth*," now joined by new factors such as "changes in human behaviour, the emergence of seemingly irrational movements including terrorism, and the growth of individual and collective selfishness." A chapter on "Global Warming and its implications" warns it "threatens the world's economic and social system" and calls its prevention "one of the greatest challenges." They frame population pressure as a driver of the broader predicament.
