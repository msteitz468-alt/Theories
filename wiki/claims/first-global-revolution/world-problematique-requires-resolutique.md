## Claim Statement

King and Schneider argue that humanity's problems — political, economic, social, cultural, psychological, technological, and environmental — are not separate but form one interacting "world problematique," and that they must therefore be met not piecemeal but with a "resolutique": a coherent, comprehensive, and *simultaneous* response that weighs each solution's impact on all the others.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The Club defines the problematique as "the massive and untidy mix of interrelated difficulties and problems that form the predicament in which humanity finds itself," and credits *The Limits to Growth* (1972, using Jay Forrester's MIT system-dynamics modelling) with first projecting these interactions quantitatively. They write that to "tackle the global problematique problem by problem and on a country-by-country basis can only worsen the situation," so "the task that faces us is… to grasp a thousand nettles at once." They explicitly deny ever advocating "a zero growth economy," saying they accepted "the pressing need for material growth in the poor countries."
