## Claim Statement

The authors argue that the end of the Cold War created an ideological and motivational "vacuum": with traditional enemies gone, nations and peoples — who, they say, have always needed "a common adversary against whom they can organize themselves and act together" — are left without unifying purpose, a void they call "a source of discouragement and even despair."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "It would seem that men and women need a common motivation, namely a common adversary against whom they can organize themselves and act together… The need for enemies seems to be a common historical factor."

They note states have historically "overcome domestic failure… by blaming external enemies," warn that "with the disappearance of the traditional enemy, the temptation is to use religious or ethnic minorities as scapegoats," and ask "Can we live without enemies?" — observing that "new enemies have to be identified… They threaten the whole human race, and their names are pollution, water shortage, famine, malnutrition, illiteracy, and unemployment." They then turn to human rights and democracy as the new "axes of reference."
