## Claim Statement

In Chapter 7, opening Part II (the "resolutique"), King and Schneider identify three urgent priorities — the "Three Immediacies" — that they propose as the focus of immediate global action: (1) the conversion of the arms industry to civil uses following disarmament; (2) securing "an environment for survival" (above all the prevention of global warming and irreversible macro-pollution); and (3) addressing the development of the poor countries of the South.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

- Ch. 7, "The Three Immediacies." The chapter restates the resolutique as "a simultaneous and comprehensive attack on the main problems at many levels," coherent in that "it attempts to look at the consequences of possible solutions to particular elements of difficulty on all levels." It "runs counter to traditional methods of planning," so that "the task that faces us is… to grasp a thousand nettles at once."
- **First immediacy — arms conversion:** "Conversion of arms plants to constructive civil uses is thus the currently accepted remedy." The authors discuss large-scale demobilization and conversion already initiated in the USSR and China, and note that in the West "only Sweden has developed an active policy of conversion," arguing "the state must play an active role" and stressing the need for "extensive retraining facilities which only governments can provide."
- **Second immediacy — "an environment for survival":** the emphasis "must be in preventing the development of… macro-pollution… to the level at which its effects are irreversible. By far the most urgent of these is global warming." They prescribe "four lines of attack": reduction of global carbon-dioxide emission (and of fossil-fuel use), afforestation especially in the tropics, development of alternative energy sources, and energy conservation and efficiency, citing the Toronto target of a 20 per cent emission cut by 2035.
- **Third immediacy — development versus underdevelopment:** "The third immediacy is a crucial element in the first global revolution." Citing World Bank estimates that "one billion human beings" in the countries of the South live below the poverty line (up from 500 million in the early 1980s), the authors warn that "underdevelopment, poverty, famine and malnutrition will persist."

## Contexts Within the Source

The Three Immediacies are presented not as the whole of the resolutique but as its most urgent starting points — "this does not exclude other types of action." The chapter is the hinge between the book's diagnosis (Part I, the problematique) and its prescription (Part II), and frames these priorities as simultaneous rather than sequential, in keeping with the resolutique's insistence on attacking interlocking problems all at once.
