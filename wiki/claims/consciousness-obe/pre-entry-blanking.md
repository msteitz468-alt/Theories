## Claim Statement

Monroe asserts that every consciousness entering human physical incarnation undergoes a deliberate blanking of its pre-entry identity, memory, and rote — the erasure of who and what it was before the human life began. This blanking is described as a required condition for the learning system to function at "maximum efficiency." Without it, the soul would retain its non-physical knowledge and the compressed learning environment would not generate genuine growth. The blanking is presented as consensual: souls formally agree to it in a pre-entry process described as analogous to a legal contract.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 10, Monroe presents the pre-entry agreement as a formal document read by the character AA before entering the human cycle:

"Agreement and Understanding. Human sojourn only. First Entry Status (FES). Organized as a school for compressed learning. Successful graduates achieve tangent rating. For duration of human sojourn, firm agreement that time-space exists, has reality… In order for learning system to function at maximum efficiency, temporary blanking of pre-entry activity is essential. Agreement to perform such blanking is hereby authorized."

The document continues: "Anything to the contrary contained herein notwithstanding, all FES remain fully established in freedom of will and consciousness at the entry point. This is guaranteed and is required by the compressed learning system in use."

The Entry Director character (Ed) explains to BB that the population of the outer ring — all the souls waiting to re-enter human life — has forgotten who they were: "They did it, all of 'em." When BB protests that losing one's identity is impossible, Ed responds: "Nobody does that… Lose what rote?... Who they were. They forgot who they were." Ed clarifies: "They are made of three types. One is the First-Timer, such as your friend. He just started to forget. Then there's the Old-Timers, who mostly remember after going the route… These hang around and do what they can to help. They don't remember quite enough to go home."

Monroe's Chapter 9 INSPEC experience illuminates the consequence: when young Monroe, as a child on his tricycle, was stunned by a nearby lightning strike, he momentarily accessed pre-entry knowledge and remembered what the INSPEC calls "the knowing" — "a glimpse of who you are before you undertake the human experience." The INSPEC states: "Such momentary recall occurs frequently during the physical process but is obscured and discarded amid the more immediate consistent impact of physical information input."

## Contexts Within the Source

Monroe frames the blanking not as an injustice but as a structural necessity: "In order for learning system to function at maximum efficiency" — if souls entered physical life with full awareness of their non-physical nature, the experiential depth of human learning would be undermined. The paradox is that forgetting is itself part of what makes the system productive.

Monroe uses this doctrine to explain the Gateway Program's spiritual function: participants "begin to know, rather than believe, that he does survive physical death" because they are recovering, partially and temporarily, the pre-entry knowledge that was blanked. The Gateway is thus presented as a partial un-blanking rather than the acquisition of new knowledge.
