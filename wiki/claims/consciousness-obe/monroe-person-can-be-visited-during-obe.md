## Claim Statement

Monroe asserts that during an OBE it is possible to travel to and observe a specific living or deceased person, and that the visit can produce verifiable perceptions of what that person is doing. However, Monroe also reports a consistent limitation: the person visited typically has no conscious memory of the encounter.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

### Living Persons: Methodology and Evidential Results

Monroe developed a specific technique for visiting people: think of the person's personality rather than their physical location, then "sense" the direction and travel toward it. He states the governing rule: "you must 'go' to a person, not to a place."

The introduction to the book (written by parapsychologist Dr. Charles Tart) describes the laboratory context for one of Monroe's best-evidenced visits:

Monroe reported during an EEG laboratory session that he "rolled out" of his body and found himself in the hallway — and "he did correctly describe that the laboratory technician was out of the room, and that a man (later identified as her husband) was with her in a corridor."

Monroe's own notes describe multiple visits to acquaintances with partial confirmation. One example (4/12/63) describes arriving at a house while concentrating on a friend, finding a woman in the garden who "looked up directly at me. With a frightened start, she scuttled into the house." Another describes visiting Dr. Puharich: "in checking with Dr. Puharich, the Locale was right, and actions were correct, but he has no memory of visit."

### The Problem of Non-Memory

Monroe identifies a consistent and frustrating pattern: the visited person recalls nothing. In one case, Monroe visited a colleague (Dr. Puharich) who had been specifically informed that Monroe would attempt to visit him. Despite this preparation, the colleague "had no conscious recollection of any such meeting." Monroe notes:

> "This has happened so frequently in such instances that it became the source of much discussion."

He proposes an explanation: communication in the Second State occurs "at a level above conscious awareness" and "the communicant rarely has any memory of it afterward."

### Visiting a Deceased Person

Monroe's most detailed account of visiting a deceased person is his attempt to reach Dr. Richard Gordon after his death from cancer. Monroe describes eventually encountering a young man who matched a photograph of Dr. Gordon at age 22 — not recognizing him at first because he had expected an elderly man. Monroe concludes that "post-death" encounters are possible in Locale II.

### Long-Distance Experiment (Cross-Country Visit)

Dr. Tart describes an experiment in which he and his wife concentrated on Monroe from California at an agreed-upon time to "pull" him across the country to their home. Monroe felt a tugging sensation and reported traveling to them — the time match was accurate (Monroe felt the pull within minutes of when Tart began), though Monroe's description of the house interior and what Tart was doing was "not good at all." Tart characterizes the result as "frustrating" — neither definitively evidential nor definitively null.

## Contexts Within the Source

Monroe is candid about the low success rate of verifiable Locale I visits: "the only evidential results provable by standard methods of confirmation have taken place while moving about via the Second Body in Locale I" — yet such results are "pitifully few in proportion to all the recorded experiments." He attributes failures to inability to control thought, to misidentify locations from aerial perspectives, and to the tendency of the Second Body to drift toward Locale II.
