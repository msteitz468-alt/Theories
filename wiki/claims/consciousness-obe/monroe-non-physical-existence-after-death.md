## Claim Statement

Monroe asserts, on the basis of his own OBE experiments, that consciousness survives physical death in some form — continuing to exist in Locale II or an equivalent non-physical realm — though he frames this as a provisional conclusion from accumulated evidence rather than certainty.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Monroe opens Chapter 7 with the question directly:

> "Any acknowledgment of the existence of the Second Body immediately demands the question mankind has pondered since the day he learned to think: do we live on? Is there life beyond the grave?"

He states his conclusion from the evidence:

> "If the human being has a Second Body, if that Second Body survives what we call death, if personality and character continue to exist in this new — old form — what then? Again, an age-old question that pleads for an answer."

> "The conclusion that some form of existence in Locale II follows life activity in the known physical world goes beyond conjecture."

### The Case of Dr. Richard Gordon

Monroe's primary evidential account involves a visit to his deceased family physician, Dr. Gordon. After Dr. Gordon's death from cancer, Monroe attempted to reach him in the non-physical:

> "For several months I thought about an attempt to 'go' to Dr. Gordon, wherever he was. He was the first person close to me who had died since the development of my 'wild talent'. I was both curious and objective."

After an unsuccessful first attempt, Monroe was informed during his second attempt that he had already seen the doctor: "Why do you want to see him again? You saw him last Saturday!" On reviewing his notes, Monroe realized he had in fact encountered a young version of the doctor on the previous visit — a short, thin man with a shock of hair who had looked at him intently — matching a photograph of Dr. Gordon at age 22, not the 70-year-old Monroe expected.

### The "Newcomer" Experience

Monroe describes encounters in Locale II with individuals apparently undergoing a post-death transition:

> "On one occasion, I had just left the physical when I felt an urgent need to go 'somewhere'. Yielding to the insistence, I moved what seemed to be a short distance and stopped suddenly in a bedroom. A boy was lying in the bed, alone. He seemed about ten or eleven in age… The boy was lonely and afraid, and seemed ill."

On a subsequent OBE, Monroe found the same boy again: "'What do I do now?' He asked it matter-of-factly. 'Where do I go?'" Monroe reports that the following day's newspaper carried the death notice of a ten-year-old boy who had died shortly before Monroe's first experiment.

Monroe also describes a parklike gathering place in Locale II where "newcomers" waited:

> "Somehow I knew that this was a meeting place, where newly arrived waited for friends or relatives. From this Place of Meeting, these friends would take each newcomer to the proper place where he or she 'belonged'."

### Inference from Locale II Structure

Monroe states that the "near" areas of Locale II — closest to the physical world in "vibratory frequency" — appear to be populated partly by those who have recently died but remain emotionally conditioned: "quite probably those who are 'dead' but still emotionally driven." He infers that the early post-death period involves the same process of emotional adaptation that living OBE travelers must undergo.

### Monroe's Stated Degree of Certainty

Monroe consistently presents these conclusions as evidence-based inference rather than proof. Chapter 7 opens with: "All I can do is be as reportorial and objective as one can be in a basically subjective experience." The chapter title "Post Mortem" signals his attempt at clinical examination of the question.

## Contexts Within the Source

Monroe wrote an explicit letter to the dying Dr. Gordon inviting him to experiment with OBE technique as a form of preparation, showing Monroe believed the skill was practically relevant to the transition of death: "you might find many ways that it can be helpful… With all the sincerity I can muster, I urge you, Dick, to think about it."
