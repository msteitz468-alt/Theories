## Claim Statement

Monroe asserts that surrounding the physical Earth are multiple concentric "rings" or bands of post-death consciousness — zones inhabited by souls at various stages of post-physical development. These rings are non-physical but real; they can be entered via OBE and are populated by vast numbers of souls. Monroe describes distinct zones within this system: an innermost ring of newly deceased who do not realize they have died; inner rings of souls trapped in repetitive post-death states (including one he describes as a mass of humanity trapped in compulsive sexual seeking); middle rings of souls in self-constructed dreamlike environments preparing for reincarnation; an outer ring containing "First-Timers," "Old-Timers," "Last-Timers," and "Seekers." Monroe describes these rings as the former "Locale II" of his first book but considers that label an understatement.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From the Chapter 11 Rescue Mission, Monroe describes the innermost zone in detail: "I began to feel uncomfortable and my percept indicated BB was more so.... Almost immediately, I began to feel uncomfortable. Any prospect of locating AA was totally up to him. I threaded our way among the countless forms hanging motionless. Actually, their movement was so slow as to be almost imperceptible. These were the ones who had just been released from their physical body via death and vaguely knew they had but didn't have the rote to do much, if anything, about it."

Monroe describes the noise of this zone: "The M Band noise was thunderous, a cacophony of fear, anger, and about every other human emotion, every desire, every need connected with human physical existence. All open, naked, uncovered, up front."

The inner ring adjacent to physical: "The inhabitants of this ring couldn't. They didn't or couldn't or wouldn't realize they were no longer physical. They were physically dead, no more physical body. Thus they kept trying to be physical, to do and be what they had been... Bewildered, some spent all of their activity in attempting to communicate with friends and loved ones still in bodies... Others were held attracted to physical sites in which they had instilled great meaning."

From Chapters 7 (Surveys and Schematics): Monroe describes being taken by his INSPEC guide to observe a zone "an enormous pile of writhing forms... each wet slippery form wiggling in and out among the others in the pile, searching, trying to do something... but never achieving satisfaction." The forms are human, physically dead, emanating sexuality, "so focused and intent on seeking sexual satisfaction they were unaware of any other existence." Monroe attempts to extract a man from this mass to speak to him but cannot get his attention.

Middle rings: "Houses, parks, fields of growing plants, woods, forests, large buildings, rows of churches, it went on endlessly. Humanoid forms were busily occupying themselves in numerous earth-type activities." These constructs are non-physical, assembled from consciousness; Monroe's KT-95 companion BB cannot perceive them because he lacks the human ident reference.

Outer ring (from Chapter 10, via BB/Ed dialogue): Contains: First-Timers (newly entering the human cycle, beginning to forget their prior existence), Old-Timers (have been human many times, remember enough to help but not enough to go "Home"), Last-Timers (final human incarnation before departure), and Seekers (humans still in physical bodies who "flick in and out"). Monroe describes Last-Timers as radiating "tremendous vital power that seemed totally under control. Within that strength were all of the values and ideals that humans hold important... They were completely open."

## Contexts Within the Source

Monroe presents these rings consistently across multiple chapters, both through his personal OBE observation and through the AA/BB narrative frame (Chapter 10), which uses the Entry Station and its rings as a plot setting. The outer ring functions as a processing zone between physical incarnations; the inner rings are characterized as areas of consciousness difficulty or entrapment.

Monroe expresses compassion for those in the inner rings and "anger at a system that could so inhibit, repress, and distort as to create the situation in front of me." He does not resolve whether the system was designed with such entrapment zones intentionally or whether they represent dysfunction.
