## Claim Statement

Parapsychologist Charles Tart argues that because the out-of-body experience convinces the experiencer that they will survive death, it is "one of the most important psychological experiences," and that "most of our great religious traditions are based on this sort of experience."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Tart, quoted by Russell:

> "Because of the immense effect on the individual's belief system — namely, convincing him that he will survive death — the OBE is one of the most important psychological experiences… I am convinced that most of our great religious traditions are based on this sort of experience. We will not be able to understand our religious heritage or our philosophies of life until we come to an understanding of OBEs."

Tart laments that OBEs are "one of the world's most important and most neglected phenomena," ignored even by psychic researchers. The foreword frames Tart's six premises, beginning "You have a soul," and casts Monroe as "a sane, down-to-earth, solid, and successful American who discovered his soul."

## Contexts Within the Source

Tart's framing elevates Monroe's case (see [[Monroe approached his OBEs as a skeptical reporter, and his explanatory concepts evolved]]) into a general claim about human religion — compatible in spirit with Monroe's own conclusion that the OBE implies survival of death.
