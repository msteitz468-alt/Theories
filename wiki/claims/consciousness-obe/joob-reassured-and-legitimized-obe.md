## Claim Statement

Russell records that Monroe's *Journeys Out of the Body* (1971) had an impact far beyond Monroe's expectations: it reassured large numbers of people who had had spontaneous OBEs that they were "not going mad" and "did not need treatment," and it helped move the out-of-body experience from fringe taboo toward a topic of serious scientific discussion.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Russell reports the book "has been reprinted almost every year since," with sales "approaching a million copies" in several languages, and that by 1976 Monroe had received "more than 15,000 letters" from readers describing their own OBEs, "most of them adding how relieved they were not to be psychotic." Monroe's two stated purposes were to comfort fellow experiencers and "to encourage science to expand its horizons." Russell quotes Monroe (1982) on the shift in attitudes — "Now it's OK to talk about out-of-body experiences as a reality" — citing the presentation of three papers on OBEs before the American Psychiatric Association, "which ten years ago would have been unheard of." Monroe said publication brought him "out of the closet, instead of being a… hidden-away person."
