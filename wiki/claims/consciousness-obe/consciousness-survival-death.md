## Claim Statement

Monroe asserts that consciousness — the non-physical energy pattern that is the "real" self — survives physical death, and that this is a direct experiential fact rather than a belief system. He distinguishes sharply between "knowing" and "believing": Gateway Program participants come to "know, rather than believe, that he does survive physical death. It doesn't make any difference what he does during his physical life, what he believes; he will go on after his physical body dies. Survival beyond physical death is not a belief system, therefore, but a simple fact as natural as being born."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Chapter 3 (Gateway Program): "The individual, of his own accord, without suggestion but through direct experience, begins to know, rather than believe, that he does survive physical death. It doesn't make any difference what he does during his physical life, what he believes; he will go on after his physical body dies. Survival beyond physical death is not a belief system, therefore, but a simple fact as natural as being born. There is no dogma related to the Gateway Program."

Chapter 1 (Old Local Traffic): Monroe describes his INSPEC's response to a question about his most recent past life: "Your last human life was spent as a monk in a monastery in Coshocton, Pennsylvania." When Monroe followed up with a Catholic monsignor friend, the friend confirmed a monastery had existed at a place called Coshocton, Pennsylvania.

Chapter 9 (Rainbow Route): Monroe's simulation/demonstration includes an episode in which he inhabits the perspective of a spouse waiting for a partner's return, then realizes it "doesn't make any difference which of us" had released from physical — the result is the same. The INSPEC confirms: "That is correct. The result is the same." This is Monroe's experiential proof that the post-death continuity of consciousness applies not as a doctrine but as an observed fact.

In the Chapter 9 epilogue (the poem-like summary Monroe writes after returning from the INSPEC realm): "For those who would die, there is life. For those who would dream, there is reality. For those who would hope, there is knowledge. For those who would grow, there is eternity."

From an INSPEC statement in Chapter 8: "In short, like it or not, you're going to continue to do and be after you can no longer hang in there physically. There is no rest not only for the wicked but for everyone else." (Monroe paraphrases this as his own synthesis of the INSPEC teachings.)

## Contexts Within the Source

Monroe is careful to separate this claim from any specific religious tradition: "There is no dogma related to the Gateway Program." He presents the claim as empirically arrived at through his personal OBE exploration and through the convergent reports of thousands of Gateway participants and Explorer team subjects. He does not define what "going on" after death means in terms of theological concepts (heaven, hell, reincarnation) except insofar as the ring/Focus level model provides a structural description.

Monroe acknowledges from Chapter 1 that the framework he uses to describe the non-physical contains choices: he deliberately avoided the term "astral projection" because of its occult connotations, adopting "out-of-body experience" (coined by Charles Tart) as a more neutral term.
