## Claim Statement

Monroe asserts, and cites independent survey data to support, that approximately 25 percent of the adult human population has had at least one spontaneous OBE. He further asserts that everyone enters an OBE-like state during deep delta sleep as a natural process, suggesting the remembered 25 percent represents only those who are aware of the experience. Multiple academic surveys across diverse populations are reproduced in the appendices, showing consistent incidence rates ranging from 14 to 34 percent across different sample types.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Monroe states in Chapter 1: "Recent surveys indicate some 25 percent of our population remembers having at least one such experience."

In Appendix I he elaborates: "We take the position that everyone goes into the OOBE state during the delta or deep-sleep state, as a natural process and in varying degrees. Therefore, one step may be to begin to remember the OOB patterns you have or are performing each night when you sleep."

The Appendix II paper (Twemlow, Gabbard, Jones — presented to APA 1980) compiles the following surveys:

- Hart (1954): 27.1% of 155 college students reported having had an OBE
- Green (1968): 34% of 380 Oxford undergraduates responded affirmatively to "Have you ever had an experience in which you felt that you were out of your body?"
- Palmer and Dennis (1975): 25% of students and 14% of townspeople in a randomly selected Virginia sample of 1,000 reported having had an OBE
- Shiels (cross-cultural): data from nearly 70 non-Western cultures showed "strikingly similar" OBE beliefs; authors cite shaman OBE capacity as a universal criterion for shamanic initiation
- South African study (122 accounts): OBE occurred most often while subject was asleep, relaxed, or dozing; over 50% of subjects claimed to be in normal mental state when it occurred

The Twemlow et al. survey itself drew 339 confirmed OBE reporters from a national periodical interview (circulation 15 million). Of these: 66% had more than one OBE; 74 subjects had more than 10 OBEs. The OBE group scored as the "norm" group on the Profile of Adaptation to Life (PAL) psychological health scale — "representing a broad range of educational age and geographic characteristics as well as having a good psychological and physical adjustment."

## Contexts Within the Source

Monroe uses the incidence data to establish that OBEs are not pathological rarities but a normal feature of human experience. He notes that public acceptance has shifted dramatically: "In most academic and intellectual societies, it is now quite acceptable to talk about OOBEs" — though he acknowledges most people remain unaware of "this facet of their lives."

The psychiatric survey data is presented not as validation of Monroe's cosmological model but as epidemiological context: the data establishes who has OBEs and under what conditions, not what the OBE is. Monroe endorses the survey authors' finding that OBEs are not associated with psychopathology.
