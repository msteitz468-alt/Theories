## Claim Statement

Russell argues that Monroe — "the almost traditional businessman and father," president of a multimillion-dollar corporation — approached his out-of-body experiences as a methodical, skeptical reporter who recorded events first and theorized cautiously, and that his explanatory framework was provisional and changed over time as he tried to "make sense of what was happening."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Russell stresses the "tension between the experiences themselves and the author's attempts to explain them, to make them fit… into a frame or a pattern." He documents that Monroe rejected the loaded term "astral" (with its "witchcraft, sorcery, incantations" associations) in favor of neutral coinages — "Second Body," "Second State," and Locales I–III. He notes that Monroe's early, physicalist concepts — especially the **"Second Body" and the connecting "cord"** (linked in older literature to the biblical "silver cord") — "become rarer and eventually disappear altogether" in the later books, suggesting "the cord is a mental construct" the mind created "as a sort of safety mechanism." A 1960s Topeka VA / Stuart Twemlow study "revealed nothing untoward in his personality," and Tart praised him for recognizing how his own mind forces experiences "into familiar patterns."

## Internal Tensions or Multiple Accounts

Russell raises but does not resolve whether some Locale II experiences were **lucid dreams** (Monroe's experience/dream distinction "does not hold up in the light of subsequent work on lucid dreaming"), and notes Monroe himself sometimes "finds it difficult to distinguish between the two."
