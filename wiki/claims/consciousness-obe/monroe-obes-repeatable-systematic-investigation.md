## Claim Statement

Monroe asserts that unlike the typical once-in-a-lifetime OBE, his experiences were repeatable on demand and subjected to systematic recording, analysis, and laboratory testing — making them, he claims, a proper subject for scientific study rather than mere anecdote.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

### The Problem of Repeatability

The introduction (by Dr. Charles Tart) identifies repeatability as the key gap in prior OBE research:

> "The fact that most of our knowledge about OOBEs comes from reports of once-in-a-lifetime experiences puts us at two serious disadvantages. The first of these is that most people cannot produce an OOBE at will, so this precludes the possibility of studying them under precise laboratory conditions… It would be of great advantage in studying OOBEs to have trained 'travelers' available who could produce the experience at will and who generally had the characteristics of a good reporter."

Tart presents Monroe as exactly such a trained traveler: "Robert A. Monroe is a successful businessman who began experiencing OOBEs quite unexpectedly over a decade ago. Coming from an academic family and having more than the average intellectual training, he realized the unusualness of these experiences and began taking systematic notes from the beginning."

### Monroe's Own Claim to Repeatability

In discussing specific Locale III experiments, Monroe states explicitly:

> "These were not spontaneous incidents, but deliberately planned and systematically repeated. As such, they would appear to be unique. Second, the experiment was repeatable by formula: (1) the setting up of the 'vibration' condition; followed by (2) a 180deg rotation; and (3) the appearance of the 'hole'. The experiment was performed not once but at least eleven times."

Monroe's broader claim: "The greatest proof of the existence of any particular phenomenon is consistency through repeated observation. Only through such reasonably careful analytical experiments, or as much so as I could make them, did I come to the conclusion of the unqualified existence of the Second Body."

### Laboratory Experiments

Monroe participated in formal EEG laboratory studies at the University of Virginia Medical School and later in California (with Dr. Tart). These involved brain wave monitoring, eye movement measurement, and heart rate recording during attempts to produce OBEs on demand. On one session, Monroe produced two brief OBEs during measurement; one showed a "Stage 1 pattern" EEG with a blood pressure drop coinciding with Monroe's reported separation.

Monroe also participated in a cross-country visit experiment in which the time match between his reported experience and the Tart household's concentration was "extremely good."

### The Statistical Chapter

Chapter 19 presents Monroe's own percentile analysis of his hundreds of recorded experiments, classifying them by locale type (Locale I, II, III, or unclassified), and summarizing conclusions: "(1) There does exist a Second Body interspersed or in conjunction with the physical body. (2) The Second Body can move and act independently of the physical body. (3) These movements and actions can be made partially under the control of the conscious mind."

### Acknowledged Limits

Monroe is also direct about what his investigation has not achieved: "Problems of controlled movement to predetermined location and unhampered immediate return to the physical body remained unsolved." He acknowledges that "this inability to control destination has been and still remains the chief barrier to the production of consistency and repeatability."

Monroe concludes: "Only extended exploration of the Second Body phenomenon by an expanded research group can bring about truly comparative studies of these areas. All that is needed is motivation."

## Contexts Within the Source

The book's final chapters pivot explicitly to methodology — a "Preliminary Exercises" chapter and "The Separation Process" chapter providing step-by-step instructions so that others may reproduce his results. Monroe's stated goal is to enable a critical mass of independent investigators: "We are speaking of something entirely different, which can be objectively investigated. The experimenter will want to proceed in a manner that will produce consistent results, perhaps not every time, but often enough to validate the evidence to his own satisfaction."
