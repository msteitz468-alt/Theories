## Claim Statement

Monroe asserts that he made direct contact with beings he calls INSPECs (short for "Intelligent Species"), which he identifies as non-physical intelligences of a profoundly different order from the former-human helper beings found in the inner rings around Earth. INSPECs existed before human life began (by Monroe's reckoning, several million years before), exist in vast numbers (Monroe receives a percept of "more than the current in-physical human population, perhaps one hundred times as many"), and have been involved with human consciousness since before the human experiment began. Their stated function is "fine-tuning" the flow of energy through the human learning process, and their technology far exceeds anything in human culture or comprehension.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Monroe's first major INSPEC encounter is described in Chapter 8. He homes in on the INSPEC's "ident" signal and reaches a point described as "just outside the portal, the gate to our reality, a conversion point." He encounters a "bright light, very intense, glowed in front of me, first ovate, reshaping into a tall humanoid form, so bright I cringed from it." The INSPEC communicates by answering Monroe's questions before he asks them.

Key statements extracted from this encounter:

On origin: "We are created and we create, just as you. If God and creator are identical, then you are God to that which you create."

On the relationship between INSPECs and humans: "The essence that is you and others in the human process was not our creation. As we, you exist before time-space human earth pattern, just as you do at this point. The in-human experience is merely an addition to what you are. However, it is an important addition."

On INSPEC numbers: Monroe asks whether the INSPEC and humans share a common origin. The INSPEC's response implies several hundred billion, described as "more than the current in-physical human population, perhaps one hundred times as many" — yet "a very few" relative to the INSPEC total.

On their function: Monroe receives a percept of "an immense machine … infinitely complex and complicated … with INSPECs climbing all over it, in and out of it, turning a knob here, a valve there, taking off a filter and cleaning it, matching waveforms on an oscilloscope." The INSPEC corrects the image: "We did not create time-space as you know it, nor the physical earth, nor the human process, nor the energy flow itself. That is not our department. Our concern is the output and the … quality of such. To this end, we adjust the internal flow as needed."

On their appearance to humans throughout history: Monroe wonders if INSPECs have been perceived as angels, gods, the devil. The INSPEC acknowledges that "certain adjustments had to be made" when they were perceived in human history, because "free will is such a vital part of the human learning experience, deviations from the design are frequent and predicted... Such adjustments are no more than… fine-tuning."

On UFOs: Monroe suggests INSPECs are what humans perceive as UFOs. The INSPEC responds: "Such are manifestations of another pattern, of which you will soon become aware" — implying UFOs and INSPECs are distinct phenomena.

In Chapter 9 (Rainbow Route), Monroe visits the INSPEC realm and discovers that what he had labeled "INSPECs" is in fact a single unified INSPEC composed of many merged I-There clusters: "We know who we are, and one I laughs and we all laugh at the name this I had given us. We are an INSPEC, just one. There are many others around us."

## Contexts Within the Source

Monroe uses the INSPEC label because it "will serve nicely at this point, as well as any other" and to avoid cultural-philosophical connotations of terms like "angels" or "gods." He notes that he "fondly began to call them INSPECs — short for 'intelligent species,' which presumes humans are not quite so."

Monroe acknowledges the paradox that INSPEC beings were answering his questions before he asked them, meaning the communication is so natural and rapid he "had missed it completely." He frames INSPECs as beyond friendship, beyond brotherhood, beyond parent — describing the emotional quality of their radiation as something "for which there was no appropriate human word."
