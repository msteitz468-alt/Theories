## Claim Statement

Monroe asserts that the out-of-body experience (OBE) is a distinct state of consciousness that is neither hallucination, dream, nor lucid dream. In an OBE, the individual is "near-totally conscious, as our civilization defines the state," with most or all physical sensory perception replicated from an external vantage point. The experience is distinguished above all by its extreme reality — "as 'real' as any physical life experience" — and by the individual's absolute certainty that they are not dreaming.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the Appendix I FAQ section, Monroe distinguishes OBE from dreaming: in a lucid dream, the dreamer can control dream content; in an OBE, the individual "can observe events taking place, but you cannot change or significantly affect them" and "can verify the authenticity of such events subsequently if you so desire." He states the individual cannot participate substantially in physical activity "because you are not 'physical.'"

In Chapter 1, Monroe describes the OBE state: "a condition where you find yourself outside of your physical body, fully conscious and able to perceive and act as if you were functioning physically — with several exceptions. You can move through space (and time?) slowly or apparently somewhere beyond the speed of light. You can observe, participate in events, make willful decisions based upon what you perceive and do. You can move through physical matter such as walls, steel plates, concrete, earth, oceans, air, even atomic radiation without effort or effect."

The Appendix II psychiatric survey data (Twemlow, Gabbard, Jones) supports this phenomenologically: subjects in the 339-person survey were "emphatic" that they knew the difference between a dream and an OBE, describing it as "more real than a dream."

## Contexts Within the Source

Monroe frames this claim as foundational to all his research. The distinction from dreaming and hallucination is the premise that justifies everything else in the book. He notes that when he first began having OBEs in 1958, medical experts recommended "psychotherapy for 'minor hallucinatory dysfunction'" — a diagnosis he rejected. He states he is "reasonably sane in a not so reasonably sane world" after 25 years of exploration, and that responsible exploration has not harmed him physically or psychologically.
