**Claim Statement**
Each of the twelve alchemists seated at the Golden Table in the Symbola is introduced with a Latin motto and an accompanying woodcut depicting a symbolic scene relevant to his teaching, followed by a biographical/legendary account and a closing set of syllogistic objections and responses ("Argumenta et Responsiones").

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Examples given by Craven: Hermes's motto, "Sol est ejus conjugii Pater, et alba Luna Mater," with a woodcut of Hermes pointing to sun and moon surrounded by flame; Maria Hebraea's motto about smoke and a five-flowered plant; Democritus pointing to a nearly-naked female holding a burning heart; Morien pointing to a man treading a dung-heap; Avicenna pointing to a flying eagle held down by a creeping toad, motto "Aquila volans per aerem et Bufo gradiens per terram est Magisterium"; Albertus Magnus pointing to a hermaphrodite figure, motto "Omnes concordant in uno, qui est bifidus"; Arnold of Villanova's woodcut of a marriage, motto "Lapis habetur ex Matrimonio Chabrici et Beiæ"; Thomas Aquinas pointing to an artificial hill with furnace; Raymund Lullius's woodcut of parents teaching a child to walk; Roger Bacon with a pair of equally-poised scales; Melchior Cibinensis saying Mass with a vision of the Virgin and Child; and the Anonymous guest, a man with a wooden leg watering trees bearing suns and moons as fruit.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven observes that "in the illustrated title-pages of Maier's works more information is communicated to the capable student than in whole volumes of other writers," reflecting the emblematic method used throughout Maier's oeuvre.
