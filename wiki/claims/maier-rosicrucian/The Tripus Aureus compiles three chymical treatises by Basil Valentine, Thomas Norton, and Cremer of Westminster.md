**Claim Statement**
Maier's *Tripus Aureus* (1618) collects and first prints together three selected chymical treatises: (1) the "Practica" with Twelve Keys and appendix of Basil Valentine, a Benedictine monk of Germany, translated from German; (2) the "Crede Mihi" or "Ordinale" of Thomas Norton, an English philosopher, translated from an English manuscript "ante annos 140"; and (3) the "Testamentum" of Cremer, Abbot of Westminster, England, published here for the first time.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
The title-page describes the three texts and their translation, "phrasi cuiusque authoris ut et sententia retenta." Craven notes the twelve keys of Basil Valentine include curious illustrative cuts — the sixth key represents an alchemical marriage ceremony (Sol et Luna), the seventh represents Chaos giving rise to the seasons, the eleventh depicts the marriage of Orpheus and Eurydice on lions. Norton's "Ordinale" was, per Craven, first published in this Latin translation by Maier, having been called "adhuc uneditus" in the Symbola the previous year; it was later published in English by Ashmole (1652). Cremer, said to have been Abbot of Westminster in the fourteenth century, allegedly learned part of the alchemical mystery from Raymund Lullius in Milan in 1330 and later worked with him in London, supplying Edward III with gold — a story Craven notes "is more than doubtful" and the "Testament" itself "has been regarded as spurious."

**Contexts Within the Source**
The Tripus Aureus was reprinted in the Musaeum Hermeticum Reformatum (1677, 1749) and translated into English by Arthur Edward Waite as part of "The Hermetic Museum" (London, 1893).
