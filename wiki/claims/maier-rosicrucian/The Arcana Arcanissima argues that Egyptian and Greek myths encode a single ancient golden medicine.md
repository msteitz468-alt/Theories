**Claim Statement**
Maier's "Arcana Arcanissima" (full title identifying it as "Hieroglyphica Ægyptio-Græca") argues that the false gods, goddesses, heroes, and sacred institutions of the ancient Egyptians and Greeks originated in a single Egyptian artifice — the making of "the golden medicine of soul and body" — and that the allegories of poets, the fabulous narrations of writers, and errors scattered throughout the whole Encyclopaedia are thereby shown, in the clearest light of truth, to derive from this one source.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
The full title (as printed) reads in part: "...ad demonstrandam falsorum apud antiquos deorum, dearum, heroum, animantium et institutorum pro sacris receptorum, originem, ex uno Ægyptiorum artificio, quod aureum animi et Corporis medicamentum peregit, deductam..." Craven summarizes: "he inquires whether these old stories may not have some other meaning, a meaning more secret and arcane... These old allegories, stories, and adventures of the gods may be properly understood as referring to scientific, philosophic, and chemical secrets." The work closes: "We Christians ... have revealed to us what was hidden in these old allegorical stories... Our God has brought us the medicine for both soul and body, truly precious and golden, once by Trismegistus, and now by our 'medicus,' Jesus Christ."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes the treatise is divided into six books: (1) Egyptian gods and hieroglyphics; (2) Grecian myths (Golden Fleece, Apples of the Hesperides) referred to the golden medicine; (3) genealogies of the gods shown to be philosophic/chemical/medicinal; (4) ancient festivals and plays as vehicles of the "charm of science"; (5) the Labours of Hercules and their meanings; (6) the Trojan Expedition. Craven quotes Morhof's "Polyhistor" as noting that Maier "seems to read more into the inscriptions and hieroglyphics than can be actually found in them."
