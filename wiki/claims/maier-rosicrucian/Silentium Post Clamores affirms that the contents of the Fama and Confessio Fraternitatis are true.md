**Claim Statement**
Maier declares that the contents of the two Rosicrucian manifestoes, the "Fama Fraternitatis" and "Confessio Fraternitatis," contain nothing contrary to reason, nature, experience, or the possibility of things, and are true; he defends the Brotherhood against charges of necromancy and superstition, credits the Order with great obligations owed by mankind for their experimental investigations and discovery of the "universal Catholicon" (universal medicine), and answers the objection that the Brotherhood promises much and performs little by noting that "many are called but few chosen," and that the five-year probation imposed on novices exists precisely so they may "learn how to govern their tongues."

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"The contents of the two manifestoes are declared to be true, and we are further informed that we owe a great debt to the Order for their experimental investigations, and for their discovery of the universal Catholicon. The popular objections preferred against it are disposed of in different chapters, e.g., the charges of necromancy and superstition." Craven further quotes: "'Nature is yet but half unveiled. What we want is chiefly experiment and tentative inquiry. Great, therefore, are our obligations to the Rosicrucians for labouring to supply this want. Their weightiest mystery is a Universal Medicine... What is contained in the Fama and Confessio is true. It is a very childish objection that the brotherhood have promised so much and performed so little. With them, as elsewhere, many are called but few chosen. The masters of the order hold out the rose as a remote prize, but they impose the cross on those who are entering... Like the Pythagoreans and Egyptians, the Rosicrucians exact vows of silence and secrecy... this has arisen from the five years' probation to which they subject even well-qualified novices before they are admitted to the higher mysteries; within this period they are to learn how to govern their tongues.'"

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes that the Society's stated claim — that all communications addressed to it, however anonymous, would reach their destination — appeared to prove false, causing grievance among those who sought health or treasure and were disappointed; Maier expostulates with them but, per Craven (citing Waite), his defense is not wholly satisfactory.
