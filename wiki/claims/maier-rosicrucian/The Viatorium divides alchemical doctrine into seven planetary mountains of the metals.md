**Claim Statement**
Maier's *Viatorium, hoc est, De Montibus Planetarum Septem seu Metallorum* (1618) divides its doctrine into seven chapters, each treating one of the seven planetary "mountains" corresponding to a metal — Mercury, Saturn (Lead), Jupiter (Tin), Venus (Copper), Mars (Iron), Luna (Silver), and Sol (Gold) — with each metal's relation to gold, to the "Tincture," and to "Medicine" discussed under three sub-heads.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven: "The treatise then divides itself into seven chapters — the first, 'De Monte Mercurii'... Chapter II. is 'De Monte Saturni'... The third chapter is 'De Monte Jovis'... The next is 'De Monte Veneris'... The next is 'De Monte Martis'... Chapter sixth is 'De Monte Lunæ' — Silver... The seventh and last section is 'De Monte Solis.'" Each chapter follows the pattern "Usu ad aurum, de usu ad Tincturam" (and to Medicine).

**Contexts Within the Source**
The preface frames the work with the allegory of Battus turned to a touchstone (Ovid's Metamorphoses), the labyrinth of the Minotaur, and the thread of Ariadne, as figures for the philosopher's search for "materia philosophica." The work opens with Tacitus's story of Caecilius Bassus's delusive vision of hidden Carthaginian treasure, applied by Maier to those who pursue chymic operations without proper method. Under "De Monte Solis," Maier states "as the seed of the wheat is in the wheat, so the seed of the gold lies in the gold," and repeats the claim that Moses knew the secret of "aurum potabile" when he dissolved the golden calf and made the Israelites drink it.
