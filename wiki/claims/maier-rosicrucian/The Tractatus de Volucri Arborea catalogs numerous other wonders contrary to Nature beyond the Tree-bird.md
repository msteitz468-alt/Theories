**Claim Statement**
Beyond the Tree-bird, Maier's tract catalogs a wide array of further "marvels contrary to Nature," including the Tartarian Lamb (Bonaretz), the Dragon's Blood tree, the Phoenix, green-skinned children found in England, ancient giants and monsters (Typhon, Briareus, Polyphemus), Incubi and Succubi, Lycanthropes (Wolfmen), and various Geniis (Sylvani, Lamiae, satyrs, witches).

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven summarizes chapter by chapter: the Tartarian Lamb or "Bonaretz," described by Sigismund von Herberstein as a zoophyte near the Caspian Sea shaped like a lamb with footstalks for legs; the Dragon's Blood tree of India, whose red gum Monardus of Cartagena identified as the plant's natural exudate; the Phoenix, understood by "the most ingenious Egyptians and Greeks" as really referring to a chemical process; green children found "in the reign of King Stephen" near "Wulspittle" in England; the story of Tages born from a ploughed clod, teaching prophetic arts to the Tuscans; Incubi and Succubi, illustrated by the tale of Philinion and Machatis from Peter Loyerus's "Liber de Spectris"; Lycanthropes among the Scythians per Herodotus and Olaus Magnus, including a captured wolf-man brought before the Russian chief Garzonius; and a final chapter "De Geniis" treating Sylvani, Lamiae, satyrs, and witches, including a discussion of Genesis 6 (the "sons of God" and "daughters of men") which Maier interprets as referring to the descendants of Seth and Cain rather than demons.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes the tract's "Conclusio Tractatus" states the author's aim: to show that what seems contrary to Nature is really wrought by the omnipotent power of God and is part of His divine plan.
