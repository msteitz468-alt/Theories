**Claim Statement**
The Symbola presents Hermes Trismegistus, "the most ancient of the Egyptian philosophers," as the fountainhead of the chemic art, deriving his wisdom through Seth the son of Adam; from Hermes it descends through Egyptian, Phoenician, and Babylonian colleges, to Biblical figures (Moses, Abraham, Joseph, Solomon, Maria Hebraea, John the Evangelist), to Greek sages (Democritus, Orpheus, Apollonius of Tyana), to Roman-era adepts (Morien), to Arabian scholars (Avicenna), and finally to medieval Christian alchemists (Albertus Magnus, Arnold of Villanova, Thomas Aquinas, Raymund Lullius, Roger Bacon, Melchior Cibinensis).

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven records that Hermes "lived near the time of the patriarch Abraham," that "by tradition from Hermes we have received the knowledge of the doctrine of the Holy Trinity," and that the Smaragdine Table was "found... in the grave of Hermes" by Alexander the Great, per Albertus Magnus. Maier holds "Moses can be proved to be a 'worker'" via the golden ark and the dissolving of the golden calf; Solomon "was a proficient," having "all wisdom from God"; Maria Hebraea (identified by some with Miriam, sister of Moses) is credited with inventing the water-bath ("Balneum Mariae"). Each subsequent national alchemist (Democritus for Greece, Morien for the Roman age, Avicenna for Arabia, Albertus Magnus for Germany, Arnold of Villanova for France, Thomas Aquinas for Italy, Raymund Lullius for Spain, Roger Bacon for England, Melchior Cibinensis for Hungary) is given a list of "congentiles" (fellow-workers) continuing the transmission.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes Maier interweaves classical myth (the Phoenix of Tacitus, Cadmus and the dragon's teeth, the golden bough of the Aeneid, the Bononian epitaph of Aelia Laelia Crispis) as allegorical confirmations of the art's continuity across nations.
