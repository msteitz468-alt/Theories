**Claim Statement**
Maier's *Ulysses* tract, subtitled "Sapientia seu Intelligentia, tanquam coelestis scintilla beatitudinis," presents Ulysses as the symbol of perfect human wisdom — a wisdom that, unlike fortune or bodily goods, cannot be shipwrecked, and which adorns manners, gives riches, and tempers virtue. The tract enumerates seven characteristics of Ulysses: (1) astutissimus (not easily deceived), (2) facundia (eloquence), (3) prudentia, (4) ingeniosissimus (ingenuity), (5) egregius in war, (6) skilled counsel, (7) patient endurance of labors and dangers.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven quotes the full title's sense: wisdom, "if in fortune's and body's goods it makes shipwreck, happily works its way by the oar of meditation and patience to the harbor." He records Maier's line-by-line treatment: "Ulysses was a man 'astutissimus.' He was not easily deceived, nor did danger or ambush overcome him... He had the eye of the serpent and the heart of the dove"; and the concluding characterization, "Ulysses, then, is the symbol of perfect human wisdom, that wisdom which adorns manners, gives riches, and tempers virtues," supported by a quotation from Wisdom of Solomon 7 ("all gold in respect of her is as a little sand"). The tract closes urging the reader to spend life "in the way of truth, justice, piety, and all virtue" until reaching "the port of true tranquility and eternal safety (Christo Salvatore duce)."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes the editor's preface states the tract "Ulysses" had been given by Maier, still living, into a friend's hands, and that the friend, publishing it after Maier's death in the summer of 1622 at Magdeburg, appended two further tracts (the "Colloquium" and the "Echo") translated from German into Latin.
