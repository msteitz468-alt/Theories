**Claim Statement**
Maier's *Verum Inventum* (1619) — subtitled "Munera Germaniae... quæ tanta sunt, ut plæraque eorum mutationem Mundo singularem attullerint" — argues that Germany's true gifts to the world were "found... vi animi et corporis" (not, as a critic alleged, discovered "ex vino"), and catalogues these in six chapters: (1) the earliest political state of Germany and its Imperial dignity; (2) continued political history through the Saxons and the Ottos; (3) the nature of the Imperial prerogative; (4) German inventions in the art of war, including gunpowder (attributed to Berthold Schwartz); (5) German literary inventions, including Gutenberg's printing (1440); and (6) German theological gifts, culminating in Luther's Reformation.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
The title states the work is a treatise on the gifts of Germany "ab ipsa primitus reperta... et reliquo Orbi communicata." Craven details: chapter four covers "warlike machines and implements," Albertus Magnus and "Bertholdus Schwartz" as inventors, the "pulveris Pyrii" (gunpowder) made of sulphur, charcoal, and salt; chapter five covers the transmission of writing from Egypt and Chaldea to the Phoenicians, Greeks, Romans, and Germans, and "the invention of printing was German, and John Gutenberg, anno 1440, issued volumes"; chapter six covers the "purificatio doctrinae Theologicae" through "Doctor Martinus Lutherus, Saxo," condemning the Roman party particularly on transubstantiation.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Two further sections treat German inventions "in Medicina" (crediting Paracelsus as successor to Albertus Magnus) and "in Chymia" (occupying thirty pages), the latter referring back to the Symbola and the Themis and defending the reality and antiquity of alchemical medicine. Craven notes the work closes with a eulogy of German princes, nobles, and military strength.
