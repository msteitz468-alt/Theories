**Claim Statement**
Maier argues that the "Tree-bird" of the Orkney Islands (the barnacle goose of popular legend) is born "without Father and Mother," arising not from ordinary seed or egg but by an admirable, marvelous process within the "innermost theatre of Nature herself," comparable to putrefaction-based generation of other lowly creatures.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven summarizes the tract's full title, *Tractatus de Volucri Arborea, absque Patre et Matre, in Insulis Orchadum...seu De ortu Miraculoso potius, quam naturali* (1619). He recounts Maier's argument that "these birds in the Orkneys are produced not from the ordinary seed, nor from an egg, but by an admirable manner in the very innermost theatre of Nature herself," reasoning by analogy from putrefaction-generated animalculae (mice and serpents from Nile mud per Diodorus Siculus, hornets from putrid horseflesh, worms from rose-hearts and human ulcers). Maier states the Sun is "the father" acting via heat on the resinous matter of trees.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes Maier corroborated the claim with testimony from "a doctor of Scotland" (identified by Craven as possibly Duncan Liddel or John Johnston) and situates the tract among a long chain of earlier tellers of the barnacle-goose legend (Giraldus Cambrensis, Hector Boece, John Gerarde, Sir Robert Moray, Olaus Wormius, John Johnston's *Thaumatographia Naturalis*).

**Opposing Information Within the Source**
Craven records that contemporaries disputed the legend: Joseph Scaliger held the geese were bred "ex putredine vestustorum Navigiorum" (from the rot of old ships) and noted no trees grow in the far north of Scotland from which they could hang; Wallace, the historian of Orkney, considered the "claikes" nothing but a kind of sea-shell (Concha anatifera); and Mackaile, an Aberdeen apothecary, expressed outright unbelief, having examined both old trees and fresh ship timbers bearing the shells.
