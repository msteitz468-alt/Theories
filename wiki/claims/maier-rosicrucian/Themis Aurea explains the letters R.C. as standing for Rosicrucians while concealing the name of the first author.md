**Claim Statement**
Themis Aurea explains that the characters "R.C." were adopted by the Brethren so that they "may not be without Name," and that although they came to be called "Rosie Crucians" (R. for Roses, C. for Cross), the Brethren themselves declared that by these letters they symbolically mean the name of their first Author, not literally a rose and a cross.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven quotes: "The characters are R.C., which they use that they may not be without Name, and every one, according to his capacity, may put an interpretation upon the letters, as soon as their first writing come forth; shortly after they were called Rosie Crucians, for R. may stand for Roses, and C. for Cross, which appellation yet remains, although the Brethren have declared that thereby they symbolically mean the name of their first Author." Each order is said to have its own emblem — "The Rhodians have the double cross, they of Burgundy the golden fleece" — and Themis further glosses: "R. signifies Pegasus, C. Iulium, if you look not to the letter, but right interpretation... is not this a claw of a rosy lion — a drop of Hippocrene?"

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes an anagram is given on page 115 of the work, and that "to live amidst Roses and under a Crosse are contrary things — joy and sorrow."
