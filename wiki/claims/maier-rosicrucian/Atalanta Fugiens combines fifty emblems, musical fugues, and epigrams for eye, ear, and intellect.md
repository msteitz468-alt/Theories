**Claim Statement**
Maier's *Atalanta Fugiens* (Oppenheim, 1617/1618) is an emblem book of "new emblems of the secrets of nature," presenting fifty engraved pictures accommodated partly to the eyes and intellect (figures, epigrams, and notes) and partly to the ears and the recreation of the mind through some fifty musical fugues in three voices, intended to be lived, read, meditated on, understood, judged, sung, and heard.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
The full title states the work is "Accommodata partim oculis et intellectui, figuris cupro incisis, adjectisque sententiis, Epigrammatis et notis, partim auribus et recreationi animi plus minus 50 Fugis Musicalibus trium Vocum... non absq; singulari jucunditate vivenda, legenda, meditanda, intelligenda, dijudicanda, canenda et audienda." Craven describes each emblem's three-part presentation: first page, part of the epigram set to music in three voices — Atalanta ("vox fugiens"), Hippomenes ("vox sequens"), and Pomum objectum ("vox morans") — with a German epigram beneath; second page, the emblem in figure with Latin verse, followed by two pages of discursus.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven quotes Waite's characterization ("Rosicrucians," 269) that the fifty engravings "emblematically reveal the most unsearchable secrets of Nature," and notes that a German translation (1708) and a second Latin edition retitled "Secretioris Naturae Secretorum Scrutinium Chymicum" (1687, omitting the music) were later issued.
