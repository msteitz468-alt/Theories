**Claim Statement**
Throughout the dialogue, Solomon and the Queen of Sheba repeatedly translate natural objects and mythological stories (the rose, Apis the ox, the phoenix, the crocodile, the Trojan horse, the golden apples of Atalanta, the Egyptian myth of Osiris and Isis) into hieroglyphs bearing on the alchemical "great work," including the Philosopher's Stone, philosophic Sulphur and Mercury, and the tincture.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven records numerous examples: the Queen asks whence "argentum vivum" (quicksilver), "the material of the work," is derived, and states "the Stone is Apis, that is Osiris"; Solomon explains the rose's red and white colors as "emblems of Gold and Silver," its green centre "an emblem of the green Lion which philosophers know well"; the phoenix "denotes the philosophic tincture"; the fish Oxyringus which swallowed the "pudenda Osiris" is tied to "the philosophic sulphur, combustible and burning"; the crocodile "refers to the sulphur found in our philosophic Nile"; and the two serpents of the Caduceus represent "the double nature of Mercury—the sun hot, the moon cold." On the sixth day, man is described as the "Parvus Mundus" (Microcosmos), with Europe as the head, Africa (the hottest part) as the breast/heart, and Asia as the belly/generative organs, paralleling the alchemical geography of the human body.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes Solomon poses the question "Quis est pater lapidis, et quis avus?" (Who is the father of the Stone, and who its grandfather?), to which the Queen answers that Saturn is grandfather, Chiron tutor, Vulcan preceptor, and Jupiter father — situating the Stone's genealogy within classical myth.
