**Claim Statement**
The "Canones declaratorii" (ten in number), appended as "Ergon et Parergon, Fr. C. R." to the Echo Colloquii Rhodo-Staurotici, define God as "the Eternal Father, incorruptible fire, and everlasting light," discuss the generation of the invisible Word of God and the "tetradic manifestation of the elements" (Pater, Filius, Spiritus Sanctus, Spiritus, Anima, Corpus, Spiritus ab aevo, Ignis, Aqua, Terra, Sulphur, Mercurius, Sol), and are followed by 120 chemical propositions allegorized through classical mythology.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven quotes Waite's "Real History" (p. 273) describing the Canones as defining God and discussing the elements, then lists examples of the 120 chemical propositions: "under the name and story of Deinogorgon, are to be seen the material and method 'hujus artis' (97)"; "'The ancient chaos is our Saturn' (98)"; "By the Phoenix, which always revives, is to be understood the multiplication of the Elixir (96)"; "Under the fables of Hercules and Antheus we are to see the preparation of the Sulphur (76)." The propositions are followed by a poem in seven verses.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven situates the Canones immediately after the Echo Colloquii Rhodo-Staurotici in the 1624 posthumous volume, followed in turn by the Christianae Religionis Summa of Joannes Diaz and the Scholasterium Christianum.
