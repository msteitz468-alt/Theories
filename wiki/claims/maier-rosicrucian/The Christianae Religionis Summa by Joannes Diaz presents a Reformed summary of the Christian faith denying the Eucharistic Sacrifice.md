**Claim Statement**
The *Christianae Religionis Summa*, by Joannes Diaz "Hispanum," appended to the 1624 Ulysses volume, is a treatise of the Reformed or Lutheran faith that admits the three historic creeds, the four general councils, and the doctrine of the orthodox fathers, but denies the virtue of the Eucharistic Sacrifice, and holds that the admission of clergy belongs to the "ministri" and the civil magistrate, terming the office "Minister or Pastor."

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven states: "This little treatise is of the Reformed or Lutheran faith, admitting, indeed, the three creeds, four general councils, the doctrine of the orthodox fathers, but denying the virtue of the Eucharistic Sacrifice, and granting the admission of clergy by the 'ministri' and the civil magistrate, the 'Minister or Pastor' being the designation given." He identifies Diaz as "a Spaniard, who had embraced the reformed opinions," murdered in 1546 "at the instance of his own brother, a doctor of the Roman Court," referring the reader to McCrie's "Reformation in Spain" for the full story, and notes the treatise was first published in French at Lyons in 1562.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven places this treatise as the third of four items appended to the posthumous Ulysses volume of 1624 (after the Colloquium and Echo, before the Scholasterium Christianum), noting the paper and printing of these appended pieces are poor and without illustrations.
