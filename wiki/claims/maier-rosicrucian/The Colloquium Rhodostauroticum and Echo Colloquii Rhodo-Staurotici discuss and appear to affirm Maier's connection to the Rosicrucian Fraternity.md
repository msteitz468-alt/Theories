**Claim Statement**
The *Colloquium Rhodostauroticum* stages a dialogue among five figures (Quirinus, Polydorus, Tyrosophus, Promptutus, and Politicus) debating the Society of the Rosy Cross and its published manifestos (the Fama and Confessio), including a list of approved theological and alchemical books. Its sequel, the *Echo Colloquii Rhodo-Staurotici*, responds to that dialogue and contains a passage that later readers (per Waite, cited by Craven) have taken to imply Maier was admitted into the Order of the Rosy Cross shortly before his death.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven describes the Colloquium as "a series of conversations on the Society of the Rosy Cross," noting Tyrosophus's mention (p. 93) of Maier's own "Symbolum Aureae Mensae" in connection with "the Stone," and a list of approved books in theology (Thomas à Kempis, Tauler, Gerhard, Savonarola) and chemistry (Trevisan, the Twelve Keys of Basil, Robert Castrensis's "Speculum," Paracelsus, Agrippa). In the Echo, Craven quotes the passage (p. 168) enumerating Maier's works and stating the Society would honor him "ante mortem ipsius, tam ingentibus honorariis, quam non minus singularium mysteriorum communicatione" — which Craven states "has given rise to the opinion that at length and shortly before his death Maier was admitted into the Order of the R.C." The Echo is dated "mense Martio, anno 1622," implying issue immediately after Maier's death.

**Contexts Within the Source**
The editor's preface to the whole volume raises but does not resolve the question directly: "Was he, Doctor Maier, ever the defender of the brotherhood of the Rosy Cross during his life? ...to this I confess I do not know," while affirming with certainty that Maier was a devout Christian, "Religionis Christi... Fratrem fuisse, notum est," a regular churchgoer of good conversation.
