**Claim Statement**
Book 5 of the "Arcana Arcanissima" treats the Labours of Hercules as a sustained allegory of the alchemical Work: Hercules represents the perfected philosophic operator, and his thirty-six labours (the Nemean Lion, the Lernean Hydra, the Stag of Ceryneia, the Erymanthean Boar, the cleansing of the Augean stables, the Stymphalian Birds, the Cretan Bull, the Mares of Diomedes, the Girdle of the Amazonian Queen, the Oxen of Geryones, the Apples of the Hesperides, and, last and greatest, the fetching of Cerberus) each correspond to stages or materials of the chemical operation.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"Hercules, in his perfection and strength, is an emblem of what is required to 'the work.'" Examples given: on the Nemean Lion — "'Leo viridis est vitrum.' ... 'leonem est solem, qui habeat lunarem naturam adjunctam'"; on the Hydra — "'Noster serpens in arte ex aqua concrevit.' This is our lizard, serpent, and hydra, which, if not properly killed, will revive again... by reason of its volatile nature"; on the Augean stables — "The cleansing refers to the purification of the vile material in the philosophic work"; on the Stymphalian Birds — "The birds are the volatile parts of the 'work,' easily dispelled." On Cerberus: "he spat, and thus called forth the poisonous plant called aconitum—a potent medicine. Hercules is the picture of perfect philosophic work. He bears in his history the key to the interpretation and intelligent understanding."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes Maier likens Hercules to Samson and to "the Divine Son himself... in his battle with Cerberus and other inhabitants of the infernal regions," drawing the parallel "to some length." Craven records that Maier ascribes to Hercules proficiency "in many arts... poetry, music, astronomy, horsemanship, chariot-driving, archery, fighting in heavy armour" as prerequisites for the work.
