**Claim Statement**
Atalanta Fugiens takes its name and governing allegory from the myth of Atalanta, who raced her suitors and speared the losers until beaten by Hippomenes through Aphrodite's golden apples; Maier explains in his preface that "Atalanta virgo mere chymica est," and that Hippomenes and Atalanta, uniting in the temple of Mars (the vessel), become lions and acquire the red colour — symbolizing the alchemical union and reddening of the work.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven quotes Maier's preface: "Atalanta 'virgo mere chymica est, et Hippomenes tanquam malo aurea in tertia tamen stabalimetur et firmantur, ... ex Hippomenes ct Atalanta coeuntibus in templo Martis Deum, hoc est vase, fiunt liones, sive rubeum acquirent colorem.'" The title-page shows the race, Venus handing the golden apples to Hippomenes, and the lovers transformed into a lion and lioness in the sanctuary of Cybele; above, Hercules reaches for the golden apples of the Hesperides.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes the story of Atalanta's killing of the wild boar and receiving its head and hide from Meleager, and her striking water from a rock at the shrine of Aesculapius, are "all explained in the Emblems."
