**Claim Statement**
In "Silentium Post Clamores," Maier explains why the Rosicrucian Order (the "Fraternitatis Germanicae de R. C.") answers petitioners with silence, arguing that from very ancient times philosophical colleges have existed among various nations for the study of medicine and natural secrets, their discoveries perpetuated by initiation from generation to generation — including the priests of Egypt (in reality alchemists, since "Isis and Osiris are sulphur and argentum vivum"), the Orphic and Eleusinian mysteries, the Samothracian Cabiri, the Magi of Persia, the Brahmans of India, the Gymnosophists, and the Pythagoreans — so that the existence of a similar association in his own day was no cause for astonishment; the Order's silence toward the vulgar mirrors the silence Pythagoras imposed on his own disciples.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"The author asserts that from very ancient times philosophical colleges have existed among various nations for the study of medicine and of natural secrets, and that the discoveries which they made were perpetuated from generation to generation by the initiation of new members, whence the existence of a similar association at that present time was no subject for astonishment... He maintains that one and all of these were instituted, not for the teaching of exoteric doctrines, but the most arcane mysteries of Nature... Moreover, the Order rightly observes that silence which Pythagoras imposed on his disciples, and which alone can preserve the mysteries of existence from the prostitution of the vulgar." (Quoted by Craven from Waite, "History of the Rosicrucians," iii. 269-271.)

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes the full title identifies the work as an "Tractatus Apologeticus" addressing both the causes of the "clamores" (the publication of the Fama and Confessio) and the causes of the Order's "Silentii" (silence, i.e. its failure to answer individual petitioners), together with a refutation of its detractors ("malevolorum refutatione"). Craven records Maier's own excuse for unanswered petitions: "'Non omnis ad omnia omnibus horis paratus est'... 'Locorum absentia, personarum distantia,' &c." — but notes (via Waite) that these excuses "could scarcely prove obstacles to men who were bound by no considerations of space and time."

**Opposing Information Within the Source**
Craven (quoting Waite) registers that Maier's arguments "as a whole can hardly be deemed satisfactory," since a truly adept Order unconstrained by space and time should have recognized worthy applicants regardless of physical distance.
