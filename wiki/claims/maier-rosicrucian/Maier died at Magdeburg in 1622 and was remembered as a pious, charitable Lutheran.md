**Claim Statement**
Maier, who had settled and practised medicine at Magdeburg (after serving from 1619 as physician to the Landgrave Moritz of Hesse), died there in 1622 "tempore Æstivo" (in the summer season), never returning to end his days in his native Holstein as he had wished. His friend, who published Maier's "Ulysses" in 1624, records that he passed away "piously," was a regular attendant at church, a Christian in life and conversation, and practised the charity of the Good Samaritan. No trace of his tomb survives; Magdeburg's official records, and the cathedral itself, were largely destroyed in the sack of the city by Tilly's forces in 1631.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"Maier was not destined to return and end his days in Holstein. He died at Magdeburg in 1622, 'tempore Æstivo.' We are told by his friend who published his 'Ulysses' in 1624, that he passed away 'piously,' and before his death gave into the hands of his friend that little treatise. It is added that he was a regular attendant at the house of God, a Christian in life and conversation, and that he practised that charity exhibited in the person of Christ as shown in the parable of the good Samaritan." On religion: "In religion, Maier appears to have been a devout Lutheran. In his 'Munera' there are several severe attacks upon the Roman Church and Court."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven reports that present cathedral authorities at Magdeburg state they have no trace of Maier's tomb, and recounts the destruction of Magdeburg: besieged by Wallenstein in 1629, then taken and burned by Tilly in May 1631 after "a heroic defence (2000 against 25,000)," with "nearly the whole population of 30,000" perishing "by fire, sword, or drowning in the river Elbe."
