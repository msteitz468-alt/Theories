**Claim Statement**
"De Circulo Physico, Quadrato" (dedicated to Maurice, Landgrave of Hesse, 1616) teaches that gold is the terrestrial image of the sun and of the "absolute circle written upon Nature," containing within its golden circle a quadrature of four equal parts; that as the sun rules the heavens and the heart rules human life, gold rules the earth; and that gold, being incorruptible and imperishable, carries a medicinal virtue for the aid and comfort of the human race, since Nature perpetually strains toward the perfection that gold alone, among metals, has already attained.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"There are three joined together in chains of Concord, for the harmony of the world. 'Cor humanum, Sol cæli atque Aurea virtus.' The sun is king; by its power the human heart beats and gold is produced... Gold is the image of the absolute circle written upon Nature. Within the golden circle is a quadrature of four equal parts... It is, in its incorruptible and imperishable nature, an active image of eternity. But it has also in it a medicinal quality, given for the aid and comfort of the human race." Chapter ten is titled "De auro æquato, quomodo agat in non æquata visceræ corporis humani et intemperiem illorum emendet." The closing "Cantilena Anacreontea" (Chapter 12) declares: "Nature ever strains after perfection; and gold being the most perfect metal, it is evident that Nature's intention is that, becoming perfect, all metals should become gold... 'The difference between lead and gold is not one of substance, but of digestion.'"

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes the work is a short twelve-chapter treatise (79 pp.) with a title-page vignette showing a circle enclosing a square, the four elements (Ignis, Terra, Aqua, Aer) inside and the four qualities (Siccum, Frigidum, Humidum, Calidum) outside.
