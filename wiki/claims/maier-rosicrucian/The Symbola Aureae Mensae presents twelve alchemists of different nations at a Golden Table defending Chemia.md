**Claim Statement**
Maier's *Symbola Aureae Mensae duodecim nationum* (1617) is structured as a banquet at a "Golden Table" at which twelve alchemists, each representing a different nation, are seated to celebrate and defend the antiquity, truth, and continuity of "Chemia" against an unnamed Adversary who had for years attacked the art.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
The title describes the work as a defense of the "virgin Chemia" against "argumentis tam vitiosis" of an adversary, restoring honor to its true practitioners. Craven summarizes: the twelve guests, in order, are Hermes (Egypt), Maria Hebraea, Democritus (Greece), Morien (Roman age), Avicenna (Arabia), Albertus Magnus (Germany), Arnold of Villanova (France), Thomas Aquinas (Italy), Raymund Lullius (Spain), Roger Bacon (England), Melchior Cibinensis (Hungary), and an "Anonymous" guest. Each is given a Latin motto and an emblematic woodcut. The work closes with a "Dessert" section recapitulating the whole and a concluding prayer and hymn ("Hermetis Regenerationis," from the Pomander).

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes the work opens with the encomiums of Socrates and Heraclitus and the praise of "Chemia," who has "reigned in Europe, Africa, and Asia," her subjects found in every nation though nations differ in habits, language, and religion. Maier gives four reasons for writing it (see related claim). Each alchemist's section closes with syllogistic "Argumenta et Responsiones" — objections against Chemia and Hermes's rebuttals.
