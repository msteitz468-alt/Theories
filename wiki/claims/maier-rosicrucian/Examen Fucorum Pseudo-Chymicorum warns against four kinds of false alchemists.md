**Claim Statement**
Maier's "Examen Fucorum Pseudo-Chymicorum," dedicated to Joachim Hirschberger, exposes and refutes the "fuci" (drones) — false chemists who prey on those wishing to learn the true art. Maier distinguishes four kinds: those with a little theoretical knowledge but no practical skill; poor men who promise what they cannot perform; those who inflate small, vulgar secrets into great ceremonies; and outright impostors who defraud victims by theft and flee or turn dishonest.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"The author, in his epigram, holds up to scorn the drones—false chemists—who impose upon those willing to learn the art, of which the professed teachers are themselves ignorant... Describing the nature of the drones in the bee-hives—lazy and greedy—he compares the pseudo-chemist with these pretending but useless bees. They are but as the Sirens and Harpies who attempted to attract Ulysses." On the four types: "There are those who may know a little of the theory of the art, yet have no practical knowledge. Those, too, who being poor, promise to perform what they have no means to perform. Then there are those who, by vulgar ostentations, display as of mighty importance some small secrets... Then there are the real impostors, who, by fraud, by theft and wicked impositions, having gained the means from their victims, seek refuge in flight or dishonesty."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes Maier cites Geber, Albertus Magnus, Khunrath ("Cunradius"), Cornelius Agrippa, and "Scotus ille Italicus" as earlier authorities warning against such frauds, and adds: "It has been said, and perhaps with some measure of truth, that Maier himself was at one time victimised by some of those 'fuci,' and that in their impostures he wasted both his means and his health." This echoes an earlier note in the Life that Maier was "wiled away from the practical path he had long followed by 'some adepts,'" per Waite's "Real History of the Rosicrucians."
