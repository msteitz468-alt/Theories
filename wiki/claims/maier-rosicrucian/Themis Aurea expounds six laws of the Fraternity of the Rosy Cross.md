**Claim Statement**
Themis Aurea, divided into twenty chapters, expounds the laws which the unnamed Founder of the Fraternity of the Rosy Cross prescribed to the brothers: (1) the profession of medicine and cures is to be made gratis; (2) no special habit is required; (3) a yearly meeting is to be held upon "the Day C"; (4) every brother is to choose his successor; (5) the letters "R.C." shall be their seal, character, and cognisance; and (6) the society is to remain concealed for one hundred years.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Craven paraphrases: "These are six:— The profession of medicine and cures to be made gratis. That no special 'habit' is necessary. A yearly meeting upon the Day C. Every brother to choose his successor. That the word R.C. shall be their seal, character, and cognisance. And, lastly, that the society should be concealed an hundred years. 'Our author of these laws is namelesse, but yet worthy of credit, unknown to the vulgar, but well known to his own society.'"

**Contexts Within the Source**
The work opens by identifying "Themis" as a poetic personification — "Daughter of Heaven and Earth, the Sister of Saturn, and Aunt to Jupiter" — representing "the true Idea of Justice, and the universal Notion of Vertue," though "there never was upon the face of the earth any such Themis." Craven notes the period of concealment was intended "to give the world time to lay aside their vanities, folly, and madnesse."
