**Claim Statement**
Maier's "Jocus Severus" stages a tribunal of birds — including the owl, crow, goose, crane, raven, nightingale, jackdaw, heron, swallow, sparrow-hawk, and cuckoo — presided over by the Phoenix as arbiter, in which, after various disputes and complaints among the birds, the Owl (bird of Minerva/Pallas) is pronounced Queen for her singular wisdom and is recognized as sacred to Pallas.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Full title: "hoc est Tribunale æquum quo Noctua Regina Avium, Phoenice arbitro post varias disceptationes et querelas Volucrum eam infestantium pronunciatur, et ob sapientiam singularem, Palladi sacrata agnoscitur." Craven summarizes: "The assemblage of the birds includes the owl, the crow, the goose, the crane, the raven, the nightingale, the jackdaw, the heron, the swallow, the sparrow-hawk, the cuckoo, &c. They meet in equal degree, and after debates and disputes, the owl, the bird of Minerva, receives the place as queen. The singular wisdom of that famous bird carries off the crown. The Phoenix presides at the meeting."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven notes the work's dedication was "written on his road from England to Bohemia," addressed "Omnibus veræ chymiæ amantibus per Germaniam," and specifically to "illi ordini adhuc delitescenti" (that order still lying hidden) in reference to the Fama Fraternitatis and Confessio, indicating the work was composed in England and links Maier's Rosicrucian sympathies to this period. Craven cites De Quincey's "Inquiry" for this detail.
