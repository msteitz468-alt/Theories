**Claim Statement**
Maier's "Lusus Serius" ("Serious Passe-Time"), dedicated to Francis Anthony, Jacobus Mosanus, and Christianus Rumphius, stages a debate before Man as judge among representatives of the four-footed creatures, birds, fish, insects, creeping things, vegetables, and minerals — the Calf, the Sheep, the Goose, the Oyster, the Bee, the Silk-Worm, and Flax — over which of them deserves sovereignty over all worldly things beneath Man; Man ultimately awards the crown to Mercury (Hermes), who is judged pre-eminent above the other competitors as the sun is above the planets.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
"Hermes sive Mercurius Rex Mundanorum omnium sub homine existentium, post longam disceptationem in Concilio Octovirali habitam, homine rationali arbitro, judicatus et constitutus est." Each competitor pleads its own services to mankind (e.g., the Calf: "We are serviceable by our labours, in tilling ground... Our dung is the food of the fields... For milk, there are too many profits to declare all"; the Bee: honey, wax for candles and church use, and the model of monarchical government; Flax: linen, sails, and paper, "hence a whole printed book from flax"). The Judge (Man) concludes: "thou Mercury so much exceeds thy competitors as the sun the planets; thou art the miracle, splendour, and light of the world. Take thy recompence, the Royal Crown, declared The King of All Worldly Things Being under the Command of Man."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Craven records that the Latin editions appeared at Oppenheim (1616, 1619) and Frankfurt (1617), and an English translation, "extremely rare," was issued in London in 1654 by Robert Hegge, of which "Half... almost was done in one afternoon over a glass of wine in a tavern."
