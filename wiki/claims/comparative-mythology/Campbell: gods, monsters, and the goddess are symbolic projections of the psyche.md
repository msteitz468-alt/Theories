## Claim Statement

Campbell maintains that the gods, demons, monsters, the goddess-mother, and the cosmogonic figures that populate world mythology are not literal external beings but symbolic projections of the human psyche — archetypal images that the unconscious generates to dramatize its own dynamics, depths, and potentials. The hero, the tyrant, the divine child, the universal goddess, and the devouring dragon each correspond to identifiable forces within the individual soul, and the great mythological narratives are therefore readable as maps of inner experience. The soul, on its return through the zones of emanation, reassumes these projections — discovering that the deities it once feared or worshipped were, all along, aspects of its own being.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

At the opening of Chapter IV ("Dissolutions"), Campbell states plainly: "The mighty hero of extraordinary powers — able to lift Mount Govardhan on a finger, and to fill himself with the terrible glory of the universe — is each of us: not the physical self visible in the mirror, but the king within." The cosmogonic hero is an image of the "world-creative divinity who during life was reflected within his heart" and to whom the soul returns at death.

The same logic is applied to the goddess-mother of Part II, Chapter I ("Mother Universe"). The universal goddess "makes her appearance to men under a multitude of guises; for the effects of creation are multitudinous, complex, and of mutually contradictory kind when experienced from the viewpoint of the created world. The mother of life is at the same time the mother of death." Far from being a literal cosmic person, she is the symbolic personification of the totality of experience — "the world-bounding frame: 'space, time, and causality' — the shell of the cosmic egg." Her multiple aspects (virgin, harlot, consort, hag of hell) are the aspects of the psyche's encounter with existence.

The psychological register is most explicit in the Dissolutions chapter, where Campbell describes how the dying soul, working through the Egyptian Book of the Dead, progressively discovers that the fearsome divine beings surrounding it are in fact aspects of itself: "the soul comes to the fullness of its stature and power through assimilating the deities that formerly had been thought to be separate from and outside of it. They are projections of its own being; and as it returns to its true state they are all reassumed."

In the chapter "Transformations of the Hero," Campbell explains why legendary biographies endow heroes with supernatural births and miraculous childhood deeds: "If the deeds of an actual historical figure proclaim him to have been a hero, the builders of his legend will invent for him appropriate adventures in depth. These will be pictured as journeys into miraculous realms, and are to be interpreted as symbolic, on the one hand, of descents into the night-sea of the psyche, and on the other, of the realms or aspects of man's destiny that are made manifest in the respective lives." The monsters, dragon-guardians, and tyrant fathers the hero must overcome are thus "threshold guardians" of the psyche itself.

Campbell further articulates a developmental logic governing the symbolic life of divine figures: "A god outgrown becomes immediately a life-destroying demon. The form has to be broken and the energies released." The outer form of a deity or a religious institution can fossilize into a tyrannical obstacle — what Campbell calls "Holdfast, the champion of the prodigious fact" — when the psychic energy it once embodied is no longer flowing through it. This is precisely the dynamic enacted in the monomythic hero's clash with the tyrant ogre.

Regarding the divine child and incarnation figures, Campbell states: "The divine being is a revelation of the omnipotent Self, which dwells within us all. The contemplation of the life thus should be undertaken as a meditation on one's own immanent divinity, not as a prelude to precise imitation, the lesson being, not 'Do thus and be good,' but 'Know this and be God.'"

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim operates as the interpretive lens throughout Part II. The cosmogonic cycle — emanation from a primal void, the virgin birth, the transformations and trials of the hero, and the final dissolution — is presented not merely as cosmology but as a symbolic account of psychic stages. The claim is inseparable from [[The monomyth (the hero's journey)]]: the hero's adventure reads psychologically as the ego's descent into the unconscious, encounter with its own projected contents (monsters, goddess, father-god), and return enriched.

It also grounds [[Campbell: myth's function is to reconcile human consciousness with the cosmos]]: myth can perform its reconciling work precisely because its figures are psychic realities, not foreign external entities.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Campbell acknowledges — without endorsing — the theological or devotional reading in which gods and savior-figures are genuinely external supernatural persons. He distinguishes between the exoteric religious stance (the worshipper who takes mythic persons literally as external beings) and the esoteric or mystical understanding (the contemplative who reads the same figures as images of inner divine reality). He notes that within Christianity, the mystic's "ecstatic descriptions of the unitive, soul-shattering experience" represent the deeper reading, while orthodox doctrine maintains the "dualistic distinction between man's 'eternal soul' and the divinity" and rejects "pantheism." Campbell does not dispute that literal readings have social and devotional validity; he argues, rather, that the deeper meaning — the psychic-projective reading — is what the symbols are ultimately pointing toward.
