## Claim Statement

Campbell proposes that the universal hero myth — the monomyth — is organized around a tripartite "nuclear unit": separation (departure) from the everyday world, initiation through trials in a supernatural realm, and return bearing a boon for humanity. He presents this three-stage arc not merely as a literary convention but as the deep structural pattern underlying rites of passage, religious narratives, and the dynamics of the individual psyche across all cultures and periods.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The formula is stated with definitional precision in the Prologue section "The Hero and the God": "The standard path of the mythological adventure of the hero is a magnification of the formula represented in the rites of passage: separation — initiation — return: which might be named the nuclear unit of the monomyth" (line 784). Campbell immediately provides a compressed narrative version of the same structure: "A hero ventures forth from the world of common day into a region of supernatural wonder (x): fabulous forces are there encountered and a decisive victory is won (y): the hero comes back from this mysterious adventure with the power to bestow boons on his fellow man (z)" (line 788).

Campbell then demonstrates the pattern across three hero traditions. Prometheus ascends to the heavens, steals fire, and descends — a departure, a feat, a return with a world-changing gift. Aeneas descends to the underworld, converses with his dead father, learns the destiny of Rome, and "returned through the ivory gate to his work in the world" (line 790). The Buddha's story supplies the most elaborate illustration: the young prince departs his father's palace, spends years wandering and in deep austerity (initiation), wins enlightenment beneath the Bo Tree, and — after being implored by the god Brahma — "went back into the cities of men where he moved among the citizens of the world, bestowing the inestimable boon of the knowledge of the Way" (line 802). Campbell further notes that the same shape appears in the Mosaic tradition: Moses ascends Sinai, receives the Tables of the Law, and returns with divine teaching to Israel (line 816).

He then explicitly maps the three stages onto the book's own structure, naming five sub-stages for departure ("The Call to Adventure," "Refusal of the Call," "Supernatural Aid," "The Crossing of the First Threshold," "The Belly of the Whale"), six for initiation ("The Road of Trials," "The Meeting with the Goddess," "Woman as the Temptress," "Atonement with the Father," "Apotheosis," "The Ultimate Boon"), and six for return ("Refusal of the Return," "The Magic Flight," "Rescue from Without," "The Crossing of the Return Threshold," "Master of the Two Worlds," "Freedom to Live") (lines 826–868). This outline constitutes the entire architecture of Part I of the book.

Campbell also aligns the tripartite hero-journey with the anthropological pattern of rites of passage: "The so-called rites of passage...are distinguished by formal, and usually very severe, exercises of severance, whereby the mind is radically cut away from the attitudes, attachments, and life patterns of the stage being left behind. Then follows an interval of more or less extended retirement, during which are enacted rituals designed to introduce the life adventurer to the forms and proper feelings of his new estate, so that when, at last, the time has ripened for the return to the normal world, the initiate will be as good as reborn" (line 612).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim supplies the operational form of the thesis stated in [[Campbell: a single "monomyth" underlies the hero stories of all cultures]]: the monomyth is universal in content (one story) and in structure (three acts). The concept hub [[The monomyth (the hero's journey)]] traces the broader cultural and scholarly influence of this three-part schema. Within the book, the three-stage schema functions both as a descriptive map of world mythology and as a prescriptive model for understanding psychological individuation — the hero's outer journey mirrors the inner journey from ego-bound consciousness toward broader spiritual realization.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Campbell acknowledges that individual myths may omit one or more of the elemental stages: "If one or another of the basic elements of the archetypal pattern is omitted from a given fairy tale, legend, ritual, or myth, it is bound to be somehow or other implied — and the omission itself can speak volumes for the history and pathology of the example" (line 880). He also notes that the return stage is often the most fraught and the one most likely to fail — the hero who has won enlightenment may be so absorbed in inner bliss that "all recollection of, interest in, or hope for, the sorrows of the world" is annihilated (line 856). These caveats are presentational rather than contrary: Campbell treats partial or failed journeys as variations that confirm rather than refute the underlying pattern. The source presents no internal counter-argument to the tripartite structure itself.
