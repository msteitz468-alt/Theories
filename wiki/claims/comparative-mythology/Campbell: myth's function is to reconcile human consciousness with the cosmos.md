## Claim Statement

Campbell argues that the primary function of myth, ritual, and symbolic art is to reconcile individual human consciousness with the wider cosmos — to bridge the private ego and the universal order within which it exists. Tribal ceremonies and seasonal festivals accomplish this by translating personal life-crises into archetypal, impersonal forms, thereby disclosing the individual as a participant in a "sustaining, timeless" communal and cosmic body. A complementary inward path — the ascetic, meditative, or heroic quest — achieves the same reconciliation by driving the seeker beneath surface identity to the depths of the psyche, where individual essence and the essence of the world are found to be one. Campbell contends that the breakdown of mythological symbols in the modern world represents a severing of the lines of communication between the conscious and unconscious zones of the psyche — the wound that the modern hero must heal.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the Epilogue, Campbell surveys the competing scholarly interpretations of mythology — "as a group dream, symptomatic of archetypal urges within the depths of the human psyche (Jung); as the traditional vehicle of man's profoundest metaphysical insights (Coomaraswamy)" — and concludes that mythology is "all of these," its meaning shaped by the question put to it. The function he emphasizes most fully is integrative: tribal rites of "birth, initiation, marriage, burial, installation, and so forth, serve to translate the individual's life-crises and life-deeds into classic, impersonal forms." Through these ceremonies "the whole society becomes visible to itself as an imperishable living unit. Generations of individuals pass, like anonymous cells from a living body; but the sustaining, timeless form remains." Seasonal festivals extend this outward still further: "As the individual is an organ of society, so is the tribe or city — so is humanity entire — only a phase of the mighty organism of the cosmos."

Campbell distinguishes two complementary paths toward this reconciliation. The first is the way of social duty and cult, in which ritual and symbol keep the individual anchored within the larger order. The second is the way of interior quest: "each carries within himself the all; therefore it may be sought and discovered within." Through ascetic practice or deep meditation the seeker strips away accidental identities — nationality, age, vocation — until "the essence of oneself and the essence of the world: these two are one." Campbell frames the climax of this inward journey as not mere self-contemplation but a realized identity: "The aim is not to see, but to realize that one is that essence; then one is free to wander as that essence in the world." From that vantage, the hero "is ever in the presence of his own essence — for he has the perfected eye to see. There is no separateness."

The cosmogonic narrative of Part II supports the same thesis structurally. The Babylonian myth of Marduk and Tiamat illustrates what Campbell calls "the basic paradox of myth: the paradox of the dual focus" — from the periphery of created existence, life is experienced as "a terrible cacophony of battle cries and pain," but from the standpoint of the emanating source it is "a majestic harmony of forms pouring into being, exploding, and dissolving. The myths do not deny this agony (the crucifixion); they reveal within, behind, and around it essential peace (the heavenly rose)." Myth thus functions to hold open both perspectives simultaneously — it reconciles ordinary suffering consciousness with the deeper cosmic harmony.

Campbell's diagnosis of modernity sharpens the claim by negation: "The lines of communication between the conscious and the unconscious zones of the human psyche have all been cut, and we have been split in two." The modern hero-task is therefore "that of questing to bring to light again the lost Atlantis of the co-ordinated soul" — restoring the connective tissue between individual consciousness and the cosmos that living myth once supplied.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the organizing thesis of the Epilogue ("Myth and Society") and is implicitly operative throughout Part II's cosmogonic cycle, which enacts the very integration Campbell describes analytically. It connects directly to [[The monomyth (the hero's journey)]]: the hero's adventure is itself a mythic vehicle for carrying the individual psyche through the threshold experiences that reconcile it with the cosmos, and the returned hero brings back a "boon" that restores this connection to the community.

The claim also underpins [[Campbell: gods, monsters, and the goddess are symbolic projections of the psyche]]: if myth's function is psychic reconciliation, then the figures populating myth — gods, demons, the goddess — are the symbolic instruments through which that reconciliation is enacted.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Campbell briefly acknowledges the rival functionalist view — that tribal seasonal rites aim to "control nature" — only to dispute it: "This is a misrepresentation." He insists "the dominant motive in all truly religious (as opposed to black-magical) ceremonial is that of submission to the inevitables of destiny." He does not present a sustained counter-argument to his own integrative thesis, though he acknowledges that no single scholarly framework (Frazer, Müller, Durkheim, Jung, Coomaraswamy, or theology) captures mythology's full range.
