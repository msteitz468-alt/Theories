## Claim Statement

Campbell argues that the hero's adventure across world mythology consistently unfolds through a sequence of identifiable stages: the Call to Adventure, the receipt of supernatural aid, the crossing of the threshold into the special world, the Road of Trials, the Meeting with the Goddess, Atonement with the Father, Apotheosis, the attainment of the Ultimate Boon, followed by stages of Return — Refusal of the Return, the Magic Flight, Rescue from Without, the Crossing of the Return Threshold, and the achievement of the state he calls Master of the Two Worlds. These stages are not rigid laws but a common grammar of adventure, and the sequence constitutes what Campbell elsewhere calls [[The monomyth (the hero's journey)]].

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The return arc of the monomyth is introduced at the opening of Chapter III with a precise formulation of the full circuit: "When the hero-quest has been accomplished, through penetration to the source, or through the grace of some male or female, human or animal personification, the adventurer still must return with his life-transmuting trophy. The full round, the norm of the monomyth, requires that the hero shall now begin the labor of bringing the runes of wisdom, the Golden Fleece, or his sleeping princess back into the kingdom of humanity, where the boon may redound to the renewing of the community, the nation, the planet, or the ten thousand worlds" (line 3858). This passage crystallises that the boon-stage is not a conclusion but a midpoint: the entire return movement — through Refusal, Magic Flight, Rescue from Without, and crossing the threshold — is structurally required to complete the round.

The Ultimate Boon section establishes the content of what is sought. Campbell writes of the guardian figures encountered at the innermost sanctum: "They are not ends in themselves, but guardians, embodiments, or bestowers, of the liquor, the milk, the food, the fire, the grace, of indestructible life" (line 2803). He surveys myths from Irish fairy tale to the Babylonian Gilgamesh epic and the Hindu churning of the Milky Ocean to show that the prize — whether a cauldron of inspiration, the plant "Never Grow Old," or the nectar Amrita — is always an emblem of this imperishable substance. The boon's proper significance, Campbell insists, is spiritual rather than literal: "The research for physical immortality proceeds from a misunderstanding of the traditional teaching. On the contrary, the basic problem is: to enlarge the pupil of the eye, so that the body with its attendant personality will no longer obstruct the view. Immortality is then experienced as a present fact: 'It is here! It is here!'" (line 3031).

The stage of Apotheosis — preceding the boon proper — is illustrated through the Bodhisattva ideal and the imagery of the mysterium coniunctionis. Campbell describes how the hero's vision at the summit discloses the identity of all opposites: "Having surpassed the delusions of his formerly self-assertive, self-defensive, self-concerned ego, he knows without and within the same repose" (line 2704). The vision of the void and the world as one prepares the hero for what he will bring back.

The Refusal of the Return is illustrated by the Indian warrior-king Muchukunda, who, after aeons of sleep, awoke and retreated again rather than re-entering the world: "Muchukunda, in other words, instead of returning, decided to retreat one degree still further from the world" (line 3882). The Magic Flight stages — in which the Welsh Gwion Bach, the Buriat shaman Morgon-Kara, and the Greek hero Jason all escape with their prizes against active opposition — show that "if the trophy has been attained against the opposition of its guardian...then the last stage of the mythological round becomes a lively, often comical, pursuit" (line 3899).

Campbell characterises the Crossing of the Return Threshold as the hardest passage of all: "The two worlds, the divine and the human, can be pictured only as distinct from each other — different as life and death, as day and night....Nevertheless — and here is a great key to the understanding of myth and symbol — the two kingdoms are actually one" (line 4111). The hero must translate the wordless, formless truth of the abyss back into the grammar of ordinary life: "How teach again, however, what has been taught correctly and incorrectly learned a thousand times, throughout the millennia of mankind's prudent folly? That is the hero's ultimate difficult task. How render back into light-world language the speech-defying pronouncements of the dark?" (line 4115).

The sequence culminates in the stage of Master of the Two Worlds, where the completed hero exercises "Freedom to pass back and forth across the world division, from the perspective of the apparitions of time to that of the causal deep and back — not contaminating the principles of the one with those of the other" (line 4206). Campbell's final stage, Freedom to Live, declares the upshot: "The goal of the myth is to dispel the need for such life ignorance by effecting a reconciliation of the individual consciousness with the universal will" (line 4305).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim describes the structural architecture of Part One of the book; it is the organising principle that makes each individual chapter intelligible as a segment of a single sequence. It is intimately tied to [[Campbell: a single "monomyth" underlies the hero stories of all cultures]], which establishes that this sequence is universal, and to [[Campbell: the hero's journey is a psychological and spiritual rite of passage]], which establishes its inner meaning. The concept hub for the whole pattern is [[The monomyth (the hero's journey)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Campbell himself acknowledges that the stage-schema is a scholarly idealization. He notes that some heroes refuse the return altogether (as with Muchukunda and certain saints "reported to have passed away while in the supernal ecstasy," line 3860), that the Magic Flight is sometimes comic and sometimes harrowing, and that the Return Threshold is frequently failed — as in the tale of Oisin, who touched the earth and collapsed into immediate old age. The norm of the full round is exactly that: a norm, from which actual myths deviate in countless ways.
