## Claim Statement

Campbell argues that beneath the surface variety of the world's hero myths — drawn from every culture, era, and religious tradition — there is one universal story, which he calls the "monomyth." He contends that "the hero with a thousand faces" is ultimately a single figure: the human psyche itself, projecting its own struggles and transformations into narrative form. This universality is not, for Campbell, a matter of cultural borrowing but of a shared psychological substrate common to all humanity.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The thesis is announced in the opening pages of the Prologue. Campbell observes that whenever one encounters the myths of the world, "it will always be the one, shape-shifting yet marvelously constant story that we find, together with a challengingly persistent suggestion of more remaining to be experienced than will ever be known or told" (Prologue, "Myth and Dream," line 566). He grounds this claim in the convergence of psychoanalytic theory — particularly the Jungian archetype — with the evidence of comparative anthropology, citing Franz Boas's observation that "Bastian was led to speak of the appalling monotony of the fundamental ideas of mankind all over the globe" and that "certain patterns of associated ideas may be recognized in all types of culture" (line 668). Campbell also marshals Frazer's argument that the resemblance between Eastern and Western dying-and-reviving god myths is "the effect of similar causes acting alike on the similar constitution of the human mind in different countries and under different skies" (line 670).

In the Preface to the 1949 edition Campbell states his method directly: "The second step will be then to bring together a host of myths and folktales from every corner of the world, and to let the symbols speak for themselves. The parallels will be immediately apparent; and these will develop a vast and amazingly constant statement of the basic truths by which man has lived throughout the millennia of his residence on the planet" (line 510). He concedes that he is deliberately foregrounding similarities over differences — "this is a book about the similarities" — and expresses the hope that "a comparative elucidation may contribute to the perhaps not-quite-desperate cause of those forces that are working in the present world for unification" (line 512).

The unity of the hero figure is stated with particular directness in the section "The Hero and the God": "Whether the hero be ridiculous or sublime, Greek or barbarian, gentile or Jew, his journey varies little in essential plan. Popular tales represent the heroic action as physical; the higher religions show the deed to be moral; nevertheless, there will be found astonishingly little variation in the morphology of the adventure, the character roles involved, the victories gained" (line 880). The book's title itself encodes the thesis: because the hero he describes "was every hero, and, looked at psychologically, was every individual, Campbell chose to title his work The Hero with a Thousand Faces" (Editor's Foreword, line 351).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the book's founding premise; every subsequent chapter is an elaboration of it. It is inseparable from [[Campbell: the hero's journey follows departure, initiation, and return]], which names the specific narrative structure Campbell identifies as universal. The concept hub [[The monomyth (the hero's journey)]] gathers the full scholarly and cultural legacy of this idea. Campbell situates the claim within a tradition running from Bastian's "Elementary Ideas" through Frazer's comparative anthropology to Freudian and Jungian depth psychology, presenting the monomyth as the meeting point of modern psychology and ancient symbolic wisdom.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Campbell himself anticipates the main objection — that he has "overlooked the differences between the various Oriental and Occidental, modern, ancient, and primitive traditions" — and addresses it by analogy: an anatomy textbook disregards racial physiological variation "in the interest of a basic general understanding of the human physique" (Preface, line 512). He does not, however, engage in sustained rebuttal of diffusionist or particularist counter-arguments within the Prologue; the source presents no internal counter-argument beyond this brief acknowledgment.
