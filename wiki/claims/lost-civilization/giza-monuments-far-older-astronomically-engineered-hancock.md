## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that the Giza monuments — the Great Pyramid and the Sphinx — embody precise astronomical and geodetic knowledge and point to an origin far older than orthodox Egyptology allows, marking them as a legacy of the lost civilization rather than the achievement of the Fourth-Dynasty Egyptians alone.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the Egypt chapters he stresses the engineering precision and cardinal/celestial alignments of the Great Pyramid, geodetic relationships he reads as encoding the dimensions of the Earth, and the case (associated with John Anthony West and Robert Schoch) that the Sphinx's weathering implies a far earlier, wetter date of construction. He ties Egyptian sacred chronology and the figure of Osiris to the same prehistoric source culture, arguing the monuments transmit inherited high knowledge.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock presents these as anomalies challenging the orthodox date, acknowledging that Egyptology attributes the monuments to known dynasties; the older dating and lost-civilization attribution are his (and his cited investigators') contested inference.
