**Claim Statement**  
The underlying projections of several Renaissance sea charts demonstrate that their prehistoric source geographers possessed an advanced understanding of spherical trigonometry, mathematical map projections, and a precise measurement of the Earth's circumference, enabling them to compute coordinates and construct grids that exceeded the capabilities of historic Greek geographers like Ptolemy.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**  
According to *Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings* (Chapters II and VII):
* **Piri Reis Projection:** Mathematical analysis of the grid by Hapgood and MIT mathematician Richard W. Strachan revealed that the map's layout was constructed using plane trigonometry. It utilized an ancient measurement of the Earth's circumference (based on Eratosthenes's calculation, implying a stadium length of 185 meters) and placed the center of its coordinate system at the intersection of the Tropic of Cancer and the meridian of Alexandria in Egypt.
* **De Canerio Map (1502):** Reconstructing the coordinate grid of the De Canerio map using spherical trigonometry successfully resolved the progressive latitudinal errors, proving that the original source map had been constructed using a sophisticated spherical projection rather than a flat grid.
* **Oronteus Finaeus Map (1531):** The mapping of Antarctica was executed on a highly advanced heart-shaped (cordiform) projection, which is mathematically impossible to construct without the use of spherical trigonometry.
* **Calculation of Longitude:** The maps show relative longitudes across the Atlantic (between South America and Africa) and within the Mediterranean that are highly accurate. Because historic navigators had no reliable method for calculating longitude until the development of the marine chronometer in the 18th century, Hapgood argues this coordinates data must have originated from a lost civilization that possessed advanced navigational instruments.

**Contexts Within the Source**  
Hapgood emphasizes that historic Greek geographers (such as Hipparchus, Eratosthenes, and Ptolemy) understood the mathematical theory of map projections but lacked the mechanical instrumentation to determine coordinates (especially longitude) for a large number of places. The maps suggest that a pre-Hellenic civilization had both the mathematical theory and the technical instruments to perform a comprehensive, global geodetic survey.
