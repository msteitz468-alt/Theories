## Claim Statement

Hapgood argues that the Oronteus Finaeus Map of 1531 depicts the **Ross Sea region of Antarctica as
ice-free** — with fjord-like estuaries, broad inlets, and rivers located close to where great
glaciers now reach the sea — implying the original survey was made during a recent temperate period
"so recent as… will be incredible to geologists."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He reports that on the map "fiord-like estuaries are seen, along with broad inlets and indications of
rivers of a magnitude that is consistent with the sizes of the present glaciers," some "located
remarkably close to the correct positions of the glaciers." He concludes: "The open estuaries and
rivers are evidence that, when this source map was made, there was no ice on the Ross Sea or on its
coasts. There had also to be a considerable hinterland free of ice to feed the rivers." He notes the
map appears compiled "from a number of local maps of different coasts" and that it aligns with a
modern map only when its segments are shifted individually, and that the IGY surveys later showed
"there is no western shore to the Ross Sea" — explaining why exactly those coasts are vague on the
old map.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hapgood concedes the idea "will, at first acquaintance, be incredible to geologists," for whom the
ice cap is "very ancient, perhaps several million years old." He observes the *interior* waterways
connecting the Ross, Weddell, and Amundsen Seas are *not* shown, suggesting "the ice cap may already
have been in existence at least in West Antarctica when the original maps were drawn" — i.e. the
source maps captured a partial, not total, deglaciation.
