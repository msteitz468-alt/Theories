**Claim Statement**  
The division of the circle into 360 degrees, divided into twelve equal arcs of 30 degrees (the twelve-wind system), did not originate with the historical Babylonians, but was inherited by them from a much older, prehistoric civilization that used it for global navigation and mapmaking.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**  
According to *Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings* (Chapter VII):
* **Venetian Chart of 1484:** Hapgood's students identified the twelve-wind system (dividing the circle into twelve 30-degree segments rather than the standard medieval 16- or 32-point compass systems) in the Venetian Chart of 1484. The map's layout was constructed using trigonometry based on this twelve-part division.
* **Pre-Babylonian Antarctic Maps:** The Oronteus Finaeus Map of 1531 contains a circle that Finaeus mistook for the Antarctic Circle, but which Hapgood demonstrates is actually the 80th parallel of latitude. Because this parallel is drawn as a mathematical division of a 360-degree globe on a map whose original source must date to a warm Antarctic period (thousands of years before Babylonia existed), the 360-degree circle must be prehistoric.
* **Zodiac and Navigation:** The division of the zodiac into twelve signs of 30 degrees each is a mathematical abstraction that correlates with the twelve-wind system. Hapgood argues that the prehistoric voyagers used the zodiac constellations as a map in the sky for pole-to-pole navigation.

**Contexts Within the Source**  
Hapgood uses this claim to show a mathematical link between his postulated lost civilization and the oldest historical cultures of the Near East. He argues that Babylonian astronomy, the sexagesimal numbering system, and Phoenician seafaring were not sudden, independent inventions, but rather a direct heritage from a common, much older global civilization that was mostly destroyed.
