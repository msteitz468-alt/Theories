## Claim Statement

Hapgood argues — following the Swedish scholar A. E. Nordenskiöld — that the medieval portolan
charts were not the work of medieval sailors but **copies of a single lost prototype from a far more
advanced antiquity**, the "normal portolano." The charts are too accurate for their era and show "no
signs of development" across two centuries, implying a finished source map that no one could improve.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hapgood quotes Nordenskiöld's reasoning: the Dulcert Portolano and its kin "were a great deal too
accurate to have been drawn by medieval sailors"; the charts "from the beginning of the 14th Century
are as good as those from the 16th" — "as though somebody early in the 14th Century had found an
amazingly good chart which nobody was to be able to improve upon for two hundred years." Nordenskiöld
"saw evidence that only one such model chart had been found and that all the portolanos… were only
copies — at one or more removes — from the original," which he named the **"normal portolano."** He
further found the portolanos "much better than Ptolemy's maps," concluding "there had been in
antiquity a geographic tradition superior to the one represented by Ptolemy." Hapgood treats this as
his point of departure for positing a lost source civilization. See the concept [[Normal Portolano]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hapgood reports that Nordenskiöld himself "was forced to leave the problem unsolved" — neither the
medieval navigators nor the known Greek geographers could have drawn the originals — and notes the
absence of any classical mention of such maps, which Nordenskiöld explained by the gulf between "the
academic mapmaker and the practical navigator."
