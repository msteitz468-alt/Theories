## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that academic archaeology is constitutionally unable to recognize evidence for a lost Ice Age civilization because it is locked inside a rigid paradigm that treats such a civilization as a priori impossible. He contends that archaeologists actively dismiss, sidestep, or ridicule anomalous evidence — from the stylistic complexity of Andean megaliths, to the deep dating at Gunung Padang, to the Younger Dryas comet hypothesis itself — not for principled scientific reasons but out of career self-protection, institutional inertia, and a deep-seated horror of overturning what their predecessors announced to be true. This resistance, Hancock warns, is not merely an academic error but a danger to civilization, because it prevents humanity from understanding both its own past and the recurring cosmic threat documented in the evidence the mainstream refuses to examine.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

On Andean archaeology specifically, Hancock writes in Part VII, Chapter 17, that the technique of stylistic dating "is not popular with archaeologists studying the monuments of the Andes — perhaps because, if they were to deploy it here, as they do elsewhere, they would be forced to question the established theory that the Incas made everything." He elaborates: "Archaeology is a deeply conservative discipline and I have found that archaeologists, no matter where they are working, have a horror of questioning anything their predecessors and peers have already announced to be true. They run a very real risk of jeopardizing their careers if they do. In consequence they focus — perhaps to a large extent subconsciously — on evidence and arguments that don't upset the applecart. There might be room for some tinkering around the edges, some refinement of orthodox ideas, but God forbid that anything should be discovered that might seriously undermine the established paradigm."

On Egyptology's attitude toward long-distance ancient voyaging, Hancock quotes Egyptologist John Baines: "The idea that the Egyptians should have traveled to America is overall absurd … and I also don't know anyone who spends time doing research in these areas, because they're not perceived to be areas that have any real meaning for the subject." Hancock responds: "this comment by Baines is more indicative of a deep-seated problem within Egyptology itself, and within archaeology in general, than of a problem in the factual findings … Archaeology is too much constrained by a rigid reference frame of what is possible and what is not, and tends to ignore, sidestep, or ridicule evidence that challenges that reference frame. This is equally true when it comes to the case for a lost civilization of the Ice Age, which again is dismissed on no good grounds other than that it is considered to be a priori impossible."

On Gunung Padang, Hancock describes "the dead hand of orthodox archaeology" halting Danny Natawidjaja's excavations, and quotes the attack by the Bandung Archaeological Center Chief who condemned the work from photographs without visiting the site. He characterizes the obstructions as part of "what almost appears to be a deliberate strategy to prevent us from learning the truth about our past."

In Part VIII, Chapter 19, Hancock escalates the argument to matters of planetary safety. He writes that the mainstream's rejection of the Younger Dryas impact hypothesis — and by extension its refusal to reconsider human prehistory — is not merely a scholarly mistake but an active danger: "keeping us all in the dark about a real and present danger that threatens the human future, simply because recognizing the existence of that danger requires some scholars to abandon long-cherished positions, is quite another." He quotes a scientist who told him that resistance to impact studies reflects an instruction from "top government officials to downplay the risk," and he frames the entire archaeological establishment as complicit — willingly or not — in a "great illusion of cosmic security."

His conclusion is explicit: "Rather than doing everything in their power to dismiss, minimize and ridicule the myths, the anomalous monuments, and the other tantalizing hints, traces and clues of a great forgotten episode of human history, the evidence of the comet impacts 12,800 years ago requires that a thorough investigation of these mysteries, drawing on the full resources of science, should be undertaken for the first time."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim runs as the connective tissue of all of Hancock's site arguments in Parts VII–VIII. Every anomalous site — Sacsayhuaman, the Coricancha, Tiahuanaco, Gunung Padang — is presented not only as evidence for [[The lost civilization hypothesis (Hancock)]] but as evidence that has been mishandled, ignored, or suppressed by the mainstream. The critique of mainstream archaeology also supports [[The Younger Dryas comet impact (Hancock)]], because the same institutional conservatism that dismisses lost-civilization claims also, in Hancock's telling, resists the Younger Dryas impact hypothesis championed by scientists like Kennett, West, and Firestone.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock himself gives partial credit to the mainstream: he notes that "even the most hostile" archaeologists have been forced to acknowledge that Gunung Padang is older than previously claimed, now calling it "a gigantic terraced tomb, which was part of the biggest megalithic culture in the archipelago" — evidence of some capacity for revision. He also concedes that stylistic dating, while logical and reasonable, carries inherent uncertainties and that many anomalous sites genuinely lack definitive objective dating. He allows that academic turf wars are a less sinister explanation for resistance than conspiracy, writing "Academic turf wars, however, are one thing" before suggesting the situation may be worse. He acknowledges that his reading of archaeological resistance as an "almost deliberate strategy" is an inference, not a proven fact.
