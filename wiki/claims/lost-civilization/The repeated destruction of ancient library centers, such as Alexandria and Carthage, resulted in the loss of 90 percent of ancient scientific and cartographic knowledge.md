**Claim Statement**  
At least 90 percent of the scientific, cartographic, and technical knowledge of the ancient world was lost to posterity because of the repeated destruction of major libraries, particularly the Library of Alexandria and the Library of Carthage.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**  
According to *Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings* (Chapter VIII):
* **Library of Alexandria:** The great repository in Egypt contained approximately one million volumes of accumulated knowledge. It suffered at least three major fires:
  1. **Julius Caesar (48 BC):** Julius Caesar's forces set fire to the library during the siege of Alexandria, destroying a third of the collection.
  2. **Christian Mobs (4th Century AD):** A Christian mob led by the local bishop destroyed the library because they viewed its scientific and philosophical works as heathen teachings that threatened Christian theology.
  3. **Arab Conquest (7th Century AD):** The remaining library was ordered destroyed by the Caliph, with the parchments allegedly used as fuel to heat the city's Roman baths for six months.
* **Library of Carthage (146 BC):** The Romans burned Carthage to the ground, destroying its library of 500,000 volumes, which detailed the maritime and scientific knowledge of the Phoenicians.
* **Loss of Aristotle's and Plato's Works:** Hapgood points out that of Aristotle's extensive writings, only his popular works (or notes taken by his students) survive, while his technical scientific treatises were lost. Similarly, Plato's serious scientific and technical works are completely lost, leaving only his popular dialogues. Of the Greek playwrights Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides, only about 10 percent of their plays survived.

**Contexts Within the Source**  
Hapgood provides this historical context to explain why no written records of his postulated prehistoric civilization have survived, leaving only copies of their maps. He argues that the historical record is highly fragmented and biased, as the works that survived were selected by medieval churchmen who prioritized moral and literary texts over technical scientific and cartographic works, which were deliberately neglected or destroyed.
