## Claim Statement

Hapgood argues that the Zeno Map of 1380 depicts **Greenland without its ice cap** — showing
interior mountains and rivers reaching the sea where great glaciers now descend — and that, per
Captain Mallery, the map's under-ice land forms match what 20th-century seismic surveys found beneath
the modern ice sheet. He treats this as further evidence the northern source maps also date from the
Ice Age.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hapgood reports the map "shows Greenland with no ice cap. The interior is filled with mountains.
Rivers are shown entering the sea, in some cases at the points where at present great mountain
glaciers are moving down through the mountains to the coast." He notes Mallery's claim that "seismic
expeditions in recent years found that the under-ice topography agreed with the Zeno Map," citing the
Paul-Émile Victor French Polar Expeditions of 1947–49 that "did cross the Greenland ice cap and make
a seismic profile of the thickness of the ice." He adds that the map's accurate latitudes *and*
longitudes for places across Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Germany, Scotland, the Shetlands and Faroes are
"unbelievable" for the 14th century, implying it was copied from a much older, more capable source.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hapgood notes the surviving map was a Renaissance copy on which a later Zeno "removed the lines of the
ancient projection… and attempted to substitute one of the new projections," and that the map "was
probably not drawn by the Zeno brothers" — i.e. the evidential value rests on the inferred lost
prototype, not the 1380 artifact itself.

## Contexts Within the Source

One of several maps Hapgood reads as preserving Ice Age geography (alongside the Hadji Ahmed map's
Antarctica and the Ibn Ben Zara map's European glaciers); a northern parallel to

and support for [[All medieval portolan charts descend from a single lost ancient source map (Hapgood/Nordenskiöld)]].
