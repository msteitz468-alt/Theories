## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that the bas-relief carvings on Pillar 43 (the "Vulture Stone") in Enclosure D at Göbekli Tepe constitute a deliberate astronomical record — or message to the future — that uses symbolic depictions of constellations to encode a precise astronomical moment. Drawing on the work of researcher Paul Burley and independently verified via astronomy software, Hancock contends the pillar depicts the winter solstice sun positioned within the constellation of Sagittarius (represented as a vulture) at the southern celestial gateway of the Milky Way, targeting the galactic center. This alignment is unique to an 80-year window spanning approximately 1960–2040 AD — a signature Hancock links directly to the epoch of the [[The Younger Dryas comet impact (Hancock)]] as a memorial carved 11,600 years before the depicted event. The anthropomorphic "dying man" figure on the pillar is read as a further reference to the catastrophe that devastated the lost civilization.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hancock re-reads Paul Burley's 2011 paper ("Göbekli Tepe: Temples Communicating an Ancient Cosmic Geography") in his hotel room in Şanlıurfa in July 2014 and describes the realization as hitting him "like a shot of adrenaline." Burley's central observation, which Hancock quotes directly, is that on Pillar 43:

> "There is a bird with outstretched wings, two smaller birds, a scorpion, a snake, a circle, and a number of wavy lines and cord-like features … The key to unlocking this early Neolithic puzzle is the circle situated at the center of the scene. I am immediately reminded of the cosmic Father—the Sun. The next clues are the scorpion facing up toward the sun, and the large bird seemingly holding the sun upon its outstretched wing … the sun figure appears to be located accurately on the ecliptic with respect to the familiar constellation of Scorpio … as close to the galactic center as it can be on the ecliptic as it crosses the galactic plane."

Hancock elaborates on why this is necessarily a signature of a specific epoch. The southern gate of the Milky Way — where the ecliptic crosses the galactic plane between Sagittarius and Scorpio — is fixed in the sky and never changes. But the particular alignment of the December-solstice sun with the galactic center is produced by precession and recurs only every 25,920 years. The current window (approximately 1960–2040 AD) is the only such window within human recorded history. As Hancock writes: "the winter solstice alignment recurs once every 26,000 years, so in 24,000 BC the sun would have been seen in Sagittarius, targeting the center of the galaxy exactly as it does today."

Burley's conclusion, quoted by Hancock: "the builders of Göbekli Tepe constructed a temple apparently highlighting a time 11,600 years in their future. Yet this scene is intentional. The symbolism is clear and in keeping with many mythologies describing this very same event — occurring at the very time we live in today!"

Hancock uses Stellarium astronomy software to work through eliminations of all four "stations of the sun" in 9600 BC: the spring and autumn equinoxes place the sun in Leo and Aquarius respectively, far from the galactic center; the summer solstice sunset is ruled out because Enclosure D is built into the northern ridge and no observation of a northwest sunset would have been possible from within it; the winter solstice in 9600 BC placed the sun in Taurus, also remote from the galactic center. He concludes by invoking Sherlock Holmes: "whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth" — only the winter solstice with the sun in Sagittarius targeting the galactic center matches, and that alignment belongs exclusively to the epoch around our own time.

On the identification of Sagittarius as a vulture, Hancock argues: "a vulture with outstretched wings makes a very good figure of Sagittarius; indeed it's a much better, more intuitive and more obvious way to represent the central part of this constellation than the centaur/archer that we have inherited from the Mesopotamians and the Greeks." He maps the surrounding figures — the scorpion (Scorpio), the serpent-holder bird (Ophiuchus/Serpens), the small hooked-beak bird (head and claws of Scorpio redeployed), and the long-necked bird below — against the corresponding constellations and finds "all this goes, in my opinion, far beyond anything that can be explained away as mere 'coincidence.'"

The "three bags" in the upper register of Pillar 43 are noted to resemble the "houses in the sky" on Babylonian Kudurru stones — symbols of Mesopotamian deities Enlil (who sent the Deluge) and Enki (who saved mankind), further reinforcing for Hancock the connection between this pillar and catastrophe mythology linked to [[The Younger Dryas comet impact (Hancock)]].

The parallel with the Mayan calendar is explicit: Hancock notes that the same 80-year window (1960–2040) is "signaled in the famous — or perhaps it would now be better to say infamous — Mayan calendar" as the end of a great cycle and a "time of great transformation and world rebirth." He calls it "strange, and indeed somewhat eerie, to find the solar and astronomical coordinates of the exact same 80-year window … carved in high relief on a 12,000-year-old pillar in Göbekli Tepe in far-off Turkey."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the astronomical centerpiece of [[The lost civilization hypothesis (Hancock)]]. Hancock frames the pillar as deliberate communication across millennia: survivors of the lost civilization, knowing precession well enough to project its effects 11,600 years forward, carved a message into Pillar 43 that would only become readable when human civilization had re-advanced sufficiently to recognize what was depicted. The builders of Göbekli Tepe are on his account the direct inheritors of the survivors of [[The Younger Dryas comet impact (Hancock)]], and the deliberate burial of the site after construction is consistent with the intention to preserve its message for the future. Hancock links this to the similar precessional encoding he finds at the Giza plateau (Enclosure D's orientation is northwest–southeast, consistent with winter-solstice sunrise alignments), and to the geodetic relationships between Göbekli Tepe, Harran, and Ur explored later in Chapter 16.

The broader Part VI also surveys other researchers' astronomical findings at Göbekli Tepe — Magli's Sirius alignments, Schoch's Orion-Pleiades alignments, and Collins/Hale's Cygnus/Deneb alignments — situating Hancock's Pillar 43 reading within a context of scholarly consensus that the site is "a profoundly astronomical site."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock acknowledges the Pillar 43 reading is his own synthesis of Paul Burley's speculative analysis, which Burley himself expressed in careful, hedged terms. The identification of the large bird as Sagittarius (rather than some other constellation), and of the surrounding figures as the specific constellations Hancock assigns to them, involves interpretation and the tacit assumption that the builders of Göbekli Tepe used constellation boundaries substantially similar to those of later Mesopotamian and modern astronomy — an assumption Hancock himself notes is not guaranteed. He acknowledges that "constellation boundaries … are not necessarily drawn in the same place by all cultures at all periods." The scorpion's position and orientation on the pillar do not directly match the modern Scorpio constellation, requiring Hancock and Burley to propose that ancient Göbekli Tepe astronomers divided the relevant sky-region differently from us. Hancock's process of "eliminating the impossible" is logically valid only if all plausible candidate alignments have been exhaustively considered, which he acknowledges depends on what we know of the site's orientation — itself still imperfectly understood.

Klaus Schmidt (the lead archaeologist at Göbekli Tepe until his death in July 2014, shortly after Hancock's visit) opposed any form of astronomical interpretation at the site, based on his view that zodiacal constellations were not recognized until Babylonian times — though Hancock notes this is contradicted by multiple astronomical researchers and by Palaeolithic cave-art evidence going back at least 17,000 years (Michael Rappengluck's work at Lascaux).
