**Claim Statement**  
The Piri Reis Map of 1513 and the Oronteus Finaeus Map of 1531 depict the coastlines, islands, and mountain ranges of Antarctica at a time when the continent's coasts were free of ice, indicating that the original surveys must have been made during a warm, pre-glacial period prior to the formation of the modern polar ice sheet.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**  
According to *Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings* (Chapters I, II, and IV):
* **Piri Reis Map (1513):** Depicts the Princess Martha Coast of Queen Maud Land, Antarctica, showing bays, rivers, and islands that are currently buried under an ice cap nearly a mile thick. Hapgood highlights that the sub-glacial topography, only verified in the mid-20th century by joint British-Swedish-Norwegian seismic surveys, matches the features drawn on the 1513 map.
* **Oronteus Finaeus Map (1531):** Shows the entire continent of Antarctica with ice-free coasts, mountain ranges, and rivers flowing into the sea. The Ross Sea is shown free of glaciers, with estuaries and river mouths indicating open water.
* **Byrd Ross Sea Core Samples:** Hapgood cites core samples taken from the bottom of the Ross Sea during the Byrd Expedition of 1948–1949. Seismic and geological testing by Dr. Jack Hough showed layers of fine-grained sediment carried to the sea by flowing rivers, which were dated by the ionium method of radioactive dating. The tests indicated that the most recent ice-free river run-off occurred around 6,000 years ago (c. 4000 BC).

**Contexts Within the Source**  
Hapgood frames this claim as the first "hard evidence" of a highly advanced prehistoric civilization. He argues that because these maps were drawn in the 16th century—long before Antarctica was officially discovered in 1820—and show details only verifiable by modern seismic equipment, they must be faithful copies of prehistoric source maps. He notes that the original mapping must have occurred during a period of temperate climate on the Antarctic coast, which he explains via his earth-crust displacement model.
