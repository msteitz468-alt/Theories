## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that the recurring American myths of a tall, bearded, light-skinned "civilizing god" who arrived after a flood to teach agriculture, law, astronomy, and architecture — **Viracocha** in the Andes, **Quetzalcoatl/Kukulkan** in Mesoamerica — are folk-memories of survivors of the lost civilization, not deities.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Part II ("Foam of the Sea") he ties Viracocha to the megalithic site of **Tiahuanaco (Tiwanaku)** near Lake Titicaca, whose engineering and apparent antiquity he argues exceed the known capacity of local cultures. In Part III ("Plumed Serpent") he reads Quetzalcoatl as the same bearded teacher-figure, and treats the precision of the Mesoamerican **Long Count** calendar and its cyclical cataclysm myths as inherited high knowledge. The shared "bearded teacher after the flood" pattern, he argues, points to a common civilizing source.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock builds on the colonial chronicles and the sites' anomalies; the identification of myth as literal memory of survivors is his interpretation, and the antiquity he assigns to Tiahuanaco is disputed by mainstream archaeology.
