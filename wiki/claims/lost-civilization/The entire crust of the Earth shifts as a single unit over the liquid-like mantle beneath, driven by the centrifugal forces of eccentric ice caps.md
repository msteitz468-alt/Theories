**Claim Statement**  
Sudden climatic shifts, including the onset and termination of ice ages, are caused by the displacement of the Earth's entire lithosphere as a single, solid piece over a semi-fluid sub-layer, driven by the centrifugal force generated by the off-center accumulation of polar ice sheets.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**  
According to *Maps of the Ancient Sea Kings* (Chapter VII):
* **Horizontal Slippage:** The Earth's crust (lithosphere) is detached from the inner body of the Earth by a weak, liquid-like subterranean layer of rock. This allows the whole crust to shift, similar to the skin of an orange sliding over its inner fruit.
* **Centrifugal Driving Force:** Polar ice sheets accumulate unevenly relative to the Earth's axis of rotation (for example, the center of the Antarctic ice cap is about 300 miles from the South Pole). As the Earth rotates, this eccentricity creates a horizontal centrifugal force that pulls at the crust.
* **Campbell's Calculations:** Physicist and collaborator James Campbell calculated the mathematical effects of the current Antarctic ice cap's mass. He concluded that the centrifugal forces generated by the ice sheets are of sufficient magnitude to cause structural failure and fracturing in the Earth's crust, leading to a shift of the lithosphere toward the equator.
* ** Laurentide Ice Sheet Shift:** Hapgood argues that during the last Ice Age, the North Pole was centered at Hudson Bay, Canada. The accumulation of ice eventually triggered a crustal shift that pulled North America approximately 2,000 miles southward, melting the glaciers and shifting Antarctica into the South Pole.

**Contexts Within the Source**  
Hapgood proposes this geological mechanism to resolve what he terms the "puzzle of the ice ages." He rejects continental drift (which he notes is too slow, moving at only one centimeter per year) and axial tilt shifts as explanations for the rapid climatic changes that ended the last Ice Age in only 10,000 years. He presents crustal displacement as the only theory that explains both the rapid melting of the Laurentide ice sheet and the relatively recent glaciation of Antarctica.
