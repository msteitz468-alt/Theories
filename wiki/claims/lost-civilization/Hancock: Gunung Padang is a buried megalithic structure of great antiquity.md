## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that Gunung Padang in West Java, long thought to be a natural hill with a surface megalithic site, is in fact a massive artificial pyramid whose man-made structures extend tens of meters underground. Drawing on the work of geologist Danny Hilman Natawidjaja, Hancock contends that core drilling and remote sensing have dated the buried construction layers to well before the Younger Dryas — the deepest strata extending back more than 22,000 years — demolishing the orthodox figure of roughly 3,000 years' age. He further argues that orthodox archaeology has actively obstructed the excavations needed to confirm these findings, exemplifying the broader suppression of evidence for a [[The lost civilization hypothesis (Hancock)]].

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hancock describes Gunung Padang as "the mysterious pyramid, for so long thought to be a natural hill, that geologist Danny Natawidjaja, through determined efforts, has brought to the attention of the world" (Part VII, Ch. 18). He explains that Danny and his team "used seismic tomography, ground-penetrating radar and other remote-sensing technologies to show that the man-made structures go down tens of meters beneath the surface." Core drilling "was undertaken producing organic materials with impeccable provenance that yielded ever more ancient carbon dates extending back, ultimately to more than 22,000 years ago — before the end of the last Ice Age, when our ancestors are supposed (according to the orthodox archaeological model) to have been nothing more than primitive hunter-gatherers, incapable of large-scale construction and engineering feats."

The remote sensing equipment also detected hidden chambers: "The largest of these lies at a depth of between 21.3 and 27.4 meters (70 to 90 feet) and measures approximately 5.5 meters (18 feet) high, 13.7 meters (45 feet) long and 9.1 meters (30 feet) wide." Even the relatively shallow second artificial layer, all that could be excavated before political interference halted the work, "yielded a radiocarbon date of 5200 BC (i.e. 7,200 years ago, nearly 3,000 years older than the orthodox dating for the Pyramids of Giza in Egypt)."

Hancock quotes a letter from Natawidjaja (October 2014): "The existence of the pyramid-like structure beneath the megalithic site is now loud and clear; even for non-specialists, it is not too difficult to understand if they come and see for themselves." He reports that even hostile archaeologists have been forced to reframe the site, now conceding it is "a gigantic terraced tomb, which was part of the biggest megalithic culture in the archipelago." Hancock believes the deep, still-unexcavated strata "go back before the cataclysmic episode of the Younger Dryas (12,800 years ago to 11,600 years ago) and deep into the last Ice Age, when the lost civilization still thrived."

He also raises the possibility that a hidden chamber deep within the pyramid may be "the Hall of Records of the lost civilization," directly connecting Gunung Padang to his broader thesis about a prehistoric civilization's knowledge being buried for future rediscovery — paralleling his argument about Göbekli Tepe: "Others were buried in the ground — time-capsules like Göbekli Tepe, and perhaps like that mysterious chamber deep beneath Gunung Padang — and primed for rediscovery when the time was right."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is developed primarily in Part VII, Chapter 18, as the culminating Indonesian site in Hancock's survey of anomalous megalithic evidence across the Pacific and Southeast Asia. It connects directly to [[The lost civilization hypothesis (Hancock)]] — Hancock explicitly states that "Indonesia must rank among the most plausible candidates anywhere on earth for the heartland in which that civilization could have evolved and grown to maturity," citing the massive land loss when Sundaland was submerged at the end of the Younger Dryas (~11,600 years ago). The buried antiquity of Gunung Padang also ties to [[The Younger Dryas comet impact (Hancock)]], since the deepest construction layers predate, and the destruction of the site's cultural context may have been caused by, the Younger Dryas cataclysm.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock himself reports that Bandung Archaeological Center Chief Desril Shanti publicly attacked the excavations in September 2014, complaining they "did not follow the standard methods that are usually applied in archaeological projects" and that she had judged them from photographs without visiting the site. She also objected to the project's funding. This intervention, combined with a change of Indonesian presidency, halted the work — a development Hancock characterizes as "the dead hand of orthodox archaeology" potentially preventing discovery of the truth. He notes that no independent corroboration of the deep date claims had been achieved before excavations were suspended, and he acknowledges that the deepest layers (pre-22,000 BP) are "as yet unexcavated, as yet unexplored, identified only by core-drilling and remote sensing equipment." The dated "deep" samples have not been published through peer review as of Hancock's writing.
