## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that flood, winter, and cataclysm myths found across widely separated cultures — Zoroastrian, Hebrew, Sumerian, Maya, Native American, and Ancient Egyptian — are not independent religious inventions but shared memories of a single real event: the Younger Dryas impact and its cascading floods, darkness, and deep freeze beginning around 12,800 years ago. He contends that our ancestors "passed through these tumultuous changes and it is inconceivable that they would not have remarked upon them," and that their eyewitness accounts "became part of revered oral traditions … passed down from generation to generation." The remarkable cross-cultural consistency — in the details of prior warmth, sudden catastrophic cold or flood, advance warnings to chosen survivors, and artificial refuges — is, Hancock maintains, evidence of a single traumatic historical substrate beneath the world's mythologies.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hancock opens Part III by cataloguing three geological events clustered around the Younger Dryas boundary and then asks how "it is inconceivable" that ancestors would not have preserved accounts of them (line 1401). He identifies several myth-traditions and reads each as a direct memory of the cataclysm:

**Native American traditions.** Hancock notes that "certain Native American 'myths' do, absolutely, seem to speak of events at the end of the last Ice Age. The terrible floods that scoured and ravaged the land are described in detail. But of even greater interest are the traditions of the 'star with the long wide tail' that 'came down here once, thousands of years ago,' that 'burned up everything' and that 'made a different world' in which 'the weather was colder than before'" (line 1401). He later quotes the Ojibwa prophecy: "The star with the long, wide tail is going to destroy the world some day when it comes low again. That's the comet called Long-Tailed Heavenly Climbing Star" (line 1551).

**Zoroastrian tradition (Zend Avesta / Vendidad).** Hancock devotes sustained attention to the story of Yima, whose god Ahura Mazda warns: "O fair Yima, upon the material world the fatal winters are going to fall, that shall bring the fierce, foul frost … snowflakes fall thick, even on the highest tops of mountains" (line 1435). The accompanying Bundahish describes an evil spirit that "sprang like a snake out of the sky down to the earth … He rushed in at noon, and thereby the sky was as shattered and frightened by him as a sheep by a wolf … he made the world quite as injured and dark at midday as though it were dark night" (line 1441). Hancock comments: "I couldn't help but be reminded of the two millennia of warm, fine weather … before the sudden lethal onset of the Younger Dryas 12,800 years ago. The Zoroastrian texts would not be far wrong in describing it as a 'fierce, foul frost' and as a 'fatal winter'" (line 1443). The Zoroastrian response — an underground survival shelter (the Vara) stocked with seeds and breeding pairs — directly parallels Noah's Ark.

**Zoroastrian flood text.** The Zoroastrian account also mentions flood: "Every single drop of rain became as big as a bowl and the water stood the height of a man over the whole of this earth" (line 1483) — which Hancock links to the meltwater pulse that accompanied the onset of the Younger Dryas.

**Maya (Popol Vuh).** The Quiché Maya account "speaks of a flood and associates it with 'much hail, black rain and mist, and indescribable cold'" and describes that "it was cloudy and twilight all over the world … The faces of the sun and the moon were covered" (line 1485). Other Maya sources add that "at midday, it got dark … Sunlight was not seen again 'until the twenty-sixth year after the flood'" (line 1485) — consistent, Hancock argues, with dust and smoke loading of the atmosphere after continent-wide comet-triggered wildfires.

**Hebrew (Noah and the Ark).** Hancock draws explicit parallels between the story of Yima/Vara and Noah/Ark: both feature a righteous man chosen by a deity for advance warning, both command the preservation of seeds and breeding pairs of all life, and both describe a vessel with a "window," a "door," and three levels. He also notes that "The Ark was illuminated by a precious stone, the light of which was more brilliant by night than by day, so enabling Noah to distinguish between day and night" (line 1503) — a detail he reads as a memory of lost technology, paralleling the Vara's "artificial lights which shine from below."

**Sumerian (Zisudra / Epic of Gilgamesh).** Hancock traces the flood narrative to the oldest written version, on a Nippur cuneiform tablet dated to the seventeenth century BC, in which the god Enki warns the king Zisudra of "a flood [that] will sweep over the cult centers. / To destroy the seed of mankind / Is the decision, the word of the assembly of gods" (lines 1666–1671). Berossos adds that Enki instructed Zisudra to bury antediluvian tablets at Sippar before the flood and to recover and redistribute them afterward (line 1751) — which Hancock reads as a project for the recovery and repromulgation of pre-cataclysm knowledge.

**Ancient Egyptian (Edfu Building Texts and related sources).** In Chapter 9, Hancock introduces the Edfu Building Texts, which describe a "Homeland of the Primeval Ones" — an island destroyed by the assault of "the Great Leaping One," a celestial serpent: "the primeval water … submerged the island … and the island became the tomb of the original divine inhabitants … the Homeland ended in darkness beneath the primeval waters" (line 1869). He notes that Plato's Timaeus prefaces its account of Atlantis with a celestial cause — the story of Phaethon and "a variation in the course of the heavenly bodies" (line 1877) — and that the Tale of the Shipwrecked Sailor describes how "a star fell, and they went up in flames through it" (line 1845). All of these, Hancock argues, "point to the same horrific epoch of prehistory" (line 1891): the Younger Dryas.

Throughout, Hancock is explicit that "what is surprising is the remarkable consistency with which traditions from every part of the globe speak not only of cataclysmic events but also of very specific warnings given to certain selected 'wise' or 'good' or 'pure' humans in advance of the impending cataclysm" (line 1405).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the linchpin of Part III. It provides the cultural-memory foundation for [[The Younger Dryas comet impact (Hancock)]] — the myths testify that the comet event was witnessed and transmitted. It also sets the stage for [[The lost civilization hypothesis (Hancock)]], because if the myths preserve accurate memory of the Younger Dryas catastrophe, they may equally preserve accurate memory of the civilized peoples who survived it and transmitted knowledge to later cultures. The Sumerian narrative of the Seven Sages (Apkallu), who tutored antediluvian civilization and whose knowledge was to be recovered post-flood, is the Mesopotamian expression of the same cultural memory, and Hancock sees parallel versions in the Edfu "companies of gods" who "wandered" the earth to found new sacred domains.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock acknowledges that the myths have obvious "mythical" or religious dimensions — Zoroastrianism is a "profoundly dualistic religion" in which the "evil spirit" is a theological adversary, not simply a comet. He concedes that no written records survive from 12,800 years ago and that oral traditions were inevitably transformed over millennia. He notes the mainstream position that no high civilization existed in the Upper Palaeolithic, and that archaeologists dismiss the Atlantis tradition as Plato's invention. He also acknowledges that the Younger Dryas Boundary impact hypothesis, while supported by Firestone, West, and Kennett, remains disputed in mainstream geology and archaeology.
