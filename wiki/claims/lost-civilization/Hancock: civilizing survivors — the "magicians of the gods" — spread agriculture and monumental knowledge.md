## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that survivors of the lost pre-Younger Dryas civilization — remembered across cultures as bearded teacher-gods, sages, sorcerers, and "magicians" — escaped the cataclysm and deliberately wandered the post-flood world, transmitting agriculture, astronomy, megalithic architecture, and sacred knowledge to indigenous hunter-gatherer populations. These figures are preserved in Mesopotamian tradition as the Seven Apkallu sages of Oannes, in the Edfu Building Texts as the Seven Sages and "Builder Gods," and in Ancient Egyptian tradition as the Shemsu Hor ("Followers of Horus"). In Hancock's view, the sudden and "fully formed" appearance of monumental architecture at sites such as Göbekli Tepe (9600 BC) and Giza, and the equally sudden emergence of agriculture in the same regions, are most plausibly explained by the input of survivors who "already knew agriculture" and "possessed architectural skills."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The Mesopotamian Apkallu sages.** In Chapter 8, Hancock reviews the Babylonian account preserved by Berossos: Oannes and the brotherhood of Seven Apkallu are said to have appeared among mankind when people "lived in a lawless manner, like the beasts of the field" (line 1715). The sages are described as "conjurers," "sorcerers," "warlocks," "magicians" in the cuneiform texts, but simultaneously as masters of practical skills: "they were masters of 'the chemical recipes,' they were medical doctors, they were carpenters, stone cutters, metal workers and goldsmiths, and they laid the foundations of cities" (line 1723, summarizing cuneiform sources). Hancock quotes the scholar Amar Annus of the University of Tartu: "The period before the deluge was the one of revelation in the Mesopotamian mythology, when the basis of all later knowledge was laid down. The antediluvian sages were culture-heroes, who brought the arts of civilization to the land. During the time that follows this period, nothing new is invented, the original revelation is only transmitted and unfolded. Oannes and other sages taught all foundations of civilization to antediluvian humankind" (line 1725). The Apkallu are often depicted as therianthropes — "part bird and part human in form" (line 1707), or part fish — and each was paired as "counselor" to an antediluvian king, "renowned for their wisdom in affairs of state and for their skills as architects, builders and engineers" (line 1707). Crucially, Hancock notes that the same "curious containers" (banduddu — "buckets") depicted in the hands of the Apkallu sages also appear on a megalithic pillar at Göbekli Tepe and on ancient monuments in Mexico (line 1713).

**Post-flood transmission.** After the Flood, Hancock reports, the Apkallu sages "had been sent back to the depths of the Abzu at the time of the Flood and ordered never to return" (line 1767). In their place, "sages 'of human descent'" continued to transmit the original revelation. The Assyrian king Ashurbanipal boasted: "I learned the craft of Adapa, the sage, which is the secret knowledge … I am well acquainted with the signs of heaven and earth … I am enjoying the writings on stones from before the flood" (line 1767). For Hancock this confirms a self-conscious chain of transmission — from antediluvian civilization, through the flood, and down to historical cultures.

**The Edfu Building Texts: "gods" who wandered the world.** In Chapter 9, Hancock presents what he regards as the Egyptian parallel. The Edfu texts describe survivors of the destroyed Homeland of the Primeval Ones: "companies of gods who were already at sea when the sacred island was flooded … sailed back to the former location of the island after the cataclysm" (line 1933). Finding only reeds and mud, they could not restore the former homeland, so instead "the gods left the original pãy-lands … They … sailed to another part of the primeval world … [and] journeyed through the … lands of the primeval age … In any place in which they settled they founded new sacred domains" (line 1947). Hancock characterizes their mission: "to repromulgate the lost civilization and the lost religion of the days before the flood" (line 1949). Their leader was the Falcon Horus; also present were Thoth the god of wisdom, the "Builder Gods," the Shebtiw, and — crucially — the Seven Sages (line 1953). He emphasizes the cross-cultural parallel: "the Apkallus were often depicted as hybrid creatures, part bird of prey, part human in appearance. Similarly, the Seven Sages of the Edfu texts are described as primeval deities who were capable of assuming 'the form of falcons' and of 'resembling falcons'" (line 1955).

**The Seven Sages as architect-magicians.** Hancock quotes Reymond's study of the Edfu texts: "the Egyptians believed that 'the ground plans of the historical temples were established according to what the Sages of the primeval age revealed to Thoth'" (line 1959). The Sages were believed to be "the only divine beings who knew how the temples and sacred places were created" (line 1961). Their knowledge, like that of the Apkallu, could thereafter "only be passed on but not invented anew" — the same Mesopotamian principle that "nothing new is invented" applies equally in Egypt (line 1961).

**The hunter-gatherer context and the Noah thesis.** In Chapter 8, Hancock proposes that the Noah narrative records the survival and migration of an agricultural people: "the Noah story cannot be literally true in every detail, we must consider the possibility that it is true in its essence, i.e. that it does record the construction of an 'Ark,' in which seeds of useful plants and breeding pairs of animals were perhaps preserved by people who already knew agriculture and who possessed architectural skills, who survived the Flood, who migrated to the lands between Mount Ararat and Göbekli Tepe, and who subsequently disseminated agricultural and architectural knowledge to the indigenous hunter-gatherers of that region" (line 1617). He adds: "The sudden, and indeed completely unprecedented, appearance of giant stone circles at Göbekli Tepe, which surely could only have been conceived and implemented by people with extensive prior experience of megalithic architecture, and the simultaneous 'invention' of agriculture in the exact same locale, are, in my view, highly suggestive of this possibility" (line 1619).

**The "Monastery of the Seven Sages" and Giza.** In Chapters 10–11, Hancock develops the idea that the civilizing survivors established long-lived institutions — "something perhaps like a monastery, with a very small, even negligible, archaeological footprint" (line 2077). He calls this the "Monastery of the Seven Sages" and proposes it was active at Giza from c. 10,500 BC through the Old Kingdom, overseeing an 8,000-year plan to bring about "the resurrection of the former world of the gods … The re-creation of the destroyed world" (lines 2251, 2071). The "Builder Gods" of the Edfu texts — "the Senior Ones who came into being at the beginning, who illumined this land when they came forth unitedly" (line 2301) — renewed themselves through "an unbroken chain of initiation and transmission of knowledge" (line 2303). These same initiates, known in later Egyptian tradition as the Shemsu Hor ("Followers of Horus"), "carried with them a 'knowledge of the divine origins' of Egypt" (line 2305).

**The final synthesis.** Hancock concludes Part IV with his clearest statement of the claim: "it seems we had the guidance, the leadership, the teachings, and the high wisdom of 'the Sages,' 'the Shining Ones' — those 'Magicians of the Gods' — who had survived from antediluvian times and whose mission was to ensure that all was not after all lost" (line 2393). He proposes that their purpose at Giza was to "send us a message" (line 2395) — specifically to encode the epoch of 10,500 BC in stone so that a future astronomically literate civilization could understand what had happened and predict a possible recurrence.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the culminating thesis of Parts III–IV. It depends directly on [[The Younger Dryas comet impact (Hancock)]] — a cataclysm is required to create survivors-with-a-mission — and on [[The lost civilization hypothesis (Hancock)]], which supplies the antediluvian civilization from which the sages come. The cross-cultural appearance of the Seven Sages motif (Apkallu in Mesopotamia, Seven Sages in the Edfu texts, Shemsu Hor in later Egypt) is Hancock's primary evidence for the global reach of the civilizing mission. The Göbekli Tepe connection — its monument phase begins exactly at 9600 BC, the end of the Younger Dryas — is offered as physical archaeological corroboration that survivor-sages appeared in southeastern Turkey in the immediate aftermath of the cataclysm.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock acknowledges that the mainstream explanation for the Neolithic Revolution is indigenous development: hunter-gatherers independently developing agriculture and monuments without any external civilizing input. He admits no inscriptions from the Fourth Dynasty directly attribute the Sphinx to survivors of a lost civilization, and that the "Monastery of the Seven Sages" hypothesis leaves essentially no verifiable archaeological footprint. He notes that the Sumerian traditions of antediluvian sages are understood by Assyriologists as myth, not history. The surface luminescence dating he discusses in Chapter 10 is ambiguous and contested, and he openly states that none of the evidence for the long-lived cult constitutes proof: "I would not want to bet my life or my reputation on it!" (line 1543, in a related context). He also recognizes that Plato's Atlantis account is rejected by mainstream archaeologists as invention.
