## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that the colossal megalithic platform at Baalbek — including the three monster blocks of the Trilithon (each weighing more than 800 tons) and the nine-block southern and northern arms of a U-shaped enclosure wall — was not built by the Romans but was already in place before the Roman Temple of Jupiter was constructed on the site. He contends that neither the Romans nor the earlier Herodian builders erected this wall; rather, they inherited it from a far more ancient, unknown engineering culture that he associates with the lost civilization he hypothesizes throughout [[Magicians of the Gods (Hancock)]]. The quarry megaliths — including the "Stone of the Pregnant Woman" (970 tons) and a newly excavated block of 1,650 tons — reinforce this claim by their inexplicable abandonment.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hancock arrives at Baalbek in July 2014 explicitly in search of evidence for [[The lost civilization hypothesis (Hancock)]]. He describes the Trilithon's three blocks as each more than 64 feet long, over 14 feet high, and nearly 12 feet wide, "fitted in place so precisely that it is impossible to insert even the edge of a razor blade into the joints." The U-shaped wall in which the Trilithon sits is, he argues, "a profoundly megalithic wall, larger in every dimension than any other that the Romans are known to have built anywhere in the world."

The key forensic point Hancock develops is that German archaeologist Daniel Lohmann identifies a pre-Roman construction phase ("Podium 1," linked to Herod the Great) on top of which the Roman temple was built, but treats the vast U-shaped outer wall as Roman "Podium 2" — purely cosmetic and non-load-bearing. Hancock finds this implausible: "it is quite unlike the practical, phlegmatic cast of mind of the Romans … to go to such extreme lengths for purely cosmetic purposes." He presses the case that the U-shaped wall "was already in place long before — perhaps even thousands of years before — Podium 1 was built."

Regarding the column drum found in the foundations below the Trilithon — cited by mainstream archaeologists as proof the Trilithon is Roman — Hancock argues it is most likely a later repair by Arab masons: "Arab masons repairing an undermined foundation would have used whatever lay at hand and the centuries of warfare, earthquakes and other disasters that Baalbek suffered meant that there were enormous quantities of broken columns lying around, as there still are to this day." He further notes that Lohmann himself confirmed no carbon dating exists for "Podium 2"; the entire orthodox chronology rests on stylistic attribution alone: "the entire edifice of archaeological chronology for the so-called 'Julio-Claudian podium' … rests on foundations in which there is no scientific dating evidence whatsoever."

The quarry evidence is central to Hancock's argument. In June 2014, the German Archaeological Institute uncovered a third, previously hidden block — weighing an estimated 1,650 tons — the "single largest block of stone ever quarried in the ancient world." Hancock argues that if the Romans had quarried these blocks and found them too heavy to move, they would have sliced them into smaller pieces for the temple construction rather than leaving them intact: "it's completely obvious that they didn't do so and therefore the fact that these gigantic, almost finished blocks remain in the quarry … suggests to me very strongly that the Romans did not even know they were there."

Professor Haroutune Kalayan's discovery of a full-scale architectural drawing of the Temple of Jupiter's pediment inscribed on the top of the southernmost Trilithon block further suggests to Hancock (though Lohmann disputes this) that "the Trilithon was already in place to serve as a trestle board for the dimensioning and ordering of the pediment blocks" before the temple was built.

Local oral tradition, cited via nineteenth-century traveler David Urquhart, held that the megalithic work predated the Flood and was later repaired: "And then came the Deluge. After that it was repaired by Solomon." Urquhart concluded that "the builders of Baalbek must have been a people who had attained to the highest pinnacle of power and science."

Hancock closes Part V with an explicit connection to the broader catastrophe narrative: "We are a species with amnesia. The devastating comet impacts that set the Younger Dryas in train 12,800 years ago … made us forget so much."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is developed as a physical, on-the-ground counterpart to [[The lost civilization hypothesis (Hancock)]] and [[The Younger Dryas comet impact (Hancock)]]. Hancock frames Baalbek as one of the sites where survivors of the lost civilization settled and left physical memorials — "among the places these 'Magicians of the Gods' chose to settle." The site's 10,000-year-old Neolithic "Tell Baalbek" layer (predating the Romans by over 8,000 years) is used to establish deep prehistoric continuity, and Hancock explicitly notes that this pushes the origins of sacred occupation at Baalbek "very close to the time when Göbekli Tepe flourished in nearby Turkey." The claim therefore connects to the Göbekli Tepe astronomy argument developed in Part VI.

Hancock is careful to distinguish his claim from "ancient astronaut" explanations, explicitly rejecting Zecharia Sitchin's "landing platform" thesis as "fundamentally flawed" and based on ignorance of the actual layout of the site.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock presents Lohmann's counter-argument at length and fairly. Lohmann holds that the U-shaped wall is Roman, built as a "standard Roman temple podium" in the Julio-Claudian era. His evidence includes: (1) the column drum in the Trilithon foundations, which he personally re-excavated and measured, finding its surface dressing "exactly the same as the column drums of the Roman Jupiter Temple"; (2) stylistic parallels with other Roman podia; (3) the fact that Roman-era construction at Baalbek generally — columns, pediment blocks — was demonstrably on a colossal scale, proving Roman capability. Lohmann is "100% certain" (his own phrase, as quoted by Hancock) the column drum is original Roman construction, not a later repair. He also provides architectural comparanda from other Roman-era Lebanese temples and notes that the unusual setback of the peristasis is paralleled at Palmyra and Aizanoi. Hancock acknowledges "Lohmann makes a very good case" but remains unpersuaded.

Kalayan himself, while arguing the Trilithon predates the Roman superstructure, provided a counterweight to Hancock's further speculation by noting a column drum of Jupiter-temple proportions in the Trilithon foundations, which "suggests that the columns were already cut, or were in process of shaping when the foundations of the Trilithon had started" — potentially placing the Trilithon within the Roman building sequence.
