## Claim Statement

Hancock's master thesis: a technologically and astronomically advanced **human** civilization existed in remote prehistory, was destroyed by a global cataclysm at the end of the last Ice Age, and left its "fingerprints" on the later civilizations its survivors helped seed — making the world's ancient cultures heirs to a forgotten common source rather than independent inventions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The book assembles the case circumstantially across continents: anomalous maps of an ice-free Antarctica, near-identical flood and "civilizing god" myths worldwide, a shared precessional astronomy, and megalithic engineering beyond the supposed capacity of its builders. Hancock frames these as evidence of "Earth's lost civilization" whose "accurate information has been passed down from people to people" (quoting Hapgood). He repeatedly stresses the civilization was **human**, explicitly distinguishing his thesis from extraterrestrial "ancient astronaut" claims.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock concedes he cannot name the civilization, fix its date precisely, or supply its physical remains (his Part VIII is titled "Where's the Body?"), and that mainstream archaeology rejects his chronology; he presents the thesis as the best explanation of converging anomalies.

## Contexts Within the Source

The umbrella for the strand-claims of each Part. It contrasts sharply with the **extraterrestrial** origin theses of [[Man was created as a 'primitive worker' to relieve the mutinying Anunnaki of gold mining]] (Sitchin) and the ancient-astronaut readings of Wallis — while resonating with the lost-Atlantis/wisdom motif of [[A single secret doctrine has been preserved by the Mystery schools across all ages]] (Hall).
