## Claim Statement

Donald Hoffman asserts that **consciousness, not spacetime and its objects, is fundamental reality**, and that objective reality "is properly described as a network of conscious agents." This monism is **[[Conscious Realism]]**: conscious agents are fundamental, and spacetime, matter, neurobiology, physics, and Darwinian evolution must *emerge* as the projected perceptual interface of certain conscious agents. "We have met reality and it is like us. We are conscious agents, and so is objective reality."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 10** reaches the claim by elimination and by Occam's Razor:
- No theory starting with objects in spacetime (the Astonishing Hypothesis; Penrose–Hameroff microtubules; Tononi–Koch IIT; the simulation hypothesis) can explain a single conscious experience; "you simply cannot cook up consciousness from unconscious ingredients" (see [[Physicalism cannot explain the origin of consciousness (the hard problem)]]).
- Occam's Razor counsels a monism; physicalism is one option, but **conscious realism** is another monism that takes conscious agents as fundamental.
- A toy universe of two interacting [[Conscious Agent (Hoffman)|conscious agents]] (each the "world" for the other) generalizes to networks of three, four, or infinitely many.
- The decline of insight as we look from human → ant → quark is a limit of *our interface*, not a real "dimmer switch" on consciousness: "We have mistaken the limits of our interface for an insight into reality." A rock is an icon — not conscious — but a low-information *portal* to a realm of conscious agents.

**Appendix** gives the formal **definition of a conscious agent** (seven-tuple of measurable spaces and Markovian kernels) and the **Conscious Agent Thesis**: "every aspect of consciousness can be modeled by conscious agents." Agents combine *ad infinitum* (yielding, in the limit, "infinite conscious agents" Hoffman likens to God); split-brain surgery is reinterpreted as a scalpel splitting one conscious agent into two.

Hoffman distinguishes conscious realism from **panpsychism** (which attributes consciousness to physical objects and, he argues, collapses into dualism) and from **solipsism** (there *is* an observer-independent reality — it is just other conscious agents).

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the book's constructive turn after nine chapters of critique. Hoffman frames it as a *promissory note* and the Conscious Agent Thesis as a deliberately falsifiable "big target." He situates the ideas in a long lineage (Parmenides, Pythagoras, Plato, Leibniz, Kant, Hegel; Buddhism, Hinduism, mystical Islam/Judaism/Christianity; Berkeley's *esse is percipi*), claiming the novelty is making them "precise and testable."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hoffman voices and answers objections in the chapter's own dialogue: that mathematizing consciousness loses its richness; that physicalism is the "proper ontology for science" (he replies science presumes *no* ontology, only a method); that unconscious mental processes refute conscious realism (he replies they may be conscious to *other agents in one's instantiation*); and the Copernican objection that conscious realism re-centers humans (he replies it grants human consciousness no special status). He is explicit that the dynamics of conscious agents that would recover physics and evolution is **not yet known** ("I'm not yet sure"), floating analogies to cryptocurrency conservation and Federico Faggin's "mutual comprehension."
