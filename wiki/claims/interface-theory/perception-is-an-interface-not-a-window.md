## Claim Statement

Donald Hoffman asserts that perception is a **user interface** — like a computer desktop — specific to each species and shaped by natural selection to hide objective reality and guide adaptive behavior, not to reveal truth. "Spacetime is our desktop, and physical objects, such as spoons and stars, are icons of the interface of Homo sapiens." This is the **[[Interface Theory of Perception (ITP)]]**.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 5** develops the desktop metaphor: a blue rectangular email icon is not the true color, shape, or location of the file; its job "is to hide that nature." Likewise "perception is not a window on objective reality. It is an interface that hides objective reality behind a veil of helpful icons."

Key moves in the text:
- **Serious vs. literal**: "I take my perceptions seriously, but not literally." (See [[Take perceptions seriously, but not literally]].)
- Objects are **icons / data structures** created with a glance and destroyed with a blink; the same is argued for one's own body ("you're actually seeing yourself as an icon inside your own data structure," ch. 8).
- A new definition of **illusion**: "a perception that fails to guide adaptive behavior" (a Necker cube's seen shape cannot be grasped; a sugar cube's can).
- Precedents and parallels: George Berkeley (*esse is percipi*), Kant (rocks and stars not mind-independent), and the biologist **Jakob von Uexküll**'s *umwelt* (each species has its own interface — though von Uexküll wrongly, per Hoffman, denied selection shaped it).

Chapters 7–9 reinforce the claim: spacetime/objects as data-compression and error-correcting code (ch. 7), color and chromatures as compressed fitness codes and synesthesia as proof of idiosyncratic interfaces (ch. 8), and attention as a fitness forager (ch. 9).

Hoffman rebuts intuitive objections: the consensus that everyone sees the same spoon is explained by shared interfaces ("we all construct our icons in similar ways"), not a public spoon; touching a Maserati with eyes closed creates a *tactile* icon, not proof of objectivity; mastering an interface (Minecraft, science, technology) is "prowess, not truth."

## Contexts Within the Source

ITP is presented as the intuitive companion to the [[Fitness-Beats-Truth (FBT) Theorem]]: the theorem proves selection favors fitness over truth; the interface metaphor makes the counterintuitive result graspable. Hoffman notes ITP is logically *independent* of [[Conscious Realism]] — one could accept the interface while denying the reality behind it is conscious.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hoffman quotes the standard vision-science view (e.g. Louis Jolyon West's *Encyclopaedia Britannica* entry; the textbook account of illusion) that perception is normally veridical, and engages skeptics by name (Michael Shermer's *Scientific American* columns on mimicry and on the fitness/truth dichotomy), arguing each objection fails. See [[Do our perceptions report objective reality?]].
