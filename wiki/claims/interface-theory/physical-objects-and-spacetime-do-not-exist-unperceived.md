## Claim Statement

Donald Hoffman asserts that spoons, stars, neurons, and all objects in space and time — and spacetime itself — **do not exist when unperceived**. "If you look and see a spoon, then there is a spoon. But as soon as you look away, the spoon ceases to exist. Something continues to exist, but it is not a spoon and is not in space and time." With Berkeley, "for spoons, quarks, and stars, ITP agrees... that *esse is percipi* — to be is to be perceived." This explicitly contradicts Francis Crick's claim that "the sun existed before there was anyone to perceive it."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 1** primes the claim with the **Necker cube**: since observers see cube A or cube B (and disagree), "neither Cube A nor Cube B is there when no one looks... There are as many cubes as there are observers constructing cubes."

**Chapter 5** generalizes: objects are "satisficing displays" / data structures created to solve fitness-gathering problems and discarded once they serve their purpose. Hoffman diagnoses the realist intuition as "reifying my habit into a preexisting spoon."

**Chapter 6** turns the claim into **testable physics predictions**: an unobserved photon has no definite polarization; an unobserved electron has no definite spin, position, or momentum. He argues physics confirms these:
- **Bell's Theorem** (John Bell, 1964) and the Delft entangled-electron experiment refute *local realism*.
- The **Kochen–Specker Theorem** (1967) refutes *noncontextual realism* — "precisely what we espouse in saying the moon is there when no one looks." The Cabello–Estebaranz–García-Alcaine construction shows certainty about a measured value does not imply the value preexisted; a Fermilab neutrino experiment shows lepton flavor has no value until observed.
- **QBism** (Fuchs, Mermin, Schack) and Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics: "reality differs from one agent to another."

Hoffman insists this is **not solipsism**: "There is an objective reality... But that reality is utterly unlike our perceptions of objects in space and time." Touching, smelling, or a friend's seeing the object merely creates further icons.

## Contexts Within the Source

The claim is the sharpest, most falsifiable consequence of the [[Interface Theory of Perception (ITP)]]. Hoffman addresses the legal/common-sense worry ("if the Maserati is my icon, can I steal it?") by distinguishing two senses of "real" — *existing* vs. *existing even when unperceived* — and noting that one's icon being distinct from another's does not license doing anything one wishes with it.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The opposing view (Einstein's local realism; Crick's metaphysical realism; common sense) is presented in detail. Hoffman notes physics has not *settled* whether realism or locality is the false assumption, and that he omits interpretations (Bohm, Everett) that try to preserve objective objects and spacetime; he claims only that ITP is "not proscribed" by physics.
