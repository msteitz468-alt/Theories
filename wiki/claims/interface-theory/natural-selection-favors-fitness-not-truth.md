## Claim Statement

Donald Hoffman asserts that evolution by natural selection does **not** shape perception to report objective reality accurately; instead it shapes perception to detect and act on **fitness payoffs**. Far from favoring true perceptions, natural selection routinely drives them to extinction. The very language of our perceptions — space, time, shape, hue, saturation, brightness, texture, taste, sound, smell, motion — "cannot describe reality as it is when no one looks. It's not simply that this or that perception is wrong. It's that none of our perceptions, being couched in this language, could possibly be right."

This is presented not as a hunch but as a mathematical consequence of evolutionary game theory: the [[Fitness-Beats-Truth (FBT) Theorem]].

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 4** establishes the result through the [[Fitness payoff function]] distinction: fitness payoffs depend "on the organism, its state, its action, and its competition," not just on the world's state, so "seeing truth and seeing fitness are two distinct strategies of perception, not one strategy seen in different lights."

The **FBT Theorem** (Hoffman conjectured, Chetan Prakash proved): "Fitness drives Truth to extinction with probability at least (N–3)/(N–1)." For the human eye's ~130 million photoreceptors, the probability of seeing reality "is effectively zero."

Supporting demonstrations in the text:
- The **hawk–dove game**: which strategy selection favors depends on payoffs and on the frequency of strategies — "fitness is no mirror of the world."
- The **kritter/stuf** example: with a bell-shaped fitness function, a Fitness perceiver knows when to forage safely; a Truth perceiver "must risk its life to forage. The truth won't make you free, it will make you extinct."
- **Justin Mark's** genetic-algorithm simulations: after hundreds of generations of coevolving perception and action, "Truth did not appear."
- The point that our brains are *shrinking* (~10% over 20,000 years) rebuts Marr's idea that bigger brains see more truth.

Hoffman rebuts a battery of objections (the content objection of Jonathan Cohen, locality, finite populations, neutral drift, the role of natural selection per Rainer Mausfeld, the math-and-logic self-refutation worry) and quotes Robert Trivers ("the conventional view that natural selection favors nervous systems which produce ever more accurate images of the world must be a very naïve view") and Steven Pinker ("our minds evolved by natural selection to solve problems that were life-and-death matters to our ancestors, not to commune with correctness").

## Contexts Within the Source

Hoffman frames the claim through **universal Darwinism** — Darwin's abstract algorithm of variation, heredity, and selection — which makes no assumption that physical objects in spacetime exist, and so can question that assumption without self-refutation. He likens Darwin's idea, with Daniel Dennett, to a "universal acid" that dissolves the objectivity of physical objects and then of spacetime itself, leaving even Darwinian evolution as "an imperfect hint... about a deeper, and as yet unknown, dynamics."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hoffman presents the opposing **veridical-perception consensus** at length (chapter 3) — Crick, Marr, Trivers, Stephen Palmer, Zygmunt Pizlo, Geisler & Diehl, Noë & O'Regan — who hold that selection makes perception accurate. He treats these as the positions his theorem refutes; see [[Do our perceptions report objective reality?]]. He concedes the theorem assumes a science of objective reality is possible (states can carry probabilities), an assumption he grants could be false.
