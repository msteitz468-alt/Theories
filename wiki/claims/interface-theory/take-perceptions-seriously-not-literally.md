## Claim Statement

Donald Hoffman asserts that although we **must** take our perceptions seriously — evolution shaped them to keep us alive — it is "a mistake of logic to assume that if we must take our senses seriously then we are required, or even entitled, to take them literally." Conflating the two is the **"Serious-Literal fallacy."** This is the argumentative key that lets the [[Interface Theory of Perception (ITP)]] answer the obvious rebuttal ("if a snake is just an icon, why not grab it?").

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Preface and chapter 5**: "I wouldn't leap in front of a speeding car for the same reason I wouldn't carelessly drag my blue icon to the trashcan. Not because I take the icon literally — the file is not blue. But I do take it seriously: if I drag the icon to the trashcan, I could lose my work."

Supporting examples:
- The **biohazard** and **ionizing-radiation** warning signs: each must be taken seriously, but neither literally depicts the hazard.
- A **sonar operator**'s green glowing dot must be taken seriously, but "torpedoes are not green, glowing dots."
- Therefore seeing a rattlesnake writhing toward you warrants flight without implying "there is something brown, sleek, and sharp of tooth when no one observes."

Hoffman names the failure to keep the distinction the source of our impulse "to reify physical objects and snipe-hunt among our figments for progenitors of consciousness."

## Contexts Within the Source

The claim is the logical lynchpin that protects ITP and [[Physical objects and spacetime do not exist when unperceived]] from being dismissed as self-evidently absurd or suicidal. It recurs whenever Hoffman fields the "but it's real, it can kill you" objection.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The objection itself ("after you die, then we'll have proof that a car is not just an icon") is voiced repeatedly in the text as the position Hoffman is rebutting; he presents no internal tension in his own distinction.
