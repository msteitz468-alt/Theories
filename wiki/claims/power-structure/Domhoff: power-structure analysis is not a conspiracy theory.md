## Claim Statement

Domhoff draws a sharp line between his sociological power-structure analysis and conspiracy theories of power. Conspiracy theories — from the 1790s Illuminati and Freemasonry scares to the Bilderbergers, a Communist-controlled Eisenhower, a UN "new world order," and the present "deep state" of pedophile plotters — posit a small, secret group of behind-the-scenes plotters seizing the government. Domhoff argues that no such conspiracy has ever taken over the United States; power instead rests with a large, visible, institutionally based leadership group that can be studied through public records.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Cultures of resentment often lead to the development of conspiracy theories about power. They allege that a small organized group of secretive and unknown outside plotters are attempting to take over the government." He dispatches each version: the Soviet collapse "put an end to" the Eisenhower-as-Communist theory; "the United Nations always has been powerless in the face of resistance by the most powerful nation-states"; and "there are no such pedophiles," while "deep state" civil servants "are supervised by individuals from the private sector, who are appointed to their positions by the president" (9,000–10,000 are fired yearly).

He then itemizes four ways "a conspiratorial view is different… from a sociological perspective on power." Conspiracy theories (1) rest on "psychological assumptions about an alleged small group… [with] an extreme desire for power," where sociologists "assume that leaders act for understandable sociological reasons, such as profit-seeking motives, or to fulfill their assigned institutional roles"; (2) assume leaders are "extremely clever," whereas "leaders often make shortsighted or mistaken decisions"; (3) place power "in the hands of only a relative handful of people, numbering at most in the hundreds, whereas sociological studies find leadership groups numbering in the thousands" who serve "in visible positions… which can be readily studied through legally required annual reports"; and (4) assume "illegal plans… can be kept secret for long periods of time, but all evidence shows secret groups are uncovered." He distinguishes this from real but soon-uncovered illegal acts by officials (Watergate, Iran-Contra, the 2016 campaign's acceptance of Russian help).

## Contexts Within the Source

The methodological keystone of the book and its single most important contribution to this wiki: it defines the difference between [[The power elite]] (visible, institutional, thousands strong) and the secret cabals posited across the New-World-Order cluster. It underwrites the empirical approach of [[Domhoff: power is measured by three indicators — who governs, who benefits, who wins]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff explicitly concedes the reality of *illegal actions* by candidates and officials — "there have been violations of laws by presidential candidates, presidents, and their appointed aides" — but insists these were limited, soon uncovered, and punished, not standing conspiracies to seize the state. He also allows that the January 6, 2021 Capitol attack would be investigated as a possible coup attempt.
