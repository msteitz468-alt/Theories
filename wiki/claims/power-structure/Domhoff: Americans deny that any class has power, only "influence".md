## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that a distinctive American cultural inheritance — the Revolutionary-era conviction that "power" belongs to "the people" — makes Americans deny that any group or class actually *has* power (only "influence"), and similarly makes them reluctant to admit that social classes exist at all. This denial, he notes, is itself a resource for the powerful: any class openly claiming power is seen as illegitimate, "which may help explain why those with power in America always deny they have any."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

On power: since the Revolution "it has been widely agreed that 'power' in the United States belongs to 'the people.'" Consequently "it was agreed by all that no group or class has 'power' in America, but only 'influence.' Any small group or class that has power over 'the people' is therefore perceived as illegitimate. This may help explain why those with power in America always deny they have any."

On class: "For most white Americans, being a member of a 'class' implies that people have permanent stations in life, which flies in the face of beliefs about equality of opportunity." They use euphemisms — "the blue bloods," "the suits," "Joe Sixpack," "the other side of the tracks" — and even the upper class evades the word. Domhoff quotes upper-class informants: "One does not speak of classes; they are felt"; "I hate [the term] upper class. It's so non-upper class to use it. I just call it 'all of us,' those of us who are wellborn." He notes that "'race'… is another factor that made the class-based nature of American society less salient than it might otherwise have been."

## Contexts Within the Source

Explains the cultural fog the book sets out to cut through: because Americans deny class and power, the visible machinery of [[The power elite]] goes unexamined. It complements [[Domhoff: power-structure analysis is not a conspiracy theory]] — the same denial that hides ordinary class power also feeds the appetite for hidden-conspiracy explanations.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff acknowledges Americans are not simply ignorant: they "are very aware of basic economic and educational differences" and "can size up social standing fairly well from such outward signs as speech patterns, mannerisms, and style of dress" — they simply pass over class "as quickly as possible." He also notes genuine, if exaggerated, grounds for the egalitarian self-image (a large middle class, real mobility for some, the absence of a feudal aristocracy).
