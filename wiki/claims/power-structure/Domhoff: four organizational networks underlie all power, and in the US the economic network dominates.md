## Claim Statement

Following sociologist Michael Mann, Domhoff holds that all power arises from control of one or more of four organizational networks — economic, political, military, and religious (ideological) — none of which is inherently primary; historical circumstance determines which becomes ascendant in a given society. In the United States, he argues, the **economic network has been dominant since early colonial times**, the political network has always stood above the military, and the religious network was formally barred from government by church–state separation. This is why American power takes the form of business class domination rather than rule by state, army, or church.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"The ability of a group or class to prevail begins with its control of one or more of four major organizational networks." The economic network handles "the extraction, transformation, distribution, and consumption of the objects of nature"; the political network "leads to government"; the military network "uses overwhelming deadly force"; and the religious network "provides people with meaning, forgiveness, solace, and a sense of community" (the most common form of a broader "ideology network" that also includes "communism and fascism").

"There is no one of the four major organizational networks that is primary to the others. Each one presupposes the others. Historical circumstances determine which one or more… become ascendant." For the US: "the economic network has been the dominant network from very early in its colonial history, and the political network always has been ascendant over the military network. The religious network… was formally kept out of government through the separation of the church and the state." Domhoff contrasts this with countries where "military force has led to the capture of the government," or where "religious groups" control the state, "such as in Iran."

## Contexts Within the Source

The structural backdrop for the entire analysis: it explains *why* the US is ruled by a corporate-economic elite rather than a state or military elite, a point Domhoff develops historically in [[Domhoff: the United States is a case of class domination rooted in the early ascendancy of business]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff stresses the framework is contingent, not deterministic: "The four power networks can combine in different ways in different times and places to create widely varying power structures," and power structures are "constantly changing." The religious network, though barred from US government, "has had its greatest role in sustaining social movements, such as the civil rights movement."
