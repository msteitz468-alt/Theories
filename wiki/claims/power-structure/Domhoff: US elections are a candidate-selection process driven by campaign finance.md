## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that the structural features of American elections — single-member-district plurality voting, the direct election of a powerful president, and the primary system — combine to produce a two-party system in which policy alternatives are systematically suppressed in favor of personality, image, and name recognition. The result is that "elections are reduced to a candidate-selection process, with very little emphasis on offering policy alternatives to the voting public." Campaign finance is the mechanism by which the corporate rich most directly shape this process: large donations create the "choke point" of start-up money that determines which candidates are viable, while Citizens United and successor rulings opened unlimited and increasingly undisclosed channels for corporate spending. This makes US elections substantially less responsive to public preferences than comparable democracies using proportional representation, and it is reinforced historically by the Southern-Democrat / Northern-Republican conservative coalition that allowed corporate interests to dominate Congress for most of the twentieth century.

## Source Evidence and Direct Quotations

**Structural constraints — why only two parties.** Domhoff explains that the "single-member-district plurality system" — selecting one representative per district by a plurality vote, not a majority — creates an inexorable two-party gravitational pull: "a vote for a third-party candidate of the right or left is in effect a vote for the voter's least-favored candidate on the other side of the political spectrum." The presidency amplifies this: "the election of a president through a plurality vote is in effect a strong version of the single-member-district plurality system, with the nation serving as the only district." By contrast, countries that adopted proportional representation (Belgium, Denmark, Sweden) did so precisely to contain rising labor parties, making even that "fairer" system a "containment strategy that very much succeeded." Electoral rules, in other words, are "first and foremost containment strategies" — the product of strategic retreats by property-owning elites.

**Elections as candidate selection, not policy choice.** In a two-party system, "candidates for elected office do not think it is helpful to focus on their policy goals in campaigning for office. Personality and name recognition are perceived as having more importance in attracting voters." Domhoff cites political scientist Walter Burnham's finding that "the absence of several of the functions of political parties in other countries, such as voter education and presentation of policy alternatives, reduced American political parties to the task of filling offices, which is one major reason why elections became a candidate-selection process."

**Campaign finance as the choke point.** The power elite "can play an important role through large campaign contributions that are used to raise name recognition and craft a winning image." Domhoff quotes a 1960 detailed study that identified start-up money as the "'choke point' in American politics": "it is the need for a large amount of start-up money — to travel around a district or the country, to send out mass mailings, and to schedule television ads in advance — that gives the corporate rich and their employees in lobbying and public relations a very direct role in the process right from the start." Donations also create psychological obligation: "there is evidence that people everywhere feel a sense of 'debt' upon receiving a gift, which leads them to want to reciprocate." The Citizens United ruling (2010), which treated corporate treasury spending as free speech, unleashed "super PACs" and pass-through "501(c)4s" that allow unlimited undisclosed donations — with the Chamber of Commerce alone spending "$31.2 [million] in undisclosed funds to help Republican candidates in 2010."

Domhoff is careful to note that money is necessary but not sufficient: "the candidate with the most money does not always win," and "'Money is a necessary condition for electoral success. But it's not sufficient, and it's never been.'" Trump's 2016 primary victories over far-better-financed opponents illustrate this caveat.

**The conservative coalition.** The deeper historical mechanism underpinning elite domination of Congress was "an informal, bipartisan bloc of conservatives" — Southern Democrats aligned with Northern Republicans — that "formed a conservative coalition in 1938, which voted together on anywhere from 15% to 40% of the votes in any given two-year session of Congress." Between 1939 and 1983, this coalition "had a majority in the House in all but four of its sessions" and "was able to block, water down, or turn to its own advantage every initiative put forward by the liberal-labor alliance between 1939 and 1996 and opposed by the corporate community." The formal Democratic control of Congress during this era was therefore "largely irrelevant in terms of understanding the domination of government policy by the power elite. The important point is that a strong conservative majority was elected to Congress, some of whom were Republicans, some of whom were Democrats."

**Social class of elected officials.** As a further measure of the candidate-selection outcome: "Between 1950 and 2010, the 10% of the workforce in business or law contributed 75% of the members of Congress, whereas less than 2% of the members of Congress were former blue-collar workers or union officials." Most presidents since 1900 were wealthy before entering national politics, and many accumulate far greater wealth after leaving office through speaking fees, corporate board memberships, and lobbying work.

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 6 is the electoral counterpart to Chapters 4 and 5: where the policy-planning and opinion-shaping networks operate between elections, the candidate-selection process explains what happens during them. The chapter completes Domhoff's three-channel model of elite influence — policy planning, opinion shaping, candidate selection — showing that each channel reinforces the others. ## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff emphasizes significant uncertainty and limits to elite control of elections. Wars, economic downturns, and public outrage (e.g., the 2008 financial crisis, rising Iraq War casualties) demonstrably shift electoral outcomes against incumbent parties regardless of campaign spending. The primary system has introduced "wild cards" — from socialist Upton Sinclair winning the 1934 California Democratic primary to Donald Trump defeating the entire corporate-backed Republican establishment in 2016. Domhoff also notes that "the four years of the Trump Administration demonstrate once again that there is too much uncertainty and volatility in the workings of the electoral system and the government for the power elite to leave anything to chance" — which is precisely why they invest in institutionalized policy-shaping channels rather than relying on elections alone.
