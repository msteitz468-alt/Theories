## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that the owners and top executives of large US corporations are not merely an economic category but a socially cohesive upper class — fewer than 0.5% of Americans — whose members are knit together from childhood through a common network of exclusive institutions: boarding schools, elite universities, social clubs, debutante seasons, and resort retreats. This social cohesion is consequential: it produces shared values, interpersonal trust, and a class-wide "sense of justified privilege" that makes policy consensus possible. Rising professional managers from outside the upper class are assimilated into it economically and socially, so that no real separation between corporate ownership and corporate control exists in practice.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Domhoff opens the chapter by identifying three reasons why the corporate–upper-class overlap matters. First, it refutes the "separation of ownership and control" thesis: wealthy stockholding families remain involved in corporate direction through family offices, holding companies, and investment partnerships, while "professional managers of middle-level origins are assimilated into the upper class both socially and economically and share the values of upper-class owners." Second, social psychology shows that "the most socially cohesive groups more often succeed in arriving at consensus when asked to deal with a problem." Third, close corporate–upper-class ties enable the conversion of economic power into status power, creating "a social psychology of justified entitlement and demand for respect" within the class and "a social psychology of envy and deference in some of those who are outside of the charmed circles."

The institutional infrastructure of the upper class begins in childhood. Domhoff describes an interlocking educational pathway — preschool, private day school, boarding school, Ivy League university — so insulated "that many upper-class students never see the inside of a public school in all their years of education." Boarding schools are described as "surrogate families that play a major role in creating a national upper-class subculture," functioning as "total institutions, isolating their members from the outside world and providing them with a set of routines and traditions that encompass most of their waking hours," with "the end result… a feeling of separateness and superiority." An informant summarises the network function precisely: "Where I went to boarding school, there were girls from all over the country, so I know people from all over. It's helpful when you move to a new city and want to get invited into the local social club."

Social clubs reinforce the network in adulthood. Domhoff reports that "men and women of the upper class often belong to clubs in several cities, creating a nationwide pattern of overlapping memberships," and that these overlaps "provide evidence for social cohesion within the upper class." Membership-network analyses of 20–30 clubs in major cities showed sufficient overlap to form regional groupings bridged by "several dozen men in three or more of the clubs, most of them very wealthy people who sat on several corporate boards." The Bohemian Grove — an annual two-week retreat in a 2,700-acre forest north of San Francisco attended by 1,500–2,500 corporate leaders, government officials, and celebrities — is offered as a microcosm of "class bonding" and renewal of shared values, even if "no business deals or policy plans are made there."

The assimilation of rising executives is documented in detail. Roughly one-third of the leaders of the nation's largest firms graduated from Harvard, Yale, or Princeton, and two-thirds from one of the 12 most heavily endowed schools. Corporate atmospheres create "a pressure toward social conformity" because "the need for trust… causes management to become so socially restricting; to develop tight inner circles excluding social strangers; to keep control in the hands of socially homogeneous peers." Successful managers are gradually drawn into upper-class charitable boards, social clubs, and private-school parent networks. At the same time, "stock-option plans, in conjunction with salaries and bonuses in the millions to tens of millions of dollars… make it possible for rising executives to accumulate several million dollars fairly quickly," enabling them to "live upper-class lifestyles."

The conclusion is clear: "the upper class at any given historical moment consists of a complex network of overlapping social circles linked by the members they have in common and by the numerous bonds of trust created by common cultural styles and values." Members develop "a class awareness, which includes feelings of superiority, pride, and entitlement, and a sense they are fully deserving of their station in life" — "a social psychology of justified privilege, which is reinforced by shared social identities and interpersonal ties."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the empirical foundation for the rest of the book. Social cohesion at the upper-class level is what makes the [[The policy-planning network]] effective: as Domhoff writes in the chapter's conclusion, "the tendency toward a general class perspective is utilized and reinforced within the policy-planning network discussed in the next chapter." Without demonstrated social cohesion, the claim that the corporate rich act collectively as a ruling class would be implausible. The chapter therefore directly supports [[Domhoff: a policy-planning network of foundations and think tanks shapes the policies that become law]] and [[Domhoff: the power elite is the leadership arm of the corporate rich]], and it deepens [[Domhoff: a nationwide corporate community is knit together by interlocking directorates]] by adding the social dimension to the structural one. See also concept page [[The social upper class]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff acknowledges several qualifications. Social indicators (prep-school attendance, club memberships, Social Register listing) produce both false positives (scholarship students at elite schools who are not upper class) and false negatives (upper-class members who omit affiliations from public biographical sources). He notes that customs can change — the Social Register lost appeal by the early 1980s — and that "not all men and women of the upper class fit the usual molds": some are dropouts, failures, or even left-liberal donors to activist causes (e.g., the Funding Exchange network). The Horatio Alger myth of rags-to-riches mobility is addressed and largely debunked statistically, but Domhoff grants that the upper class is "a mixture of old and new members" with genuine social mobility into it from below.
