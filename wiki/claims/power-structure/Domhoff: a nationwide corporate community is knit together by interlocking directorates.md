## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that the several thousand large corporations, banks, and financial firms in the United States are not isolated competitors but form a coherent community bound together by overlapping boards of directors, shared use of the same law, accounting, advertising, and public relations firms, and membership in the same overarching business associations. This network, which Domhoff calls the corporate community, is empirically measurable through membership network analysis of interlocking directorates, and the resulting data show that the overwhelming majority of major corporations are directly or indirectly tied to one another. Domhoff treats corporate cohesion as the structural foundation of upper-class power, and the network as a mechanism through which "common business and political perspectives can gradually emerge."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Domhoff's starting point is that corporations "have many types of connections and common bonds. They include shared ownership; longstanding patterns of supply and purchase; and the use of the same legal, accounting, advertising, and public relations firms. Corporations also share common (overlapping) members on their boards of directors." He explains that "for research purposes, the most objective starting point for grasping the full scope of the corporate community is in the interlocks created when a person sits on two or more corporate boards."

Using membership network analysis of Fortune 500 data for 2010–2011, Domhoff and colleagues found that "87.5% of the Fortune 500 were connected to each other." When the directors of six overarching business associations — the National Association of Manufacturers, the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, the Conference Board, the Committee for Economic Development, the Business Council, and the Business Roundtable — were added to the database, "453 of the 506 organizations (89.5%) were part of one large network." The network also shows "considerable 'redundancy,'" meaning "it would not fall apart if some of the links were broken."

The Business Roundtable and the Business Council are identified as the core coordinators: "the Business Roundtable, with 209 members, and the Business Council, with 132 members, shared 70 members in common." Together, they link "two-fifths (40%) of the Fortune 500 companies and the other general business associations … directly or indirectly."

Domhoff also documents historical depth. In New York in 1836, "all but two of the 20 largest banks, ten largest insurance companies, and ten largest railroads were linked into one common network." The modern national network coalesced during the merger wave of 1895–1904, and "the corporate community has remained relatively stable in its contours and membership ever since."

Beyond financial and industrial firms, Domhoff shows that "many corporate law firms are members of the corporate community, by virtue of the fact that numerous corporate directors are partners in corporate law firms." Defense contractors and high-tech companies are equally embedded: Lockheed Martin "had director interlocks with six non-defense companies," Boeing had ties to 12, and "the 21 defense companies large enough to be in the Fortune 500 are completely integrated into the corporate network."

The result, Domhoff concludes, is that "the major national-level businesses in the United States are closely interconnected in enough ways to be called a corporate community. Despite the constant competition and deal-making among them … the directors of the companies in the corporate community, through their numerous interactions at board meetings and in business associations, provide the basis for a cohesive corporate leadership group to emerge."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the empirical backbone of Chapter 2 and sets up the entire book's argument. The corporate community's cohesion is the precondition for the formation of [[The power elite]] (Chapter 1's concept), the policy-planning network ([[Domhoff: a policy-planning network of foundations and think tanks shapes the policies that become law]]), and the upper-class social world ([[Domhoff: the corporate owners and managers form a socially cohesive upper class]]). Domhoff is explicit that the interlocks "provide the best starting point because they are the most visible and accessible of the many links among corporations," but they are only a starting point; policy views are developed in the nonprofit policy-planning network examined in Chapter 4.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff acknowledges that interlocking directorates have "sometimes" been overstated in past research: "The importance of corporate interlocks can be greatly overstated and has been in the past." He notes they are "not important in reducing the 'resource dependencies' regarding loans, raw materials, and other resources" and that "general policy discussions very often [do not] take place at the meetings of boards of directors." Their primary significance is informational and relational, not a mechanism of direct operational control.
