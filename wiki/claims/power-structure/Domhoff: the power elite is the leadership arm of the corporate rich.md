## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that the power elite — a concept he adapts from C. Wright Mills — is the active leadership group drawn from the intersection of the corporate community, the social upper class, and the policy-planning network. It is not synonymous with any of those three networks: it comprises only those individuals who participate across at least two of them in consequential leadership or trustee roles. Functioning as the organisational arm through which the corporate rich exercise power over government, the power elite should be understood as an institutionalised leadership group rather than a conspiratorial clique. Its coherence flows from shared class background, social ties, and the deliberative work done in the policy-planning network.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Domhoff introduces the concept formally in Chapter 4 by locating it within the tripartite structure he has been building across the book: "The foundations, think tanks, and policy-discussion groups in the policy-planning network, in concert with the large corporations and financial institutions in the corporate community, provide the organizational basis for an institutionalized leadership group for the corporate rich. This leadership group is called the power elite."

The key theoretical move is integration: "The concept of a power elite makes it possible to integrate class and organizational insights in order to create a more complete theory of power in America." The policy-planning network adds to the structural (economy-based) power described in earlier chapters and to the social cohesion documented in Chapter 3, providing a third pillar — "expert power" — while also performing the day-to-day work of bridging the corporate community and government.

The boundaries of the power elite are defined precisely, using a Venn diagram logic (Figure 4.2): "Joining with the social upper class and the corporate community, the policy-planning network provides the final organizational base for creating the social cohesion that makes the creation of a power elite possible. A person can be a member of one of the three networks, or two of the three, or all three." Domhoff explicitly clarifies a common source of confusion: "not all members of the dominant class are involved in governing and not all members of the power elite are part of the dominant class."

Three categories of people are excluded from the power elite even though they are adjacent to it:

1. "Upper-class people who are only socialites and sit on no boards of directors in the corporate community or the policy-planning network" — they contribute financially and through campaign donations but "play no part in the exercise of power."
2. "Corporate executives, just below the CEO and board level, who are neither upper class nor involved in policy planning" — they focus on internal corporate roles.
3. "Policy experts who are neither members of the upper class nor on boards of directors of corporations; they are employees of the power elite, who spend all their time doing research and writing reports."

Domhoff closes Chapter 4 by situating expert power alongside the structural and status power described earlier: "Structural power, status power, and expertise are formidable independent of any participation in politics and government, but they are not enough to make owners and top executives a dominant class because they do not ensure domination of government." The power elite therefore also shapes public opinion, finances elections, and works to place its members and proxies inside the state — activities developed in subsequent chapters.

## Contexts Within the Source

The power elite is the conceptual hinge of the book. It synthesises the corporate community (Chapter 2), the social upper class (Chapter 3), and the policy-planning network (Chapter 4) into a unified leadership category that can then be traced into government, elections, and opinion-shaping (Chapters 5–7). It updates C. Wright Mills's original concept by grounding it in membership-network analysis and empirical studies of foundations, think tanks, and policy-discussion groups rather than relying on Mills's more impressionistic account. See the concept page [[The power elite]]. ## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff acknowledges that the power elite does not always act in concert. Chapter 4 notes "constant bickering" between moderately conservative and ultraconservative factions within the corporate community, and that the network's experts "are often wrong." The power elite failed to prevent the passage of a stronger National Labor Relations Act than it wanted in 1935 and was unable to halt the growth of unions for three decades thereafter. Domhoff also grants that the inclusionary (liberal-labour) alliance retains the potential to elect a liberal Congress and president capable of passing redistributive legislation, which is precisely why "the corporate rich and the power elite" work so hard to shape elections and public opinion. These are treated as structural constraints the power elite must manage, not as evidence that the concept is inapplicable.
