## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that the corporate rich and [[The power elite]] translate their structural, status, and expert power into concrete government outcomes through two distinct but mutually reinforcing processes. The special-interest process works through lobbying, PAC donations, foundation gifts, and tax loopholes to advance the narrow, short-run interests of individual wealthy families, corporations, and business sectors. The policy-planning process works through think-tank recommendations, presidential advisory committees, federal agency advisory boards, and the direct appointment of corporate leaders to Cabinet and executive positions to steer major government policy. Together, these two avenues ensure that the corporate community wins on the issues that matter most to it — and loses far more rarely than any rival coalition.

## Source Evidence and Direct Quotations

**The special-interest process** is defined as "the many and varied means by which wealthy families, individual corporations, and business sectors gain the tax breaks, subsidies, regulatory rulings, and other governmental assistance they need to realize their narrow and short-run interests." It operates through "frequent personal contact with elected officials and their staff," and its key ingredients are "the information and financial support the lobbyists have to offer." Former members of Congress, agency employees, corporate government-liaison executives, lawyers, and public relations specialists all participate. In 2012 alone, 521 former Congressional and agency employees worked as lobbyists for the TV/movie/music industry, 423 for the automotive industry, and 279 for finance and credit companies.

Beyond direct lobbying, corporations reinforce access through PAC donations and a more obscure channel: foundation gifts to legislators' local charities. One systematic study found that "donations from corporate foundations to charitable organizations 'located in a congressional district increase when its representative obtains seats on committees that are of policy relevance to the firm associated with the foundation.'" These donations are "2.8 times higher than the PAC donations" and disappear if the legislator leaves the relevant committee.

Tax manipulation is a major tool. In 2020, "55 corporations, including Fed Ex and Nike, did not pay federal taxes, and a few of them received tax rebates." Corporate lobbyists also drill loopholes into legislation ostensibly accepted in principle: a corporate lobbyist explained that even though he gave money to officials who voted for the 1990 Clean Air Act, "How a person votes on the final piece of legislation is not representative of what they have done" — a process that produced "40 pages of exceptions, extensions, and other loopholes" in the final act.

**The policy-making process** is described as "even more important" than the special-interest process "in order to maintain overall general domination." Recommendations from the policy-planning network reach government through multiple channels: Congressional testimony, presidential advisory committees, and federal departmental advisory boards. The influence is quantifiable. A study of 295 policy statements that the Council on Foreign Relations presented to Congressional committees between 1982 and 2002 found that "the preferences of the CFR were consistent with the legislative outcomes on 75% of the proposals." When the CFR and well-off conservatives shared the same preferences, "their shared preferences prevailed on 89.5% of the issues." By contrast, public opinion had no influence on legislative outcomes.

Historical data on presidential and departmental advisory committees reinforce the pattern. Of 15 presidential commissions on foreign and military policy between 1945 and 1972, 12 were headed by members of the Council on Foreign Relations. During the Obama Administration, 15 of 23 White House advisory committees included one or more corporate directors. Corporate representation was concentrated in committees related to the economy (e.g., 20 of 26 members of the President's Council on Jobs and Competitiveness were corporate directors), while committees on education, bioethical issues, or the arts had none.

**Appointees to government** are the final and most direct avenue. Domhoff cites multiple studies arriving at the same conclusion: "the majority in most Republican and Democratic administrations have been corporate directors and their corporate lawyers, or members of boards of trustees in the policy-planning network, and hence members of the power elite." From 1934 to 1980, "64% of the appointees to the major cabinet, diplomatic, and court posts were members of the corporate community." A study of 205 cabinet members from 1897 to 1972 found "60% were members of the upper class and 78% were members of the corporate community," with "no differences in the overall percentages for Democrats and Republicans." Examples from the Clinton, George W. Bush, Trump, and Biden administrations are traced in detail, showing the persistent revolving door between Wall Street (Goldman Sachs, ExxonMobil, Alcoa, Raytheon), the policy-planning network (Council on Foreign Relations, Carnegie Corporation), and Cabinet posts such as Secretary of State and Secretary of the Treasury.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is Ch. 7's central empirical demonstration. Domhoff frames government domination as "the final and most visible aspect of corporate domination, which has its roots in the economic structure, control of the investment function, and the proposals provided by the policy-planning network." Without the fear of investment slowdowns, corporate political donations, and the organizational work of the policy-planning network, "the power elite's involvement in lobbying, policy formation, and federal advisory groups would matter much less than they do." The chapter concludes that "the general success of the power elite in shaping government policy does not discount the reality of continuing conflict over government policies or the possibility of major policy changes in the face of future large-scale economic, political, or military failures."

This claim is grounded in the [[Class-domination theory of power]] and builds on [[Domhoff: a policy-planning network of foundations and think tanks shapes the policies that become law]] and [[Domhoff: US elections are a candidate-selection process driven by campaign finance]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff acknowledges real limits to corporate dominance. The most consequential corporate loss in modern US history — the National Labor Relations Act of 1935 — occurred under exceptional political conditions (massive labor militancy, a landslide Democratic majority). The 2008 financial collapse temporarily opened space for liberal reform, and modest reforms did pass. Domhoff explicitly notes that the liberal-labor alliance did achieve minimum wage increases, Social Security improvements, and environmental regulations in the 1970s. He also notes that Republican and Democratic administrations differ in their lower-level appointees, with Democrats more willing to place liberals in regulatory agencies. However, he treats these as exceptions that prove the structural rule.
