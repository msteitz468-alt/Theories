## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that the United States exhibits a historically distinctive form of class domination rooted in two related conditions: the early and unopposed ascendancy of the economic network — merchants, bankers, and later corporate owners — before any strong central government, professional military, or established church could develop to check it; and the deep racial, ethnic, and religious divisions among wage-earners that have persistently prevented working-class political solidarity. Together, these conditions produced a power structure uniquely favorable to the corporate rich, and more so than in any European democracy. Domhoff also stresses that this power structure is not static — it is constantly transformed by technological change, social movements, and inter-network conflicts — so that specific forms of domination shift even as the structural advantage of the economic network persists.

## Source Evidence and Direct Quotations

**Why domination exists at all:** "Class domination developed in the United States on the basis of centuries of conflict among contending classes, ethnic groups, and racial groups, which resulted in legislation and legal precedents." Domination is defined as a situation in which "the main objectives of a group or class are carried out with relatively little resistance," made possible because the corporate rich "have been able to establish the organizational bases they need to maintain power," including corporations and tax-free nonprofits. Domhoff summarizes the cumulative result: "The net result is that the power elite score very high on all three power indicators: who occupies seats of government power, who benefits the most as far as wealth and income, and who wins when there are alternative policy proposals."

**The comparative explanation — why the US is exceptional:** "Very generally, there are two separate but related historical reasons for class domination in the United States. First, the corporate community in America is stronger because it did not have to contend with feudal aristocrats, strong states, and the hierarchy of an established church, all of which had a pervasive influence on Western European countries over many centuries of conflict." Second, "those who work for wages and salaries are weaker as an economic class than in other democratic countries because they discriminate against each other on the basis of race, ethnicity, and religion and therefore cannot become a social class of freely intermarried families."

The colonial roots are central: "The prerevolutionary history of the United States as a set of separate colonial territories, only lightly overseen by the appointed governors representing the British crown, left ample room for the development of social classes based on wealthy merchants and slaveholders." The founders' constitutional compromises "created a government with divided and limited powers, which was designed to accommodate the concerns of both Southern slave owners and Northern merchants and other business owners." The result was a federal government that "did not have to make large expenditures for war preparations, as most European countries did," leaving it small and pliable.

In contrast, "the nation-states that survived the severe competition among many small states in Europe were the ones with strong central governments and large military organizations. These countries came into the modern era with strong states, which had longstanding ties with the old aristocracy. The rising class of business owners had to compete for power and social status and never became fully dominant." Critically, "there were big corporations in the United States well before there was any semblance of a 'big government' at the national level. These corporations and their associated policy-planning organizations therefore were able to play a major role in creating new administrative agencies and regulatory bodies."

**The application of the four-network theory:** Domhoff applies sociologist Michael Mann's framework of four power networks (economic, political, military, and religious) to explain "why the economic network has always had the ascendancy in the United States over the other three, which is far from the case in other countries." The dominance of the economic network, "in conjunction with the somewhat conflicting needs of Northern business interests and Southern planters, helps account for the impediments to majority rule, the decentralized structure of the government, the weakness of the federal bureaucracy, and the strongholds of committee power within Congress. The American federal government lacks the capacity to provide full and timely services for everyone because the power elite want to limit the independence of elected officials and government employees."

**The defeat of the working class and the role of race:** Employers in the North "used violent methods to defeat attempts at class-wide union organization. In doing so, they had the support of the local and state governments." Domhoff reports that "more violence was directed against the American labor movement than any other labor movement in a Western democracy." The key comparative point: "business owners in Britain and France were forced by government to compromise with unions because landed aristocrats and bureaucracies still had great power in both countries." In Canada, too, "employers could not defeat" unions, which "persist in many industrialized countries." The American exception is that employers kept all gains and "continue to eliminate unions in the process."

Racial divisions among workers compounded the defeat: the craft unions "would not relinquish the caste-like exclusions they had imposed on the increasing numbers of African-American workers," and the two-party system made it impossible to form an independent labor party.

**The constantly changing nature of the power structure:** Domhoff insists the power structure is not fixed. "There are constant changes in each of the four networks. The four networks interact and come into conflict in unexpected ways, due to newly created organizational forms, newly invented technologies, new methods of communication, military innovations, and new spiritual movements. Thus, there is an emergent and evolving quality to social organization." No social scientist anticipated the Great Depression, the New Deal, the civil rights movement, or the rise of the religious right. "The only thing anyone knows for sure is that unexpected conflicts and crises often occur because of the dynamic and constantly changing nature of the power structure."

He applies this principle to recent history: class domination "actually increased from 1970 to 2020 in spite of increases in individual freedom," because even as individual rights expanded, "corporate power nonetheless became greater, and the wealth and income distributions became more concentrated." The liberal-labor alliance splintered over racial integration, unions were decimated, and the Supreme Court aided employers. "It is therefore possible to have class domination in a society based on individualistic liberal values."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is Ch. 8's theoretical synthesis of the entire book. It places the [[Class-domination theory of power]] in comparative and historical context, explaining not just that class domination exists but why the United States is a distinctive case. The chapter draws together findings from every previous chapter — the corporate community ([[Domhoff: a nationwide corporate community is knit together by interlocking directorates]]), the policy-planning network ([[Domhoff: a policy-planning network of foundations and think tanks shapes the policies that become law]]), elections ([[Domhoff: US elections are a candidate-selection process driven by campaign finance]]), and government domination ([[Domhoff: the corporate rich dominate government through the special-interest and policy-planning processes]]) — and situates them in the early, uncontested rise of the economic network. [[The power elite]] is framed as the organizational expression of this long-term structural advantage.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff's strongest internal qualification is his insistence on the unpredictability and dynamism of power structures. He writes explicitly: "no social scientist anticipated there would be a Great Depression, or a New Deal in reaction to it," nor the civil rights movement, nor the religious right, nor the events of 2000–2020. Social scientists "can at best outline the structure of power and analyze trends." This is not a contradiction of the class-domination thesis, but it is a significant epistemic caveat: the structural advantage of the corporate rich does not predetermine every outcome. Major disruptions — financial collapse, social movements, wars — can and have altered the balance, at least temporarily.
