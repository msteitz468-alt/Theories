## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that distributive power, though a relationship, can be measured as an underlying "trait" through three empirical indicators: **Who governs?** (over-/under-representation of a class in institutional positions), **Who benefits?** (the distribution of valued things, above all wealth and income), and **Who wins?** (who prevails on contested policy issues, with union density as the key measurable proxy). No single indicator is decisive, but where two or three converge the inference of concentrated power is strong — and in the US, he says, all three converge to show power is highly concentrated.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Three types of power indicators are used in this book… They are (1) who governs? (2) who benefits? and (3) who wins?… it strengthens the results if all three lead to the same conclusion." On *who governs*: "If a group or class is highly overrepresented or underrepresented in relation to its proportion of the population, it can be inferred that the group or class is relatively powerful or powerless." On *who benefits*: "Those who have the greatest share of what people want are, by inference, the powerful" — and the data are stark: "the top 1% owned 38% of all privately held wealth" (2018) and "over 40% of all financial wealth" (2013), while "CEOs at the largest corporations made… 320 times as much" as the typical worker in 2019.

On *who wins*: "Power can be inferred by determining who successfully initiates, modifies, or vetoes the policy alternatives." The "unequivocal" measure here is "union density" (union members ÷ non-farm employees), which "is inversely related to changes in the income and wealth distributions" — a "negative correlation of .89." Union density peaked at "34.2%, in 1945" and fell to "6.2% in 2019" in the private sector. Domhoff cautions that the corporate community "may have the power to keep key issues in the realm of non-issues."

## Contexts Within the Source

The empirical engine of the whole book — the means by which the later chapters substantiate [[The power elite]]'s dominance. It follows directly from, and is presented as the rigorous alternative to, [[Domhoff: power-structure analysis is not a conspiracy theory]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff is candid about each indicator's weaknesses: *who governs* fails if "some government officials may not really possess the power they are thought to have," *who wins* is "the most difficult to use… accurate[ly]" because memories cloud and key matters never reach the agenda, and wealth figures "may be underestimates" given offshore tax havens. He stresses the indicators are probabilistic, which is why he triangulates with all three.
