## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that the Supreme Court has served throughout American history as the ultimate institutional guardian of corporate property interests, and that its composition and rulings are a direct product of corporate community influence over the presidency. Justices are drawn overwhelmingly from corporate law backgrounds; their appointment is a primary motivation for the corporate community's investment in electoral politics. The 6-3 ultraconservative majority consolidated by 2021 has used this power to roll back union rights, voting rights, and individual protections for employees — while consistently expanding corporate prerogatives and immunities.

## Source Evidence and Direct Quotations

**Historical pattern of corporate background:** The appointees to the Supreme Court "have been primarily from the upper and upper-middle classes, attended one of a half-dozen to a dozen high-status law schools, and an 'inordinate number were corporate attorneys before their appointments.'" Virtually all "shared the ideological and political views of the presidents who appointed them." Among the nine justices during the first Obama Administration, six "had been members of corporate law firms early in their careers and then became law professors or government lawyers." Crucially, "None of them had ever done legal work for unions, environmental groups, or consumer groups."

**The court as backstop for business:** The Supreme Court "served as the backstop for business power in the nineteenth century and then for the emerging corporate community from the outset of the twentieth century." Its importance to corporate leaders is such that "these biographical findings underscore how important it is to the corporate community to elect presidents who will make pro-business appointments to the federal courts, and especially the Supreme Court."

**The Chamber of Commerce's Litigation Center and the corporate litigation strategy:** After the 1960s, the "corporate community's success rate was also improved by the work of the Chamber of Commerce's Litigation Center" — an organization that Chief Justice Roberts himself worked for in 2001–2002, preparing briefs on business-related cases. The results were direct. In 2010, the National Chamber Litigation Center's victories "read like a top ten list of corporate wishes," including thwarting class-action gender discrimination claims at Wal-Mart; "forcing individuals to have their claims against big corporations settled in arbitration rather than gaining access to the courts"; "defeating claims for damages caused by the defective design of vaccines"; "limiting recovery by whistleblowers"; and "blocking suits by private citizens against brokerage firms for making false statements about securities."

**The 6-3 ultraconservative majority and voting, union, and reproductive rights:** The court's 2013 ruling in a 5-4 decision declared "two key provisions in the 1965 Voting Rights Act were unconstitutional, which freed the white nationalist Southern states to develop new methods of voter suppression." In 2019, the 5-4 Republican majority ruled that "partisan gerrymandering claims present political questions beyond the reach of the federal courts." By 2021, "white nationalist Republicans were free to move ahead with their plans as far as the Supreme Court is concerned," as the conservative 6-3 majority gave the green light for restrictive voting rights by upholding Arizona's restrictions.

On union rights, in June 2018 a 5-4 Republican majority ruled "that unions for government employees have no right to collect dues from those who they represent but do not belong to the union" — extending right-to-work laws nationwide for public employees. In June 2021, the conservative majority overturned a California law that had allowed union organizers to speak with workers on company property before and after work, ruling "these talks were equivalent to taking over the owners' private property" and thereby reducing "union organizers to illegal trespassers on any corporate property in the United States."

**The 2000 presidential election:** The independent power of the court was "fully demonstrated in the 2000 presidential election" when the five conservative Republican appointees, "who usually decried 'judicial activism' and emphasized 'states rights,'" nonetheless "found a way to override the Florida Supreme Court and stop the counting of uncounted votes." Domhoff frames this as "a perfect example of why members of the corporate community work so hard to win elections" — the outcome "preserved white conservative dominance for the next 15 years."

**The current majority:** The 2021 court "included three women, but now it has seven Catholics, two Jews, and six ultraconservatives." The ultraconservative majority "can permit further limits on abortion rights and affirmative action programs in colleges and universities, simply by concluding the court does not have the power to reject legislation created by elected officials at the state level. At the same time, there is an even larger majority that is likely to side with corporations when individuals and unions seek redress for restrictions placed upon them by their employers."

## Contexts Within the Source

The Supreme Court section closes Ch. 7's comprehensive demonstration that corporate power runs through all three branches of the federal government — the executive (via appointments), the legislative (via lobbying and the policy-planning process), and the judiciary. Domhoff frames the court within the [[Class-domination theory of power]]: "As in the past, the composition of the Supreme Court, along with most of its decisions, still provides good evidence for the power of the corporate community and the [[The power elite]] within the federal government." The court's protection of individual freedoms (free press, civil rights era rulings) is acknowledged but treated as a partial and now-eroding exception. See also [[Domhoff: the corporate rich dominate government through the special-interest and policy-planning processes]] for the broader context of executive-branch domination.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff does not present a principled internal counter-argument to this claim. He acknowledges that the Warren Court (1953–1968) "played a major role in creating the potential for a civil rights revolution" through school desegregation, voting reapportionment, and civil liberties rulings — and that the court "upholds many of the individual freedoms that Americans have come to expect and enjoy." He also notes that some justices "voted more liberally than Republican presidents expected." However, he observes that even the Warren Court "continued to make rulings which favored corporations over unions," so the pro-corporate tilt was never fully suspended. He treats individual-rights rulings as compatible with, not contradictory to, corporate dominance.
