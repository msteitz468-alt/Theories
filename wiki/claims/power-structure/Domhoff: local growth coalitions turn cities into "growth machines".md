## Claim Statement

Domhoff argues that the most consequential local businesses in the United States are organized into place-based "growth coalitions" whose primary goal is to intensify land use and maximize rents from land and buildings in their city or region. These coalitions — composed of landowners, developers, building contractors, local bankers, utility executives, and department store owners — dominate city governments and pursue growth at the expense of neighborhood residents, environmental quality, and workers. Drawing on sociologists Harvey Molotch and John Logan, Domhoff describes cities as "growth machines" driven by the logic of real-estate extraction rather than the broader profit-from-goods logic of the national corporate community.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Domhoff identifies the core composition and goal of growth coalitions: "Members of these coalitions share a common interest in intensifying land use in their geographical locale, starting with the landowners themselves, and the developers and building contractors who work closely with them. Executives from local banks, gas and electric companies, and department stores are part of the growth coalitions as well because they have a strong stake in the growth of the local community."

He draws a conceptual distinction between this local "place entrepreneur" logic and corporate profit-making: "the place entrepreneurs at the heart of local growth coalitions are trying to maximize rents from land and buildings, which is a little different than the goal of the corporate community, namely, maximizing profits from the sale of goods and services." The concept of rents includes "purchases of land and buildings as well as payments that tenants or homebuyers make to landlords, realtors, mortgage lenders, and title companies."

Domhoff identifies three major sources of conflict generated by growth coalitions. First, the relationship with corporations: corporations can threaten to relocate if environmental regulations tighten or if cheaper locations emerge, creating "a 'race to the bottom' as cities offer tax breaks, less environmental regulation, and other benefits to corporations in order to tempt them to relocate. In the process, local public services and environmental conditions are degraded."

Second, conflicts with neighborhoods: "Neighborhoods are something to be used and enjoyed in the eyes of those who live in them, but neighborhoods are often seen as sites for further development by growth coalitions, which justify new developments with the doctrine of 'the highest and best use for land.'" This "expansionist, land-clearing strategy contributed greatly to inner-city tensions from the 1960s onward because African Americans, who were the most frequently displaced group, could not readily find housing in white neighborhoods, forcing them into crowded tenements with high rents and few amenities."

Third, Domhoff notes that "the success rate of neighborhoods in conflict with the growth coalitions is very low. Since the primary focus of residents is on their everyday lives, they usually do not persist in their protests and seldom join larger coalitions with other neighborhoods in the city." The few successful exceptions were mostly "university towns and other relatively small cities, such as Burlington in Vermont, Madison in Wisconsin, and Berkeley, Palo Alto, Santa Barbara, Santa Cruz, and Santa Monica in California."

Crucially, Domhoff argues that despite their local power, growth coalitions "do not provide the organizational base for any significant opposition to the corporate community at the national level." They are subordinate actors — place-based, limited in scope, and ultimately dependent on attracting national corporate investment to fuel their land-value appreciation.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim appears in Chapter 2 as part of Domhoff's systematic survey of economic actors — small farmers, small businesses, local growth coalitions — that might seem to form countervailing power to the national [[The corporate community]], but in Domhoff's analysis do not. Growth coalitions are of particular interest because they are locally powerful and generate visible urban conflicts, yet remain structurally subordinate to and dependent upon national corporate investment. This argument sets up the later chapters by establishing that no significant organized opposition to the corporate community exists outside the labor movement and the liberal-left political alliance. See also [[Domhoff: the corporate owners and managers form a socially cohesive upper class]] and [[Domhoff: the power elite is the leadership arm of the corporate rich]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Domhoff acknowledges that growth coalitions were historically cooperative partners of the national corporate community — "in the past, the growth coalitions had cooperative relationships with corporations because attracting corporate facilities was one of the best ways local growth coalitions could intensify land use in their cities." The relationship is thus not simply one of subordination but of mutual if unequal dependence. He also concedes that neighborhood resistance occasionally succeeded in mid-sized progressive cities, demonstrating that growth coalition power is not absolute at the local level even if it is dominant.
