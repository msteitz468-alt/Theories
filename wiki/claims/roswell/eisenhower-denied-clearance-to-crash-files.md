## Claim Statement

Berlitz and Moore claim that when President Dwight Eisenhower — a former five-star Army general — began inquiring during his first term into the rumored "Roswell saucer capture," he made the "startling (one might even say frightening) discovery that even though he was President, as well as a former general of the Army, he did not possess the necessary clearances" to access the crash-recovery information, which a secrecy-minded faction of the intelligence community withheld even from the White House.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Chapter 7 states that "during his first term as President, Eisenhower began to make inquiries into the reality of the 'Roswell saucer capture.'" The authors then report, attributed to "a former high-level CIA operative who shall remain nameless," that "one of his first problems . . . was his startling (one might even say frightening) discovery that even though he was President, as well as a former general of the Army, he did not possess the necessary clearances to be permitted access to such information." They explain that "the intelligence agencies of the time were then enjoying a period of action untrammeled by . . . supervision," so that "certain classified and sensitive information might be secured for a time from even the President." An "understandably anonymous source" is quoted: "Some of the higher-ups in the intelligence community didn't trust Ike and were hesitant to deal with him. These people frequently went off on their own tangents . . . and either conveniently forgot to seek directives from the White House, or ignored them when they did come."

The chapter adds that Eisenhower "encountered a split within the military establishment on this matter" over declassification, secrecy being justified because any nation that could reverse-engineer the discs' propulsion "would therefore be in a position to control the planet earth." It further relays reports (from researchers such as Desmond Leslie and Frank Scully's widow, and a letter by Gerald Light) that Eisenhower secretly examined recovered wreckage at Muroc/Edwards on or around February 20, 1954, and afterward "ordered silence and further study," scrapping a planned public disclosure.

## Contexts Within the Source

The authors present the episode as evidence that the recovery was real and so tightly compartmentalized that even the commander-in-chief could be locked out — illustrating their broader thesis that the case is buried under extraordinary, self-perpetuating secrecy.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The chapter rests entirely on anonymous "CIA-linked" sources and second- and third-hand researcher accounts (Leslie's unnamed "Air Force man," an unrecalled carpenter, the disputed Gerald Light letter), which the authors themselves frame as rumor and "twice-told tales" that nonetheless "corroborate" one another.
