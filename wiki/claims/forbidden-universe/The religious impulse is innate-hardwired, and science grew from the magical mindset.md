## Claim Statement

Picknett and Prince contend that the human religious and magical impulse is not a superstition to be overcome but a genuine, biologically "hardwired" feature of *Homo sapiens* — and that it is precisely this impulse, not a turn away from it, which gave birth to modern science. The authors argue that the great pioneers of the Scientific Revolution were motivated not by materialist rationalism but by an "unashamedly metaphysical and magic-oriented philosophy," and that "magic made the modern world."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The claim runs from the Introduction through Chapter 1. In the Introduction, the authors quote developmental psychologist Bruce Hood (2009 British Science Association meeting): "superstition is hardwired, being there from the beginning." They also quote anthropologist Pascal Boyer: "Religious thinking seems to be the path of least resistance for our cognitive systems. By contrast, disbelief is generally the work of deliberate, effortful work against our natural cognitive dispositions."

The authors then deploy this as a launching point for their central thesis: "it is an exquisite irony that one of the products of this magical mindset was science itself. It is, as we will see, what motivated all of the great pioneers of the scientific revolution."

Chapter 1 opens with the programmatic statement:

> "To today's materialist-rationalists, the unpalatable fact is that a magical mindset not only bubbled along through the Renaissance, but it was magic that inspired and drove the whole of that era's explosion of thought and achievement. In a very real way, magic made the modern world."

The authors target Dawkins and Hitchens directly, arguing that their "scientism" — science elevated to an ideology — fails to account for the universal human propensity for magical thinking. They also argue that Dawkins' own evolutionary explanation (religion as by-product of a useful instinct to obey elders) addresses only *why religions develop* as institutions, not *why the underlying mystical impulse exists at all*.

The Hermetic philosophy — via [[Frances Yates]]'s historiography — is the authors' primary exhibit: per [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]], Hermeticism "effectively created the Renaissance" and the contributions to science of Copernicus, Bruno, and Newton "would simply have been impossible without Hermeticism."

## Contexts Within the Source

- The claim frames the entire book's argument, structuring the opposition to Dawkins/Hitchens "scientism."
- It underpins the specific historical claims in every chapter: Copernicus's Hermetic sun-worship, Bruno's magical cosmology, Galileo's Hermetic connections, Newton's alchemy.
- The thesis is that the *loss* of the Hermetic root of science — not its presence — is what went wrong, and that recovering it has implications for twenty-first century science.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors do not dispute that the scientific method, once established, outgrew its Hermetic incubator. They acknowledge modern science's success on its own terms. Their argument is genealogical — about *origins* — not a claim that practising scientists must be magicians. They also acknowledge that Dawkins' critique of organized religion as a system of authority and control has considerable force.
