## Claim Statement

Picknett and Prince argue that Copernicus's proposal of heliocentric astronomy was not a product of detached mathematical reasoning but was emotionally and intellectually driven by Hermetic sun-worship — specifically the Hermetic tradition's identification of the sun as the "visible god" and "second god," a relay station for divine creative power. The smoking-gun evidence is Copernicus's own citation of Hermes Trismegistus on the thirty-first page of *On the Revolutions*, immediately beneath the key diagram, describing the sun as "Trismegistus' visible God."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The authors quote the passage directly (their Latin translation): "Siquidem non inepte quidam lucernam mundi, alii mentem, alii rectorem vocant. Trismegistus visibilem Deum …" — rendered as: "[It is] Trismegistus' visible God."

They note that Hermeticism "lays great emphasis on the sun, which is regarded as a kind of relay station for God's creative and sustaining power and described in turn as the 'visible god' and a 'second god'" (citing *Asclepius*). Hermetic Treatise XVI states: "the sun is situated at the centre of the cosmos, wearing it like a crown," with eight spheres "depending from" it — language the authors argue describes a heliocentric model taken for granted by the Hermetic writers, centuries before Copernicus.

The book further establishes that Copernicus's three revolutionary ideas — Earth's motion in space, its axial rotation, and planetary orbits around the sun — "all appeared in the Hermetica," and that *Asclepius* contains a passage about the "rotation of the world" (cited as a prefiguring of Earth's rotation). Copernicus had studied in Rome and Padua in the 1480s–90s, "where [the Hermetica] was on everyone's lips."

[[Frances Yates]] is quoted at length: "One can say, either that the intense emphasis on the sun in this new worldview was the emotional driving force which induced Copernicus to undertake his mathematical calculations on the hypothesis that the sun is indeed at the centre of the planetary system; or that he wished to make his discovery acceptable by presenting it within the framework of this new attitude. Perhaps both explanations would be true, or some of each."

Tobias Churton is also quoted: "One gets the impression that Copernicus is saying: the truth of the matter was already there, but went unseen because we judged things from an earthly perspective. But Hermes, at the beginning of science, he saw it."

The authors note the downstream consequence: the heliocentric debate became "of keen interest to Hermeticists" because proving it would vindicate their "semi-sacred texts," and "the Hermeticists were among [Copernicus's] most ardent supporters."

## Contexts Within the Source

- This is the chapter's core argument, establishing the Hermetic root of the Scientific Revolution's first landmark event.
- It sets up the Bruno chapter: [[Giordano Bruno]] saw Copernicus as the mathematical vindicator of what Hermeticists already knew, and declared that *proving* heliocentrism would trigger the new Hermetic age.
- The claim also explains why the Church eventually condemned heliocentrism in 1616 (seventy-seven years after publication) — only after Bruno had given it explosive Hermetic-revolutionary significance.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge that the church's initial reaction to *On the Revolutions* was not hostile — Pope Paul III himself listened enthusiastically to a lecture on it. The theological problem with heliocentrism was minor (a handful of Old Testament verses implying Earth's immobility), and Protestant objections (e.g. Luther's) were mainly about astronomy being "wrong" for so long. The book argues it was Bruno's Hermetic revolutionary programme that retrospectively made heliocentrism dangerous, prompting the 1616 ban.
