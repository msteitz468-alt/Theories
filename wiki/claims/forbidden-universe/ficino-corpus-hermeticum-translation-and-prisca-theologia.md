## Claim Statement

Picknett and Prince argue that Marsilio Ficino's 1463 Latin translation of the Corpus Hermeticum — commissioned by Cosimo de' Medici on his deathbed over the nearly complete translation of Plato — was the foundational event of the Renaissance Hermetic revival, and that it crystallised the concept of the *prisca theologia*: the belief that Hermes Trismegistus was an ancient Egyptian sage who possessed a primordial, pre-Christian wisdom that underlay all subsequent religions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The authors recount that Cosimo's agent, a monk named Leonardo de Pistoia, returned from Macedonia with a Greek manuscript of the first fourteen treatises of the Corpus Hermeticum. Cosimo ordered Ficino in 1463 to "drop his translation of Plato forthwith" and focus exclusively on the Hermetica — an urgency attributed to Cosimo becoming gravely ill. Ficino's translation was "by far his most popular work," evidenced by many manuscript copies and several printed editions from 1471, and it "sent seismic shockwaves throughout the academic community in Florence and beyond."

On the *prisca theologia*, the source quotes Ficino's key declaration (via Tim Parks): "The whole world, it seemed, had always followed a single faith whose ancient priests included Zoroaster, Hermes Trismegistus, Orpheus, Pythagoras, Plato, St Paul, St Augustine." Ficino concluded that a "magical current" flowed beneath and linked otherwise irreconcilable belief systems, from which he developed the idea of "natural magic."

On Hermes as ancient sage, the authors explain that the *prisca theologia* rested on the Renaissance belief — later demolished by Isaac Casaubon in 1614 — that the Hermetica dated from the zenith of Egyptian civilisation and thus pre-dated both Moses and Plato. Since even the Bible acknowledged Egypt's civilisation pre-dated Israel's, and since there was "even a suggestion that Moses himself learned great secrets from the Egyptians," Hermes was seen as a pagan prophet who had received the same revelations later crystallised in Judaism and Christianity.

The practical consequence: many in the highest echelons of the Catholic Church considered Hermeticism compatible with Christianity. Pope Alexander VI (the Borgia pope) wrote Pico della Mirandola a fan letter absolving him from charges of heresy and commissioned Hermetic frescoes — including depictions of Hermes Trismegistus and Isis — for his personal Vatican apartments, the Appartamento Borgia, which survive today.

E. L. Tuveson is quoted: "with the translation by Ficino of the Hermetica in the fifteenth century, a kind of 'new force' had entered the Western world."

## Contexts Within the Source

- This claim is the hinge between the preamble on the Hermetica's content and the specific argument about Copernicus.
- The *prisca theologia* framework is what allowed Copernicus to invoke Hermes in a scientific treatise without scandal — and what made Bruno's more radical version so threatening.
- Ficino is positioned as the gateway through which Hermeticism entered the mainstream of Renaissance intellectual life and eventually the sciences.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge that many Catholic thinkers tried to adopt the Hermetic philosophy while rejecting its magical practice — a compromise they note was rejected by Pico della Mirandola (and later Bruno), who argued the two were inseparable. Some early Church figures, such as St Augustine, explained Hermes's apparent foresight of Christianity as the work of "worried demons," not genuine inspiration.
