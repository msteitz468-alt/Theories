## Claim Statement

Picknett & Prince argue that Isaac Casaubon's 1614 philological proof that the *Corpus Hermeticum* dated from the early Christian era — not from ancient Egypt — was one of the most culturally consequential scholarly interventions in European history, even though Casaubon himself was largely aiming at a different target. The redating stripped Hermeticism of its claim to be the original prisca theologia, empowered Catholic opponents (especially Marin Mersenne), accelerated the separation of science from its occult roots, and consigned Hermeticism to the esoteric underground for centuries. The authors further argue, following modern scholars, that Casaubon was substantively wrong about the Egyptian origins of Hermetic ideas.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The "watershed" judgment.** The authors quote historian Garth Fowden that Casaubon's work is "the watershed between Renaissance occultism and the scientific rationalism of the new age," and [[Frances Yates]]'s characterisation of the Renaissance belief as "the great Egyptian illusion." The book is described as a "bombshell" that "to some seemed terminal" for Hermeticism.

**Casaubon's argument.** Working from a 1554 printed Greek edition, Casaubon observed that no ancient author mentioned Hermes Trismegistus before early Christian writers; he identified Platonic and New Testament influences in the texts; and he demonstrated that the Greek belonged to the late BCE/early CE period. He concluded the texts were pious Christian-era forgeries, though he accepted a real Egyptian figure once called Thrice-Great Hermes existed.

**Slow but devastating uptake.** The text's impact was delayed — Tommaso Campanella, for example, "was either unaware of it, or rejected its message" for another quarter-century. It "took ten years for Casaubon's discovery to be used against Hermeticists" and "a full half a century to become widely known and accepted." The first to weaponise it against living Hermeticists was Marin Mersenne, in Paris in 1623 — the year of the "Invisibles" scare.

**Cumulative suppression.** Combined with the Thirty Years War (political defeat) and Cartesian mechanism (philosophical displacement), Casaubon's critique created a triple hammer blow: "In half a century Isaac Casaubon had challenged it historically, the Thirty Years War had dashed its hopes politically and now Descartes was undermining it philosophically."

**Why Casaubon was wrong.** Modern historians have rejected his central claim of New Testament dependence: "Any potential link is indirect, as both texts derive from the same blend of theological and philosophical speculation." The Greek dating proves only composition in Greek, not the origin of ideas. The authors cite Garth Fowden's *The Egyptian Hermes* (1986) and Karl Luckert's *Egyptian Light and Hebrew Fire* (1991) as establishing that the cosmology of the Hermetica is authentically Heliopolitan — traceable to the Pyramid Texts (c.2500–2200 BCE). A 160 BCE inscription names "Thoth, the three times great," confirming an Egyptian origin for the "Trismegistus" epithet long predating the Christian era. The authors conclude: "Isaac Casaubon — whose work is still trotted out to trash the value of the Hermetica — was just plain wrong."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The source notes that Casaubon was at least right about the linguistic dating of the Greek: "Modern historians agree that Casaubon … reached roughly the right conclusions … as far as the actual composition of the Hermetica is concerned." The error lay in treating late composition as proof of late ideas and of Christian dependence. The Cambridge Platonists had already formulated this objection in the seventeenth century.
