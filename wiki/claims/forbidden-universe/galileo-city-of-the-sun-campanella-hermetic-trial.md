## Claim Statement

Picknett and Prince argue that the conventional account of Galileo's 1633 trial — usually framed as a clash between science and religion, or between two obstinate egos — misses the decisive factor: the Church and the Jesuits feared that Galileo's proof of heliocentrism would trigger the Hermetic revolution that Bruno had predicted and Campanella had attempted to bring about by force. Galileo's deep association with known Hermetic-revolutionary figures gave the Inquisition grounds to treat his work as politically and theologically explosive.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The Hermetic chain into Padua.** The book opens Chapter 3 by noting that Bruno's arrest left the Chair of Mathematics at Padua University vacant, and it went to "none other than Galileo Galilei." Galileo immediately entered the orbit of Gian Vincenzo Pinelli ("Galileo's mentor") and met [[Tommaso Campanella (1568–1639)]] in 1592 — a meeting that began "an important and lifelong association." Traiano Boccalini (author of the Bruno-inspired *News from Parnassus*) and the controversial friar Paolo Sarpi (who led legal challenges to papal authority) were also among Galileo's associates.

**Why the Church was alarmed.** The book argues that the Jesuits compared Galileo's *Dialogue* to "the writings of Luther and Calvin" — an apparently disproportionate response — because they understood its Hermetic subtext. Galileo himself wrote to a friend: "the Jesuit Fathers have impressed the most important persons 'in Rome' with the idea that my book 'the Dialogo' is execrable and more dangerous to the Holy Church than the writings of Luther and Calvin." The authors comment: "There was something else behind the Church's anxiety, something massive but unstated which lies somewhere in the significance of the heliocentric theory to the dangerous Hermeticists."

**Bellarmino as the connecting thread.** The cardinal tasked with warning Galileo off in 1616 was Roberto Bellarmino — "the same man who had interrogated Bruno in his last months, and was responsible for his condemnation and execution." Bellarmino was "recalled to Rome specifically to deal with Galileo" and "even interviewed Galileo in the same room as he had interviewed Bruno." The book states this "was not a coincidence."

**Campanella's *City of the Sun* and the Hermetic utopia.** Campanella's major work — *City of the Sun* (written c. 1600–1602, published Frankfurt 1623) — envisioned a utopian republic run on Hermetic-astrological principles, derived from the *Lament* in *Asclepius*. The book quotes specialist George Lechner: "In it, Campanella developed the notion of a new city-state, led by a philosopher-priest-king, and guided by Hermetic magical principles." From prison, Campanella wrote *Defence of Galileo* (1622) urging him to stand firm because heliocentricity was "a return to ancient truth and as portending a new age."

**Galileo's intellectual debt to Bruno.** After his 1616 warning, Galileo sought permission to visit Campanella in his Neapolitan prison — a request refused by Duke Cosimo. The *Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems* (1632) is argued to be modelled structurally on Bruno's *Ash Wednesday Supper*, including the proto-relativistic "moving ship" thought experiment, which Bruno had formulated over forty years earlier. Kepler had criticised Galileo as early as 1610 for "not honestly acknowledging the intellectual debt he owed to Bruno."

**The trial's real subtext.** "Neither side could admit what Galileo's trial was really about." Galileo never made the Hermetic implications overt; the Church could not publicly highlight the Hermetic significance of heliocentrism without drawing further attention to it. "The Hermetic factor was therefore present, however, but simply relegated to the background — which is why there is a distinct sense of something missing in the conventional story of the trial."

Galileo was found "vehemently suspect of heresy" and forced to recant, kneeling before the altar of Santa Maria sopra Minerva — the same basilica from which Bruno had set out to his execution thirty-three years earlier.

## Contexts Within the Source

- This chapter completes Part One's argument: Copernicus invoked Hermes, Bruno weaponised heliocentrism, Galileo's trial was shaped by fear of that weaponisation.
- Campanella's *City of the Sun* provides the chapter's title and embodies the Hermetic utopian vision that the Inquisition was working to suppress.
- The chapter also notes Campanella later called the infant Louis XIV "the Sun King" in recognition of his Hermetic potential — tracing the tradition's continuity into the seventeenth century.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors are careful to say Galileo "was by no means as innocent as he tried to appear" while stopping short of asserting he was definitively a Giordanisti member. They present the Hermetic connection as a strong inference from circumstantial evidence. They also note that Pope Urban VIII's political vulnerabilities — rather than personal animosity to Galileo — drove the prosecution, and that the "clash of egos" narrative is "hardly the worst interpretation of the evidence."
