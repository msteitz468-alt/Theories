## Claim Statement

Picknett & Prince's central concluding thesis in Part One is that the scientific revolution — conventionally said to run from Copernicus to Newton — *was* the Hermetic revolution, and that the subsequent separation of science from its occult roots was a historically contingent suppression rather than a natural or logical progression. The *Asclepius* Lament — an Egyptian Hermetic text prophesying the extinction of the gods' wisdom — is used as the governing metaphor for what happened to Hermeticism after the seventeenth century.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The Hermetic revolution thesis.** "The scientific revolution, usually considered to have started with Copernicus and ended with Newton, was in fact the Hermetic revolution. Science emerged from the world of the occult in a very real and direct way. All the major players relied not just on the Hermetica's exhilarating image of humanity but also on its model of creation." The authors list specific discoveries owed to the Hermetica: the heliocentric theory; the laws of planetary motion; the concept of an infinite universe; the idea of other solar systems with habitable planets; the theory of gravity; the Newtonian laws of motion; the circulation of the blood; the Earth's magnetism; the basic principles of information theory and computer science.

**The separation was historically contingent.** "It is often assumed that science emerged when thinking people began to question religion. This is not so: it was a specific reaction against Hermeticism — one that was actively encouraged by those members of the Catholic Church who backed Descartes' new method." The mechanistic philosophy won not on intellectual merit alone but because of "the exigencies of the time": after the Thirty Years War, Hermeticism was politically toxic, associated with Rosicrucianism and Protestant reform. "One of the consequences of the power politics of the day was that it became expedient to be seen as an occult-denier, especially when the opposite could get you burnt at the stake."

**The Catholic-Protestant paradox.** "A paradoxical collusion between the forces of science and religion" drove out Hermeticism: the Catholic Church condemned it as demonic; Protestant intellectuals backed away because Catholics had made it a point of contention. "Scientists not only denied the very existence of their predecessors' inspiration, but also had no choice but to denigrate its source."

**Long suppression.** Hermeticism survived the Enlightenment but "became firmly the province of the occult underground and the world of secret initiatory societies." Study of the *Corpus Hermeticum* "as anything other than a historical curiosity came to be reserved for students of the esoteric and magic." Later it re-emerged in Romantic poets, Hegel ("an unashamed Hermeticist" whose Hermetic passion was only acknowledged in 2001 in Glenn Alexander Magee's *Hegel and the Hermetic Tradition*), and the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn (1880s).

**The cosmic joke.** Glenn Alexander Magee is quoted: "It is surely one of the great ironies of history that the Hermetic ideal of man as magus, achieving total knowledge and wielding Godlike power to bring the world to perfection, was the prototype of the modern scientist." The idea that humanity is of limitless potential — the spirit of science — "also came from the teaching of Hermes."

**The Asclepius Lament.** The *Lament* in the *Asclepius* (part of the Hermetic corpus) foresaw the time when the gods would depart from Egypt and the sacred wisdom would be lost. The authors use this as both a metaphor for Hermeticism's actual suppression and as evidence that the Egyptian priests who wrote the Hermetica were themselves already experiencing cultural extinction under Greek and Roman domination — composing the texts as a desperate act of preservation.

**Why Casaubon still matters.** "Had Casaubon never put quill to paper, Egypt might well have remained a focus of scholarly respect … Casaubon was wrong anyway." His error, combined with pro-classical bias in academia, explains why "twentieth-century academics such as Garth Fowden and Karl Luckert … never have faced such an uphill struggle to persuade their colleagues that all of the extraordinarily powerful and inviting subjects … had Egyptian rather than Greek roots."

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 8 is both a summation and a bridge to Part Two, in which the authors argue that Hermeticism is once again relevant to modern physics — particularly fine-tuning cosmology, quantum mechanics, and the anthropic principle. The "Lament" frames the book's emotional and rhetorical conclusion to Part One.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge that the break from Hermetic philosophy "allowed science to develop without the constraints of a metaphysical framework, leading to the explosion of discoveries and world-changing technologies." They allow that "up to the first half of the twentieth century, that argument might have worked" — it is only with quantum mechanics and cosmology that Hermeticism becomes urgently relevant again.
