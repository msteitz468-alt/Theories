## Claim Statement

Picknett & Prince argue — following modern scholarship, especially Garth Fowden's *The Egyptian Hermes* (1986) and Karl Luckert's *Egyptian Light and Hebrew Fire* (1991) — that [[Isaac Casaubon (1559–1614)]] was right that the *Corpus Hermeticum* was composed in Greek during the Hellenic period, but wrong about its sources. The ideas expressed are authentically Egyptian, traceable to the Heliopolitan theology of the Pyramid Texts (c.2500–2200 BCE). Renaissance Hermeticists such as [[Giordano Bruno]] and [[Isaac Newton (1643–1727)]], who believed the Hermetica preserved ancient Egyptian wisdom, were therefore vindicated in substance even if the tradition of direct authorship by Hermes Trismegistus himself was mistaken.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Casaubon's error about content.** Modern historians have "roundly rejected" Casaubon's central claim that the Hermetica derived from the New Testament. "Any potential link is indirect, as both texts derive from the same blend of theological and philosophical speculation" from the Hellenic-Jewish-Egyptian intellectual melting pot of Alexandria. The connection between the *Pimander* and John's Gospel is via Philo of Alexandria's concept of the Logos — not direct dependence in either direction.

**Stylistic evidence for Egyptian origins.** Unlike Greek philosophical dialogues, the Hermetic texts use a "question-and-answer session between master and pupil — more in keeping with the traditional Egyptian wisdom literature." Authors are anonymous, attributing works to a divine figure — "a typically Egyptian practice." The astronomy in the Hermetica uses the Egyptian thirty-six-decan system, not the Greek twelve-sign zodiac.

**"Thrice Great" as Egyptian honorific.** The Egyptians emphasised greatness by repeating its glyph — and "this practice seems to have been specifically reserved for Thoth." A 160 BCE Demotic inscription by the scribe Hor (Horus) appeals to "Thoth, the three times great" — the earliest known use of this form of address, long predating the Christian era. Egyptologist J. D. Ray is quoted on its significance for "a most remarkable figure in the history of thought."

**The Heliopolitan source.** Karl Luckert traced both Neoplatonism (shown by him to be "neo-Egyptian" rather than genuinely Platonic) and Hermeticism to the theology of Heliopolis, preserved in the Pyramid Texts — "the most ancient religious writings known from anywhere in the world." The Heliopolitan Great Ennead of nine gods as aspects of the creator-god Atum maps directly onto Hermetic cosmology: the one God whose creative flow emanates outward into material creation, with human beings containing his divine spark and capable of returning to their divine source. The authors write: "The cosmology of the Hermetica is, ultimately, also that of the first flourishing of the Ancient Egyptian culture."

**Connections to Heliopolis.** Asclepius, a central figure in the Hermetica, is a thinly-disguised version of Imhotep — deified architect of the Step Pyramid of Saqqara (c.2620 BCE) and priest of Heliopolis. The astronomer-scribe Hor records in 160 BCE that he was instructed by "the priest of the chapel of Imhotep" in Heliopolis. The Egyptologist connection between Serapis (whose cult produced the Hermetica's library) and Heliopolis runs through the priest Manetho, who was both a Heliopolitan priest and instrumental in establishing Serapis worship.

**Plotinus as Egyptian, not Greek.** Luckert demonstrated that Plotinus' philosophy — the basis of "Neoplatonism" — was in fact Egyptian: his concept of the high soul and low soul matches the Egyptian ka and ba precisely; "there is nothing that corresponds to this idea in the Greek religion." As Neoplatonism and Hermeticism "are simply two sides of the same coin," both trace to Heliopolitan tradition.

**Vindication of the Hermeticists.** "The belief of Renaissance Hermeticists such as Bruno and Newton that the Hermetic works represented the wisdom of that great civilization is absolutely vindicated."

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 7 ("Egypt's True Legacy") is the book's main positive argument for Egyptian authenticity, structured around sections "The Original Time Lord," "The Sacred City," and "Visible and Invisible Gods." Chapter 8 draws the cultural conclusion: Casaubon's error set back Egypt's scholarly reputation for centuries and is the chief reason Hermeticism has been marginalised in histories of science.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors acknowledge that the precise origin of Heliopolitan theology remains uncertain: "A definitive answer about the origins of the Atum religion remains impossible because of a lack of relevant historical information." They explicitly reject "ancient astronaut" explanations as "a desperately non-Hermetic idea."
