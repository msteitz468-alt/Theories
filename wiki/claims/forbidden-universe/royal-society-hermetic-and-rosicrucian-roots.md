## Claim Statement

Picknett & Prince argue that the Royal Society of London (founded 1660, chartered 1662) grew directly out of the Rosicrucian reform network — via the Hermetic polymath Samuel Hartlib's "Antilia"/"Invisible College" circle — and that a behind-the-scenes power struggle during its founding extinguished the Rosicrucian/reforming vision in favour of Bacon's narrower mechanistic model. The Society's 1667 official history, they suggest, was designed to erase this Hermetic genealogy.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The Cambridge Platonists as Hermetic intermediaries.** A group centred on Christ's College Cambridge — misleadingly named the "Cambridge Platonists" — maintained an unbroken Hermetic line from Marsilio Ficino's Florentine Academy. Their leader Henry More stated that his thinking derived from "the Platonick Writers, Marsilius Ficinus, Plotinus himself, Mercurius Trismegistus and the Mystical Divines." Historians McGuire and Rattansi demonstrated that the group derived their philosophy from the *Corpus Hermeticum* via Ficino and Pico della Mirandola. This group provided the intellectual environment in which [[Isaac Newton (1643–1727)]] would later be radicalized.

**Samuel Hartlib and the Invisible College.** Samuel Hartlib (1600–62) — Hermeticist, Paracelcist, astrologer, promoter of [[John Dee]]'s mathematical works — operated as a Rosicrucian clearing-house and tried to found a "pansophic college" under the code-name "Antilia" (from Andreae's *Christianopolis*). Robert Boyle's letters of 1646–47 referred to Hartlib's circle as the "Invisible College" — a term the authors describe as "almost certainly a kind of Rosicrucian in-joke," echoing the "Invisibles" scare in Paris. Boyle called Hartlib the "midwife and nurse" of this college.

**The founding struggle.** The Royal Society emerged when two groups merged: Wilkins' Oxford Wadham College circle and royalist natural philosophers returning from exile. John Wilkins — the driving force — cited the *Fama Fraternitatis* in his *Mathematicall Magick* (1648) and acknowledged Dee and Fludd as his mathematical sources. Behind-the-scenes papers (lost for three centuries, rediscovered in the mid-twentieth century) reveal that Baron Skytte, a Swedish nobleman, pushed for a Hartlib-style Rosicrucian charter — only to be blocked by other founding members. Hartlib wrote despairingly in April 1661: "There becomes nothing of Lord Skytte's business." After the charter was granted, John Worthington condemned the Society as "materialistic and for nothing but what gratifies externall sense" — underlining that the Rosicrucian/reforming vision had been defeated.

**Francis Bacon's concealed Hermeticism.** Francis Bacon, the Society's intellectual patron, is shown by Paolo Rossi's *Francis Bacon: From Magic to Science* (1957) to have been "as much a devotee of the Hermetic tradition as the other thinkers." He believed in the *anima mundi*, included magic, astrology and alchemy in his field, and compared James I to [[Hermes Trismegistus]] in the first paragraph of *The Advancement of Learning*. His *New Atlantis* (1627) shows awareness of the *Fama Fraternitatis*. But Bacon suppressed his esoteric interests publicly — as a royal courtier who needed James I's patronage, an occult-friendly pose was dangerous.

**Masonic and intelligence links.** Sir Robert Moray, who secured the royal charter, had the distinction of the first recorded Masonic initiation on English soil (1641). Robert Hooke complained that Henry Oldenburg, the Society's foreign secretary, "made a trade of intelligence" — passing correspondence to the Secretary of State. The authors raise the question of whether the Royal Society partly served as an intelligence-gathering cover.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim occupies the bulk of Chapter 6 up to the section "The Greatest Scientific Genius." It is the institutional link between the Rosicrucian movement (Ch. 4–5) and Isaac Newton (Ch. 6 second half).

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The authors note that the "Hermetic version lost" and that those who prevailed had legitimate scientific ambitions — the mechanistic experimental philosophy had genuine intellectual momentum independent of any power struggle. The Royal Society's scientific achievements are not disputed.
