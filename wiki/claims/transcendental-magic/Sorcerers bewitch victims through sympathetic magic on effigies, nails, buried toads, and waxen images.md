## Claim Statement
Lévi catalogs the operative techniques of sorcerers' "bewitchments," distinguishing them from the true Magus's power (who "bewitches without ceremonial and by his mere reprobation"). The sorcerer's methods described include: killing an animal placed in magnetic connexion with the victim via hair or garments, then piercing its extracted heart with nails or thorns for three days while cursing the victim's name; driving consecrated nails into the victim's footprints; baptizing a toad with the victim's name, feeding it a cursed Host, and burying it at the victim's threshold to become "a nightmare and vampire" haunting his dreams; and forming a wax image of the victim from consecrated oil and ashes of Hosts, clothing and "sacramenting" it, then cursing and torturing it — a technique made "more infallible" if the victim's hair, blood, or a tooth can be procured.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"They procure hair or garments of the person whom they seek to destroy; they select an animal which seems to them symbolic of the person... They give it the same name and then slay it with one blow of the magic knife. They cut open the breast, tear out the heart, wrap it, while still palpitating, in the magnetized objects and hourly, for the space of three days, they drive nails, red-hot pins, or long thorns therein, pronouncing malediction upon the name of the bewitched being."

"Another bewitchment... consists in consecration of nails to works of hatred by means of the stinking fumigations of Saturn and invocations of evil genii. They follow the footsteps of the person... and drive the nails cross-wise into every imprint of his feet."

"A fat toad is selected; it is baptized; the name and surname of the person to be bewitched is given it; it is made to swallow a Consecrated Host over which the formulae of execration have been pronounced. The animal is then wrapped in magnetized objects, bound with the hairs of the victim... and is buried at the threshold of the bewitched person's door... The elementary spirit of the toad will become a nightmare and vampire, haunting the dreams of the victim."

"The sorcerers of the Middle Ages... mixed baptismal oil and ashes of Consecrated Hosts with a modicum of wax... they formed an image as far as possible resembling the person whom they desired to bewitch. They clothed this image with garments similar to his; they administered to it the sacraments which he received; they called down upon its head all maledictions... This bewitchment is more infallible if the hair, blood and above all a tooth of the victim can be procured. It was this which gave rise to the proverbial saying: You have a tooth against me."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi treats these as "guilty proceedings" of criminal sorcerers ("Let us stigmatize at this point some of their guilty proceedings"), contrasting them with the true Magus's bloodless power of condemnation by mere disapproval. He warns that practitioners of such bewitchments "expose themselves more often to be the first that are killed by their infernal machinery."
