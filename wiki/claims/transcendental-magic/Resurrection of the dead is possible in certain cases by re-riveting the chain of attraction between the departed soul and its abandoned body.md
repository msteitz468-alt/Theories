## Claim Statement
Death is "a phantom of ignorance" and not the end of life but "the continuation and transformation of life." When the body is removed by accident but not yet worn out or destroyed, it can in certain cases be reassumed by the departed soul, either through its own effort or through the assistance of a stronger will. To raise the dead, the operator must identify and seize the "chains of attraction" connecting the soul to the body it has just quitted, then project sufficient will to link them instantaneously — a rare and never-infallible feat, achievable only through the highest initiation, and possible chiefly in deaths by congestion, suffocation, exhaustion, or hysteria.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Death is a phantom of ignorance; it does not exist; everything in Nature is living... The body is a garment of the soul. When this garment is worn out completely, or seriously and irreparably rent, it is abandoned and never rejoined. But when it is removed by some accident without being worn out or destroyed, it can, in certain cases, be reassumed, either by our own efforts or by the assistance of a stronger and more active will than ours. Death is neither the end of life nor the beginning of immortality: it is the continuation and transformation of life."

"To raise up a dead person we must rivet suddenly and energetically the most powerful chains of attraction which connect it with the body that it has just quitted. It is, therefore, necessary to be acquainted previously with this chain, then to seize thereon, finally to project an effort of will sufficiently powerful to link it up instantaneously and irresistibly."

"The resurrection of a dead person is the masterpiece of magnetism, because it needs for its accomplishment the exercise of a kind of sympathetic omnipotence. It is possible in the case of death by congestion, by suffocation, by exhaustion or by hysteria."

Lévi cites the biblical raisings of Jairus's daughter, the Shunamite's son by Elisha ("Eliseus"), Eutychus by St Paul, and Dorcas by St Peter as historical instances, alongside two contemporary anecdotes of the dead rising to protest the destruction of their wills, and Greuze's painting of a similar event.

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi frames resurrection as a syllogism forced by orthodoxy: "the bodies corporate protect religion, and religion asserts positively the fact of resurrections; therefore resurrections are possible" — a line the translator's footnote characterizes as Lévi mocking his own argument ("he had taken out a licence in mockery").

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Lévi concedes that materialistic science would explain any apparent resurrection as "lethargy" and that he is willing, "out of respect for men with diplomas," to let his theory be termed "the art of curing exceptional and aggravated trances" instead.
