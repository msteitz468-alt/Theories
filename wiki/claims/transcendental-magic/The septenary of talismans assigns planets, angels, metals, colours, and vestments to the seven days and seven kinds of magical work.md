## Claim Statement
The world is governed, in the belief of the ancients, by seven "secondary causes" (Trithemius's *secundii*) called by the Hebrews the seven great archangels (Michaël, Gabriel, Raphaël, Anaël, Samaël, Zadkiel, Oriphiel), corresponding to the seven planets, the seven days of the week, the seven Christian sacraments, seven kinds of magical work, and seven bodily/facial correspondences. Each day of the week requires the Magus to wear a specific vestment colour, jewel, and metal, burn specific perfumes, and use specific floral wreaths when consecrating talismans, which are metal sheets bearing Pantacles or characters given a defined ritual consecration.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"In the belief of the ancients, the world is governed by seven secondary causes—secundii, as Trithemius calls them—which are the universal forces designated by Moses under the plural name of Eloim, gods... The Hebrews termed them the seven great archangels, giving them the names of Michaël, Gabriel, Raphaël, Anaël, Samaël, Zadkiel and Oriphiel."

"Magical works are also seven in number: (I) works of light and riches, under the auspices of the Sun; (2) works of divination and mystery, under the invocation of the Moon; (3) works of skill, science and eloquence, under the protection of Mercury; (4) works of wrath and chastisement, consecrated to Mars; (5) works of love, favoured by Venus; (6) works of ambition and intrigue, under the influence of Jupiter; (7) works of malediction and death, under the patronage of Saturn."

"The Magus who intends undertaking the works of light must operate on a Sunday, from midnight to eight in the morning, or from three in the afternoon to ten in the evening. He should wear a purple vestment, with tiara and bracelets of gold... On Tuesday, a day for the operations of vengeance, the colour of the vestment should be that of flame, rust or blood, with belt and bracelets of steel."

"What is termed a talisman is a sheet of metal, bearing either Pantacles or characters and having received a special consecration for a defined intention... The Pentagram should be engraved invariably upon one side of the talisman... The reverse must bear the Sign of Solomon, that is, the six-pointed star composed of two superposed triangles."

Lévi also specifies the exact construction of instruments: the magical wand "must be one perfectly straight branch of almond or hazel, cut at a single blow with the magical pruning-knife or golden sickle, before the rising of the sun"; the sword "must be of pure steel, with a cruciform copper hilt having three pommels"; the lamp "must be composed of the four metals—gold, silver, brass and iron."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi credits Gaffarel with having "demonstrated scientifically the real power of talismans" and cites the Napoleonic substitution of the Star of Honour for the Cross of St Louis as a historical instance of the Pentagram's symbolic power, calling Napoleon "the greatest instinctive and practical magician of modern times."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnote is dismissive of the interpretive claim linking talismanic profanation-rules to underlying causes: "It is needless to say that this explanation is without authority of any kind, for the hypothesis of Talismanic Magic would not stultify itself in any way so obvious." Waite also disputes the authenticity of Lévi's cited source for the wand and sword designs, noting the *Enchiridion of Pope Leo III* "is not the work of the Pope to whom it is attributed: it is a production of the twelfth century."
