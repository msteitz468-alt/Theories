## Claim Statement
"What is vulgarly called Necromancy has nothing in common with resurrection," and it is doubtful whether the operator truly contacts the souls of the dead. There are two kinds: Necromancy of light (evocation by Prayer, Pantacle, and Perfumes) and Necromancy of darkness (evocation by blood, imprecations, and sacrilege). Lévi states he has practiced only the first and advises no one to attempt the second. Evoked spectres appear as they exist in the memory of those who knew them and as impressed on the Astral Light; they never reveal mysteries of the afterlife, reply only by signs or interior impressions rather than audible voice, and can be felt to touch the living but cannot themselves be touched.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"What is vulgarly called Necromancy has nothing in common with resurrection, and it is at least highly doubtful whether, in operations connected with this application of magical power, we really come into correspondence with the souls of the dead whom we evoke. There are two kinds of Necromancy, that of light and that of darkness — the evocation by Prayer, Pantacle and Perfumes, and the evocation by blood, imprecations and sacrilege. We have practised only the first, and advise no one to devote themselves to the second."

"It is certain that the images of the dead do appear to the magnetized persons who evoke them; it is certain also that they never reveal any mysteries of the life beyond. They are beheld as they still exist in the memories of those who knew them, and doubtless as their reflections have left them impressed on the Astral Light.... spirits, or at least the spectres pretended to be such, may indeed touch us occasionally, but we cannot touch them."

Lévi gives a detailed two-week ritual procedure for the "evocation of love" (preparing a memorial room with the deceased's portrait veiled in white and favorite flowers, fasting, a solitary meal with two place settings, a fire of cypress-wood and incense cast seven times, and thrice-repeated calling of the name), and a more solemn twenty-one-day procedure of meditation, vegetarian diet, and fasting for the "evocation of knowledge and intelligence," describing his own evocation of Apollonius of Tyana using the Nuctenteron and the Magical Philosophy of Patricius.

He also describes "Black Magic" necromancy — practices of Thessalian sorcerers and Roman Canidias (slitting a black sheep's throat over a pit, evoking Hecate) and medieval tomb violation with corpse-fat unguents — and the Grand Grimoire's "Composition of Death" ritual for raising the dead from graves, which he transcribes but explicitly advises against testing.

## Contexts Within the Source
Presented as completing "the revelation of this arcanum" begun with the discussion of resurrection, distinguishing legitimate motive-driven evocation (love or intelligence) from evocation performed "out of pure curiosity," which Lévi calls "fruitless fatigue."
