## Claim Statement
Lévi argues that the Thessalian tradition of love-philtres (associated with Apuleius, Canidia in Horace's Epodes, and the Eclogues of Virgil and Theocritus) works chiefly through the imagination and nervous system rather than any intrinsic virtue in the substances used, and that "true philtres, and those most dangerous, are invisible" — currents of the Astral Light exchanged through breath, glance, and contact. A genuine magician needs no potion: he compels attention by making himself remarkable (even through terror or horror), then withdraws interest to provoke the target's own curiosity and vanity, since "those who resort to potions are old, idiotic, ugly, impotent." He warns of specific physiological dangers (prussic-acid-scented substances, nicotine) and recounts documented cases (Mlle Ranfaing, Père Girard) where alleged love-potions produced hysterical, demonomaniac symptoms diagnosed at the time as possession.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Those who resort to potions are old, idiotic, ugly, impotent. Where, indeed, is the use of the philtre? Anyone who is truly a man has always at his disposal the means of making himself loved."

"The absorption of one will by another frequently changes a whole series of destinies... Such are the currents of vital radiating light, which, mingling and interchanging, produce attractions and sympathies, as magnetic experiments leave no room to doubt... the true philtres, and those most dangerous, are invisible."

"The history of the Church tells us that an arch-heretic named Marcos infatuated all women by breathing on them... The Cure Gaufridy, who was burnt as a sorcerer, pretended to enamour all women who came in contact with his breath. The notorious Father Girard, a Jesuit, was accused by his penitent, Mile Cardier, of destroying her self-control by breathing on her."

On Mlle Ranfaing (quoting Dom Calmet): a physician named Poirot, "failing to obtain a hearing" for marriage, "gave her potions to induce love, and these caused extraordinary derangements in the health of the lady... she was believed to be possessed" and was subjected to exorcism by multiple clergy including in Hebrew and Greek, with the "demon" giving pointed replies; "the physician Poirot was arraigned as a magician, confessed like all others under torture, and was burnt."

On danger substances: "Prussic acid is the most terrible agent in these envenomings of thought... The use of tobacco, by smoking or otherwise, is a dangerous auxiliary of stupefying philtres and brain poisons. Nicotine, as we know, is not less deadly than prussic acid."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi frames the whole chapter as a warning "to give arms to the weak" against those who "seek to poison reason," while also using the Ranfaing case to criticize the exorcists and Dom Calmet for misdiagnosing drug-induced hysteria as demonic possession: "I am merely astonished that men so serious failed to notice the pretended demon's difficulty over answering in a tongue foreign to the sufferer."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnote on Dom Calmet notes Lévi elsewhere "describes Calmet as a scholar of vast erudition but deficient in critical faculty," and that his Treatise on Apparitions "is marked by excessive credulity" — qualifying the reliability of the Ranfaing source narrative even as Lévi uses it.
