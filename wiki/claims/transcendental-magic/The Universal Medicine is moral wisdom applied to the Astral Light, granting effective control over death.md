## Claim Statement
Lévi teaches that most physical disease originates in moral disease ("great passion...corresponds always to a great malady"), that the Hermetic "Universal Medicine" means moral temperance and truth appropriated as daily sustenance rather than a literal potable-gold elixir, that death is gradual rather than instantaneous, and that resurrection from apparent death is genuinely possible for "elementary souls" whose moral will has not yet detached from astral/vital attraction.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The majority of our physical complaints come from our moral diseases, according to the one and universal dogma, and by reason of the law of analogies." Named examples: "Alexander the Great died of pride... Francis I died of an adultery. Louis XV died of his Parc-aux-Cerfs... Marat...was perishing of rage and envy."

"It is in the same sense that the Hermetic masters have said: Make gold potable, and you will have the Universal Medicine—that is to say, appropriate truth to your needs, let it become the source at which you daily drink, and you will in yourself have the immortality of the sages... By growth in reason and goodness man becomes immortal."

On death: "Death is never instantaneous; it is, like sleep, gradual. So long as the blood has not become absolutely cold, so long as the nerves can quiver, a man is not wholly dead...the soul can be recalled, either by accident or by a strong will." He argues from evidenced cases of persons found "having evidently come to life" in their coffins, having "bitten through their clenched hands," concluding "resurrection is possible" but "only for elementary souls," since "great men and true sages are never buried alive."

He also warns against surgical anaesthesia: "by diminishing sensibility we diminish life, and what we subtract from pain under such circumstances turns to the profit of death... Nature is not violated with impunity." He condemns mummification as "against Nature; it is an attempt to create death."

## Contexts Within the Source
Directly continues the Great Work / Philosophical Stone doctrine, reframing the alchemical "elixir of life" and "potable gold" as ethical rather than literal chemical claims, while leaving open a literal claim about resurrection's physical possibility.
