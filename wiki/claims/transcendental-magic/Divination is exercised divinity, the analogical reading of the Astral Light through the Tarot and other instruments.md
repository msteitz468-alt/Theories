## Claim Statement
Lévi defines divination (divinare, "to exercise divinity") as the Great Magical Arcanum itself — prophecy is "to see beforehand the effects which exist in causes, to read in the Astral Light," and miracle-working is "to act upon the Universal Agent and subject it to our will" — realized practically through instruments like the Tarot, coffee-grounds, water-scrying, and somnambulism, all working by fixing attention on the analogical reflections of the Astral Light.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"To divine (divinare) is to exercise divinity... the two signs of human divinity, or of divine humanity, are prophecies and miracles." Lévi states an essential condition of true divination is refusal to be tested: "the sibyls burned their books when Tarquin refused to appraise them...Agrippa perished of want rather than obey those who demanded a horoscope."

On instruments: "divinatory instruments...are simply a means of communication between diviner and consultant, serving merely to fix the two wills upon the same sign... it is thus that lucidity is procured by coffee-grouts, mists, the white of egg, etc." "The Tarot, that miraculous work which inspired all the sacred books of antiquity, is the most perfect instrument of divination, by reason of the analogical precision of its figures and numbers." He discusses Etteilla (Alliette) at length as a diviner of genuine "obstinate perseverance and incontestable good faith" who nonetheless "ended only by misplacing the keys" of the Tarot "through want of their proper understanding."

On transmutation as a live claim: "gold can be made really and materially by means of the Stone of the Sages, which is an amalgam of Salt, Sulphur and Mercury...But to succeed in the Great Work, one must be divinus...it is indispensable to have renounced...the advantage of wealth." He cites Raymund Lully and Nicholas Flamel (initiated via the Kabalist Abraham's book Aesh Mezareph) as successful adepts who "remained poor" despite enriching others.

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi presents this as the climactic chapter of the Doctrine before the summary chapter, promising a full method for reading the Tarot "on the probable chances of destiny...and above all, upon problems of philosophy and religion" at the close of the Ritual.
