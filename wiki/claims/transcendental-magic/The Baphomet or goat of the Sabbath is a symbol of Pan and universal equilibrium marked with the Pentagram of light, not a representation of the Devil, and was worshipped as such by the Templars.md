## Claim Statement
The goat-figure of the Sabbath — identified across mythologies as Ahriman, Typhon, Python, the old serpent, and "the Baphomet of the Templars" — is, in Lévi's reading, a benign hieroglyph rather than a devil: it represents the god Pan, worshipped by Neoplatonists, Gnostics, and (in Lévi's "profound conviction") by the Grand Masters of the Templars themselves. The figure bears the Sign of the Pentagram with one point ascendant on its forehead, marking it as "a symbol of the light," with hands forming the sign of occultism (pointing to the white moon of Chesed and the black moon of Geburah) to express "the perfect concord between mercy and justice." The name "Baphomet," spelled kabalistically backwards, derives from the abbreviations TEM. OHP. AB. — "Templi omnium hominum pacis abbas" ("the father of the temple of peace of all men").

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Yes, in our profound conviction, the Grand Masters of the Order of the Templars worshipped the Baphomet, and caused it to be worshipped by their initiates... But the adorers of this sign do not consider, as do we, that it is a representation of the devil: on the contrary, for them it is that of the god Pan, the god of our modern schools of philosophy... the god of the primitive Gnostic schools; the Christ also of the dissident priesthood."

"The goat which is represented in our frontispiece bears upon its forehead the Sign of the Pentagram with one point in the ascendant, which is sufficient to distinguish it as a symbol of the light. Moreover, the sign of occultism is made with both hands, pointing upward to the white moon of Chesed, and downward to the black moon of Geburah. This sign expresses the perfect concord between mercy and justice."

"The name of the Templar Baphomet, which should be spelt kabalistically backwards, is composed of three abbreviations: TEM. OHP. AB., Templi omnium hominum pacis abbas, 'the father of the temple of peace of all men.'"

"A sculptured coffer was disinterred recently in the ruins of an old Commandery of the Temple, and antiquaries observed upon it a baphometic figure, corresponding by its attributes to the goat of Mendes and the androgyne of Khunrath. It was a bearded figure with a female body, holding the sun in one hand and the moon in the other, attached to chains.... The sign all the same was discovered to be obscene and diabolical by the learned men who examined it."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi states this "for the edification of the vulgar" and "the justification of the demonologist Bodin," presenting it as a deliberate provocation against the Church that "persecuted Templars, burnt magicians, excommunicated Freemasons." He connects the symbol to the two Judean goats (the sacrificed and the scapegoat) and to Gnostic identifications of Christ with sacrificial animal figures.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Lévi notes that "learned men who examined it" judged the disinterred baphometic coffer figure "obscene and diabolical" — the opposite of his own interpretation — without resolving the disagreement.
