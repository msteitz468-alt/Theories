## Claim Statement
Lévi retells, on the authority of the demonologist Bodin, an "almost unknown abomination" concerning the death of King Charles IX. Dying of an undiagnosable disease, Charles was subjected by his mother Catherine de Medicis (already suspected of causing the illness) to the "Oracle of the Bleeding Head": a beautiful, innocent child was prepared for first communion, then a Mass of the Devil was celebrated at midnight before an image of the demon with an inverted crucifix; the child, given the white host, was beheaded immediately after communion, his severed head set upon a black host on the paten, and an oracle sought from it in answer to a secret question the King dared not speak aloud. The head allegedly spoke in Latin, "Vim patior" ("I suffer violence"), at which Charles, terrified, cried repeatedly "Away with that head!" until he died.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Attacked by a disease of which no physician could discover the cause or explain the frightful symptoms, King Charles IX was dying. The Queen-Mother, who ruled him entirely... consulted her astrologers and then had recourse to the foulest form of Magic, the ORACLE OF THE BLEEDING HEAD."

"A child was selected, of beautiful appearance and innocent manners; he was prepared for his first communion by the almoner of the palace. When the day or rather night of the sacrifice arrived, a monk, an apostate Jacobin... celebrated a Mass of the Devil at midnight in the sick-room... It was offered before the image of the demon, having a crucifix upside down under its feet and the sorcerer consecrated two hosts, one black and one white. The white was given to the child... and he was murdered on the steps of the altar immediately after his communion. His head, cut by one blow from the body, was set palpitating upon the great black host which covered the bottom of the paten, and then transported to a table where mysterious lamps were burning."

"A strange and feeble voice, which had nothing human about it, was heard presently in the poor little martyr's head, saying in Latin: Vim patior — 'I suffer violence.' At this reply... a horrible trembling seized the monarch, his arms stiffened and he cried in a hoarse voice: 'Away with that head! Away with that head!' and so continued screaming till he gave up the ghost."

Lévi adds an interpretive gloss on the attendants' reaction: "His attendants, who were not in the confidence of this frightful mystery, believed that he was pursued by the phantom of Coligny... That which tormented the dying man was not, however, a remorse but the hopeless terror of an anticipated Hell."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi presents the story within a broader indictment of Bodin's Demonomania of Sorcerers, which he characterizes as "profoundly Machiavellian," secretly aimed against Catholicism even while ostensibly defending it: "The Kabalist Bodin, Israelite by conviction and Catholic by necessity, had no other intention... than to impeach Catholicism in its works." He uses the anecdote to argue that "great criminals eluded" the "infamous and sanguinary justice" that consumed "poor fools, hysterical women and idiots" executed for sorcery.
