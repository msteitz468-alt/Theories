## Claim Statement
Magical operations carry real danger of madness, terrible disease, or death from excessive nervous excitement, and are especially unsafe for "nervous persons... women, young people and all who are not habituated in perfect self-control." Magic must never be practiced as entertainment or to convince others through phenomena, since skeptics will always attribute results to conjuring tricks. Citing the ninth Tarot Key (the hermit) and the eighth Key (a woman between two pillars holding a sword and a balance), Lévi teaches that the Magus must live retired yet not isolated, avoid making magic his sole trade, and above all "equilibrate forces and restrain the zeal of... initiative" — alternating and balancing opposed tendencies (mildness/anger, energy/forbearance) rather than acting from unbridled passion, since unchecked passions "produce blindly the opposites of their unbridled desire."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The operations of science are not devoid of danger... Terrible and incurable diseases can be occasioned by excessive nervous excitement... We cannot sufficiently dissuade nervous persons, and those who are naturally disposed to exaltation, women, young people and all who are not habituated in perfect self-control and command of fear."

"Never yield to the desire of convincing others by phenomena. The most astounding would prove nothing to those who are not convinced already... All those Magi who divulged their works died violently, and many were driven to suicide, like Cardan, Schroepffer, Cagliostro and others."

"Then, and this is an indispensable condition, we must know how to equilibrate forces and restrain the zeal of our own initiative. This is the meaning of the eighth Key of Hermes, wherein a woman is seated between two Pillars, with an upright sword in one hand and a balance in the other. To equilibrate forces they must be maintained simultaneously and caused to act alternately: the use of the balance represents this double action."

"The milder and calmer you are, the more effective will be your anger; the more energetic you are, the more valuable will be your forbearance... Human passions produce blindly the opposites of their unbridled desire, when they act without direction."

"The man who dedicates himself to works of science must take moderate daily exercise, abstain from prolonged vigils, and follow a wholesome and regular rule of life... Another precaution must be observed equally, and that is never to experiment when ill."

## Contexts Within the Source
This chapter closes the initial "Preparations"-through-"Warning" arc of the Ritual, explicitly returning to and reinforcing the equilibrium doctrine of Chapter II, and citing the Sermon on the Mount ("Forgive your enemies... so shall ye heap coals of fire upon their heads") as, in Lévi's reading, "a mystery of positive magical science."
