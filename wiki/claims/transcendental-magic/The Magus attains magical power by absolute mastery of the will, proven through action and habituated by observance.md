## Claim Statement
Magical power is fundamentally the exercise of trained human will rather than an external gift. The operator must be "impassible, sober and chaste, disinterested, impenetrable and inaccessible to any kind of prejudice or terror," and must learn to will through habituation, using apparently arbitrary observances (rites, privations, repeated small acts) as gymnastics that fix and strengthen volition. Power exists in inverse proportion to material interest, and faith translated at once into action, without hesitation, is what makes a magical intention effective.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Would you reign over yourselves and others? Learn how to will. How can one learn to will? This is the first arcanum of magical initiation... They recognized no will until it had produced its proofs, and they were right. Power is justified by attainment."

"The Magus must be impassible, sober and chaste, disinterested, impenetrable and inaccessible to any kind of prejudice or terror. He must be without bodily defects and proof against all contradictions and all difficulties. The first and most important of magical operations is the attainment of this rare pre-eminence."

"Hence, by a providential or fatal law, the magician can only exercise omnipotence in inverse proportion to his material interest; the alchemist makes so much the more gold as he is the more resigned to privations."

"In order to accomplish a thing we must believe in our possibility of doing it, and this faith must be translated at once into acts... Believe in your power, then act."

"An idle man will never become a magician. Magic is an exercise of all hours and all moments. The operator of great works must be absolute master of himself."

## Contexts Within the Source
Presented as the theoretical basis underlying the practical preparations of Chapter I, and reinforced by a long footnote citing Lévi's "general theory of will," a series of twenty-two numbered axioms from *La Clef des Grands Mystères* (e.g., "Nothing can resist the will of man when he knows what is true and wills what is good").

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnote flags an internal tension: "This speculation on the occult power of perseverance calls to be checked by Lévi's alternative thesis that the great works of adeptship presuppose personal detachment and complete absence of self-interest." Waite also criticizes the will-doctrine passages as "little better than a play upon words."
