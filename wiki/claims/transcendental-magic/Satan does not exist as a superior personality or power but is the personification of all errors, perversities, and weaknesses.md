## Claim Statement
"As a superior personality and power Satan does not exist." He is "the personification of all errors, perversities and consequently of all weaknesses"; since God is defined as "He Who exists of necessity," His antagonist must be defined as "he who does not exist at all." Black Magic's aberrations — sanguinary sacrifices, monstrous idolatry, the horrors of the Sabbath as commonly described — are explained by Lévi as products of the Manichaean heresy, which "misconstrued" Zoroaster's doctrine of two balancing forces into "a negative divinity, subordinate but hostile to the active Deity," splitting the Star of Solomon into opposed triangles and imagining "a trinity of night."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Let us affirm categorically, to combat the remnants of Manichaeanism which are appearing sporadically among Christians, that as a superior personality and power Satan does not exist. He is the personification of all errors, perversities and consequently of all weaknesses. If God may be defined as He Who exists of necessity, may we not define His antagonist and enemy as necessarily he who does not exist at all?... No one has ever blasphemed God. The insults and mockeries addressed to His disfigured images attain Him not."

"We have named Manichaeanism, and it is by this monstrous heresy that we shall explain the aberrations of Black Magic. The misconstrued doctrine of Zoroaster and the magical law of two forces constituting universal equilibrium, have caused some illogical minds to imagine a negative divinity, subordinate but hostile to the active Deity. An impure duad comes thus into being. Men were mad enough to halve God; the Star of Solomon was separated into triangles, and the Manichaeans imagined a trinity of night. This evil God, product of sectarian fancies, inspired all manias and all crimes."

Lévi cites Father Ventura's declaration (after reading Lévi's "Doctrine") that the Kabalah was "an invention of the devil" and the Star of Solomon "another diabolical device to persuade the world that Satan was the same as God," treating this as proof of the very Manichaean error he critiques.

## Contexts Within the Source
This claim directly follows and grounds Lévi's reinterpretation of the Baphomet, and precedes his account of the three categories of Sabbath testimony (fantastic/imaginary, genuine secret assemblies of adepts, and criminal Black Magic gatherings), which he attributes in part to potions and narcotic unguents (citing Baptista Porta's recipes) producing hallucinated "nightmare" visions rather than real diabolical encounters.
