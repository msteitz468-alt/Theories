## Claim Statement
Waite's Biographical Preface presents Éliphas Lévi Zahed as the pseudonym of Alphonse Louis Constant, a shoemaker's son born about 1810, educated for the priesthood at Saint Sulpice, expelled before ordination for unorthodox opinions, imprisoned for six months over a pamphlet ("The Gospel of Liberty"), briefly a disciple of the self-proclaimed prophet Ganneau, married in a runaway match to Noémie Cadiot (who later left him, had the marriage annulled, and became the sculptor "Claude Vignon"), and who died in 1875 having received the last rites of the Catholic Church he had never formally renounced.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"ÉLIPHAS LÉVI ZAHED is a pseudonym which was adopted in his occult writings by Alphonse Louis Constant... The author of the Dogme et Rituel de la Haute Magie was born in humble circumstances about the year 1810, being the son of a shoemaker... he entered on his clerical novitiate, took minor orders and in due course became a deacon. He was also appointed a professor at the Petit Séminaire de Paris. But at some uncertain date subsequently... he was expelled suddenly for holding opinions contrary to the teaching of the Roman Catholic Church."

On Ganneau: "Esquiros introduced him to Ganneau, a distracted prophet of the period, who had adopted the dress of a woman... He was, in fact, a second incarnation of Louis XVII."

On imprisonment: Constant's pamphlet "The Gospel of Liberty... [led to] the imprisonment of the author for a term of six months" (a footnote gives an alternative title, La Voix de la Famine, 1846).

On marriage: "in spite of any pledge implied by his diaconate, he effected a runaway match with Mile Noémie Cadiot, a beautiful girl of sixteen. This lady bore him two children, who died in tender years, and subsequently she deserted him... she even asserted her position further by obtaining a legal annulment of her marriage... The lady... became famous as a sculptor, exhibiting at the Salon and elsewhere under the name of Claude Vignon."

On death: "His contradictory life closed in 1875 amidst the last offices of the Church which had almost expelled him from her bosom."

## Contexts Within the Source
Waite frames this biography explicitly as fragmentary and uncertain ("we have few available materials for a life of the 'Abbé' Constant"), drawing on secondary reports, some of which he flags as dubious (e.g., a "vicious story" about a faked death and second clerical career, and disputed claims about a vow of celibacy for secular priests).
