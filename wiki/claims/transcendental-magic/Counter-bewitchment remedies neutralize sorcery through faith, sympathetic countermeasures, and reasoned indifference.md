## Claim Statement
Lévi prescribes specific remedies matched to each form of bewitchment described in the same chapter, alongside a general psychological doctrine that fear is itself the mechanism of bewitchment ("it acts after the manner of contagious maladies") and that reasoned confidence is the surest protection. For the toad-burial curse, the victim must carry a living toad in a horn box; for the pierced-heart curse, he must eat a lamb's heart with sage and onion and carry a Talisman of Venus or the Moon; for the wax-image curse, a more elaborate protective image must be made, hung with seven talismans, placed within a Pentagram, anointed daily, and burnt after seven days. The wise physician-priest should also lead the bewitched person toward an act of goodness to his tormentor and "the communion of salt."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"A potent means of resisting bewitchment is not to fear it; it acts after the manner of contagious maladies. In times of epidemic, the terror-struck are the first to be attacked. The secret of not fearing an evil is not to think about it."

"They must persuade such a person to do some act of goodness to his tormentor, render him some service which he cannot refuse, and lead him directly or otherwise to the communion of salt. A person who believes himself bewitched by the execration and interment of the toad must carry about him a living toad in a horn box. For the bewitchment of the pierced heart, the afflicted individual must be made to eat a lamb's heart seasoned with sage and onion, and to carry a Talisman of Venus or of the Moon in a satchel filled with camphor and salt."

"For bewitchment by the waxen figure, a more perfect figure must be made, and set about as far as possible with things belonging to the person; seven talismans must be hung round the neck; it must be placed in the middle of a great Pantacle representing the Pentagram, and each day must be rubbed slightly with a mixture of oil and balm, after reciting the Conjuration of the Four... At the end of seven days the image must be burnt in consecrated fire."

On threats specifically: "A threat is a real bewitchment, because it acts powerfully on the imagination... The first among all is to be reasonable and just, giving no opportunity or excuse to anger."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi addresses this material to two audiences: he advises "persons who are nervous, feeble, credulous, hysterical, superstitious devotees" to avoid occult study entirely and "drink water," while reserving the active remedies for "the wise... they are priests and physicians" who may be called to cure the bewitched. He connects the whole doctrine to his broader equilibrium theory of the Astral Light, describing bewitchment as "an actual poisoning of some current of Astral Light."
