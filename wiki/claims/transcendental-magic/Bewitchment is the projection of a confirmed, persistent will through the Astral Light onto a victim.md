## Claim Statement
Lévi teaches that envoûtement (bewitchment) is real, "not only possible but in some sense necessary and fatal," worked through the projection of a "will confirmed by an act" via the Astral Light, distinguishes voluntary from involuntary and physical from moral bewitchment, and states that jealous or self-interested motives neutralize a bewitcher's power while unmixed hatred is efficacious and dangerous to reverse.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Every real will is confirmed by acts; every will confirmed by an act is an action. Every action is subject to a judgement, and such judgement is eternal...Bewitchment is a homicide, and the more infamous because it eludes self-defence by the victim and punishment by law." Bewitchment is "not only possible but in some sense necessary and fatal. It is going on continually in the social world, unconsciously both to agents and patients."

"A frank, absolute hatred, unleavened by rejected passion or personal cupidity, is, under certain given conditions, a death-sentence for its object... a jealous person will never efficaciously bewitch his rival, and a greedy heir will never by the mere fact of his will succeed in shortening the days of a miserly and long-lived uncle," because "a desire, being an attraction, counterbalances and annuls the power of projection." "Witchcraft, properly so called, that is, ceremonial operation with intent to bewitch, acts only on the operator, and serves to fix and confirm his will."

Lévi cites the Kabalist Jean Bodin's Demonomania as motivated by warning "against dangerous incredulity," parallel to Eudes de Mirville's contemporary project. He states "the astral movement being circular, every azotic or magnetic emission which does not encounter its medium returns with force to its point of departure," interpreting the Gospel demons cast into swine as "the rupture of a magnetic current infected by evil wills," and notes remedies: substitution, "rupture or deflection of the astral current," and Paracelsus's sympathetic-magic cures applied to representations of afflicted members rather than the members themselves. He credits "Brother Hilarion Tissot" with curing the "insane" by "unconsciously using the magnetism of Paracelsus."

## Contexts Within the Source
Continues directly from the sidereal-body doctrine of the two preceding chapters; Lévi treats the eyes, hands (especially the thumb), and certain antipathetic animals (toad, basilisk, "tard") as instruments/guards against fluidic projection, terming excess "ASTRAL INTOXICATION."
