## Claim Statement
Lévi asserts that the shepherds of Chaldea drew the first constellations as the first letters of the kabalistic alphabet, citing M. Moreau de Dammartin's derivation of the Juggler/Aleph from the constellation of the Crane, Pope Joan/Beth from the Ram, and the Empress/Ghimel from the Great Bear. He gives the Kabalist Gaffarel's astrological table linking zodiac signs to pairs of named genii, then presents a full table of the twenty-two days of the lunar month, each correlated to one of the twenty-two Tarot Keys and to an event of Genesis (e.g., the first day to the moon's own creation; the fourth, baleful day to the birth of Cain; the fifteenth, "Typhon or the Devil," to the birth of Ishmael), concluding that "all secrets of the Kabalah and Magic... are enclosed in this hieroglyphic book of Thoth, Enoch or Cadmus."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"It is said that the first constellations marked out by the shepherds of Chaldea were also the first letters of the kabalistic alphabet... the Chinese Tseu, the Hebrew Aleph and the Greek ALPHA expressed hieroglyphically by the figure of the juggler, would be borrowed from the constellation of the Crane... The Chinese Tcheou, the Hebrew BETH... corresponding to Pope Joan or Juno, were formed from the head of the Ram."

Gaffarel's genii of the zodiac are listed in full: "For the Ram, SATAARAN and SARAHIEL; for the Bull, BAGDAL, and ARAZIEL... for the Fishes, RASAMASA and VACABIEL."

On the lunar-day table: "Here follow the special characters of all days of the moon, distinguished by the twenty-two Tarot Keys and by the signs of the seven planets. 1. The Juggler, or Magus. The first day of the moon is that of the creation of the moon itself... 15. Typhon, or the Devil. Birth of Ishmael. Day of reprobation and exile... 29. The Fool of the Tarot. Day of failure and miscarriage in all things."

"We see also what diverse significations are included in the twenty-two Keys which form the universal alphabet of the Tarot, together with the truth of our affirmation, that all secrets of the Kabalah and Magic, all mysteries of the elder world, all science of the patriarchs, all historical traditions of primeval times, are enclosed in this hieroglyphic book of Thoth, Enoch or Cadmus."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi frames star-reading as fundamentally a projection of the observer's inner state onto an ambiguous field of points: "The sky is thus the mirror of the human soul, and when we think that we are reading in the stars it is in ourselves we read." He credits the lunar-day table to "old Hebrew Kabalists" as transmitted by "John Belot and others."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Lévi himself expresses reservation about Gaffarel's method: "we confess that the configurations are frequently arbitrary in the highest degree... We are deterred therefore from reproducing a copy of Gaffarel's planisphere." Waite's footnotes add that "Belot knew nothing of Tarot cards" and that "it is impossible to understand Lévi's equivalents for Hebrew numbers," casting doubt on the historicity of the correlation Lévi asserts.
