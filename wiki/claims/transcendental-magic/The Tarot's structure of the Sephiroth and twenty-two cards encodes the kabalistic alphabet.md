## Claim Statement
Lévi asserts the Tarot is a kabalistic hieroglyphic book composed of a kabalistic alphabet of twenty-two figures and four suits, whose combination with the Sephirotic decade generates the entire system of Divine Names, and that the deal of the cards yields true oracular meaning because "the faith of the ancients attributed nothing to chance."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"This hieroglyphic book is composed of a kabalistic alphabet and of a wheel or circle of four decades...The twenty-two figures of the alphabet represent, in the first place, the thirteen dogmas, and secondly, the nine beliefs authorized by that Jewish religion." Figure I (the Juggler) "represents the active principle"; Figure II ("Pope Joan") "represents dogmatic unity based upon numbers...the personification of the Kabalah or the Gnosis." The four suits — Clubs, Cups, Swords, Circles/Pantacles — are "hieroglyphics of the Tetragram": Club as JOD, Cup as He, Sword as Vau, Circle as final He.

Lévi assigns each numbered card to a Sephira via verse ("Four signs present the Name of every name. I KETHER. The four Aces...") through to "10 MALKUTH. The four Tens," and gives worked examples: "the five of Clubs signifies rigorously GEBURAH of JOD...the seven of Cups signifies the victory of mercy...the eight of Swords signifies conflict or eternal equilibrium." He credits "the erudite Kabalist Gaffarel...employed by Cardinal Richelieu" as first identifying the Tarot oracles with the Hebrew THERAPH or THERAPHIM.

## Contexts Within the Source
Presented as the practical, divinatory extension of the Sephirotic doctrine just outlined; Lévi promises "further details" in the Ritual concerning "the marvellous Book of the Tarot."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnotes dispute several of Lévi's specific correspondences and historical claims (e.g., that Postel never mentions the Tarot, that Gaffarel never references Tarot cards in his writings), but these are editorial notes rather than contradictions within Lévi's own text.
