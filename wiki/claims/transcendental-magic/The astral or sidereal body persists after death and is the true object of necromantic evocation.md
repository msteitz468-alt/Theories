## Claim Statement
Drawing on the Hebrew "book concerning the Revolution of Souls," Lévi teaches that at death man leaves two corpses — a terrestrial body and an aerial/sidereal body still animated by universal movement until it is slowly absorbed by the stars — and that this sidereal body, or "larva," is what Necromancy actually evokes and communicates with, not the departed soul itself.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"We read in the Hebrew book concerning the Revolution of Souls that there are three classes of souls—the daughters of Adam, the daughters of angels and the daughters of sin...there are also three kinds of spirits—captive, wandering and free." At death, "the divine spirit...ascends by itself above and leaves two corpses below, one upon earth, the other in the atmosphere; one terrestrial and elementary, the other aerial and sidereal."

If a man "lived well the astral body evaporates like a pure incense"; if he "lived in sin, his astral body, which holds him prisoner, still seeks the object of its passions and wishes to return to life...watches over treasures which it possessed and buried...loses successively all the members which have ministered to his iniquities; then he dies a second time and for ever, because he loses his personality and his memory."

"It is these aerial bodies which are evoked by Necromancy. We enter into communion with larvae, with dead or perishing substances, by this operation. The beings in question, for the most part, cannot speak except by a ringing in our ears...and commonly they can reason only by reflecting our thoughts and our reveries." Lévi adds a doctrine of the "embryonic" state, in which not-yet-purified souls "remain captive for a longer or shorter period in the astral body," and states elsewhere that some living persons are "in reality dead" — walking sidereal remnants void of "moral and affectionate sense," resembling "brucalaques and vampires."

## Contexts Within the Source
This doctrine grounds Lévi's personal evocation narrative later in the same chapter and his account of lycanthropy and bewitchment in the following chapters, all treated as effects of the sidereal body's independent activity.
