## Claim Statement
Lévi condemns "venomous Magic, or rather sorcery" as the most criminal abuse of magical science, describing historical poisoning techniques disguised under alchemical language (e.g., "the Powder of Succession" masquerading as "the Powder of Projection"), and explains popular superstitions, charms, amulets, and the evil eye (jettatura) as degraded survivals of once-meaningful kabalistic/Pythagorean symbolism.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"We have now to approach the most criminal abuse to which magical sciences can be put, namely, venomous Magic, or rather sorcery... If human justice, instead of punishing the adepts, had proscribed only the nigromancers and poisoning sorcerers... its severity would have been well placed." Lévi references Alexandre Dumas's Monte Cristo for poisoning technique and describes recipes from the Grand Grimoire disguised as "Method of Making Gold," and quotes John Baptist Porta's toad-poison recipe from Natural Magic.

He asserts: "the secret of the Powder of Projection, in more than one old book which claims to be Hermetic, is in reality that of the Powder of Succession." Sorcerers historically "were poor, repulsed by all and consequently full of hatred," and "poisoned in their turn all those who were weak enough to fear them."

On superstition: "Superstition is derived from a Latin word which signifies survival. It is the sign surviving the thought; it is the dead body of a Religious Rite." He interprets Pythagorean maxims ("Do not pick up what falls from the table...kill not the serpent when it slips into your garden") as originally ethical allegories degraded into "vain observances." He treats talismans and pantacles (the wheel of Pythagoras, the Cherub of Ezekiel, the Indian Adda-Nari) as originally meaningful "kabalistic syntheses," citing Coglenius's descriptions of the talismans of Solomon and Rabbi Chael, and the Magical Calendars of Tycho Brahe and Duchentau.

He recounts the "prophetic supper of Cazotte" (as described by Laharpe) as a coded episode of initiate justice among illuminati, and lists a series of deaths of "adepts of good faith" and "betrayers" (Cagliostro, Catherine Theos's band, Schroepffer, Kotzebuë) as consequences of violating the "Great Arcanum."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi frames the chapter as a moral warning ("we write not to instruct but to warn") rather than an instructional text on poisoning.
