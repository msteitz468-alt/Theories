## Claim Statement
Because the operative universe runs on paired, opposed forces, every instrument used in ceremonial magic must exist in duplicate: two swords, two wands, two cups, two chafing-dishes, two pantacles, two lamps, two superposed vestments of contrasted colours, and at least two metals worn on the person. Duplicated crowns of laurel, rue, mugwort or vervain serve a divinatory purpose, one being worn and the other burned as an augury from its crackling and smoke. The ancients similarly used two altars and two victims (one white, one black) in evocations, and required either one or three operators, since a duad alone signifies immobility or death absent a third, equilibrating element.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"All magical instruments must be duplicated; there must be two swords, two wands, two cups, two chafing-dishes, two pantacles and two lamps; two vestments must be worn, one over the other, and they must be of contrasted colours, a rule still followed by Catholic priests; lastly, two metals must be worn at least, or otherwise none."

"The crown of laurel, rue, mugwort or vervain must be double, in like manner; one of them is used in evocations, while the other is burnt, the crackling which it makes and the curls of the smoke which it produces being observed as an augury."

"In their evocations they constructed two altars and immolated two victims, one white and one black; the operator, whether male or female, holding a sword in one hand and a wand in the other, had one foot shod and the other bared. Moreover, either one or three persons were required for magical works, because the duad would mean immobility or death in the absence of an equilibrating motor; and when a man and a woman participated in the ceremony, the operator was always a virgin, a hermaphrodite, or a child."

## Contexts Within the Source
Presented as the practical corollary of the equilibrium doctrine set out earlier in Chapter II: "everything is ruled and predetermined by the one and universal dogma of Hermes, that of analogy in the three worlds," so the rites are "prearranged by the science itself" rather than arbitrary.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnote states flatly: "There seems no authority in the records of magical practice for these duplications, those of the instruments included," indicating this rule, like the forty-day purification, is presented by Waite as Lévi's own invention rather than a documented traditional requirement.
