## Claim Statement
Lévi states that attainment of the Sanctum Regnum (the "Holy Kingdom" of the Magi) requires four indispensable conditions — "an intelligence illuminated by study, an intrepidity which nothing can check, a will which cannot be broken, and a prudence which nothing can corrupt" — summarized as To know, to dare, to will, to keep silence, and inscribed upon the four symbolic forms of the sphinx (man, eagle, bull, lion).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Chapter I: "Magic, which the men of old denominated the SANCTUM REGNUM, the Holy Kingdom, or Kingdom of God, REGNUM DEI, exists only for kings and for priests... To attain the Sanctum Regnum, in other words, the knowledge and power of the Magi, there are four indispensable conditions — an intelligence illuminated by study, an intrepidity which nothing can check, a will which cannot be broken, and a prudence which nothing can corrupt and nothing intoxicate. To know, to dare, to will, to keep silence — such are the four words of the Magus, inscribed upon the four symbolical forms of the sphinx."

He elaborates the sphinx symbolism: "his the human head, in order to possess speech; his the eagle's wings, in order to scale the heights; his the bull's flanks, in order to furrow the depths; his the lion's talons, to make a way on the right and the left, before and behind."

Chapter IX (Initiation) restates the formula in terms of the initiate's attributes — "the lamp of Trismegistus, the mantle of Apollonius, and the staff of the patriarchs" — concluding: "He knows, he dares and is silent."

## Contexts Within the Source
This fourfold formula recurs as the ethical/practical core of the entire Doctrine, distinguishing the true "magician," who "avails himself of a force which he knows," from the "sorcerer," who "seeks to misuse that which he does not understand" (Chapter I).
