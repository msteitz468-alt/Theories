## Claim Statement
Lévi narrates a first-person account of a ceremonial evocation of the phantom of Apollonius of Tyana in London in 1854, after twenty-one days of prescribed preparation, arranged by an unnamed elderly English lady initiate, in a cabinet fitted with concave mirrors, a marble altar inscribed with the Pentagram, chafing dishes, and magical vestments — resulting in a visible, partially tactile apparition and a lasting physical after-effect (arm numbness).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"In the spring of the year 1854 I had undertaken a journey to London...I prepared during a period of twenty-one days, scrupulously observing the rules laid down in the thirteenth chapter of the 'Ritual.'" The preliminaries "terminated on 24 July"; the goal was "to evoke the phantom of the divine Apollonius and interrogate it upon two secrets."

The cabinet "contained four concave mirrors and a species of altar having a white marble top, encircled by a chain of magnetized iron," inscribed with "the Sign of the Pentagram." Lévi wore "a white garment...similar to the alb of our catholic priests" and "a crown of vervain leaves, intertwined with a golden chain," holding "a new sword" and the Ritual. After kindling fires of "charcoal of alder and laurel wood," he "beheld distinctly, before the altar, the figure of a man of more than normal size, which dissolved and vanished away," then, after further evocation within a drawn circle, "there was a man in front of me, wrapped from head to foot in a species of shroud...lean, melancholy and beardless." Something "touched my hand which was holding the sword, and the arm became immediately benumbed as far as the elbow," an effect that "remained numbed and painful" for "several subsequent days." The apparition did not speak audibly; questions "answered themselves" in Lévi's mind, giving the reply "Death!" / "Dead!"

Lévi qualifies the experience: "Am I to conclude from all this that I really evoked, saw and touched the great Apollonius of Tyana? I am not so hallucinated as to affirm or so unserious as to believe it...I affirm solely that I did see and that I did touch." He repeated the experiment "on two further occasions" with similar but less detailed results, and states he later "re-read carefully the life of Apollonius" and noted correspondence between the apparition's gaunt appearance and Apollonius's historically recorded imprisonment and starvation late in life.

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi frames necromancy as evocation of "aerial bodies" (sidereal remnants) preserved and visible via the Astral Light rather than the descent of souls from higher spheres: "there is no proof whatsoever that spirits leave the higher spheres to communicate with us...we evoke the memories which they have left in the Astral Light."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Lévi explicitly withholds full endorsement of literal spirit-return, attributing the phenomena instead to "an actual drunkenness of the imagination" produced by "the preparations, the perfumes, the mirrors, the pantacles," while still insisting on the "real efficacy of magical ceremonies." He also states he came to suspect his unnamed hostess of continued, unsanctioned practice of "Necromancy and Goëtia."
