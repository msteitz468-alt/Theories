## Claim Statement
As a supplement to his chapter on bewitchment, Lévi distinguishes mandragores and androids from mere sympathetic wax images. The natural mandragore is a narcotic, aphrodisiac root resembling a human or phallic form, which some alchemists speculated might be "the umbilical vestige of our terrestrial origin," leading some to attempt cultivating it into an artificial human, others to cross "monstrous pairs" of animals with human seed (producing only "shameful crimes and barren deformities"), and others to build androids by "galvanic machinery" — one of which was attributed to Albertus Magnus and supposedly destroyed by St Thomas Aquinas with a blow of his stick. Lévi identifies the "real and serious android of the ancients" as an occult secret first divulged by Mesmer: the projection of the Magus's will into another body "organized and served by an elementary spirit," i.e., a magnetized subject.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The natural mandragore is a filamentous root which presents more or less as a whole either the figure of a man, or that of the virile members. It is slightly narcotic and an aphrodisiacal virtue was ascribed to it by the ancients, who represented it as being sought by Thessalian sorcerers for the composition of philtres."

"Some alchemists... indulged in reveries on the culture of the mandragore and experimented in the artificial reproduction of a soil sufficiently fruitful and a sun sufficiently active to humanize the said root, and thus create men without the concurrence of the female. Others... crossed monstrous pairs and projected human seed into animal earth, only for the production of shameful crimes and barren deformities. The third method of making the android was by galvanic machinery. One of these almost intelligent automata was attributed to Albertus Magnus, and it is said that St Thomas destroyed it with one blow from a stick because he was perplexed by its answers."

"The real and serious android of the ancients was a secret which they have kept hidden from all eyes, and Mesmer was the first who dared to divulge it. It was the projection of the will of the Magus into another body, organized and served by an elementary spirit; in more modern and intelligible terms, it was a magnetic subject."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi treats the Albertus Magnus/Aquinas anecdote explicitly as allegory rather than literal history: "This story is an allegory: the android was primitive scholasticism, which was broken by the Summa of St Thomas."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnote directly disputes Lévi's Mesmer claim: "A magnetic subject in the experiments of Mesmer was not a subject possessed by an elementary spirit, and the form in which Lévi expresses his notion is little short of nonsense... the challenge against him would be to produce his evidence. There is none in the known records."
