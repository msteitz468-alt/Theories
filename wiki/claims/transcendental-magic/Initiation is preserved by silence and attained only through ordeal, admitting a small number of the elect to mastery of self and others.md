## Claim Statement
The science of Magic is preserved by silence and perpetuated only by initiation, which requires ordeal and contest rather than mere disclosure of secrets. Though many aspire, only a small number — "the elect" — succeed in attaining mastery, and this elect properly constitutes a natural priesthood and kingship (the Sacerdotal and Royal Art). The old initiations (Egyptian temples of Thebes and Memphis) demanded the candidate abandon his life and liberty to the masters and pass through terrors — funeral pyres, torrents, precipices — as tests of will before admission.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"THE science is preserved by silence and perpetuated by initiation... Noli ire, fac venire, was the device of Rabelais... The destiny of man, as we have said, is to make or create himself... All men are called into the lists, but the number of the elect — that is, of those who succeed — is invariably small... Men who are masters of themselves become easily masters of others."

"Hence it was that the end of the old initiations was termed indifferently the Sacerdotal Art and the Royal Art. The antique magical associations were seminaries for priests and kings, and admission could be obtained only by truly sacerdotal and royal works — that is, by transcending all weakness of Nature."

"What, in fact, did the candidate in the old initiations? He abandoned his life and liberty entirely to the masters of the temples of Thebes or Memphis; he advanced resolutely through unnumbered terrors... he ascended funeral pyres, swam torrents of black and raging water, hung by unknown see-saws over unfathomed precipices.... Now, this is precisely what must be done, and what has been done invariably, by those who aspire to the SANCTUM REGNUM of magical omnipotence."

"Initiation by contest and ordeal is therefore indispensable for the attainment of the practical science of Magic."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi presents this as the essential law of religious and magical life, contrasting it with the modern "neglect of initiation," which he blames (via a critique of Rousseau's doctrine of natural equality) for social and intellectual chaos, and which he links to the decline of the priesthood since the "Gnostic disclosures" alienated the Church from kabalistic truth.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
None presented; Lévi treats the necessity of ordeal-based initiation as an unqualified law of Nature, though he notes the "law of silence is not therefore absolute and inviolable, except relatively to the uninitiated multitude."
