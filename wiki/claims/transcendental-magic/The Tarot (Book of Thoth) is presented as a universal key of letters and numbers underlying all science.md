## Claim Statement
Lévi describes an ancient, untranslated book "preserved unmutilated in primeval characters, on detached leaves" — identifiable as the Tarot ("Book of Hermes") — as "a book which is the summary of all sciences, which can resolve all problems by its infinite combinations," calling it "a universal key" explained only by William Postel, and structures his own twenty-two-chapter Doctrine around its twenty-two Trumps Major and corresponding Hebrew letters.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"This book, which may be older than that of Enoch, has never been translated, but is still preserved unmutilated in primeval characters, on detached leaves, like the tablets of the ancients... It is, in truth, a monumental and extraordinary work, strong and simple as the architecture of the pyramids... a book which is the summary of all sciences, which can resolve all problems by its infinite combinations... It is to be counted unquestionably among the very great gifts bequeathed to us by antiquity; it is a universal key, the name of which has been explained and comprehended only by the learned William Postel."

A footnote (from Lévi's own later Clef des Grands Mystères, quoted by Waite) identifies the book explicitly: "This monument is the Tarot of the Bohemians, the original of modern playing-cards. It consists of twenty-two allegorical letters and four series of ten hieroglyphics each, corresponding to the four letters of the Name Jehovah... all science is contained in this mysterious book."

Waite notes editorially that "there are twenty-two Trumps Major in the sequence of Tarot cards, on which account Éliphas Lévi divides his Doctrine and Ritual into twenty-two chapters each," each headed by a Hebrew letter and a Tarot correspondence.

## Contexts Within the Source
The Tarot doctrine is the structural key to the whole Doctrine and Ritual: each of the twenty-two chapters is headed by a Hebrew letter and an associated Tarot/Sephirotic term (e.g., Chapter I "DISCIPLINA ENSOPH KETHER," Chapter V "GEBURAH ECCE").

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnote to Chapter I calls the resulting correspondence scheme "utter confusion," giving examples such as the Tarot Juggler/Magus not corresponding coherently to "the Candidate for initiation," and inconsistent Hebrew/Roman letter equivalences (e.g., Roman I equated with Hebrew TETH, K with JOD).
