## Claim Statement
Waite argues that Éliphas Lévi "invented an occult philosophy before he explained occultism," that the aggressive occult dogmatism and hypothesis of the Astral Light expounded in the Doctrine and Ritual (1855–56) was progressively qualified, allegorized, and effectively voided in his later books, and that Lévi was fundamentally a materialist and disbeliever in real communication with spirits, not the initiated adept of a genuine secret school.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"It follows that Éliphas Lévi invented an occult philosophy before he explained occultism; that it is his own and no other's; that when in his later books he added, revised and altered — amidst many contradictions in the process — we are confronted only with the shifting moods of a brilliant mind which had no certain anchorage, outside a hypothesis for explaining certain phenomena."

"For Éliphas Lévi, more especially at the period of his initial occult enterprise, was fundamentally a materialist — a materialist, moreover, who at times approached perilously towards atheism, as when he stated that God is a hypothesis which is 'very probably necessary'; he was, further, a disbeliever in any real communication with the world of spirits."

Waite recounts having once believed Lévi belonged to "a circle of occult initiation" but states: "I know now that it was not extant in the days that Lévi wrote. The hypothesis of a universal glass of vision and an agent of magical power was neither the invention of Lévi nor the secret of a nineteenth century sanctuary of occult knowledge: it belongs to the records of the past."

On the retraction pattern: "There was a time when he thought otherwise — a moment, a year or just so long as it took him to write Le Dogme et Rituel de la Haute Magie, for the exposition of his hypothetical discovery concerning Astral Light... But he felt later on that the iridescent verbiage of these volumes had credited too much to the fascination of their subject, and in later writings he applied a pumping process to all that he had said of this order."

Waite also criticizes Lévi's scholarship generally: "he is at the same time without evidence of depth; splendid in ready generalization, he is without accuracy in detail... His History of Magic is a case in point... it swarms with historical inaccuracies."

## Contexts Within the Source
This is Waite's own critical editorial framing, presented explicitly as his assessment (not Lévi's), intended to prepare the reader for "amazing discrepancies between the Doctrine and Ritual of Transcendental Magic and the volumes which followed."
