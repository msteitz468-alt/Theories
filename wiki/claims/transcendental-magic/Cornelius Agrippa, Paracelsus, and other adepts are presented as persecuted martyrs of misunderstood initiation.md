## Claim Statement
Lévi presents Cornelius Agrippa, Paracelsus, Raymond Lully, William Postel, Swedenborg, Saint-Martin, Cagliostro, and Cazotte as a lineage of true adepts who bore a secret they could not divulge, were persecuted, ridiculed, or destroyed by a world that misunderstood them, and specifically defends Agrippa as "less of a magician than any," unfairly regarded by "the vulgar" as "a more potent sorcerer than all" merely because "he was sometimes a cynic and mystifier."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
In the Introduction: "He whom we behold perishing poor and abandoned is Cornelius Agrippa, less of a magician than any, though the vulgar persist in regarding him as a more potent sorcerer than all because he was sometimes a cynic and mystifier." Of Paracelsus: "the marvellous Paracelsus, always drunk and always lucid"; of Postel: "William Postel writing naively to the fathers of the Council of Trent, proclaiming that he has discovered the absolute doctrine... The Council heeds not the maniac, does not vouchsafe to condemn him." Of Raymond Lully: "One has discovered the universal science and goes vainly seeking death to escape the agonies of his triumph: he is the Majorcan Raymond Lully."

Chapter IX's closing catalogue: "He remembers the bereavement and murder of Orpheus, the exile and lonely death of Moses, the martyrdom of the prophets, the tortures of Apollonius, the Cross of the Saviour. He knows the desolation in which Agrippa died, whose memory is even now slandered; he knows what labours overcame the great Paracelsus, and all that Raymond Lully was condemned to undergo that he might finish by a violent death. He remembers Swedenborg simulating madness and even losing reason in order to excuse his science; Saint-Martin and his hidden life; Cagliostro, who perished forsaken in the cells of the Inquisition; Cazotte, who ascended the scaffold."

A footnote in the Doctrine (from Lévi's Correspondence with Baron Spédalieri) elaborates his view of Agrippa: "He was only a bold vulgarizer, and fortunately very superficial in his studies, never possessing the keys of the Sepher Yetzirah and Zohar... Too shallow for a Magus, he was pleased to pass as a magician and a sorcerer."

## Contexts Within the Source
This lineage of martyred adepts is invoked rhetorically at both the start (Introduction) and close (Chapter IX) of the Doctrine to frame occult science as a persecuted, secret tradition whose practitioners "bear with them to their tomb" a secret they dare not reveal fully.
