## Claim Statement
Lévi defines the Great Work primarily as an inward, spiritual operation — "the creation of man by himself...the perfect emancipation of his will" — that is nonetheless mutually dependent on a material operation of metallic transmutation, both governed by the doctrine of the Emerald Tablet of Hermes, and symbolized in the Tarot by the Hanged Man.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The Great Work is, before all things, the creation of man by himself...assuring him universal dominion over Azoth and the domain of Magnesia...This Agent...determines the forms of modifiable substance, and we can really arrive by means of it at metallic transmutation and the Universal Medicine. This is not a hypothesis; it is a scientific fact already established." Lévi cites Nicholas Flamel and Raymond Lully as having "indubitably distributed immense riches," while "Agrippa never proceeded beyond the first part of the Great Work, and he perished in the attempt."

He quotes the Emerald Tablet: "Thou shalt separate the earth from the fire, the subtle from the gross, gently, with great industry...Thus was the world created," and interprets the "first operation, which is wholly inward" as liberation of the soul via "Philosophical Salt" (wisdom), "Mercury" (skill), and "Sulphur" (will/energy). The reversed word ART yields "T...triad, theory and travail; R, realization; A, adaptation."

On the Tarot symbol: the Hanged Man, bound and suspended by one foot with legs crossed and head/elbows forming a triangle, signifies "the end and perfection of the Great Work," and is identified as "the adept, bound by his engagements and spiritualized," also compared to Prometheus and, "vulgarly," to Judas.

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi frames alchemical writers (Bernard Trevisan, Basil Valentine, Mary the Egyptian) as allegorists whose works must be read via the "one dogma": "That which is above is proportional to that which is below, and reciprocally."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnotes challenge the historicity of the Flamel legend and dispute Lévi's specific Tarot-letter correspondence for the Hanged Man, calling the "intermediary TAU" claim "nonsense in Hebrew," though this is editorial commentary rather than an internal contradiction in Lévi's text.
