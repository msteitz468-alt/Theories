## Claim Statement
The Abbot Trithemius (teacher of Cornelius Agrippa in Magic), in his *Steganography*, explains that to evoke a spirit is to enter into that spirit's "dominant thought," and by raising oneself morally higher along the same line, one draws the spirit along and it serves the operator. To conjure (from *cum jurare*, "to swear together") is instead to oppose the resistance of a current and a chain to an isolated spirit by making a common act of faith; the operator does this in the name of a circle or association rather than individually, symbolized by the hieroglyphic circle drawn around the operating Magus, from which he must not step without losing all his power.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The Abbot Trithemius, who in Magic was the master of Cornelius Agrippa, explains, in his Steganography, the secret of Conjurations and Evocations after a very natural and philosophical manner... He tells us that to evoke a spirit is to enter into the dominant thought of that spirit, and if we raise ourselves morally higher along the same line, we shall draw the spirit away with us, and it will serve us. To conjure is to oppose the resistance of a current and a chain to an isolated spirit—cum jurare, to swear together, that is, to make a common act of faith. The greater the strength and enthusiasm of this faith, the more efficacious is the conjuration."

"We may act individually when evoking a spirit, but to conjure we must speak in the name of a circle or an association: this is the significance of the hieroglyphical circle inscribed about the Magus who is operating, and out of which he must not pass unless he wishes at the same moment to be stripped of all his power."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi introduces this distinction as the basis for treating the real possibility of evocation and conjuration as demonstrable "in virtue of the Great Magical Dogma of the hierarchy and of universal analogy," though he concedes the "phenomenal reality" of results is "a matter of experience" rather than proof. He illustrates the danger of unchecked evocation with the cautionary case of Schroepffer, "the famous illumine of Leipzig," who was overwhelmed by his own hallucinations and killed himself.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnote is sharply critical: "The answer of Lévi is worthless, failing a canon of criticism as to what constitutes 'evident impossibility'. The possibility of conjuring spirits raises a question of fact... and Lévi offers no evidence that they have [been conjured]. His own evocation of Apollonius is at best only evidence of auto-hallucination."
