## Claim Statement
Lévi explains werewolf legends as the sidereal body of a sleeping man whose "savage and sanguinary instincts are typified by the wolf" wandering abroad and becoming visible to impressionable witnesses, with wounds inflicted on the visible werewolf-form actually transferring, by "odic and sympathetic congestion," to the sleeping physical body.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Lévi first establishes three evidentiary premises: "(I) That no person has ever been killed by a werewolf, except by suffocation, without effusion of blood and without wounds; (2) That werewolves, though tracked, pursued and even maimed, have never been killed on the spot; (3) That persons suspected of these transformations have always been found at home...more or less broken up, sometimes dying, but invariably in their natural form."

He parallels this with bilocation claims — "the visible and real presence of P. Alphonsus Ligouri beside the dying pope, whilst the same personage was simultaneously seen at home," and the "simultaneous presence of the missionary Francis Xavier in several places at once" — attributing both to the sidereal body's ability to travel: "this body frequently remains awake while the latter sleeps, and passes in thought through all space." He cites Swedenborg as having "frequently beheld spirits in the shape of various animals" in his "somnambulistic intuitions."

"Let us now make bold to say that a werewolf is nothing else but the sidereal body of a man...who...is sleeping painfully in his bed and dreams that he is a wolf indeed... The hurts inflicted on the werewolf do actually wound the sleeping person by an odic and sympathetic congestion of the Astral Light."

Lévi extends the same mechanism to maternal imagination and gestation, citing "a woman who had been present at the execution of a man who was broken on the wheel" who "gave birth to a child with all its limbs shattered," and to the possessions of the Ursuline nuns of Loudun (concerning Urban Grandier) and of Louviers, and the case of Gaufridi and Magdalen de la Palud, all explained as hysterical/nervous transmission via the Astral Light rather than literal diabolic action.

## Contexts Within the Source
This chapter extends the sidereal-body doctrine of the preceding "Necromancy" chapter to living persons, and sets up the discussion of bewitchment and possession in Chapters XV–XVI.
