## Claim Statement
Lévi distinguishes his "Transcendental Magic" sharply from goëtic (demon-evoking) and necromantic magic. He presents his system instead as "at once an absolute science and religion," intended not to destroy existing forms of worship but to regenerate and direct them by reconstituting a circle of initiates who guide "the blind masses." He anticipates a future universal Church uniting Greek Orthodoxy, Roman Catholicism, and a transfigured Buddhism.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Hereunto therefore we have made it plain, as we believe, that our Magic is opposed to the goëtic and necromantic kinds. It is at once an absolute science and religion, which should not indeed destroy and absorb all opinions and all forms of worship, but should regenerate and direct them by reconstituting the circle of initiates, and thus providing the blind masses with wise and clear-seeing leaders."

"This religion has existed always in the world, and under many names has been ever the one and ruling religion. It has now among the nations of the earth three apparently hostile forms, which are destined, however, to unite before long for the constitution of a universal Church. I refer to the Greek orthodoxy, Roman Catholicism and a final transfiguration of the religion of Buddha."

## Contexts Within the Source
This statement caps Chapter I's discussion of the operator's religious duties, following Lévi's instruction that the Magus "may and should fulfil the duties and practise the rites of the cultus to which we belong," and identifying "the most magical" form of worship as that which most realizes the miraculous.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnotes note an underlying tension in Lévi's self-description as Catholic: he quotes Lévi elsewhere claiming to be "more catholic than the pope and more protestant than Luther," while also affirming elsewhere that "outside the Ship of Peter there is only the abyss" — a set of statements Waite presents as difficult to reconcile.
