## Claim Statement
Lévi defines miracles as "the natural effects of exceptional causes" rather than effects without causes, and argues that occult therapeutics work through the physician's will and the patient's faith rather than the material substances dispensed: "It is the knowledge and self-reliance of the physician which constitute the virtue of his prescriptions." He gives insufflation (warm breath attracting/positive, cold breath repelling/negative) and magnetic passes as the operative techniques, illustrated by anecdotes of unlicensed folk-healers (an old merchant curing ~10,000 patients with "oil, insufflations and prayers"; a "Sister Jane-Frances" whose identical elixir and plaster cured all ailments regardless; a gardener whose cures ceased the moment his brother's ridicule destroyed his self-belief) and closes by tracing Extreme Unction to "the pure and simple practice of the Master's Traditional Medicine."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"We have defined miracles as the natural effects of exceptional causes... a single miracle of this class [an effect without a cause] would destroy the universal harmony and reduce the universe to chaos."

"It is the knowledge and self-reliance of the physician which constitute the virtue of his prescriptions, whence thaumaturgy is the only real and efficacious remedy... The water of homoeopathists is truly a magnetized and enchanted water, which works by means of faith."

"Insufflation is one of the most important practices of occult medicine, because it is a perfect sign of the transmission of life... The warm breathing corresponds to positive and the cold breathing to negative electricity... Warm and prolonged insufflation recruits the circulation of the blood, cures rheumatic and gouty pains... Cold insufflation soothes pains occasioned by congestions and fluidic accumulations."

On the merchant-healer: "His sole specifics were oil, insufflations and prayers. The institution of a law-suit against him for the illegal exercise of medicine established in public knowledge that ten thousand cures had been attributed to him in the space of about five years."

On Sister Jane-Frances: "The remedy being the same in all diseases, it would appear needless for her to be acquainted with the cases of her patients, but she listened to them invariably with great attention and only dispensed her specific after learning the nature of the complaint. There was the magical secret. The direction of the intention imparted its special virtue to the remedy, which was insignificant in itself."

On the gardener: "he had, however, a sceptical brother who derided the sorcerer, and the poor gardener, overwhelmed by the sarcasms of this infidel, began to doubt himself, whereupon all the miracles ceased, the sufferers lost confidence and the thaumaturge, slandered and despairing, died mad."

On Extreme Unction: "What we term Extreme Unction was the pure and simple practice of the Master's Traditional Medicine, both for the early Christians and in the mind of the apostle Saint James... This divine therapeutic science was lost gradually, and Extreme Unction came to be regarded as a religious formality."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi explicitly endorses "charlatanism" as a legitimate instrument when it inspires confidence: "What is vulgarly called charlatanism is a great means of real success in medicine, assuming that it is sufficiently skilful to inspire great confidence... In medicine, above all, it is faith which saves."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnote states that Lévi's own summary elsewhere ("Magnetic passes serve only to direct the will of the operator... They are signs and nothing else, expressing and not performing the act of will") "stultifies" the chapter's main thesis that the fingers actively diffuse the Astral Light. Waite also corrects Lévi on Extreme Unction's doctrinal status: "In the mind of the Holy Latin Church, Extreme Unction is so little a formality... that it is the essential safeguard of the departing soul."
