## Claim Statement
Lévi closes the Doctrine by declaring analogy "the final word of science and the first word of faith" and "the sole possible mediator between finite and infinite" — the single principle underlying the Kabalah, alchemy/metallic transmutation, astrology/occult medicine, and magic alike — and states this Universal Arcanum is represented in the Tarot by a naked girl with a magnetic rod in each hand, analogous to the Cherub of Ezekiel and the Indian Adda-Nari.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Analogy is the final word of science and the first word of faith. Harmony consists in equilibrium, and equilibrium subsists by the analogy of contraries... Analogy is the sole possible mediator between finite and infinite." "Analogy yields all forces of Nature to the Magus; analogy is the quintessence of the Philosophical Stone, the secret of perpetual motion, the quadrature of the circle, the Temple resting on the two pillars JAKIN and BOAZ, the key of the Great Arcanum, the root of the Tree of Life, the science of good and evil."

"Metallic transmutation takes place spiritually and materially by the positive key of analogies. Occult medicine is simply the exercise of the will applied to the very source of life, to that Astral Light." The final Tarot card is described: "a naked girl, who touches the earth only by one foot, has a magnetic rod in each hand, and seems to be running in a crown held up by an angel, an eagle, a bull and a lion," analogous to "the cherub of Jekeskiel" (Ezekiel) and "the Indian symbol of Adda-Nari."

Lévi frames the withholding of full instruction as hierarchical necessity: "the elect of intelligence are always few on earth... absolute science, being an omnipotence, must be the exclusive possession of the most worthy... Give back initiation to priests and kings, and order will come forth anew." He closes by identifying reason itself with the Absolute and warning against "the conception of an absolute God outside or independent of reason," which he calls "the idol of Black Magic and the phantom of the fiend."

## Contexts Within the Source
This chapter closes Part I ("Doctrine") of the book, immediately preceding the transition to "The Ritual of Transcendental Magic," and explicitly recapitulates themes from every earlier chapter (Kabalah, Great Work, Astrology, Divination) under the single rubric of analogy.
