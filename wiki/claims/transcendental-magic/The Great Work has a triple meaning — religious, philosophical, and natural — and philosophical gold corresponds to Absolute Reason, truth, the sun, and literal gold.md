## Claim Statement
The dream of alchemists — to be ever rich, always young, and to die never, by changing base metals into gold and possessing the Universal Medicine and Elixir of Life — rests on secrets that "have a triple meaning: they are religious, philosophical and natural." Philosophical gold is, in religion, the Absolute and Supreme Reason; in philosophy, truth; in visible nature, the sun; and in the subterranean and mineral world, literal gold. The search for the Great Work is therefore called the Search for the Absolute, and the operation itself "the operation of the sun."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"To be ever rich, to be always young and to die never: such, from all time, has been the dream of alchemists. To change lead, mercury, and the other metals into gold, to possess the Universal Medicine and the Elixir of Life — such is the problem which must be solved to accomplish this desire and to realize this dream. Like all magical mysteries, the secrets of the Great Work have a triple meaning: they are religious, philosophical and natural. Philosophical gold in religion is the Absolute and Supreme Reason; in philosophy, it is truth; in visible nature, it is the sun: in the subterranean and mineral world, it is the purest and most perfect gold. Hence the search after the Great Work is called the Search for the Absolute, and this work itself is termed the operation of the sun."

"The Universal Medicine is, for the soul, supreme reason and absolute justice; for the mind, it is mathematical and practical truth; for the body, it is the quintessence, which is a combination of gold and light."

"All masters of science recognize that it is impossible to achieve material results until we have found the plenary analogies of the Universal Medicine and the Philosophical Stone in the two superior degrees. Then, it is affirmed, is the labour simple, light and inexpensive: otherwise, it consumes to no purpose the life and fortune of the bellows-blower."

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi presents this triple analogy as the interpretive key that separates true adepts (Paracelsus, Nicholas Flamel, Raymund Lully) from mere metallurgists (Agrippa, Etteilla, Louis Cambriel), asserting that hermetic art is "at one and the same time, a religion, a philosophy and a natural science."
