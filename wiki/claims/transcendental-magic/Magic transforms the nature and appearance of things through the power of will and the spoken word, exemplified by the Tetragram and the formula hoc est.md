## Claim Statement
Magic "does change the nature of things, or rather modifies their appearances at pleasure, according to the strength of the operator's will." The spoken word creates its own form: when one held infallible confers a name on a thing, that thing is really transformed into the substance signified. The Tetragram, "the supreme word of Magic," signifies "It is that which it shall be," and the Christian "hoc est" (this is) of the sacrifice is an application of this same principle, producing transubstantiation. Invisibility and the Ring of Gyges are likewise explained not by physical concealment but by "fascination" — the magnetic paralysis of onlookers' attention so that light reaches the eye without impressing "the eye of the soul."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Magic does change the nature of things, or rather modifies their appearances at pleasure, according to the strength of the operator's will and the fascination of ambitious adepts. The spoken word creates its form, and when a person held infallible confers a name upon a given thing, the latter is really transformed into the substance signified by the name."

"The true Ring of Gyges is the will; it is also the wand of transformations, and by its precise and strong formulation it creates the magical word. The omnipotent terms of enchantments are those which express this creative power of forms. The Tetragram, which is the supreme word of Magic, signifies: 'It is that which it shall be,'... The hoc est of the Christian sacrifice is a translation and application of the Tetragram: hence this simple utterance operates the most complete, most invisible, most incredible and most clearly affirmed of all transformations."

"Fascination is accomplished by magnetism. The Magus inwardly forbids a whole assembly to see him, and it does not see him.... The whole secret of invisibility consists therefore in a power which is capable of definition — being that of distracting or paralysing attention, so that light reaches the visual organ without impressing the eye of the soul."

Lévi illustrates with the legend of St Elizabeth of Hungary's loaves turning to roses, a saint commanding a fowl to become a fish, and St Spiridion of Tremithonte transforming salt bacon into a penitential feast, plus his account of a priest evading Revolutionary pursuers by feigning preoccupation rather than fleeing.

## Contexts Within the Source
Lévi links the doctrine to metempsychosis (reinterpreted as the astral body assuming animal "sympathetic impressions" rather than literal transmigration) and to Nebuchadnezzar's transformation in the Book of Daniel, treated as "magical allegory... mistaken for an actual history."
