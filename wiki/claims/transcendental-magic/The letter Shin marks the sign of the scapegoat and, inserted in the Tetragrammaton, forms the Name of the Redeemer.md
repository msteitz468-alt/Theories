## Claim Statement
In evocation, the triad is treated as the "foundation of magical doctrine," symbolized by a triangle within the grand circle whose apex is oriented toward heaven or the abyss depending on the spirit sought. The Hebrew letter Shin, commonly traced on kabbalistic pantacles associated with the fulfilment of desire, is also "the sign of the scapegoat in mystic Kabalah." Saint-Martin observed that when inserted into the Incommunicable Tetragram (YHVH), it forms the Name of the Redeemer (Yeshua/YHShVH). The medieval mystagogues represented this same idea in their nocturnal assemblies through the symbolic goat carrying a lighted torch between its horns.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The triad, being the foundation of magical doctrine must be necessarily observed in evocations; for it is the symbolical number of realization and effect. The letter [Shin] is commonly traced upon kabalistic pantacles which have the fulfilment of a desire for their object. It is also the sign of the scapegoat in mystic Kabalah, and Saint-Martin observes that inserted in the Incommunicable Tetragram it forms the Name of the Redeemer... It is this which the mystagogues of the Middle Ages represented in their nocturnal assemblies by the exhibition of a symbolical goat, carrying a lighted torch between its two horns."

"Within the grand circle of evocations a triangle was usually traced, and the side towards which the upper point should be directed was a matter for careful observation. If the spirit were supposed to be from heaven, the operator placed himself at the top, and set the altar of fumigations at the bottom; but if the spirit came from the abyss this method was reversed."

Lévi adds that "the Gnostic Agapae and pagan priapic orgies which followed in its honour sufficiently revealed the moral consequence which the adepts drew from the exhibition," promising fuller treatment "in the fifteenth chapter" on the Sabbath and Black Magic.

## Contexts Within the Source
This passage links the triangle-of-evocation geometry directly to the goat symbolism that Lévi elsewhere (Chapter VI) associates with the Baphomet of the Templars, treating the goat as "Nature doomed to anathema but ransomed by the sign of light."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Waite's footnote disputes the historicity of Lévi's Kabbalistic sourcing directly: "That is to say, in Éliphas Lévi's imagined or manufactured version, for neither in true and original Kabalism, nor in the commentaries of late successors like Isaac de Loria, is there any trace or notion of the so-called Templar Baphomet." Waite instead cites the Zohar's actual treatment of the scapegoat (Part II fol. 33a; Part III fol. 28a, 63a) as concerning a sacrificial goat sent to Satan and Israel's confession of sins, unconnected to any Baphomet figure.
