## Claim Statement
Lévi identifies the alchemists' Philosophical Stone with the "true Salt of the Philosophers" (a component of Azoth) and, allegorically, with "supreme and immovable reason" itself — the reconciliation of faith (the unknown, believed) and science (the known) — symbolized by the black stone Elagabalus, the cubic Stone, and the twin Pillars of Solomon's Porch, Jakin and Boaz.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The ancients adored the Sun under the figure of a black stone, which they named Elagabalus, or Heliogabalus... This Stone, say the masters in Alchemy, is the true Salt of the Philosophers, which is the third ingredient in the composition of AZOTH." Their Salt "is represented under the figure of a cubic stone, as may be seen in the TWELVE KEYS of Basil Valentine."

"It is the foundation of absolute philosophy, it is supreme and immovable reason... we must separate our certitudes from our beliefs... the essence of the things of faith is the unknown and the indefinite, while it is quite the reverse with the things of science... the Philosophical Stone is the true certitude which human prudence assures to conscientious researches and modest doubt."

On the Pillars: "Such is the significance of the two Pillars of Solomon's Porch, one named Jakin and the other BOAZ, one white and the other black. They are distinct and separate, they are even contrary in appearance, but if blind force sought to join them by bringing them close to one another, the roof of the temple would collapse." Lévi links this to political theory: "the spiritual power is weakened whensoever it attempts to usurp the temporal, while the temporal power becomes the victim of its encroachments on the spiritual. Gregory VII ruined the Papacy."

He describes the Stone as "square in every sense, like the heavenly Jerusalem of St John," its facets inscribed with the Tetragram, ADAM, HEVA, AZOT, and INRI, and notes the AZOTH figure (from the Sieur de Nuisement) is "occasionally given" "the symbolic head of the goat of Mendes," making it "the Baphomet of the Templars and the Word of the Gnostics."

## Contexts Within the Source
Builds directly on the Great Work doctrine of Chapter XII, treating the Stone as the necessary prerequisite ("before even dreaming of the metallic work") to any material transmutation.
