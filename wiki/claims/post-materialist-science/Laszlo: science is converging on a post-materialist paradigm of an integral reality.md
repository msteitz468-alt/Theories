## Claim Statement

Laszlo contends that frontier science across multiple disciplines — quantum physics, quantum biology, consciousness research, parapsychology, and NDE studies — is accumulating anomalous evidence that cannot be explained within the classical materialist paradigm. He argues that these findings, taken together, point toward an emerging post-materialist paradigm in which reality is understood as integral, in-formed, and nonlocally interconnected, and in which the Akashic field provides the unifying explanatory framework. In Laszlo's view, this convergence is not yet mainstream but is inevitable: "the old models are not adequate for what we see and experience and for what science is demonstrating."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Laszlo states the paradigm-shift thesis directly in the synthesis: "gone forever, it seems, are the conventional views that identify consciousness solely with the workings of the brain, and which confine the displays of consciousness to the here and now. Why? The reason is, quite simply, that these conventional views cannot account for the Akashic phenomenon, and other ways in which consciousness is manifest in the world. The old models are not adequate for what we see and experience and for what science is demonstrating. That's why the old models may be superseded by the hypothesis described in this book" (lines 5723–5724).

He proposes a specifically scientific basis for the new paradigm: "work at the frontiers of quantum physics, quantum biology, and quantum brain research shows that the brain is physically capable of giving rise to experiences based on information that comes from the external world without having been conveyed by the body's exteroceptive senses … The human brain … is not merely a classical biochemical system. It is also, and most remarkably, a 'macroscopic quantum system' … This finding throws serious doubt on the classical tenet that every extra- or non-sensory experience must be pure fantasy" (lines 5793–5801).

Stanislav Grof, whose contribution Laszlo frames as paradigmatic evidence, characterises the situation as follows: "various disciplines of modern science have amassed an extraordinary array of observations, which cannot be accounted for or adequately explained in terms of the materialistic worldview. These 'anomalous phenomena' came from a wide range of fields from astrophysics, quantum-relativistic physics, and chemistry to biology, anthropology, thanatology, parapsychology, and psychology" (line 4788). Grof credits Laszlo with providing "an elegant interdisciplinary solution for the anomalies plaguing modern science" through his connectivity hypothesis and Akashic field concept (line 4796–4800).

Van Lommel's NDE research is presented as another pillar: his prospective study of 344 cardiac arrest patients, published in *The Lancet*, found that "patients experienced all these NDE elements during the period of their cardiac arrest, while there was a total cessation of blood supply to their brain," leading to "the inevitable conclusion" that current neuroscience cannot explain the data (line 4697). He quotes Bruce Greyson's view that "the paradoxical occurrence of a heightened, lucid awareness and logical thought processes during a period of impaired cerebral perfusion raises particular perplexing questions for our current understanding of consciousness and its relation to brain function. A clear sensorium and complex perceptual processes during a period of apparent clinical death challenge the concept that consciousness is localized exclusively in the brain" (line 4705).

Marilyn Schlitz, calling for "a new paradigm that embraces our wholeness," frames the convergence as an educational and cultural necessity: "We are alive in a time of enormous complexity … Are there insights that come when we bridge inner and outer ways of knowing that may help us navigate twenty-first-century life and times? For me the answer is yes" (line 4406). She argues that "together we can build a new paradigm that embraces our wholeness, not just our parts" (line 4458). Dossey, addressing healing research, writes that "researchers are currently exploring hypotheses from several areas of science that are cordial to the remote effects of prayer and intentionality. As a theoretical framework gradually emerges, spirituality and the remote effects of healing will begin to seem less foreign" (line 5705).

Laszlo's postscript reaffirms his confidence in the direction: "ever more scientists and creative minds are having Akashic experiences. The enterprise of building an explicit and testable concept of the fundamental nature of reality will go on; it's 'work in progress'" (line 6091). He identifies his Akashic-field hypothesis as the pivot: "The idea that interconnection among open systems in nature is universal and physically real was another basic intuition … Thanks to this realization, I have never doubted that we humans … communicate with each other and with other complex systems in ways that extend beyond the ordinary bounds of space and time. It has not surprised me that such communication has been discovered in discipline after discipline, first of all in quantum physics, cosmology, biology, and consciousness research" (line 6075).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim situates the entire book within a history-of-science narrative. Laszlo positions the Akashic field hypothesis not as speculative metaphysics but as the next step in a sequence of paradigm shifts (an implicit Kuhnian frame). Part Three of the book (research chapters by Schlitz, van Lommel, Grof, and Dossey) is structured to supply empirical anchoring for the claim. The claim connects to [[Laszlo: the universe is an in-formed, holographic, interconnected whole]] (the proposed replacement paradigm) and to [[Laszlo: "akashic experiences" are evidence of an interconnected in-formed reality]] (the experiential evidence base). Together the three claims form Laszlo's complete argument in the book. See also [[The Akashic field (Laszlo)]] and [[Post-materialist science]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Laszlo is aware that "for the modern mind a credible explanation is a scientific explanation" and that, without one, the Akashic experience will continue to be dismissed (line 5779). He acknowledges that his scientific underpinning — quantum effects in warm biological tissue, quantum holography in the brain, nonlocal consciousness persisting after death — remains at the frontier and is not settled: "the final word has not been spoken" on key pieces of the physics (line 5825), and after-death communication remains "a major challenge for science" that calls for "sustained research" before it can be considered explained (line 5999). Schlitz's account of divergent experimenter effects in DMILS experiments (significant results in her hands, null results in Wiseman's) illustrates unresolved methodological problems for the research programme Laszlo is championing (lines 4326–4338). Laszlo himself notes that the twenty-one testimonies are too small a sample for rigorous social-science evaluation (line 5761).
