## Claim Statement

[[Rupert Sheldrake]] proposes that all self-organizing systems — crystals, proteins, cells, organisms, minds — inherit their characteristic forms and behaviors not solely through genes or fixed mathematical laws, but through a non-local resonance with all similar past systems. He calls this process morphic resonance. On this view, nature possesses an inherent, cumulative collective memory: each individual both draws upon and contributes to the habits of its kind, and those habits grow stronger the more often they are repeated anywhere in the universe. This reconceives biological inheritance, instinct, learning, and even individual memory as resonant phenomena rather than purely material storage processes.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sheldrake summarizes the core principles of morphic resonance as follows: "Morphic fields are shaped by morphic resonance from all similar past systems, and thus contain a cumulative collective memory. Morphic resonance depends on similarity, and is not attenuated by distance in space or time. Morphic fields are local, within and around the systems they organize, but morphic resonance is non-local" (lines 1456–1457). He adds that "Morphic resonance involves a transfer of form or in-form-ation rather than a transfer of energy" (line 1458).

The hypothesis predicts observable consequences for crystallization. When chemists synthesize a new compound, "it might be difficult to obtain crystals of this compound because a morphic field for this crystal-form does not yet exist … The second time the compound crystallizes, there will be an influence from the first crystals by morphic resonance all over the world. The third time, there will be an influence from the first and the second crystals, and so on. This influence builds up cumulatively" (line 1477). Historical pharmaceutical cases — such as the ritonavir polymorph crisis — are cited as circumstantial evidence that crystallization habits propagate globally in ways that standard chemistry cannot fully explain (lines 1493–1495).

For biological inheritance, Sheldrake argues that genes are grossly overrated: "DNA molecules are molecules. They are not 'determinants' of particular structures … They merely code for the sequences of amino acids in protein molecules" (line 2237). Morphogenetic fields supply what genes cannot: "Morphic resonance may underlie the inheritance of form, and behavior. This resonance is physical, but not material. Similarly, morphic resonance may play a major role in cultural inheritance" (line 2463). He illustrates this with a TV-set analogy: "Genetic mutations may affect an animal's form and behavior, but this does not prove that form and behavior are programmed in the genes. They are inherited by morphic resonance, an invisible influence on the organism coming from outside it, just as TV sets are resonantly tuned to transmissions that originate elsewhere" (line 2384).

For memory specifically, Sheldrake contends that more than a century of neuroscience has failed to locate engrams: "More than a century of intensive, well-funded research has failed to pin down memory traces in brains. There may be a very simple reason for this: the hypothetical traces do not exist … Instead, memories may depend on morphic resonance from an organism's own past. The brain may be more like a television set than a hard-drive recorder" (line 2584–2585). He cites Karl Lashley's famous conclusion that learned habits survived massive surgical removal of cortical tissue, and reports a cross-generational rat-maze study in which "Scottish and Australian rats took up more or less where the Harvard rats had left off, and their descendants learned even faster" — a result Sheldrake attributes to morphic resonance rather than genes (line 2749). He also points to the Flynn effect (rising IQ scores across decades) as consistent with morphic resonance making standardized tests progressively easier as more people complete them (line 2751–2767).

At the broadest level, Sheldrake argues that the book's key thesis is: "The idea that the 'laws of nature' are fixed while the universe evolves is an assumption left over from pre-evolutionary cosmology. The laws may themselves evolve or, rather, be more like habits … There may be an inherent memory in nature. All organisms may participate in a collective memory of their kind" (line 1564).

## Contexts Within the Source

Morphic resonance is the unifying hypothesis running through [[Science Set Free (Sheldrake)]]. It is introduced in the chapter on laws versus habits (Chapter 3, around lines 1450–1564), developed as a biological inheritance mechanism in Chapter 6 (lines 2369–2465), and applied to individual and collective memory in Chapter 7 (lines 2497–2808). It also connects to this book's broader argument that materialism is an unexamined belief system — see [[Sheldrake: materialism is a belief system, not a proven fact]] — and to the thesis that minds extend beyond the skull — see [[Sheldrake: minds are extended beyond the brain (telepathy and the sense of being stared at)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sheldrake acknowledges that the most obvious conventional explanation for phenomena like the global spread of new crystal forms is contamination by microscopic "seed" particles carried on air currents or by travelling scientists: "One favorite story in the folklore of chemistry is that migrant scientists carry these seeds around the world from lab to lab. Some chemists are said to have beards that 'harbour nuclei for almost any crystallization process'" (line 1499). He concedes these alternatives are plausible but argues they are untested and that morphic resonance makes different, testable predictions (lines 1502–1503).

For biological inheritance, the epigenetic inheritance of acquired characteristics is admitted as real but distinguished from morphic resonance: "Although epigenetic inheritance breaks the taboo against the inheritance of acquired characteristics, it does not challenge the materialist assumption that heredity is material; it is another kind of material inheritance" (line 2363). Critics (implicit in his framing) would argue that missing heritability and protein-folding difficulties reflect only the current limits of computational power, not the need for a new field concept.

For memory, Sheldrake notes that brain damage does correlate with memory loss, but argues this does not prove storage: "The fact that injury and brain degeneration … lead to loss of memory does not prove that memories are stored in the damaged tissue. If I snipped a wire or removed some components from the sound circuits of your TV set, I could render it speechless, or aphasic. But this would not mean that all the sounds were stored in the damaged components" (line 2586). Mainstream neuroscience remains committed to synaptic and connectome-level storage models and regards the lack of a discovered engram as a problem of experimental resolution, not evidence against material storage.
