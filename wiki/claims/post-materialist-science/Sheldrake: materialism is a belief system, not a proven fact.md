## Claim Statement

[[Rupert Sheldrake]] argues throughout [[Science Set Free (Sheldrake)]] that materialism — the philosophical position that matter is the only reality and that consciousness is merely a product of brain activity — is not an established empirical finding but an unexamined ideological commitment. He contends that materialists have never produced "extraordinary evidence" for their extraordinary claim, that rival materialist theories of consciousness (eliminativism, epiphenomenalism, emergentism, cognitive functionalism) each fail and remain contested among philosophers, and that materialism cannot in principle explain why subjective experience exists at all. He further argues that in practice the materialist worldview functions as a creed that distorts science education, medical research funding, and the treatment of psychic phenomena.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sheldrake's sharpest formulation of his central charge appears in Chapter 4 ("Is Matter Unconscious?"): "The central doctrine of materialism is that matter is the only reality. Therefore consciousness ought not to exist. Materialism's biggest problem is that consciousness does exist" (line 1575). He documents the philosophical impasse by quoting the materialist philosopher John Searle's own diagnosis: "A philosopher advances a materialist theory of the mind … He then encounters difficulties … the theory in question has left out some essential feature of the mind … And this leads to ever more frenzied attempts to stick with the materialist thesis" (line 1622). He adds the testimony of philosopher Galen Strawson — himself a materialist — who called the denial of consciousness "the strangest thing that has ever happened in the whole history of human thought, not just the whole history of philosophy" (line 1628).

Sheldrake argues that the dogmatism is explicit in the positions of leading materialists. Daniel Dennett's rule — "dualism is to be avoided at all costs" — is quoted directly, and Sheldrake comments: "The dogmatism of Dennett's rule is not merely apparent: the rule is dogmatic … 'Materialism at all costs' demands the denial of the reality of our own minds and personal experiences" (lines 1581–1585). Francis Crick admitted that his "Astonishing Hypothesis" that "'you,' your joys and your sorrows, your memories and your ambitions … are in fact no more than the behavior of a vast assembly of nerve cells" was not proved, and even conceded "a dualist view might become more plausible" (lines 1587, 1632–1634).

On the relationship between materialism and science, Sheldrake contends in Chapter 11 ("Illusions of Objectivity") that "the materialist philosophy or 'the scientific worldview' is not a vision of undeniable, objective truth. It is a questionable belief system superseded by the development of the sciences themselves" (line 3738). He cites physicist Bernard d'Espagnat's observation that quantum theory made classical materialism untenable: "the principles of physics itself have undergone such an evolution that they cannot even be formulated without referring … to the impressions — and thus to the minds — of the observers. As a consequence, materialism is bound to change" (line 3771).

The belief-system character of materialism also manifests, Sheldrake argues, in systematic closure to evidence. Describing his encounter with Richard Dawkins, who refused to look at experimental evidence on telepathy while on camera, Sheldrake writes: "He looked uneasy and said, 'I don't want to discuss evidence.' 'Why not?' I asked. He replied, 'There isn't time. It's too complicated. And that's not what this program is about'" (lines 3332–3336). He generalizes: "In no other field of scientific endeavor do otherwise intelligent people feel free to make public claims based on prejudice and ignorance. No one would denounce research in physical chemistry, say, while knowing nothing about the subject. Yet in relation to psychic phenomena, committed materialists feel free to disregard the evidence and behave irrationally and unscientifically while claiming to speak in the name of science and reason" (line 3346).

In Chapter 10 ("Is Mechanistic Medicine the Only Kind that Really Works?"), the consequences are traced concretely: "The mechanistic approach is at its best when dealing with mechanical aspects of the body … But it suffers from tunnel vision: all living organisms, including people, are physico-chemical machines, or 'lumbering robots.' Hence a materialist medical system confines its attention to the physical and chemical aspects of human beings … while ignoring anything that does not fit in" (line 3392). Placebo research reveals the theoretical inadequacy directly: "If materialism were an adequate foundation for medicine, placebo responses ought not to occur. The fact that they do occur shows that people's beliefs and hopes can have positive effects on their health and healing" (line 3527). And comparative effectiveness research is held back by ideology: "Decades of the materialist worldview has shaped the way that medicine is taught in medical schools, biased the funding of medical research, and shaped the policies of national health services and private insurance companies" (line 3646).

Finally, Sheldrake summarizes the scope of the problem in Chapter 11: scientists are not disembodied objective observers but social beings subject to peer pressure, funding pressures and paradigmatic assumptions: "The determination of the fundamental constants is a case in point. When the speed of light, c, apparently dropped by 20 kilometres per second from 1928 to 1945, laboratories all around the world reported measurements close to the consensus value … the scientists probably discarded measurements that didn't fit, and 'corrected' the remaining data until they converged on the expected value as a result of 'intellectual phase locking'" (line 3809).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim functions as the overarching meta-argument of [[Science Set Free (Sheldrake)]]. Every chapter can be read as a case study of materialism operating as dogma rather than hypothesis: fixed laws vs. evolutionary habits (Chapter 3), consciousness denied (Chapter 4), purposes excluded (Chapter 5), genes overrated (Chapter 6), memory traces never found (Chapter 7), minds locked in skulls (Chapter 8), psi dismissed without evidence review (Chapter 9), and placebo responses awkward for mechanism (Chapter 10). The claim is the philosophical scaffolding for the other two specific claims: [[Sheldrake: morphic resonance gives nature an inherent collective memory]] and [[Sheldrake: minds are extended beyond the brain (telepathy and the sense of being stared at)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sheldrake himself concedes that materialism's strongest argument is the failure of dualism to explain mind-body interaction: "The strongest argument in favor of materialism is the failure of dualism to explain how immaterial minds work and how they interact with brains" (line 1595). He does not defend classical substance dualism; his alternative — fields of mind extended in space and time, shaped by morphic resonance — is intended as a third path, but critics would note it inherits the burden of explanation that troubled dualism.

He also grants that modern medicine has been "amazingly successful" and that its successes — antibiotics, immunization, surgery — "were not specifically dependent on the mechanistic theory of life or the materialist worldview" (line 3432), which softens his critique of mechanistic medicine considerably. The most accurate form of his complaint is thus not that materialism is false across the board but that it has been applied as a totalizing creed in domains (consciousness, psychic phenomena, healing) where its explanatory power is genuinely weak.

Sheldrake's evidence that materialism functions as "belief system" rather than empirical conclusion is largely sociological and rhetorical (citing Dennett's rule, Dawkins's refusal to view data, consensus pressure on constants). Defenders of scientific materialism would argue that these are examples of individual scientist behavior, not properties of the philosophical position, and that the self-correcting mechanisms of science eventually address such biases.
