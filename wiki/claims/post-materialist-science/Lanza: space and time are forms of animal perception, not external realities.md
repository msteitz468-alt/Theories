## Claim Statement

[[Robert Lanza]] argues in [[Biocentrism (Lanza)]] that space and time are not external, self-existing containers within which the universe unfolds, but are rather cognitive tools — "forms of animal understanding" — generated by the nervous systems of living observers. Invoking Kant, Einstein's relativity, and quantum entanglement, Lanza maintains that the apparent solidity and independence of the spatiotemporal framework dissolves under scrutiny: both space and time vary with the observer's state of motion and perception, and quantum experiments show that entangled particles behave as if no separation in space or time exists at all. On Lanza's view, removing the living observer removes the very scaffolding that makes space and time coherent concepts.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Lanza states his thesis most economically in the Sixth and Seventh Principles of Biocentrism, which he articulates across multiple chapters in the assigned range. The Sixth Principle reads: "Time does not have a real existence outside of animal-sense perception. It is the process by which we perceive changes in the universe." The Seventh Principle states: "Space, like time, is not an object or a thing. Space is another form of our animal understanding and does not have an independent reality. We carry space and time around with us like turtles with shells. Thus, there is no absolute self-existing matrix in which physical events occur independent of life."

The First Principle reinforces the point: "What we perceive as reality is a process that involves our consciousness. An 'external' reality, if it existed, would — by definition — have to exist in space. But this is meaningless, because space and time are not absolute realities but rather tools of the human and animal mind."

Lanza draws on Einstein's relativity to support this demotion. High-speed travel causes space to contract so dramatically that "the entire universe is only a few steps from end to end" and "illustrates that it has no inherent, let alone external, structure. It is, rather, an experiential commodity that goes with the flow and mutates under varying circumstances." He cites the aberration of starlight at near-lightspeed, where "the entire contents of the cosmos appear to hover in a single blindingly bright ball," as evidence that space has no bedrock form. From quantum theory he draws the EPR experiments since 1997, arguing they present "only two choices. Either the first particle communicates its situation far faster than the speed of light . . . or else there really is no separation between the pair at all." He treats this non-locality as "yet another layer to the scientific conclusion that space is illusory."

Lanza explicitly credits Kant: "Immanuel Kant, ahead of his time, said that 'we must rid ourselves of the notion that space and time are actual qualities in things in themselves . . . all bodies, together with the space in which they are, must be considered nothing but mere representations in us, and exist nowhere but in our thoughts.'" He also builds on Einstein's own caution that space "is what we measure with a measuring rod" — arguing the emphasis should fall on the "we," the living observer.

His conclusion, expressed in Appendix 2, is: "thus, space and time can be easily restored to their place as forms of animal-sense perception. They belong to us, not to the physical world."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim underpins the entire biocentric framework. Without it, the observer cannot be elevated to a constitutive role in reality. It also sets up [[Lanza: death is an illusion because consciousness is not bound to time]], since if time is only a feature of animal perception, it cannot enforce an absolute endpoint on consciousness. It likewise supports [[Lanza: the universe's fine-tuning is explained by the centrality of the observer]], because if space and time are observer-generated, then the "universe" cannot be said to pre-exist the observer who shapes it.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Lanza acknowledges the near-universal commonsense experience that space and time are real and external: "Most of us live without thinking abstractly about space. Like time, it's such an integral part of our lives that its examination is as unnatural as scrutinizing walking or breathing." He also notes that Einstein's relativity is widely interpreted as positing an objective, external space-time entity — and admits his biocentric re-reading is one of multiple compatible interpretations: "One must choose relativity over the compensatory [biocentric] alternatives as a matter of free choice" (quoting philosopher Lawrence Sklar). He concedes that thermodynamics' second law appears to require time (entropy demands directionality), though he argues this too can be reread as mere random motion rather than proof of time's independent existence. Finally, Lanza is candid that his framework "has its logical limits" and that language and logic are imperfect tools for grasping what lies outside familiar experience.
