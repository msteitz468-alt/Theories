## Claim Statement

Laszlo argues that the "theories of everything" (TOEs) being developed by theoretical physicists — string theory and its variants — are at best physical theories of everything: they unify the fields and forces of matter and energy but exclude life, mind, and consciousness entirely. A genuine TOE must be *integral*, encompassing physical things, living things, and "things of mind and consciousness" within a single coherent framework. Laszlo calls this an "I-TOE" (integral theory of everything). He further claims that such a theory is achievable through science — not through metaphysics alone — and that the key ingredient missing from physics-only TOEs is the recognition that "in-formation" is a third fundamental constituent of reality alongside matter and energy. This meta-thesis frames the entire book and distinguishes his project from both reductive physicalism and from non-scientific spirituality.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Laszlo states the core distinction in the Introduction: "In-formation, I claim, links all things in the universe, atoms as well as galaxies, organisms the same as minds. This discovery transforms the fragmented world-concept of the mainstream sciences into an integral, holistic worldview. It opens the way toward the elaboration of a theory that has been much discussed but until recently has not been truly achieved: an integral theory not just of one kind of things, but of all kinds — an integral theory of everything" (line 479).

He specifies what "integral" means in Chapter 1: "A theory that deserves this name must be a theory truly of everything — an integral theory of all the kinds of things we observe, experience, and encounter, whether they are physical things, living things, social and ecological things, or 'things' of mind and consciousness" (line 677).

On the insufficiency of physicists' TOEs: "It is clear … that even if the current efforts were to be crowned with success, success would not crown the creation of a genuine TOE. At the most, physicists would come up with a physical TOE — with a theory that is not a theory of every thing, only of every physical thing. A genuine TOE would include more than the mathematical formulas that give a unified expression to the phenomena studied in this branch of quantum physics. There is more to the universe than vibrating strings and related quantum events. Life, mind, culture, and consciousness are part of the world's reality, and a genuine theory of everything would take them into account as well" (line 753).

He acknowledges a prior attempt by Ken Wilber but finds it incomplete: "Ken Wilber, who wrote a book with the title A Theory of Everything, agrees: he speaks of the 'integral vision' conveyed by a genuine TOE. However, he does not offer such a theory; he mainly discusses what it would be like, describing it in reference to the evolution of culture and consciousness — and to his own theories. An actual, science-based integral theory of everything is yet to be created" (line 757).

On why a new, third fundamental factor is needed: "In order to account for the presence of a significant number of particles in the universe … and for the ongoing … evolution of the existing things, we need to recognize the presence of a factor that is neither matter nor energy … It is information — information as a real and effective factor setting the parameters of the universe at its birth, and thereafter governing the evolution of its basic elements into complex systems" (line 785). He grounds this in Bohm's formulation: "The great physicist David Bohm called it 'in-formation,' meaning a process that actually 'forms' the recipient. This is the concept we shall adopt here" (line 789).

On the ambition and scope of the project: "An integral theory of everything would bring us closer to understanding the real nature of all the things that exist and evolve in space and time, whether they are atoms or galaxies or mice and men. It gives us an encompassing and yet scientific view of ourselves and of the world; a view that we very much need in these times of accelerating change and mounting disorientation" (line 483).

He also situates the project within a broader cultural diagnosis: "At its cutting edge, the new cosmology discovers a world where the universe does not end in ruin, and the new physics, the new biology, and the new consciousness research recognize that life and mind are integral elements in the world, and not accidental by-products" (line 471).

On method — working from simple starting conditions upward rather than forcing consciousness into existing equations: "A genuine TOE can be created. Although it is beyond the string and superstring theories in the framework of which physicists attempt to formulate their own super-theory, it is well within the scope of science itself … It is simpler, and more sensible, to look for the basic laws and processes that give rise to these entities, and to their interrelations" (line 765).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the book's orienting meta-thesis. The Introduction announces it; Chapter 1 develops and defends it against the physicists' TOE programme. Every subsequent chapter is organised around supplying the evidence (the puzzles of coherence in Chapters 2–3) and then the solution (in-formation rooted in the quantum vacuum, developed in Chapters 4 and beyond) that would make such an I-TOE scientifically credible. The claim is thus not an isolated position but the architectural principle of the entire argument. ## Opposing Information Within the Source

Laszlo concedes that string theory is mathematically sophisticated and that physicists like Weinberg sincerely believe a purely physical account of reality is adequate and complete: "The physicist Steven Weinberg is adamant that the universe as a physical process is meaningless; the laws of physics offer no discernible purpose for human beings" (line 459). Laszlo does not dismiss the physics programme itself — he regards it as genuine progress — but argues that its scope is inherently limited. He also notes that even the physicists' own physical TOE remains unrealised: "At the time of writing, a string-theory-based TOE remains an ambition and a hope: nobody has come up with the super-equation … Indeed there are so many problems with it that ever more physicists claim that a fundamentally new concept may be needed" (line 741). His own I-TOE is presented as a "science fable" in the technical sense he defines: a reasoned, testable hypothesis that is not yet confirmed, and whose confirmation depends on the success of the in-formation / Akashic-field programme he develops in the remainder of the book.
