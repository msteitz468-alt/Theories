## Claim Statement

[[Dean Radin]] argues that human beings can sense future events through two distinct channels: consciously, through forced-choice precognition experiments in which participants guess upcoming targets at rates exceeding chance; and unconsciously, through physiological "presentiment," in which the autonomic nervous system responds to an emotionally charged stimulus *before* that stimulus is displayed. Radin contends that these two bodies of evidence converge to show that information from the future influences the present nervous system, in ways that defy any ordinary, forward-only causal account.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Radin describes a reaction-time paradigm developed by Holger Klintman at Lund University, Sweden, in which participants' *first* response in a color-naming task was faster when the subsequent color name would *match* the initial color patch — meaning the upcoming stimulus retroactively speeded an earlier response. After five successive double-blind experiments, the combined odds against chance were 500,000 to 1. Radin sums up: "after five successful experiments, each of which used a somewhat different design to provide conceptual replications of the same hypothesized effect, the combined results of the experiments resulted in overall odds against chance of 500,000 to 1."

Radin's own laboratory work at the University of Nevada, Las Vegas, examined what he termed "presentiment" — a subset of precognition involving unconscious physiological anticipation rather than conscious prediction. Participants viewed a random sequence of calm and emotionally arousing photographs while skin conductance, heart rate, and finger blood volume were recorded continuously. The key finding was that the body reacted *before* the target was displayed: "Before SD saw both types of pictures, her electrodermal activity began to rise, revealing that she was anticipating the subsequent target. What's surprising is that her electrodermal activity increased more if the future picture was going to be emotional. This difference is what we called a 'presentiment effect,' and it's a close analog in the autonomic nervous system to what Klintman found in reaction time: it appears that a person's 'future' experience can affect his or her nervous system in the present."

The combined presentiment results across twenty-four participants viewing 900 pictures showed that electrodermal activity, heart rate, and blood volume all pre-reacted to emotional (versus calm) targets before display, in the expected directions. Heart rate "began to rise before emotional pictures were seen, as though the participants were steeling themselves against seeing the shocking picture." An independent replication by Dick Bierman at the University of Amsterdam, using his own electrodermal hardware and sixteen participants, successfully reproduced the core effect: "Electrodermal activity was significantly higher before presentation of emotional pictures, as compared to the same activity before presentation of calm pictures."

Radin also notes that participants were almost entirely unaware that their physiology had pre-reacted: "When asked after the experiment if they were consciously aware of the upcoming pictures, nearly all participants said no, supporting the idea that presentiment is largely an unconscious process." He ties these findings to a broader meta-analytic database on forced-choice precognition and to Klintman's reaction-time series, concluding: "Klintman's reaction-time studies and the physiological presentiment experiments confirm the results of the forced-choice precognition meta-analysis. They suggest that under certain circumstances we can consciously or unconsciously respond to events in our future, events that we have no normal way of knowing."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim represents the culmination of Chapter 7, which surveys the precognition evidence arc from forced-choice card-guessing (meta-analytic work reviewed in earlier chapters) through Klintman's time-reversed interference paradigm to Radin's own presentiment experiments. Radin frames presentiment as narrower than full precognition — the body anticipates without conscious foreknowledge — and presents it as a converging line of evidence supporting [[Radin: mind directly influences matter (psychokinesis on random systems)]] (Chapter 8), which examines the complementary question of active mental influence rather than passive future sensing. The two chapters together form the empirical core of his case that mind and time interact in ways not accounted for by standard physicalism. Link to [[Post-materialist science]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Radin acknowledges that the presentiment findings are surprising even from a psi-friendly perspective, because no conscious psi perception is reported. He notes that the standard skeptical position attributes any precognition result to chance, selective reporting, or methodological artifact, but argues these are addressed by the experimental controls (built-in orienting-response validation, randomized target selection, blinding) and by successful independent replication. He does not claim to have identified a mechanism; the physiological level at which "future information" enters the nervous system remains unexplained. He also flags that the University of Iowa neuroscience study (published in *Science*) finding unconscious anticipatory electrodermal activity during a card-deck gambling task "might" reflect psi — but acknowledges this is speculative, since the conventional interpretation attributes the effect to implicit learning.
