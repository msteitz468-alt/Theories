## Claim Statement

Laszlo argues that a single class of anomaly — non-local coherence that is quasi-instant, space-and-time-transcending, and far stronger than any known energetic coupling can explain — appears across four otherwise unrelated domains: quantum physics (entanglement), cosmology (fine-tuning and large-scale structural uniformity), biology (the ultra-coherence of organisms and directed genomic mutation), and consciousness research (transpersonal and telesomatic effects). He contends that these "puzzles of coherence" cannot be resolved by the fragmented, domain-specific theories currently in use. Their simultaneous appearance across domains implies a common underlying factor: an active, physically effective form of information — "in-formation" — that connects all things. The presence and necessity of this connecting field is the core scientific argument that, in Laszlo's framing, sets the stage for the Akashic-field hypothesis.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Laszlo introduces the chapter's unifying theme: "We pursue our search for the I-TOE with a catalog of the findings that puzzle scientists today … These are puzzles of coherence. Intended is not the ordinary, garden variety of coherence, but the remarkable variety where the parts of the coherent system are so finely adjusted to each other that a change in any one of them introduces change in all the others. Moreover the changes propagate through the system quasi-instantly, and are enduring. It is as if the parts of the system are 'nonlocal' — not limited to just where they are, but are in some way everywhere throughout the system. In this chapter we examine how this strange form of coherence appears in the physical world, in the living world, and in the world of consciousness" (line 989).

**Quantum physics.** On entanglement and nonlocality: "quanta have matterlike properties … but none of the quanta are truly separate from one another, for — once having shared the same state — they remain interlinked no matter how far they may be from each other" (line 1001). On the EPR experiments: "an instantaneous effect propagates from A to B, conveying precise information on what is being measured. B 'knows' when A is measured, for what parameter, and with what result, for it assumes its own state accordingly. A nonlocal connection links A and B, notwithstanding the distance that separates them" (line 1113). On the scope of this nonlocality: "separation does not divide particles from each other … It is not even necessary that the particles should originate in the same quantum state. Experiments show that any two particles, be they electrons, neutrons, or photons, can originate at different points in space and in time; if they once come together within the same system of coordinates, that is enough for them to continue to act as part of the same quantum system even when they are separated" (line 1125).

**Cosmology.** Laszlo lists the cosmological puzzles — CP violation, missing mass, accelerating expansion, the horizon problem, coherence of cosmic ratios — and then focuses on fine-tuning: "The phenomenon cosmologists call the 'fine-tuning of the universal constants' is particularly vexing. The three dozen or more physical parameters of the universe are so precisely adjusted that together they create the highly improbable conditions under which life can emerge on Earth — and presumably on other planets as well — and evolve to progressively higher levels of complexity" (line 1261). He draws the implication directly: "The coherence of our universe tells us that all its stars and galaxies are connected in some way. And the astonishing fine-tuning of its physical constants suggests that at its birth the vacuum in which our universe emerged was not randomly structured" (line 1277). He characterises all of these as sharing a common element: "The deeper the new high-powered instruments probe the far reaches of the universe, the more mysteries they uncover. For the most part, these mysteries have a common element: they exhibit a staggering coherence throughout the reaches of space and time" (line 1175).

**Biology.** On the organism's inner coherence: "The living organism is extraordinarily coherent: all its parts are multidimensionally, dynamically, and almost instantly connected with all other parts. What happens to one cell or organ also happens in some way to all other cells and organs — a connection that recalls (and in fact suggests) the kind of 'entanglement' that characterizes the behavior of quanta in the microdomain" (line 1355). On the impossibility of chance: "the evidence is indirect but cogent, for in the absence of such correlation, the probability that complex organisms could have evolved on Earth in the 600 million years that have elapsed since the late Cambrian era would be negligibly small" (line 1405). Quoting Marco Bischof's synthesis: "'Quantum mechanics has established the primacy of the inseparable whole. For this reason … the basis of the new bio-physics must be the insight into the fundamental interconnectedness within the organism as well as between organisms, and that of the organism with the environment'" (line 1421).

**Consciousness.** On transpersonal connections: "the connections that bind 'my' consciousness to the consciousness of others, well known to traditional — so-called primitive, but in fact in many respects highly sophisticated — peoples, are rediscovered today in controlled experiments with thought and image transference, and the effect of the mind of one individual on the mind and body of another" (line 1429). On Grinberg-Zylberbaum's experiments: transferred EEG potentials "appeared consistently in about 25 percent of the cases … The results did not depend on spatial separation between senders and receivers — the transferred potentials appeared no matter how far or how near they were to each other" (line 1533). Summarising the implication of dowsing research: "Keen concluded that dowsable fields are created and maintained in an 'Information Field that pervades the universe.' The brain interacts with this field and perceives dowsable fields as holograms … an instance of nonlocal interaction between the brain and the field by different and even distant individuals" (line 1557).

**Synthesis: the common factor.** At the opening of Chapter 4, Laszlo draws together all four domains: "The surprising 'nonlocal' forms of coherence crop up in fields as diverse as quantum physics, cosmology, evolutionary biology, and consciousness research … it is clear that nonlocal coherence has important implications. It signals that there is not only matter and energy in the universe, but also a more subtle yet real element: an element that connects, and which produces the observed quasi-instant forms of coherence" (lines 1645–1649). He names the next step: "Identifying this connecting element could solve the puzzles at the forefront of scientific research and point the way toward a more fertile paradigm. We can take the first step toward this goal by affirming that information is present, and has a decisive role, in all principal domains of nature … it is 'in-formation' — the active, physically effective variety that 'forms' the recipient, whether it is a quantum, a galaxy, or a human being" (lines 1653–1654).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim constitutes the analytical spine of Part One. Chapter 3 supplies the empirical catalog; Chapter 4 opens with the synthesis that converts the catalog into a positive argument for an in-formation field. Laszlo frames the argument explicitly as preparation for the Akashic-field hypothesis: "We join David Bohm, Harold Puthoff, and other scientists in looking for its roots in the complex and as yet not fully understood quasi-infinite energy sea called quantum vacuum" (line 1657). The claim thus bridges the "puzzles" of Chapters 2–3 to the proposed solution developed in the remainder of Part One. See also [[Laszlo: an integral "theory of everything" must include consciousness, not just matter]] (the meta-thesis that frames why these puzzles matter), [[The Akashic field (Laszlo)]], and [[Post-materialist science]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Laszlo acknowledges that each individual puzzle has mainstream candidate explanations — inflation theory for the horizon problem, string-theoretic anthropic reasoning for fine-tuning, decoherence arguments for why quantum entanglement does not straightforwardly scale to biology or consciousness. He also concedes that his "in-formation" framing is at the stage of a "science fable" rather than a confirmed theory: "Initially, nobody knows for sure which of the seeds will grow and bear fruit. The field is in ferment, in a state of creative chaos" (line 965). The Darwinian framework he challenges is presented with its own defenders: he cites Richard Dawkins's "blind watchmaker" metaphor as the mainstream position his biological puzzles must displace (line 1401). Conservative physicists such as Bell and Clarke are cited as postulating nonlocality as a deeper reality, but Laszlo notes this remains speculative: "Independently of the truth of such speculations, it is clear that nonlocal coherence has important implications" (line 1649).
