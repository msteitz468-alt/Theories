## Claim Statement

Radin argues that the ganzfeld telepathy experiments represent the single strongest and most methodologically rigorous line of laboratory evidence for ESP. Across more than 2,500 sessions reported by researchers worldwide between 1974 and 1997, hit rates consistently exceed the 25 percent chance expectation and yield combined odds against chance exceeding a million billion to one. Radin contends that both the principal psi advocate and the principal skeptic involved in formal meta-analyses agreed that the results cannot be attributed to chance, selective reporting, or any identified design flaw — effectively forcing the question out of Stage 1 ("impossible") denial.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The ganzfeld procedure places a receiver in mild sensory deprivation — halved ping-pong balls over the eyes, white noise through headphones, reclined in a quiet room — while a sender in a separate, isolated room concentrates on a randomly selected target (photograph or video clip). The receiver speaks aloud any images or feelings that arise; afterward, the receiver is shown four possible targets and ranks them for correspondence. A "direct hit" is recorded only if the actual target is ranked first, giving a 25 percent chance baseline.

Radin traces the 1985 meta-analysis by Charles Honorton (the leading ganzfeld psi researcher) and the independent counter-analysis by skeptic Ray Hyman. Across the 28 of 42 available experiments that reported actual hit rates, "the results were even more astounding than Hyman and Honorton had expected: odds against chance of ten billion to one." The combined hit rate for the 762 sessions in the 25 studies using the standard 4-target design was 37 percent — 12 percentage points above chance. Radin notes that this effect size is "about ten times" larger than the aspirin effect on heart-attack prevention that was simultaneously celebrated as a medical breakthrough.

To address concerns that success might be concentrated in one or two labs, Honorton calculated outcomes separately for each of the ten contributing laboratories; "Significantly positive outcomes were reported by six of the ten labs, and the combined score across the ten laboratories still resulted in odds against chance of about a billion to one." When Honorton's own lab and the contested Sargent lab were removed entirely, "The resulting odds against chance were still ten thousand to one."

On the file-drawer question, the calculation showed that "423 file-drawer experiments" — a ratio of 15 unpublished failures per published study — would be required to nullify the results. Hyman himself agreed that this was implausible given the research community's size. Their joint 1986 communiqué recorded: "We agree that there is an overall significant effect in this data base that cannot reasonably be explained by selective reporting or multiple analysis."

Beginning in 1983, Honorton ran a fully computer-controlled "autoganzfeld" series specifically engineered to meet every methodological criterion that the joint communiqué had specified. Over 354 sessions, the hit rate was 34 percent (122 direct hits), producing odds against chance of 45,000 to 1 — essentially the same effect size as the earlier database. Hyman's response was that "Honorton's experiments have produced intriguing results. If independent laboratories can produce similar results … then parapsychology may indeed have finally captured its elusive quarry."

By early 1997, six independent research groups (at Edinburgh, Amsterdam, Cornell, the Rhine Research Center, Gothenburg, and Utrecht) had published replication attempts. While only the 1985 meta-analysis, the autoganzfeld, and the Edinburgh series individually crossed the 95 percent confidence threshold for significance, "each of the six replication studies (after the autoganzfeld) resulted in point estimates greater than chance." Combined across all 2,549 sessions ever reported, the overall hit rate was 33.2 percent — "unlikely with odds against chance beyond a million billion to one." Radin concludes: "We are fully justified in having very high confidence that people sometimes get small amounts of specific information from a distance without the use of the ordinary senses. Psi effects do occur in the ganzfeld."

Some of the qualitative receiver-target correspondences cited by Radin are striking. In one autoganzfeld session, the target was a video clip of horses galloping in a snowstorm; the receiver, describing mental imagery aloud, reported: "I keep going to the mountains. … It's snowing. … Spinning. Like on a carousel, horses. Horses on a carousel, a circus …" In another, the target was a collapsing suspension bridge from a 1940s newsreel; the receiver described "some thin, vertical object, bending to the left … almost like a ladder-like bridge over some kind of chasm that's waving in the wind … A bridge, a drawbridge over something … The bridge is lifting, both sides now … It's closing, it's coming down."

## Contexts Within the Source

This is presented in Chapter 5 (Telepathy) as the culmination of more than a century of telepathy research, beginning with the 1880s thought-transference studies of Barrett and Lodge, running through Rhine's ESP card tests, and the Maimonides dream-telepathy program (450 sessions, 63 percent hit rate, odds of 75 million to 1). The ganzfeld is positioned as methodologically superior to all prior designs because it is theoretically motivated (sensory reduction amplifies faint psi signals, in line with Henri Bergson's filter theory of consciousness), because proponents and skeptics jointly agreed on experimental standards before the key replication series was run, and because the autoganzfeld used computer controls and professional magicians to audit security. The ganzfeld results feed directly into the book's central claim — [[Radin: the accumulated scientific evidence shows psi is real]] — as its strongest single exhibit.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Radin's account records Hyman's sustained resistance even after conceding the statistical results. Hyman's consistent position — acknowledged by Radin — is that the absence of an identified flaw does not rule out an unidentified one: "it is impossible in principle to say that any particular experiment or experimental series is completely free from possible flaws." Radin notes the "randomization flaw" dispute between Hyman and Honorton in the 1985 meta-analysis: Hyman claimed to find a significant correlation between randomization deficiencies and positive outcomes; Honorton did not; independent commentators (including Harvard's Robert Rosenthal) sided with Honorton, finding "no significant relationships between the supposed flaws and the study outcomes." Radin also acknowledges that the ganzfeld hit rate (~33–37 percent) is modest in absolute terms — a 9–12 percentage point lift above chance — and that most individual sessions are non-hits, requiring meta-analytic combination to achieve high confidence. The theoretical mechanism by which sensory deprivation would facilitate telepathy (as opposed to other psi forms) remains unresolved.
