## Claim Statement

[[Rupert Sheldrake]] argues that the fourth dogma of modern science—that the laws and constants of nature are fixed and eternal—is an unexamined assumption inherited from Platonic philosophy and Christian theology, not an empirically established fact. He proposes instead that what scientists call "laws of nature" are better understood as evolving habits: regularities that have built up through repetition over cosmic time and are reinforced through a process he calls morphic resonance. On this view, the "constants" of nature need not be constant, and some measurable evidence from metrology (particularly data on the gravitational constant G, the fine-structure constant α, and the speed of light c) already hints at real variation that the current paradigm systematically suppresses.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sheldrake opens Chapter 3 with the challenge to the orthodox position: "Most scientists take it for granted that the laws of nature are fixed. They have always been the same as they are today, and will be forever. Obviously this is a theoretical assumption, not an empirical observation. On the basis of two or three hundred years of earthbound research, how can we be sure that the laws were always the same and always will be, everywhere?"

He identifies the metaphysical problem at the core of eternal laws in a secular context: "The very idea of a law of nature is anthropocentric. Only humans have laws. For the founders of modern science, the metaphor of law was appropriate because they thought of God as a kind of cosmic emperor whose writ ran everywhere … But for materialists there is no God and no transcendent mind in which these laws can be sustained. So where are they? And why do they still share the traditional attributes of God? Why are they universal, immutable and omnipotent, and why do they transcend space and time?"

He notes that philosophers of science who try to sidestep this by treating laws as mere generalisations effectively concede his point: "This amounts to an admission that the laws of nature evolve and may not be fixed forever. In an evolutionary universe, nature evolves, so the generalizations that describe nature must evolve as well."

**On the variation of constants.** Sheldrake documents anomalies in published metrological data. Regarding the Universal Gravitational Constant G: "Between 1973 and 2010, the lowest value of G was 6.6659, and the highest 6.734, a 1.1 percent difference … The difference between recent high and low values is more than forty times greater than the estimated errors." He also cites a 1998 US National Institute of Standards and Technology dataset showing that G varied by 1.3 percent within a period of a few months.

On the fine-structure constant α, he describes Australian astronomer John Webb's observations of distant quasars: Webb's team "found that α was slightly smaller in distant parts of the sky, suggesting that it had changed over billions of years … by 2010 more data from different parts of the sky not only confirmed Webb's findings, but also gave new results that were quite unexpected. The variation in α depended on which way the telescopes were facing. The constant seemed to be larger on one side of the universe than the other." Sheldrake quotes Webb and John Barrow: "If α is susceptible to change, other constants should vary as well, making the inner workings of nature more fickle than scientists ever suspected."

On the speed of light, Sheldrake documents that "all around the world from about 1928 to 1945, the speed of light dropped by about 20 kilometers per second" and then rose again after the late 1940s. He argues this episode was suppressed by what metrologist Brian Petley called "intellectual phase locking"—the tendency for experimenters to stop refining results once they converge on a value close to the expected one. The embarrassment was eventually resolved by bureaucratic fiat: "In 1972, the embarrassing possibility of variations in c was eliminated when the speed of light was fixed by definition."

**The evolutionary habits alternative.** Sheldrake summarises his positive proposal: "The alternative to Platonism is the evolution of the regularities of nature. They are more like habits, and grow stronger through repetition. There is a kind of memory in nature: what happens now is influenced by what happened before."

He traces this idea to several predecessors. The American philosopher Charles Sanders Peirce wrote: "There were slight tendencies to obey rules that had been followed, and these tendencies were rules which were more and more obeyed by their own action." Peirce also argued that "Matter is merely mind deadened by the development of habit to the point where the breaking up of these habits is very difficult." William James drew the same implication from evolution: "If one takes the theory of evolution radically, one ought to apply it not only to the rock-strata, the animals and plants, but to the stars, to the chemical elements, and to the laws of nature." And Alfred North Whitehead stated plainly: "People make the mistake of talking about 'natural laws'. There are no natural laws. There are only temporary habits of nature."

**Morphic resonance.** Sheldrake ties these threads to his own hypothesis: "My own hypothesis is that the formation of habits depends on a process called morphic resonance. Similar patterns of activity resonate across time and space with subsequent patterns … All [self-organizing systems] draw upon a collective memory and in turn contribute to it." He illustrates this with copper sulphate crystals following previous crystal habits and orb-web spiders following "the habits of countless ancestors, resonating with them directly across space and time." Very ancient habits—like those of photons and protons, formed billions of years before the Big Bang produced hydrogen atoms—have run in such deep grooves that they show "little or no change today," which is why they appear constant to short-range human observation.

## Contexts Within the Source

This argument develops dogma 4 from [[Sheldrake: modern science is constrained by ten unproven dogmas]]. It draws on the cosmological disruptions discussed in Chapter 2 (dark energy, the Big Bang as a violation of conservation laws) to argue that an evolutionary universe logically requires evolving regularities. The morphic resonance hypothesis introduced here returns as the main explanatory framework across Chapters 4–10 for topics such as biological inheritance, instinct, memory and collective learning.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sheldrake acknowledges that the standard approach—averaging measurements and discarding outliers—is not simply dishonest; metrologists have genuine reasons to suspect instrument error. He cites Brian Petley's sympathetic account of "intellectual phase locking" as a psychological phenomenon rather than a conspiracy. He also notes that Paul Dirac proposed changes in G within an orthodox mathematical framework—varying constants do not require abandoning the idea of mathematical laws per se, only their claim to absolute eternal fixity.

Sheldrake concedes that very ancient regularities (electron charge, atomic structure) are so deeply established as habits that they are effectively indistinguishable from fixed laws on any human observational timescale. His claim is about their ontological status and their ultimate origin, not a prediction of noticeable daily fluctuations in most constants. He admits that the morphic resonance hypothesis "applies to all self-organizing systems" in a way that is not yet fully operationalised or definitively tested.

The multiverse model—popular in cosmology precisely because it avoids a designer God—presupposes that different laws and constants are "imprinted" in each universe at its Big Bang, but as Sheldrake notes, "The mechanisms that might 'imprint' the basic laws and constants in a new universe are obviously far beyond anything we understand" (citing cosmologist Martin Rees). This is not a refutation of evolving habits so much as an alternative Platonic speculation.
