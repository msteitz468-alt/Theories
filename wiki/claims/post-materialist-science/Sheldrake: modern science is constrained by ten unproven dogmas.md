## Claim Statement

[[Rupert Sheldrake]] argues in [[Science Set Free (Sheldrake)]] that contemporary science is not the open-minded inquiry it claims to be, but is instead governed by ten core assumptions that have "hardened into dogmas" and are treated as settled facts rather than testable propositions. He contends that these dogmas collectively constitute a materialist ideology absorbed unconsciously by scientists through "a kind of intellectual osmosis," and that they actively suppress inquiry rather than advance it. His thesis is that science would be "freer, more interesting and more fun" if these assumptions were converted into open questions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sheldrake opens the Introduction by stating plainly: "In this book, I argue that science is being held back by centuries-old assumptions that have hardened into dogmas. The sciences would be better off without them." He identifies the root conviction as follows: "Contemporary science is based on the claim that all reality is material or physical. There is no reality but material reality. Consciousness is a by-product of the physical activity of the brain. Matter is unconscious. Evolution is purposeless. God exists only as an idea in human minds."

He then enumerates what he calls "the ten core beliefs that most scientists take for granted" (Introduction, lines 236–256):

1. **Everything is essentially mechanical.** "Dogs, for example, are complex mechanisms, rather than living organisms with goals of their own. Even people are machines, 'lumbering robots,' in Richard Dawkins's vivid phrase, with brains that are like genetically programmed computers."
2. **All matter is unconscious.** "It has no inner life or subjectivity or point of view. Even human consciousness is an illusion produced by the material activities of brains."
3. **The total amount of matter and energy is always the same** (with the exception of the Big Bang).
4. **The laws of nature are fixed.** "They are the same today as they were at the beginning, and they will stay the same forever."
5. **Nature is purposeless, and evolution has no goal or direction.**
6. **All biological inheritance is material**, carried in DNA and other material structures.
7. **Minds are inside heads** and are nothing but activities of brains. "When you look at a tree, the image of the tree you are seeing is not 'out there,' where it seems to be, but inside your brain."
8. **Memories are stored as material traces in brains** and are wiped out at death.
9. **Unexplained phenomena such as telepathy are illusory.**
10. **Mechanistic medicine is the only kind that really works.**

Sheldrake characterises the belief system this encodes as follows: "Together, these beliefs make up the philosophy or ideology of materialism … This belief system became dominant within science in the late nineteenth century, and is now taken for granted. Many scientists are unaware that materialism is an assumption: they simply think of it as science, or the scientific view of reality, or the scientific worldview."

He distinguishes the cultural inertia that sustains these dogmas from any evidential basis: "These beliefs are powerful, not because most scientists think about them critically but because they don't … the belief system that governs conventional scientific thinking is an act of faith, grounded in a nineteenth-century ideology." He names this dynamic "promissory materialism" (attributing the term to philosopher Karl Popper): a stance that "depends on issuing promissory notes for discoveries not yet made."

Sheldrake's stated method is to "turn each of these ten doctrines into a question," treating the assumption as "the beginning of an inquiry, rather than as an unquestionable truth." The book's ten chapters each interrogate one dogma in turn.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the book's framing thesis. Every subsequent chapter deploys it: Chapter 1 questions the machine metaphor (dogma 1); Chapter 2 questions conservation of matter/energy (dogma 3); Chapter 3 questions fixed laws (dogma 4), and so on. The argument connects directly to [[Sheldrake: the laws of nature are evolving habits, not fixed laws]], which develops dogma 4 at length.

The Prologue contextualises why these dogmas are sociologically entrenched: Francis Bacon's vision of science as an "established priesthood" linked to state power, the rise of institutional science, and the missionary zeal of twentieth-century atheist materialism all explain why the ideology persists. Sheldrake notes that "few professional scientists challenge them openly, at least before they retire or get a Nobel Prize."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sheldrake acknowledges that the materialist worldview has produced genuine and spectacular results: "The facts of science are real enough; so are the techniques that scientists use, and the technologies based on them." He is also careful to call the book "pro-science," not anti-science, and admits that most scientists view the anomalies he raises as problems that "will eventually be solved by more research along established lines." The historian of science Thomas Kuhn's account of paradigms is cited to show that normal science necessarily operates within shared frameworks—so the existence of dogmatic assumptions is not pathological per se, only when those assumptions block genuine inquiry. Sheldrake also notes that many scientists privately hold philosophical or religious views incompatible with strict materialism, suggesting the ideology is performed publicly rather than universally believed.
