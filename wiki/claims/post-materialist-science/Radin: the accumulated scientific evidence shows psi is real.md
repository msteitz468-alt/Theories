## Claim Statement

Radin argues that a century of controlled, independently replicated laboratory experiments, when evaluated cumulatively via meta-analysis, establishes that psychic (psi) phenomena are real effects rather than products of chance, fraud, or wishful thinking. He contends that the scientific question is no longer whether any evidence exists but what a proper cumulative evaluation of that evidence reveals — and that such evaluation yields highly persuasive, positive results. Radin places this conclusion in a sociological frame, characterizing the scientific community as moving from Stage 1 ("impossible") to Stage 2 ("perhaps real but weak") acceptance.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Radin opens the book by stating the central thesis directly: "The idea is that those compelling, perplexing, and sometimes profound human experiences known as 'psychic phenomena' are real. … we know that these phenomena exist, because of new ways of evaluating massive amounts of scientific evidence collected over a century by scores of researchers." He is explicit that the evidentiary base is cumulative and institutional: "Psi has been shown to exist in thousands of experiments. There are disagreements over how to interpret the evidence, but the fact is that virtually all scientists who have studied the evidence, including the hard-nosed skeptics, now agree that something interesting is going on that merits serious scientific attention."

He marshals five separate U.S. government-sponsored scientific reviews (1981–1995) to show that the evidence has been taken seriously at the highest institutional levels. The 1995 American Institutes for Research review, conducted for the CIA, produced the finding by statistician Jessica Utts: "The statistical results of the studies examined are far beyond what is expected by chance. Arguments that these results could be due to methodological flaws in the experiments are soundly refuted. … Such consistency cannot be readily explained by claims of flaws or fraud." Crucially, the review's skeptical co-author, Ray Hyman, conceded: "The statistical departures from chance appear to be too large and consistent to attribute to statistical flukes of any sort. … I tend to agree with Professor Utts that real effects are occurring in these experiments."

Radin's methodological argument centers on meta-analysis — "the analysis of analyses." He draws an analogy to medicine, noting that only 5 of 25 individual aspirin-and-heart-attack studies were individually "successful," yet the combined analysis established a genuine therapeutic effect beyond doubt. He applies the same logic to psi: "when we use all the relevant studies in the analysis rather than just the 'good' studies, most of the problems related to reviewer bias are prevented." By this standard, across ESP card tests (186 publications, more than 4 million trials, 1882–1939), dream-telepathy, ganzfeld, remote-viewing, and psychokinesis studies, combined results produce "odds against chance … more than a billion trillion to one." The file-drawer correction required to nullify these results — more than 626,000 unreported negative studies — is, he argues, implausible given the small community of researchers involved.

He also addresses the quality-of-evidence objection by citing psychologist Larry Hedges's finding that "some experiments in the soft sciences are as replicable as those in the hard sciences" and that even particle-physics data exhibit roughly comparable inconsistency rates (~45%) to those seen in psi research. Radin concludes: "when psi research is judged by the same standards as any other scientific discipline, then the results are as consistent as those observed in the hardest of the hard sciences."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the book's organizing thesis. Radin develops it across four themes — Motivation, Evidence, Understanding, and Implications — and it is restated or reinforced in almost every chapter. The detailed meta-analytic evidence supporting this claim is distributed across chapters 5 through 9 (telepathy, clairvoyance, precognition, psychokinesis, and healing). The sociological explanation for why the scientific community has been slow to accept the evidence is the subject of chapters 13–15. This claim is foundational to the companion claim [[Radin: ganzfeld experiments demonstrate telepathy beyond chance]], which represents one of the strongest specific lines of evidence Radin cites.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Radin acknowledges several substantive objections. The "file-drawer problem" — that journals preferentially publish positive results — is a standing concern, though he argues that the file-drawer estimates required to negate the accumulated database are wildly implausible. He quotes Ray Hyman's persistent refusal to attribute positive results to genuine psi: even after conceding that the results exceed chance and cannot be attributed to known statistical or methodological flaws, Hyman falls back on the position that "it is impossible in principle to say that any particular experiment or experimental series is completely free from possible flaws." Radin also concedes that psi effects tend to be small and statistically unstable across individual experiments, requiring aggregation to achieve high confidence. He notes the "experimenter effect" — the finding that some researchers consistently obtain positive results and others do not — as a genuine complication for interpreting the database.
