## Claim Statement

[[Rupert Sheldrake]] argues that the materialist doctrine — that minds are nothing but activity inside skulls — conflicts with direct experience and is falsified by experimental evidence. He proposes instead an "extended mind" hypothesis: perceptual fields, attention, and intention literally reach beyond the body into the surrounding world. This accounts for the sense of being stared at (which he treats as evidence that looking involves an outward projection of perceptual fields) and for telepathy (which he regards as a non-local resonant connection between bonded individuals). Both phenomena, he argues, have been demonstrated in controlled scientific trials and are consistent with a field theory of mind that replaces the brain-as-container model.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sheldrake opens Chapter 8 with the challenge to the orthodox position: "Materialism is the doctrine that only matter is real. Hence minds are in brains, and mental activity is nothing but brain activity. This assumption conflicts with our own experience. When we look at a blackbird, we see a blackbird; we do not experience complex electrical changes in our brains" (line 2819). He frames his alternative through field theory: "We are used to the fact that fields exist both within and outside material objects. The field of a magnet is inside it and also extends beyond its surface … In this chapter I suggest that the fields of minds are within brains and extend beyond them" (line 2835).

On vision, Sheldrake revives the ancient extramission tradition: "My own suggestion is that the outward projection of visual images is both psychological and physical. It occurs through perceptual fields … When I look at a person or an animal, my perceptual field interacts with the field of the person or animal I am looking at, enabling my gaze to be detected" (line 2938). He notes that surveys in Europe and North America found "between 70 and 97 percent of adults and children reported experiences" of feeling themselves stared at or making others turn round by staring (line 2951).

On experimental evidence for the sense of being stared at, Sheldrake describes a large body of direct-looking studies: "Typically, about 55 percent of the guesses were right, as opposed to 50 percent expected by chance. Although the effect was small, because it was so widely replicated it was highly significant statistically … More rigorous experiments subjects and starers were separated by windows or one-way mirrors, eliminating the possibility of subtle cues by sound or even smell. They were still able to tell when they were being watched" (line 2990). The NEMO Science Centre Amsterdam study involved "more than eighteen thousand pairs … with positive results that were highly significant statistically" (line 2992). Crucially, the effect also operated through closed-circuit television: "In laboratory tests, many people respond physiologically to being watched through CCTV … The subjects' galvanic skin response was recorded … The subjects' skin resistance changed significantly when they were being looked at" (line 2996). Sheldrake concludes: "The effects of attention at a distance show that minds are not confined to the insides of brains" (line 3000).

On telephone telepathy, Sheldrake describes his experimental protocol in which callers were randomly selected by the experimenter: "By guessing at random, subjects would have been right about one time in four, or 25 percent. In fact, the average hit rate was 45 percent, very significantly above the chance level. This above-chance effect has been replicated independently in telephone telepathy tests at the universities of Freiburg, Germany, and Amsterdam, Holland" (line 3212). He notes that the effect was stronger with emotionally close callers than strangers, and did not fall off with distance: "emotional closeness is more important than physical proximity" (line 3216).

On animals, he reports that in about 50 percent of UK and US dog-owning households the dog was said to anticipate the owner's return, and he carried out formal filmed experiments with a terrier named Jaytee: "On average, Jaytee was at the window only 4 percent of the time during the main period of Pam's absence, and 55 percent of the time when she was on the way back. This difference was very significant statistically" (line 3160). He also reports controlled tests with an African grey parrot, N'kisi, in which the bird said words corresponding to images its owner was viewing in a separate room "much more frequently than expected by chance" (line 3168).

Sheldrake summarizes: "Our minds are extended in every act of perception, reaching even as far as the stars … When we look at something, in a sense our mind touches it … Minds are extended beyond brains not only in space but also in time, and connect us to our own pasts through memory and to virtual futures, among which we choose" (line 3060).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the central thesis of Chapters 8 ("Are Minds Confined to Brains?") and 9 ("Are Psychic Phenomena Illusory?") of [[Science Set Free (Sheldrake)]]. It depends on and reinforces the other two claims: materialism's failure to explain consciousness (see [[Sheldrake: materialism is a belief system, not a proven fact]]) creates the philosophical opening, while the morphic-field framework (see [[Sheldrake: morphic resonance gives nature an inherent collective memory]]) supplies the physical mechanism Sheldrake proposes for telepathic connection. He also links the extended mind to the placebo research in Chapter 10, where minds demonstrably affect bodies in ways that exceed purely mechanical causation.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Sheldrake engages skeptical objections directly. He acknowledges the standard explanations for telephone telepathy — chance coincidence and unconscious knowledge of the caller's routine — but notes: "I searched the scientific literature to find out if these standard arguments were supported by any data or observations. I could find no research whatever on the subject. The standard skeptical arguments were evidence-free speculations" (line 3208). He reports that his experimental design rules out routine-based prediction because callers rang at randomly selected times unknown to the subject.

On the ganzfeld telepathy research, he notes that skeptic Ray Hyman agreed the data showed a significant effect but attributed it to methodological flaws; subsequent studies designed to satisfy Hyman's own criteria still produced a 34 percent hit rate versus 25 percent chance (line 3138–3139). For the stare-detection experiments, critics argue that even well-controlled direct-looking studies cannot eliminate subtle auditory or behavioral cues entirely. Sheldrake regards the CCTV and skin-conductance studies as the strongest counter to this objection.

On the broader framing, he concedes: "In no other field of scientific endeavor do otherwise intelligent people feel free to make public claims based on prejudice and ignorance … Yet in relation to psychic phenomena, committed materialists feel free to disregard the evidence and behave irrationally" (line 3346) — an acknowledgment that his reading of the evidence is sharply contested within mainstream science.
