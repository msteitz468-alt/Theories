## Claim Statement

Laszlo proposes that a wide range of anomalous first-person experiences — including transpersonal connection, intuition at a distance, distant healing, past-life recall, near-death and out-of-body episodes, and after-death communication — constitute evidence that human consciousness can access an underlying information field in nature. He calls this field the Akashic field (A-field) and argues that the twenty-one personal testimonies assembled in the volume collectively demonstrate that the Akashic experience "is not confined to mystics, psychics, shamans, and gurus" but is available to people of diverse backgrounds. For Laszlo, the recurrent themes of interconnection, nonlocal perception, and holographic life-review embedded in these testimonies point toward "an interconnected, in-formed reality."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the book's concluding synthesis, Laszlo states directly that "this collection of firsthand reports on the Akashic experience covers a great deal of ground. It covers the lived aspect of the experience as well as its practical utility" and that it "testifies that the Akashic experience occurs for a wide variety of people and comes in a great variety of forms" (lines 5760–5761). He adds that the testimonies together show "the Akashic experience is not confined to mystics, psychics, shamans, and gurus. It is encountered by people with a wide variety of backgrounds and great diversity of interests" (lines 5761–5762).

Multiple contributors describe experiences that Laszlo frames as A-field access. The Dutch cardiologist Pim van Lommel, reporting on his prospective NDE study published in *The Lancet*, describes patients who have "an enhanced consciousness with lucid thoughts, emotions, memories, and sometimes perception from a position outside and above the lifeless body" during confirmed cardiac arrest (line 4603), and concludes that "our waking consciousness … is only a complementary aspect of our whole and infinite nonlocal consciousness … stored in a dimension beyond our concept of time and space with nonlocal and universal interconnectedness. One could call this … the Akashic field to which conditions at the portals of death provide a special kind of access" (line 4762). Stanislav Grof, summarising decades of consciousness research, writes: "My observations indicate that we can obtain information about the universe in two radically different ways. Besides the conventional possibility of learning through sensory perception, and analysis and synthesis of the data, we can also find out about various aspects of the world by direct identification with them in an altered state of consciousness" and that "each of us thus seems to be a microcosm that also has access to the information about the entire macrocosm" (line 5040). He further states: "I do not know any scientific conceptual framework other than Ervin Laszlo's connectivity hypothesis and his concept of the Akashic field that would provide a reasonable explanation for these paradoxical findings" (line 5056).

Marilyn Schlitz, a researcher at the Institute of Noetic Sciences, describes statistically significant distant-intention effects across fourteen randomised double-blind DMILS experiments and argues that "breakthroughs come at the points of intersection between worldviews, disciplines, and ways of knowing and being" (line 4342), and that attending to "the collective realms of interconnections, such as we find in the Akashic experience, is a way of reclaiming our natural consciousness" (line 4444–4448). Reconnective healer Eric Pearl reports that healing effects grow *stronger* with physical distance and persist even through video-screen separation, prompting him to describe the experience as "a more conscious and aware interaction with the field" identified as "the Akashic field of spirituality, healing, intelligence, and evolution" (line 3802–3806). Artist Alex Grey recounts a simultaneous shared LSD vision in which both he and his wife perceived "interconnectedness with all beings and things in a vast Universal Mind Lattice" (line 3489), which he explicitly connects to the A-field as "a Universal Creative Force connecting all beings and things" (line 3569).

Laszlo encapsulates his final evaluation: "The Akashic experience, I conclude, is a veridical extra- or non-sensory experience, a rediscovery and revalidation of the spontaneous insights and intuitions that have accompanied and inspired human culture and consciousness throughout the centuries and millennia of our history" (line 6007).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the organising thesis of the entire volume. Parts One and Two collect twenty first-person testimonies; Part Three pivots to research perspectives (Schlitz, van Lommel, Grof, Dossey) that frame the testimonies scientifically. Laszlo's own "Summing Up" chapter and postscript (lines 5751–6092) synthesise all accounts into the argument that the experiences point to a real, scientifically explicable information field. The claim connects directly to [[Laszlo: the universe is an in-formed, holographic, interconnected whole]] (the metaphysical framework) and to [[Laszlo: science is converging on a post-materialist paradigm of an integral reality]] (the scientific-paradigm argument). It also links to [[The Akashic field (Laszlo)]] as the proposed mechanism.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Laszlo acknowledges that the sample of twenty-one testimonies "is not an adequate basis for evaluation" by social-science standards because "the sample size is too small" (line 5761). He also notes that the materialist paradigm dismisses these experiences as "strange" and "esoteric," and that "modern people not only dismiss the experience when recounted by others but also repress it if and when it happens to them" (line 5771). Schlitz describes enduring debates over experimenter effects and reproducibility in psi research, noting that her collaboration with sceptic Richard Wiseman produced divergent results under identical conditions, leaving "the differences in our experimental outcomes" an open question (line 4342). Laszlo does not resolve this methodological puzzle.
