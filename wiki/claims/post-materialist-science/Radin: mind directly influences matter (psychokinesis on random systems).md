## Claim Statement

[[Dean Radin]] argues that mental intention can directly bias the behavior of random physical systems — tumbling dice and electronic random number generators (RNGs) — away from chance expectation in the direction the participant intends, without any normal physical intermediary. Drawing on meta-analyses spanning sixty years of dice experiments and three decades of RNG experiments, he contends that the aggregate effect is too large, too consistent, and too well-controlled to be explained by chance, selective reporting, poor methodology, or the efforts of a small number of exceptional performers, and that it constitutes persuasive evidence for a real mind-over-matter phenomenon.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Dice experiments.** Radin and Diane Ferrari conducted a 1989 meta-analysis of all relevant English-language dice-tossing studies from 1935 to 1987: 148 experiments by 52 investigators in which 2,569 participants attempted to mentally influence 2.6 million dice throws. The overall experimental hit rate was 51.2 percent where 50 percent was expected by chance, yielding "odds against chance of more than a billion to one." Control studies — in which no mental influence was applied — came in at 50.02 percent, well within chance. The effect survived outlier removal: even after deleting 35 percent of studies (comparable to trimming rates used in the physical sciences), "the overall effect observed in the remaining 96 studies still resulted in odds against chance of more than three million to one." The file-drawer calculation found that 17,974 unreported null studies would be required to nullify the result — equivalent to each of the 52 investigators producing one unpublished negative study per month for 28 years, which Radin judges implausible. From this he concluded: "a genuine mind-matter interaction did exist with experiments testing tossed dice. The effect had been successfully replicated in more than a hundred experiments by more than fifty investigators for more than a half-century."

**RNG experiments.** Radin and Roger Nelson conducted a 1987 meta-analysis covering 832 studies (597 experimental, 235 control) by 68 investigators from 1959 to 1987. RNG experiments use electronic circuits driven by quantum-level noise (electronic noise or radioactive decay) to generate sequences of random bits; participants attempt to bias the output toward more 1s or more 0s. "The overall experimental results produced odds against chance beyond a trillion to one. Control results were well within chance levels with odds of two to one." The average experimental effect was approximately 51 percent, the same order of magnitude as in the dice studies. The file-drawer required to nullify the effect was 54,000 studies — about 90 times the number actually reported.

RNG experimental quality improved significantly over time (odds against chance of 500 to 1 that improvement was non-zero), and — contrary to the standard skeptical prediction — hit rates were not inversely related to quality: "we found that the observed hit rates were not related to experimental quality." Distance between participant and RNG produced no systematic decline in effect size: "results of local and remote experiments were indistinguishable." Time delays between a participant's mental effort and the actual generation of RNG data likewise produced no falloff.

The Princeton PEAR laboratory's long-term dataset (1,262 independent experiments, 108 participants) closely replicated the prior meta-analytic results: "the main RNG effect for the full PEAR database of 1,262 independent experiments, generated by 108 people, was associated with odds against chance of four thousand to one." There were no star performers: the effect "reflected an accumulation of small effects from each person rather than a few outstanding results from 'special people.'" Pairs of participants produced larger effects than individuals, especially opposite-sex bonded couples, and results were best described as "small changes in the probabilities of individual random events rather than as a few instances of wildly large effects."

Skeptically monitored RNG studies, in which independent third-party observers selected which trials received mental effort and which served as controls — preventing any experimenter fraud or motivated inattention — were replicated five times: "overall odds against chance of twelve thousand to one." Physicist Helmut Schmidt wrote of one such study: "The present study confirms the existence of the [psi] effect under particularly well-controlled conditions where the participation of independent observers precludes experimenter error, or even fraud." Radin summarizes: "After sixty years of experiments using tossed dice and their modern progeny, electronic RNGs, researchers have produced persuasive, consistent, replicated evidence that mental intention is associated with the behavior of these physical systems."

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 8 follows immediately from [[Radin: precognition and presentiment show the future can be sensed]] (Chapter 7). Radin distinguishes *passive* psi (sensing future states) from *active* psi (causing physical states to change), though he acknowledges that in experiments with random systems the two cannot always be cleanly separated: a participant might alternatively be selecting favorable moments to observe the RNG rather than directly forcing its output. The chapter situates RNG research within broader physics discussions of the observer in quantum mechanics — noting that physicist Bernard d'Espagnat wrote in *Scientific American* that "The doctrine that the world is made up of objects whose existence is independent of human consciousness turns out to be in conflict with quantum mechanics" — and cites theoretical physicist Henry Stapp's 1994 *Physical Review* paper attempting to reconcile mind-matter interaction with generalized quantum theory. Radin frames the modest (~1 percent above chance) effect sizes not as a weakness but as consistent with the physics of random systems, where "uncommon events… may be unusual, but they are not physically impossible." ## Opposing Information Within the Source

Radin is candid that the effect sizes are small and that skeptics have progressively escalated their objections as earlier critiques (fraud, chance, design flaws, selective reporting) were addressed. He notes that large-scale "macropsychokinesis" — mentally bending metal or moving objects — lacks the controlled replication record that dice and RNG studies have, and that "the jury is still out when it comes to the reality of metal bending and moving small objects." He also acknowledges a persistent interpretive ambiguity: because both precognition and psychokinesis could produce above-chance RNG results (the participant might sense when to act rather than force the outcome), a clean experimental separation of the two has not yet been achieved. The mainstream physics literature contains essentially no independent attempts to replicate these findings, which Radin attributes to disciplinary taboo rather than to any empirical disconfirmation.
