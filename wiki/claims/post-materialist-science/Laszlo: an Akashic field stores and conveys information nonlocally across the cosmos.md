## Claim Statement

Laszlo proposes that a fundamental cosmic information field — which he names the Akashic field (A-field) — underlies all physical reality, recording, conserving, and transmitting the "in-formation" of everything that has ever existed. This field is identified with the unified vacuum (the zero-point field, or ZPF) that contemporary physics posits as the substrate beneath all known forces and particles. Laszlo revives the ancient Sanskrit concept of *Akasha* — the primordial, all-pervasive cosmic medium described in Hindu cosmology — and argues that cutting-edge science has independently converged on the same reality. Unlike ordinary matter or energy, the A-field spans the entire cosmos and conveys information nonlocally, without any attenuation across space or time.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the book's Introduction, Laszlo frames the physical basis of the A-field explicitly. He writes that the unified vacuum — "effectively a cosmic plenum" — "carries the famous zero-point field (ZPF), and grand-unified and super-grand-unified theories ascribe all the fields and forces of nature to it. It is the unified field sought by Einstein near the end of his life" (line 787). Crucially, this vacuum is not empty: "space is a superdense sea of fluctuating energies, and not only of energies but also of information. As physicist John Wheeler remarked, the most fundamental feature of the universe is information — other physical quantities are more like incidentals. Information is present throughout space and time, and it is present at the same time everywhere" (line 829).

Laszlo then identifies the Akashic field as the information-bearing aspect of this substrate: "The unified field is a space-filling medium that underlies the manifest things and processes of the universe. It carries the universal fields: the electromagnetic, the gravitational, and the strong and the weak nuclear fields. It carries the ZPF, the field of zero-point energies. And it's also the element of the cosmos that records, conserves, and conveys information. In the latter guise it's the Akashic field, the rediscovered ancient concept of Akasha" (line 833).

The connection to Sanskrit cosmology is deliberate. Laszlo notes that "in ancient India the Sanskrit word Akasha meant 'cosmic sky,' similar to our concept of space. But Akasha referred not only to space in the modern sense but also and above all to the higher spheres of life and existence. The Hindu seers believed that all things arise from, and re-descend into, the cosmic source they called Akasha. . . . Akasha was said to embrace the properties of all five elements. And it was believed to conserve the traces of everything that ever happened in space and in time. Akasha is the enduring memory of the cosmos: it is the 'Akashic Record'" (line 799). He also invokes Nikola Tesla's early twentieth-century intuition of "an 'original medium' that fills space and compared it to Akasha, the light-carrying ether" (line 821), suggesting the A-field hypothesis has a precedent in modern physics that was later suppressed and is now being rediscovered.

The nonlocal character of this information storage and transmission is indicated by Laszlo's description of Wheeler's claim that information is "present at the same time everywhere" (line 829), and by the numerous contributors in the volume — including Maria Sági, who after decades of remote healing concludes: "My experience shows that it's possible to receive information on the condition of a patient over any distance, and from his present as well as from his past. This for me is significant evidence that a nonlocal information field mediates the exchange of information between healer and patient. The Akashic field is not an abstract theoretical concept but a working reality" (line 2995).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the foundational hypothesis of the entire book. Laszlo advances it in his Introduction as the theoretical anchor for the twenty firsthand Akashic experience accounts that follow. All subsequent testimonies — from remote healing (chapter 8), classroom field phenomena (chapter 7), business intuition (chapter 9), and near-death experiences (chapter 16) — are presented as empirical evidence for the A-field. The claim connects directly to [[Laszlo: consciousness is connected beyond the brain through the in-formation field]], which is the experiential and psychological corollary of this physical hypothesis. The A-field is also described as the rediscovery of what ancient traditions encoded as [[The Akashic field (Laszlo)]]. Laszlo situates his proposal within the broader project of [[Post-materialist science]], noting that "the currently dominant paradigm of separate material things connected by mechanistic relations of cause and effect is failing; there are ever more things and processes it cannot account for" (line 783).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Laszlo acknowledges that the concept of a space-filling ether was historically rejected by mainstream physics after Einstein, and that Tesla's analogous proposal "fell into oblivion" when physicists "refused to consider anything like an 'ether' that would fill space" (line 825). He presents the A-field hypothesis as a reconceptualization that avoids the classical ether's problems, but he does not offer formal mathematical derivation within this volume; he refers readers to his earlier book *Science and the Akashic Field* for the detailed scientific rationale (line 773). The source presents no sustained internal counter-argument to the A-field hypothesis itself.
