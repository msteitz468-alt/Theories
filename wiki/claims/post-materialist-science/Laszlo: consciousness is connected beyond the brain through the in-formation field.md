## Claim Statement

Laszlo argues that individual consciousness is not confined to the brain and body but is linked, through the Akashic field (A-field), to other minds and to nature at large. This linkage is the basis of the full spectrum of transpersonal, telepathic, intuitive, and healing experiences that he calls "Akashic experiences." He contends that classical empiricism — the view that "there can be nothing in the mind that was not first in the eye" — has been empirically refuted by the range and consistency of such experiences, and that the A-field provides the physical mechanism by which consciousness extends beyond its apparent biological housing. The shift from a "skin-enclosed" ego to an "interconnected transpersonal consciousness" is, for Laszlo, both a scientific conclusion and an evolutionary imperative.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Laszlo states the thesis directly in the Introduction: "There is more to human experience than we had thought. Classical empiricism, the long-dominant philosophy of the Anglo-Saxon world, proclaimed that there can be nothing in the mind that was not first in the eye. But classical empiricism was mistaken. Our perception is not limited to wave-propagations in the electromagnetic field and in the air, and to our bodily sensations. We are connected to the world in ways that are deeper and more wide-ranging than our eyes and ears can testify" (line 843).

The phenomenological signature of this connection is, he explains, uniform across all varieties of Akashic experience: "Whatever else it may contain, the Akashic experience conveys the sense that the experiencing subject is not separate from the objects of his or her experience — the sense that 'I, the experiencing subject, am linked in subtle but real ways to other people and to nature.' In deeper experiences of this kind there is a sense that 'the cosmos and I are one'" (line 769).

Laszlo attributes to the A-field the mechanism that makes this connection possible: "The Akashic experience gives clear testimony that we are connected to an information and memory field objectively present in nature" (line 773). He frames the social and evolutionary consequence as a transition: "People will shift from the ego-centered skin-enclosed consciousness of the modern age to the interconnected transpersonal consciousness foretold by thinkers such as Sri Aurobindo, Jean Gebser, Richard Bucke, Rudolf Steiner, Stanislav Grof, Don Beck, Ken Wilber, and Eckhart Tolle" (line 873). He also endorses contributor Edgar Mitchell's formulation that access to the A-field should be regarded "not as our sixth sense, but as our first — it is actually our most basic sense" (line 863).

The first-person accounts in Parts One and Two corroborate the claim at the experiential level. Christopher Bache describes how, without conscious intent, his consciousness appeared to tap into "some kind of informational field that held" his students' life experience (line 2576), and concludes that "because consciousness is a field and fields are by nature porous, the transformation of the individual cannot be isolated from the transformation of others nearby" (line 2754). He further observes that minds can "link together, even 'phase lock' with other minds to form larger operational wholes," a tendency he sees as "probably underwritten by the space- and time-transcending Akashic field itself" (line 2758). Maria Sági's remote-healing work provides clinical evidence: "My experience shows that it's possible to receive information on the condition of a patient over any distance, and from his present as well as from his past. This for me is significant evidence that a nonlocal information field mediates the exchange of information between healer and patient. The Akashic field is not an abstract theoretical concept but a working reality" (line 2995). Parapsychologist Stanley Krippner, reflecting on a telepathic episode, concludes that the event suggested "that our psyches had met in the Akashic field," describing it as "a 'psi field' that is trans-temporal and trans-spatial in nature" (line 2177).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the experiential and psychological complement to [[Laszlo: an Akashic field stores and conveys information nonlocally across the cosmos]], which supplies the physical substrate. Together they constitute Laszlo's core argument: the A-field is both a cosmological reality (the information-bearing aspect of the unified vacuum) and the medium through which individual consciousness participates in a transpersonal whole. The book's twenty contributor accounts — spanning near-death experiences, remote healing, precognitive dreams, classroom field effects, and creative inspiration — are all presented as independent verification of the same underlying connectivity. The concept of [[The Akashic field (Laszlo)]] is the organizing hub. Laszlo explicitly situates the claim within [[Post-materialist science]] by arguing that the materialist-reductionist paradigm — in which mind is merely an epiphenomenon of the brain — "cannot account for" the well-documented range of nonlocal experience (line 895).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Laszlo acknowledges that "modern people dismiss ideas of a wider sphere of reality and consider experiences of it as mere fantasy" (line 855), and that the various Akashic experiences described in Part Three "cannot be explained by the concepts and methods of conventional science — it doesn't fit its materialist and reductionist paradigm" (line 895). He treats this as a limitation of the prevailing paradigm rather than as a refutation of the A-field hypothesis. Individual contributors note that anecdotal accounts have "very little evidential value for strictly scientific purposes" (Stanley Krippner, line 2165). The source presents no sustained internal argument that the A-field explanation is wrong; skeptical objections are cited only to be set aside as signs of paradigm-lag rather than principled counter-evidence.
