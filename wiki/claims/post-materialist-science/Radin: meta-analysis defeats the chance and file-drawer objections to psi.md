## Claim Statement

[[Dean Radin]] argues that quantitative meta-analysis — the statistical pooling of results across many independent experiments — reveals a small but robust psi effect that cannot be explained by chance, selective reporting of positive results ("the file-drawer problem"), or variations in experimental quality. Across every major psi domain he reviews, Radin shows that the number of unpublished null-result studies that would be required to nullify the observed cumulative effect is impossibly large, and that experimental quality correlates positively, not negatively, with effect size. He further contends that mainstream science's continued resistance to this evidence is explained not by the data but by cognitive and sociological mechanisms — prior commitment, cognitive dissonance, and institutional politics — that operate independently of the evidence's actual strength.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Radin introduces the file-drawer argument directly in his meta-analytic surveys. For the early ESP card tests conducted from 1882 to 1939 — a four-million-trial database reported in 186 publications — he calculates that "the combined results … translate into tremendous odds against chance — more than a billion trillion to one." The file-drawer calculation requires "more than 626,000 reports … more than 3,300 unpublished, unsuccessful reports for each published report" to nullify the effect. For the high-security ESP card studies alone (1935–1939), "the file-drawer estimate is 29,000 studies … a ratio of 861 unpublished studies for each published study." He benchmarks this against the threshold proposed by Harvard psychologist Robert Rosenthal: "a ratio of five unpublished studies to one published study is sufficient to consider the observed effect 'robust.'" The ESP card database exceeds this threshold by a factor of more than 170.

For the forced-choice precognition experiments (309 studies), "the combined result … produced odds against chance of 10^25 to one — that is, ten million billion billion to one … [and] the number of unpublished, unsuccessful studies required to eliminate these astronomical odds was 14,268." For the RNG/mind-matter interaction studies, the file-drawer number was "17,974 … for each study we found, 121 additional, unretrieved, and unsuccessful studies would have been required to nullify the observed effect. That many studies would have required each of the fifty-two investigators involved in these experiments to have conducted one unpublished, nonsignificant study per month, every month, for twenty-eight years." The sheep-goat meta-analysis (73 publications, 685,000 guesses) required "some 1,726 unpublished, nonsignificant studies for each published study to eradicate this effect."

On experimental quality, Radin draws on the Harvard psychologists Monica Harris and Robert Rosenthal, who were commissioned by the National Research Council (NRC) to evaluate ganzfeld experiments alongside four other research domains. Harris and Rosenthal concluded that "only the ganzfeld ESP studies regularly meet the basic requirements of sound experimental design" and that "it would be implausible to entertain the null [hypothesis] given the combined [probability] from these 28 studies … We estimate the obtained accuracy rate to be about 1/3" — against a chance expectation of 1/4. The NRC committee chairman subsequently asked Rosenthal to withdraw these favorable conclusions; the committee chairman later explained, "I didn't feel we were obliged to represent every point of view." Radin treats this as an instance of institutional suppression and notes that the NRC's public claim of "no scientific justification" directly contradicted the committee's own commissioned background paper.

Radin's summary statement, opening Theme 4 of the book, is explicit: "the effects observed in a thousand psi experiments are not due to chance, selective reporting, variations in experimental quality, or design flaws. They've been independently replicated by competent, conventionally trained scientists at well known academic, industrial, and government-supported laboratories worldwide for more than a century, and the effects are consistent with human experiences reported throughout history and across all cultures."

On why mainstream science remains resistant, Radin draws on parapsychologist Charles Honorton's 1993 analysis, which showed that "virtually all the skeptical arguments used to explain away psi over the years had been resolved through new experimental designs." He quotes the concession of skeptical psychologist Ray Hyman — himself a member of the NRC committee — that some parapsychological effects are "astronomically significant," noting that this "shifts the focus of the debate away from the mere existence of interesting effects to their proper interpretation." Radin's explanation for continued resistance invokes psychologist Leon Festinger's concept of cognitive dissonance: "When we are publicly committed to a belief, it is disturbing even to consider that any evidence contradicting our position may be true." He argues that the most prominent institutional skeptics — several of whom founded or sat on the executive council of the Committee for the Scientific Investigation of Claims of the Paranormal (CSICOP) — have deep public commitments that make genuine reconsideration functionally impossible: "Commitment stirs the fires of cognitive dissonance and makes it progressively more difficult to even casually entertain alternative hypotheses."

He further identifies structural distortions in how psi research reaches mainstream scientists: textbook misrepresentation (a 1991 survey found that most introductory psychology textbooks presented "an outdated and often grossly misleading view of parapsychology"), media compression that strips caveats and alternative explanations, and the "sleeper effect," whereby the source of information (credible versus tabloid) is forgotten over time while the content remains — causing legitimate experimental findings to be mentally filed alongside ghost stories.

## Contexts Within the Source

This methodological argument is the book's spine. Radin builds it cumulatively across Part I (specific psi domains — dream telepathy, ganzfeld, ESP card tests, precognition, RNG studies, distant healing) and Part III (Understanding — the skeptics chapter, the cognitive-bias chapter). The cumulative case is what licenses his broader theoretical and metaphysical claims in Part IV: because chance and file-drawer cannot explain the data, the data demand theoretical accommodation, and that accommodation, Radin argues, will ultimately require revising the metaphysical foundations of science itself.

This claim supports [[Radin: group attention perturbs random systems (field consciousness)]] by providing the meta-analytic framework within which the field-consciousness and casino studies are situated as further (non-laboratory) converging evidence.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Radin is careful to acknowledge what meta-analysis cannot do and what critics have legitimately raised. He notes that the technique is only as good as the studies retrieved — hence the file-drawer calculation rather than a claim of definitive proof. He concedes that "any one or two experiments can be explained away as being due to chance or poor design" and that not all individual studies are equal in quality. He quotes Ray Hyman's acknowledgment that the ganzfeld results cannot be attributed to specific identified flaws, while noting that Hyman still resists the psi interpretation — a position Radin treats as philosophically coherent but no longer scientifically defensible given the evidence. He also quotes psychologist Philip Anderson's dismissal of psi in a 1990 Physics Today editorial, and acknowledges that psi remains without an agreed theoretical mechanism: "so far, hardly anything predicts psi." The absence of a predictive theory, he concedes, is a genuine asymmetry with the case of quantum nonlocality, which had Bell's theorem behind it before experiments confirmed it. On fraud: Radin notes that while some individual researchers have faced fraud allegations, dismissing an entire field on those grounds would "force us to throw out virtually every realm of science — since fraud exists in all human endeavors."
