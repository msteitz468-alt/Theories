## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts: **"Do not aim to be coldly rational when making financial decisions. Aim to just be pretty reasonable."** "Reasonable is more realistic and you have a better chance of sticking with it for the long run, which is what matters most when managing money." Something "can be technically true but contextually nonsense."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 11**: The opening analogy is fever — it is "rational to want a fever if you have an infection. But it's not reasonable," because fevers hurt and patients want relief (Julius Wagner-Jauregg's malaria therapy for syphilis). **Harry Markowitz**, a Nobel laureate, split his own portfolio "50/50 between bonds and equities" to "minimize my future regret," not by his own optimization models — "Markowitz is neither rational or irrational. He's reasonable."

A 2008 Yale study showed leveraging young savers 2-to-1 is mathematically optimal but "almost absurdly unreasonable," since "No normal person could watch 100% of their retirement account evaporate" and continue. Housel endorses "loving" your imperfect strategy (commitment correlates with performance), home bias, and small individual-stock allocations as *reasonable* because they help you endure. John Bogle invested in his son's high-fee funds "for family reasons... life isn't always consistent."

## Contexts Within the Source

This chapter underwrites Housel's own "psychologically reasonable, not coldly rational" choices in chapter 20 (a paid-off mortgage at low rates; a large cash allocation). "Good decisions aren't always rational. At some point you have to choose between being happy or being 'right.'"

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel presents the rational/academic view (mathematically optimal strategies) at length and does not deny its internal correctness — only its real-world durability. He notes some reasonable-seeming behaviors (acting on forecasts, day trading) can be "dangerous," endorsing them only "in small amounts."
