## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that **getting money and keeping money are two different skills**: "Getting money requires taking risks, being optimistic... But keeping money requires the opposite... humility, and fear... frugality." If he had to summarize money success in a single word, "it would be 'survival'" — "The ability to stick around for a long time, without wiping out... is what makes the biggest difference."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 5**: **Jesse Livermore** made the equivalent of $3 billion shorting the 1929 crash, becoming one of the richest men in the world, then "made larger and larger bets," lost everything, and took his own life. The same crash ruined **Abraham Germansky**. Both were "very good at getting wealthy, and equally bad at staying wealthy."

Survival evidence: "40% of companies successful enough to become publicly traded lost effectively all of their value over time"; the Forbes 400 has ~20% turnover per decade. Buffett's success is reframed as what he *didn't* do — "He didn't get carried away with debt. He didn't panic and sell during the 14 recessions he's lived through... He survived." **Rick Guerin**, once an equal partner with Buffett and Munger, "was in a hurry," used margin, and was forced to sell his Berkshire stock during the 1973–74 downturn. Nassim Taleb: "You need to avoid ruin. At all costs."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter names three survival traits: wanting to be "financially unbreakable" rather than chasing big returns; planning on the plan not going according to plan (room for error); and a "barbelled personality—optimistic about the future, but paranoid about what will prevent you from getting to the future." It directly feeds [[Room for error (margin of safety) is essential because money lives in a world of odds, not certainties]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel acknowledges the emotional cost of the survival mindset: holding cash in a bull market means "you look and feel conservative," and "that 9% gap will gnaw at you every day" — yet he argues preventing "one desperate, ill-timed stock sale can do more for your lifetime returns than picking dozens of big-time winners."
