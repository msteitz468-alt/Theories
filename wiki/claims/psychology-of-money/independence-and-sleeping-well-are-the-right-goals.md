## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts, describing his own family's finances, that the right goal is **independence and "whatever maximizes for sleeping well at night,"** not the highest returns or most luxurious lifestyle. "We think it's the ultimate goal; the mastery of the psychology of money." He frames this as an example, not a prescription: "this is what works for us... to each their own."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 20**: Prompted by Sandy Gottesman's interview question "What do you own, and why?", and noting that "Half of all U.S. mutual fund portfolio managers do not invest a cent of their own money in their funds" and that doctors "die differently" than they treat patients. Charlie Munger: "I did not intend to get rich. I just wanted to get independent." Housel's family froze its lifestyle in their 20s, pushing the savings rate "continuously higher"; "Independence is our top goal."

Specific (admittedly "indefensible on paper") choices: they own their house with no mortgage despite low rates ("the worst financial decision we've ever made but the best money decision"); they hold ~20% of assets in cash ("cash is the oxygen of independence") so they're never forced to sell stocks; and every stock they own "is a low-cost index fund," bought via dollar-cost averaging. His strategy "relies on a high savings rate, patience, and optimism that the global economy will create value."

## Contexts Within the Source

This chapter operationalizes the whole book: reasonable > rational (chapter 11), savings rate (chapter 10), room for error (chapter 13), and tails ("there is little correlation between investment effort and investment results"). It is explicitly bracketed by chapter 19's framing of financial advice as akin to medicine — universal truths, but "no one right answer."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel repeatedly flags that his choices are not optimal and not recommended: a paid-off mortgage and large cash position are "close to indefensible on paper, and I'm not recommending it to others." He concedes "there's a chance I'll change it in the future," having already shifted from individual stocks to index funds.
