## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that in business and investing **"Tails drive everything"**: "a small number of events can account for the majority of outcomes," so an investor "can be wrong half the time and still make a fortune." It follows that "it's normal for lots of things to go wrong, break, fail, and fall."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 6**: Art dealer **Heinz Berggruen** acquired masterpieces "like index funds. They bought everything they could," and a few Picassos made the portfolio. **Disney**'s *Snow White* (83 minutes) offset more than 400 mostly money-losing cartoons. Hard data: per Correlation Ventures, of 21,000+ venture financings (2004–2014), "65% lost money" and ~0.5% returned 50x+, driving most industry returns. Per J.P. Morgan, "Forty percent of all Russell 3000 stock components lost at least 70% of their value and never recovered," while "7% of component companies" drove the index's 73-fold rise.

Within winners, tails recur: in 2018 Amazon (Prime/AWS) drove 6% of the S&P 500's returns and Apple (iPhone) ~7%. At the 2013 Berkshire meeting Buffett said he made most of his money on 10 of 400–500 stocks; Munger: "If you remove just a few of Berkshire's top investments, its long-term track record is pretty average."

## Contexts Within the Source

The claim reframes investor behavior: success is "determined by how you respond to punctuated moments of terror," doing "the average thing when all those around them are going crazy" (the Sue/Jim/Tom recession example). It returns in chapter 20: "the world is driven by tails—a few variables account for the majority of returns," which is why "there is little correlation between investment effort and investment results."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel notes the claim is "hard to deal with, even if you understand the math. It is not intuitive that an investor can be wrong half the time and still make a fortune," which "causes us to overreact" when things fail.
