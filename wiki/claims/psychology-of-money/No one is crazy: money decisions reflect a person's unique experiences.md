## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that **"People do some crazy things with money. But no one is crazy."** Every financial decision "makes sense to them in that moment," because people are "anchored to a set of views about how money works" formed by their own unique generational and personal experiences. "What seems crazy to you might make sense to me."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 1**: "Your personal experiences with money make up maybe 0.00000001% of what's happened in the world, but maybe 80% of how you think the world works." Housel cites economists **Ulrike Malmendier and Stefan Nagel** (2006), who found from 50 years of the Survey of Consumer Finances that "individual investors' willingness to bear risk depends on personal history" — people who came of age with high inflation or weak stock markets invest differently for life. "Not intelligence, or education, or sophistication. Just the dumb luck of when and where you were born."

Examples: JFK had "no first-hand knowledge of the Depression"; Bill Gross's career rode a generational collapse in interest rates; the poor buy lottery tickets because "buying a lottery ticket is the only time in our lives we can hold a tangible dream." Housel also stresses how *new* money concepts are — the 401(k) dates only to 1978, the Roth IRA to 1998 — "We're not crazy. We're all just newbies."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter is the book's opening move and supplies a refrain ("no one is crazy") that returns when explaining bubbles (chapter 17) and closes the book (chapter 20: "No one is crazy"). It grounds the soft-skill thesis by explaining *why* behavior, not logic, governs money.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel grants that people making these decisions "can be misinformed... can be bad at math... can have no idea what they're doing" — i.e., the decisions can be objectively wrong even while being subjectively sensible. He says of the Foxconn-worker anecdote, "I don't know what to make of this. Part of me wants to argue, fiercely."
