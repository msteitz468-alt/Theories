## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that **bubbles form, and individuals get hurt, because "investors often innocently take cues from other investors who are playing a different game than they are."** Assets do not have one rational price; the right price depends on the holder's time horizon. "Bubbles do their damage when long-term investors playing one game start taking their cues from those short-term traders playing another."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 16**: The price to pay for Google stock "depends on who 'you' are" — a 30-year holder, a day trader, etc. "Bubbles form when the momentum of short-term returns attracts enough money that the makeup of investors shifts from mostly long term to mostly short term." In 1999 a day trader could rationally pay any price for Yahoo! "as long as it moved in the right direction that day"; the 2004 housing bubble was driven by flippers (house flips "rose fivefold during the bubble") who didn't care about price-to-rent ratios.

The harm: Cisco hit $60 (a $600B valuation Burton Malkiel showed implied it would exceed the U.S. economy), but for a long-term investor "$60 was the only price available," set by traders "playing a different game." The fix: "going out of your way to identify what game you're playing." Housel wrote his own mission statement: "I am a passive investor optimistic in the world's ability to generate real economic growth... over the next 30 years."

## Contexts Within the Source

The claim extends to consumption — "So much consumer spending... is socially driven," and copying the lifestyle of someone "playing a different game" (the lawyer chasing partnership) leads "down a path of potential disappointment." It connects to chapter 1 ("no one is crazy") and chapter 18 (we can't see the stories in others' heads).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel is careful not to call short-term traders irrational: "I don't think you can call all of them irrational"; "Momentum attracts short-term traders in a reasonable way." The problem is not their behavior per se but long-term investors mistakenly imitating it.
