## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that **"The hardest financial skill is getting the goalpost to stop moving"** — developing a sense of "enough." Without it, "an insatiable appetite for more will push you to the point of regret," and people risk things that are invaluable (reputation, freedom, family, happiness) for money they do not need.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 3**: Opens with John Bogle's anecdote of Joseph Heller telling Kurt Vonnegut, of a billionaire host, "I have something he will never have… enough." Cautionary cases: **Rajat Gupta** (worth ~$100M) committed insider trading reaching for billionaire status; **Bernie Madoff**, already legitimately rich from market-making, ran his Ponzi scheme anyway; **Long-Term Capital Management** traders "risked what they did have and did need" (Buffett: "To make money they didn't have and didn't need, they risked what they did have and did need").

Housel's four points: (1) "If expectations rise with results there is no logic in striving for more"; (2) "Social comparison is the problem here" — the ceiling is so high "virtually no one will ever hit it"; (3) "'Enough' is not too little"; (4) "There are many things never worth risking, no matter the potential gain."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter sets up the next ("the most powerful tool for building enough is remarkably simple" — compounding) and connects to [[Wealth is what you don't see: it is income not spent, not visible consumption]] and [[Your savings rate matters more than your income or investment returns]]. Housel: "Happiness... is just results minus expectations."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel anticipates the objection that "enough" leaves "opportunity and potential on the table" and answers it directly: "I don't think that's right." He grants that "wanting to surpass your peers can be the fuel of hard work," but argues "life isn't any fun without a sense of enough."
