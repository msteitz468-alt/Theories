## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that the **broadly shared prosperity of 1945–~1973 set American expectations of comparable, middle-class lifestyles that persisted long after the economy stopped delivering them (from the 1970s on)** — and that this gap between sticky expectations and rising inequality drives the "big stretch" of household debt and present-day political discontent. "Expectations move slower than reality on the ground."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Postscript** (a 10-step narrative): After WWII, fear of renewed depression led to deliberately low interest rates (the Fed held short rates near 0.38%), the GI Bill, and a consumer-credit boom; combined with pent-up demand and a "hidden 1930s productivity boom," this produced a boom in which "the country got rich by making the poor less poor." "Real income for the bottom 20%... grew by a nearly identical amount as the top 5% from 1950 to 1980," and lifestyles converged (TV, catalogs, similar cars).

From the 1970s, growth became uneven: "Between 1993 and 2012, the top 1 percent saw their incomes grow 86.1 percent, while the bottom 99 percent saw just 6.6 percent growth." But the post-war *expectation* of comparable lifestyles stuck, so people like the illustrative "Peter" took on heavy debt to match the legitimately rich "Joe" — household debt-to-income climbed "from around 60% in 1973 to more than 130% by 2007" (the "big stretch"), ending in the 2008 crisis. Housel reads the Tea Party, Occupy Wall Street, Brexit, and Donald Trump as each "a group shouting, 'Stop the ride, I want off,'" amplified by social media.

## Contexts Within the Source

The postscript explains the cultural backdrop to the book's behavioral themes — especially social comparison (chapter 3) and "different games" (chapter 16). Housel says it is a deliberately "big events" narrative that "leaves out many details."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel repeatedly disclaims causal and moral verdicts: "it doesn't matter why it happened... It just matters that it did happen," and "You don't have to think that's morally right or wrong." He scoffs at "linking the rise of Trump to income inequality alone... And you should. These things are always layers of complexity deep." He also says he is "not pessimistic" — noting low unemployment, faster low-income wage growth, and stabilized college costs — while predicting the "this isn't working" mood "may stick around."
