## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that **history is a poor guide to the financial future**: "It is smart to have a deep appreciation for economic and investing history... But it is not, in any way, a map of the future." He names the **"historians as prophets" fallacy**: "An overreliance on past data as a signal to future conditions in a field where innovation and change are the lifeblood of progress."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 12**: Scott Sagan: "Things that have never happened before happen all the time." Investing "is not a hard science... It's a massive group of people making imperfect decisions" (Feynman: "Imagine how much harder physics would be if electrons had feelings"). Two dangers: (1) over-reliance makes you "miss the outlier events that move the needle the most" (Depression, WWII, dot-com, 9/11, 2008); (2) history "doesn't account for structural changes" — the 401(k), Roth IRA, modern venture capital, and the changing composition of the S&P 500 and recession spacing all postdate much historical data.

The key case is **Benjamin Graham**: *The Intelligent Investor*'s formulas mostly "do not work" now because Graham "discarded the formulas he presented in the previous edition" across five editions. Before he died Graham disavowed elaborate security analysis: "the situation has changed a great deal since then."

## Contexts Within the Source

Housel qualifies the claim with a nuance that connects to chapter 1: "The further back in history you look, the more general your takeaways should be." General human behavior under greed and fear "tend to be stable"; specific trades, sectors, and causal relationships are "evolution in progress." The chapter sets up chapter 13's room for error.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel does not advocate ignoring history ("That doesn't mean we should ignore history") and quotes John Templeton ("The four most dangerous words in investing are, 'it's different this time'") — before noting Templeton "admitted that it is different at least 20% of the time," and Michael Batnick's recursive rejoinder. The chapter holds both that overconfidence in "it's different" is dangerous and that the world genuinely does change.
