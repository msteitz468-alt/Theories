## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that **"wealth is what you don't see"**: it is "financial assets that haven't yet been converted into the stuff you see," i.e. income *not* spent. "The only way to be wealthy is to not spend the money that you do have. It's not just the only way to accumulate wealth; it's the very definition of wealth." He distinguishes **rich** (a current income/visible spending) from **wealthy** (hidden, unspent assets and options).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 9**: "Roger," who drove a Porsche, turned up one day in an old Honda — the Porsche "was repossessed after defaulting on his car loan." Seeing a $100,000 car tells you only that the owner "has $100,000 less than they did before they bought the car (or $100,000 more in debt)." Rihanna nearly went bankrupt and her advisor asked "if you spend money on things, you will end up with the things and not the money." Bill Mann: "the way to be rich is to spend money you have, and to not spend money you don't have."

Housel's diet analogy: being rich is doing the workout then eating the big meal; wealth is "turning down that treat meal." Because wealth is invisible, it is hard to learn by imitation — "Ronald Read became many people's financial role model" only after death.

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter bridges the Man in the Car Paradox (chapter 8) and the case for saving (chapter 10), and feeds the chapter 19 summary line "Less ego, more wealth." "Wealth is created by suppressing what you could buy today in order to have more... options in the future."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel grants that "there are, of course, wealthy people who also spend a lot of money on stuff," but notes that "even in those cases what we see is their richness, not their wealth." He does not present the rich/wealthy distinction as contested within the source.
