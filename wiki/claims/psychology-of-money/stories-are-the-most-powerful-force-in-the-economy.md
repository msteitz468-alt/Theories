## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that **"stories are, by far, the most powerful force in the economy"** — "the fuel that can let the tangible parts of the economy work, or the brake that holds our capabilities back." Economic capacity (tools, knowledge, factories) can be unchanged while output collapses, if the stories people tell themselves change.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 18**: An alien hovering over New York sees "about the same" economy in 2009 as 2007 — same buildings, factories, universities, patents, and *better* technology — yet households are "$16 trillion poorer," 10 million more are unemployed, and the stock market is halved. "There was one change the alien couldn't see... The stories we told ourselves about the economy." In 2007 the story was housing stability and prudent bankers; "In 2009 we stopped believing that story. That's the only thing that changed."

Housel distinguishes this **narrative damage** from tangible damage (Germany 1945's bombed factories; Japan's shrinking workforce): "In 2009 we inflicted narrative damage on ourselves, and it was vicious." Once "home prices will keep rising" broke, "mortgage defaults rose, then banks lost money... which led to layoffs... and on and on."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter pairs this with the **appealing fictions** claim ([[The bigger your appetite for a thing to be true, the more vulnerable you are to appealing fictions]]) and the point that "we form a complete narrative to fill in the gaps" of an incomplete worldview (Housel's one-year-old daughter; Kahneman on hindsight). It explains bubble dynamics from chapter 16.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel notes that we usually think growth comes from "tangible things—how much stuff do we have and what are we capable of?" — the intuitive view his claim corrects. He does not deny tangible capacity matters; his point is that stories govern whether that capacity is used.
