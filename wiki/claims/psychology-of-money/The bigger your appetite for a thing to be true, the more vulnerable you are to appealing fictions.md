## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that **"The more you want something to be true, the more likely you are to believe a story that overestimates the odds of it being true."** He calls these **"appealing fictions"**: beliefs that arise "when you are smart, you want to find solutions, but face a combination of limited control and high stakes." The defense is room for error: "The bigger the gap between what you want to be true and what you need to be true to have an acceptable outcome, the more you are protecting yourself."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 18**: Ali Hajaji burned his sick son with a stick on a folk remedy — "When you have no money, and your son is sick, you'll believe anything." The Great Plague of London drove Londoners to "prophecies... dreams, and old wives' tales" and "Infallible preventive pills." Financially: people heed track-record-less TV forecasters because "the stakes are so high"; "85% of active mutual funds underperformed their benchmark over the 10 years ending 2018" yet hold ~$5 trillion; Madoff "told a good story, and people wanted to believe it."

Housel notes even institutions do this: the Fed's last-2007 forecast bounded 2008 growth at +1.6% to +2.8%, but the economy contracted >2%, "meaning the Fed's low-end estimate was off by almost threefold," because "expecting anything worse is too painful to consider."

## Contexts Within the Source

The claim is one of two lessons Housel draws "about a story-driven world" (the other being [[Stories, not just tangible capacity, are the most powerful force in the economy]]). It reinforces why "room for error, flexibility, and financial independence... are indispensable" (chapters 10, 13). "There is no greater force in finance than room for error, and the higher the stakes, the wider it should be."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel grants the rational kernel behind the bias: "If there's a 1% chance that someone's prediction will come true, and it coming true will change your life, it's not crazy to pay attention." The error, he says, is that people "can't, or don't, calibrate low odds," defaulting "to a firm belief that what they want to be true is unequivocally true."
