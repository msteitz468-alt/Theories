## Claim Statement

Morgan Housel asserts that **"people are poor forecasters of their future selves,"** so long-term financial plans are undermined not only by a changing world but by changing personal goals. "It is one thing to say, 'We don't know what the future holds.' It's another to admit that you, yourself, don't know today what you will even want in the future."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 14**: Housel's hardworking friend fought his way to becoming a doctor, then told Housel repeatedly, "Awful career, man." He names the **End of History Illusion** (Harvard's Daniel Gilbert): "the tendency for people to be keenly aware of how much they've changed in the past, but to underestimate how much their personalities, desires, and goals are likely to change in the future." Gilbert: people "from age 18 to 68 underestimate how much they will change." (Tattoos removed, divorces, the five-year-old who wants to drive a tractor.)

Two recommendations: avoid the extreme ends of financial planning (the simplicity of having little, or the thrill of having everything, "wear off," while the downsides become "enduring regrets"); and accept changing your mind, abandoning sunk costs — Daniel **Kahneman** rewrote chapters from scratch because "I have no sunk costs." "Sunk costs... make our future selves prisoners to our past, different, selves."

## Contexts Within the Source

The claim qualifies the compounding thesis (Munger: "never interrupt it unnecessarily") by acknowledging that people who evolve "don't want to keep doing the same thing for decades on end." Balance "at every point in your life becomes a strategy to avoid future regret and encourage endurance."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Housel notes the tension this creates with compounding, which "works best when you can give a plan years or decades to grow," and offers no clean resolution: "There is no easy solution to this problem." He observes both that austere and luxury lifestyles can make people "perfectly happy" — each with its own risks.
