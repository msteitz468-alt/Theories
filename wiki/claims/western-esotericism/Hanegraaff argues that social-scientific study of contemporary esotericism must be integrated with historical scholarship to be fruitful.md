## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that sociologists and anthropologists studying contemporary esotericism, New Religious Movements, the "Cultic Milieu," and "Occulture" cannot fully understand their subject unless they combine social-scientific method with serious historical scholarship about the religious and intellectual traditions involved; an ahistorical social science of esotericism remains incomplete, though such an integrated approach is still "somewhat unusual."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"However, in order to achieve its full potential, such research should make optimal use of historical scholarship about the religious and intellectual traditions in question. As demonstrated, for example, by the American scholar Mary Farrell Bednarowski, already several decades ago, one cannot really understand contemporary New Religions unless one takes their worldviews seriously and places them in a historical context. Only in this manner will it be possible to see how traditional esoteric beliefs have been modified, transformed and put to new uses under the impact of new developments such as modernization or secularization. Such an integrated approach remains somewhat unusual in social scientific studies of esotericism, and will hopefully become more normal as the field develops." He frames this within his account of how the "Secularization Thesis" failed to predict the 1960s–70s vogue of esotericism, and how sociologists (e.g. Massimo Introvigne, J. Gordon Melton, Eileen Barker, James R. Lewis) and anthropologists (e.g. Tanya M. Luhrmann on ritual magic, Michael F. Brown on Channeling) have since built a substantial, though still historically under-integrated, body of research on NRMs, the Cultic Milieu, and Occulture.

## Contexts Within the Source
Closes the "The social sciences and popular culture" section of Chapter 8, functioning as Hanegraaff's summary prescription for how that discipline should relate to the study of esotericism, paralleling his earlier calls (in the religion/philosophy/science and arts sections) for combining disciplinary expertise with genuine engagement with esoteric material rather than dismissing or ignoring it.
