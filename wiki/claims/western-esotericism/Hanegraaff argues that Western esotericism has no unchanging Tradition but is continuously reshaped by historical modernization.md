## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues against presenting Western esotericism as a "universal worldview" grounded in timeless "enduring truths" exempt from history. He insists on historicity: esotericism has been radically transformed by a long chain of historical events — from the rise of Christianity through the Scientific Revolution, colonialism, and the internet — and of all these transformations, "modernization" (encompassing secularization and disenchantment) is the most decisive. He also diagnoses the emotional resistance to this historicism, tracing it to the Eranos circle's fear (especially in Mircea Eliade) that strict historical relativism reduces history to meaningless contingency — "the terror of history" — and to the threat historiography poses to religious belief, exemplified by Isaac Casaubon's 1614 philological demonstration that the Corpus Hermeticum was not ancient Egyptian wisdom but a late-antique text.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"In this chapter, we will emphasize the other side of the coin, by focusing on discontinuities and differences... against the tendency... of presenting Western esotericism as some universal worldview or spiritual perspective based on universal 'enduring truths' that are somehow exempt from history and change." On Casaubon: "in 1614, the great philologist and textual critic Isaac Casaubon demonstrated conclusively... that it could not have been written earlier than the first centuries of the Christian era. This was a heavy blow to the intellectual credibility of Renaissance Hermetism." On Eliade: "Eliade referred to this nihilist implication as 'the terror of history', and spent his life fighting against it." Conclusion: "everything we know about these currents contradicts the essentially conservative idea of a universal Tradition or an unchanging esoteric worldview... what we see in the history of Western esotericism... is continuous and mostly unpredictable change, transformation, renewal and creative invention."

## Contexts Within the Source
This is the framing argument of Chapter 7, contrasted with the "structural" chapters (Four and Five) on worldviews and ways of knowing. Hanegraaff explicitly balances it against those chapters at the chapter's close: the field is held together not by a stable essence but by its shared status as "rejected knowledge" since the Enlightenment.
