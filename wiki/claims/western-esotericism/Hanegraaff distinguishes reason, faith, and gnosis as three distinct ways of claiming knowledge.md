## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff proposes a threefold typology to classify claims of knowledge relevant to Western esotericism: "reason" (claims communicated in discursive language and independently testable — science, philosophy, scholarship), "faith" (claims communicated in discursive language but not independently testable — e.g. Christian dogma, accepted on authority), and "gnosis" (claims that are neither testable nor even communicable in discursive language, yet held with total conviction and treated as superior to the other two).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"If anybody claims to know something and wishes to be taken seriously [in philosophy/science]... he or she must be able to communicate what s/he claims to know by means of discursive language... and somehow it must be possible for others to test or evaluate whether it is true or not... the term 'reason' will be used as shorthand for this first approach." Of faith: "these are statements of belief that have to be accepted on the basis of authority and tradition, but cannot be tested independently in any way." Of gnosis: "there are claims of knowledge that go even a step further: like those in the previous category they cannot be tested independently, but remarkably enough, it is claimed that they cannot be communicated by discursive language either!... This third type... is of fundamental importance in the history of Western esotericism" (Ch. 5).

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff stresses Western esotericism does not simply reject reason and faith; rather its representatives typically hold that reason and faith are legitimate but incomplete, and that only gnosis reaches "the truth itself" — a position he grounds in the Corpus Hermeticum's distinction between logos (reasoned discourse), pistis (faith), and nous (mind, the faculty of gnosis).
