## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that Agrippa's Platonic cosmos still depended on the traditional Ptolemaic (geocentric, spatially "vertical") universe, and that the Copernican revolution made this spatial model untenable. Beginning with Giordano Bruno, God could no longer be pictured as residing "above the stars" but had to be reconceived as an omnipresent power permeating an infinite universe — a position he terms panentheism ("God is omnipresent in the world and the world is somehow contained in God").

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"In an infinite universe, the infinite God could no longer be found in any spatial location but had to be present everywhere, as an invisible power that permeates the whole of reality – a bit like the force of life that animates the body" (Ch. 4). Hanegraaff finds this "cosmic religiosity" already anticipated in the Hermetic literature of antiquity (quoting a passage urging the reader to "make yourself grow to immeasurable size... conceive yourself to be in all places at the same time... then you can understand God"), and traces its modern resonance to American transcendentalism, the Esalen "human potential" movement, and channelled texts such as Jane Roberts's "Seth Books."

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff frames panentheism as welcomed, especially since the 1960s, as an alternative both to traditional Christian otherworldliness/sin and to existentialist pessimism — congenial to environmentalist ethics and to the idea of human consciousness as "the vanguard of spiritual evolution."
