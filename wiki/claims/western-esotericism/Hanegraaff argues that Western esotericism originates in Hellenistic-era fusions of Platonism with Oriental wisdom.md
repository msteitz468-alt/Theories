## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that the origins of Western esotericism lie in the Hellenistic culture of late antiquity, where Platonism was transformed into a religious worldview centered on salvational *gnōsis*, under the widespread assumption ("Platonic Orientalism") that Plato's philosophy derived from ancient Oriental wisdom. He identifies three key currents from this period as foundational: Hermetism (the Egyptian Hermes Trismegistus tradition, Corpus Hermeticum, Asclepius), Gnosticism (Christian-context dualism of divine spark versus an ignorant demiurge, illuminated by the 1945 Nag Hammadi discovery), and Theurgy (Neoplatonic ritual practice for divine ascent, per Iamblichus and the Chaldaean Oracles).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The origins of Western esotericism are in the Hellenistic culture of late antiquity... Platonism was transformed into a religious worldview with its own mythologies and ritual practices, focused on the attainment of a salvational gnōsis... This widespread understanding of Platonism as 'religious wisdom from the East' may be referred to as Platonic Orientalism." On Hermetism: "the most important of these texts are the so-called Corpus Hermeticum... a larger work that has survived only in Latin, under the title Asclepius; and an initiatic text known as the Treatise of the Eight and the Ninth, discovered only as recently as 1945." On Gnosticism: "gnosticism has traditionally been perceived as a dualistic religion which taught that sparks of the divine light had become imprisoned in the world of matter." On Theurgy: "it clearly involved a ritual practice in which the gods were believed to manifest themselves and get in connection with the Neoplatonic practitioners," with Iamblichus as its "most central authority."

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff notes ongoing scholarly controversy — whether Hermetic writings reflect actual ritual communities or a purely literary phenomenon, and whether "gnosticism" should be discarded as a term after Nag Hammadi complicated the simple orthodoxy/heresy antithesis. He treats all three currents as sharing a concern with altered/ecstatic states of consciousness as the vehicle of direct experience of the divine.
