## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that Western esotericism's defining trait is "radical trans-disciplinarity": for historical reasons rooted in the Enlightenment-era demarcation of religion, philosophy and science, the field became "academically homeless" after the eighteenth century, and even today none of the traditional disciplines — not even the study of religion, where it has become best established over the last two decades — is by itself a sufficient academic context for studying its development, manifestations, and cultural significance.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Opening of Chapter Eight: "This book opened with an emphasis on the radical trans-disciplinarity of Western esotericism. For historical reasons that were explained in Chapter Three, this field of research became 'academically homeless' after the eighteenth century; and even today, none of the traditional disciplines – not even the study of religion, where it has established itself most clearly over the last two decades – is entirely sufficient as an academic context for studying its development and its manifestations, or analyzing its significance to Western culture." He adds that "the other side of the coin" is that this homelessness lets esotericism "set up a home base in any of the programmes that now exist in the humanities and (to a smaller extent) the social sciences," and states the chapter's purpose is to survey the field discipline-by-discipline (religion/philosophy/science, visual arts, literature, music, social sciences) to help students "find their way into this unfamiliar domain."

## Contexts Within the Source
This is the framing thesis for the whole of Chapter 8, which Hanegraaff structures as successive discipline-by-discipline surveys (religion-philosophy-science; visual arts; literature; music; social sciences and popular culture), each illustrating a facet of the same homelessness.
