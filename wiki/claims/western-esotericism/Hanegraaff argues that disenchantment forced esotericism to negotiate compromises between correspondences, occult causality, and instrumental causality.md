## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff distinguishes three pre-Enlightenment models of how the universe works — (1) "correspondences" (Plotinus's holistic cosmic sympathy, later identified by Antoine Faivre as the first intrinsic characteristic of esotericism), (2) "occult causality" (invisible forces/rays/spiritus, e.g. al-Kindi and Ficino), and (3) "instrumental causality" (mechanical, billiard-ball cause and effect). As instrumental causality became scientifically dominant (Max Weber's "disenchantment"), esotericism after the eighteenth century did not simply reject this model but developed creative compromises to preserve belief in spiritual reality without sacrificing scientific respectability — most decisively Emanuel Swedenborg's model of a spiritual world "wholly separate from, and yet in perfect sync with" the material world, which became the "bedrock assumption of spiritualism and occultism."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
On the three models: correspondences, per Plotinus, hold that "this whole universe is in a state of sympathy, and is like one living creature"; occult causality (al-Kindi, Ficino's universal spiritus) explains distant influence via invisible media; instrumental causality is "the familiar 'mechanical' or billiard-ball model." On Swedenborg: "This was accomplished by presenting the spiritual world as wholly separate from, and yet in perfect sync with, a material world that answered to the laws of post-Cartesian physics... it was a creative new departure based upon the best science of his day and age, and an excellent example of esoteric innovation." On the broader pattern: "the dominance of instrumental causality since the Enlightenment has resulted in multiple attempts at avoiding its materialist and reductionist implications by developing some new kind of Naturphilosophie... we therefore encounter complicated attempts at negotiating some kind of compromise between the conflicting options of instrumental causality, occult causality and correspondences." Examples cited include ether theories, Rupert Sheldrake's "morphic resonance," and Jung and Pauli's theory of synchronicity.

## Contexts Within the Source
Presented as the first of five "radical aspects of modernization" in Chapter 7 (correspondences/causality, expanding geographic horizon, evolutionism, psychology, and the religious supermarket), used to demonstrate historical discontinuity against claims of an unchanging esoteric worldview.
