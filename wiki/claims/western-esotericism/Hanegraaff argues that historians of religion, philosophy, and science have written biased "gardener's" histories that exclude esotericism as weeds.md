## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that the historiography of religion, philosophy and science has been written on the basis of normative distinctions between "correct" and "incorrect" versions of each field — producing church-centered histories of Christianity, philosophy overviews that treat Descartes as the start of "real" philosophy, and histories of science built on anachronistic real-vs-"pseudo" science divides. He characterizes this as the perspective of a "gardener" who cultivates favored plants while removing "weeds," and argues that the "new historiographies" since the 1990s (including the study of Western esotericism) instead take the perspective of a "biologist," treating the weeds and mushrooms of history as equally worthy of study — and insists this is not merely a difference of taste, since the gardener's view is "simply incorrect" from a scientific standpoint.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"In all three fields, the core problem is that their histories have been written on the basis of normative distinctions between 'correct' and 'incorrect' religion, philosophy and science." He gives the garden metaphor: historians "have been thinking like gardeners intent on cultivating their own flowers and plants while silently removing the weeds as much as possible," whereas the "new historiographies... look at that same garden of history from the perspective of the biologist: what they see is a complex biosystem that deserves attention in its own right." He states flatly: "the perspectives of the gardener and the biologist represent not just personal opinions: the former is simply incorrect." He urges students to decide "whether they want to be gardeners or biologists," and, invoking Kant's sapere aude, calls for the courage "to investigate it critically and independently" rather than trust standard textbooks uncritically.
