## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues, drawing on Tanya Luhrmann's cognitive theory of "prototypes," that scholars of esotericism actually reason by comparing candidate phenomena to a mental "best example" of the class rather than by applying explicit formal criteria (such as Antoine Faivre's famous four-plus-two characteristics). This produces three main, mutually incompatible models of what counts as "Western esotericism": (1) an "enchanted" pre-Enlightenment worldview flourishing in the early modern period (Faivre, Frances Yates); (2) a modern/postmodern "occult" phenomenon centered on post-Enlightenment social organizations; and (3) a universal "inner" spiritual dimension of religion as such ("religionism").

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Scholars have provided various sets of formal criteria to define what should or should not fall under the category of 'Western esotericism', but in reality they are almost always reasoning by prototype... Depending on the models they have in mind, certain historical currents may be included by some scholars but excluded by others, and this accounts for most of the confusion about the field and its boundaries. The three most common models underlying current concepts of Western esotericism appear to be the following: (1) an 'enchanted' pre-Enlightenment worldview... (2) a wide array of 'occult' currents... (3) a universal, 'inner' spiritual dimension of religion as such." He quotes Luhrmann's ostrich/sparrow-versus-cow example to illustrate prototype reasoning versus criterial reasoning.

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff walks through each model's strengths and weaknesses (Faivre and Yates for Model 1; social-scientific studies of the occult for Model 2; religionism for Model 3) and explicitly declines to adopt any of the three as the normative basis for his own Guide, opting instead for a pluralistic, non-prototypical approach to the field across its full historical range.
