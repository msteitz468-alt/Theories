## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that within "metaphysical mediation," Western esotericism produced two dominant, historically and geographically distinct paradigms: the "Platonic" model, a spatial "great chain of being" congenial to Catholic hierarchical unity, and the "Alchemical" model, a temporal narrative of transmutation congenial to Protestant experiences of historical rupture after the Reformation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
The Platonic model is "deductive: it begins with the whole and then proceeds to assign to everything its proper place downwards through the great chain of being," with humanity (per Pico della Mirandola) placed "in the middle of the world" able to choose upward or downward. The Alchemical model, by contrast, is "inductive: it begins with the most concrete, tangible realities of nature and the physical body, and works its way upwards," modeled on stages like the *nigredo* and drawing on Paracelsus and Jacob Böhme's theosophy, where "the body itself must be transmuted and reborn together with the soul" rather than escaped. Hanegraaff illustrates the contrast with an image: Platonic man stands on a bridge choosing left or right; Alchemical man stands before a chasm with no bridge yet built, and "must try, somehow, to build a bridge... always at risk of crashing down." He traces the Alchemical model's later influence through German Idealism (Schelling, Hegel) and into Jungian psychology, where alchemical transmutation imagery models psychological "individuation."

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff stresses the two models are analytic "ideal types" rather than strictly separated in practice — Renaissance *occulta philosophia* freely synthesized both — but that they carry genuinely different internal logics (spatial/deductive vs. temporal/inductive) rooted in Catholic and Protestant sensibilities respectively.
