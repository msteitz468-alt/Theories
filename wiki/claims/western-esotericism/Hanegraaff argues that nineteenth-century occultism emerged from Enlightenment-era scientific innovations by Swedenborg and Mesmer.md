## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues it is "no coincidence" that the two most influential forces of nineteenth-century Western esotericism — Emanuel Swedenborg's visionary theology of correspondences and Franz Anton Mesmer's Animal Magnetism — both originated in the work of trained Enlightenment scientists, not as unchanged survivals of older esoteric tradition. He traces three major developments from Mesmerism's induced "artificial somnambulism": Spiritualism (via the 1848 Fox sisters), the new sciences of psychology/psychiatry (the "unconscious," Charcot, Jung), and New Thought/Mind Cure (Phineas Quimby). Nineteenth-century "occultism" proper (Éliphas Lévi, Papus, Guénon and Traditionalism in France; Blavatsky's Theosophical Society, the Golden Dawn, Crowley in the anglophone world) then rediscovers older Renaissance and ancient materials but reconceptualizes them through this modern lens.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"It is no coincidence, then, that the two most influential forces of innovation in Western esotericism during the nineteenth century had their origins in the work of Enlightenment scientists." On occultism generally: "all the ancient, medieval and Renaissance traditions discussed above were rediscovered and reconceptualized by groups and individuals who had been influenced by spiritualist and somnambulist practice and tried to find a 'third way' between traditional Christianity and positivist science. This phenomenon is usually referred to as occultism." He distinguishes a Catholic-inflected "occultism of the right" in France (Lévi, Papus, Guénon's Traditionalism) from an anti-Christian "occultism of the left" in the anglophone world (Blavatsky, the Golden Dawn, Crowley).

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff stresses (against a "positivist cliché") that esoteric/occultist worldviews are not static survivals but are "thoroughly transformed" by modern cultural and intellectual developments, a theme he flags as fully developed in his later Chapter Seven ("Modernization"). He also notes psychology's own historical entanglement with the occult, only severed with twentieth-century psychoanalysis and behaviourism.
