## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that before discussing the practical dimension of Western esotericism, one must dispose of "the concept of 'magic' as a universal reified category pitted against 'religion' and 'science'." This magic–religion–science triad, he contends, was invented by armchair anthropologists and sociologists of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries (the schools of Tylor/Frazer and Mauss/Durkheim), building on Enlightenment polemics against "superstition" that were themselves indebted to Protestant polemics against "paganism" and "heresy." The triad is "riddled with ethnocentric and moralistic prejudice," producing systematic distortions in the study of religion generally and of esotericism in particular.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The idea that some practices are inherently 'magical', as opposed to 'religious' or 'rational/scientific', was created by armchair anthropologists and sociologists during the latter half of the nineteenth and the first half of the twentieth century... As a result, the famous magic–religion–science triad is riddled with ethnocentric and moralistic prejudice, leading to systematic distortions and misperceptions... Perhaps no other category has been more harmful to an adequate understanding of esotericism and its role in Western culture, and to the study of religion in general." He adds that practices commonly called "magic" fit easily within the categories of "religion" (e.g., invoking angels or demons) or "science" (e.g., medieval practitioners of magia naturalis studying occult forces in nature are practitioners of early natural philosophy). He notes the critique applies equally to attempts to rehabilitate "magic" as a positive category (an enchanted alternative to dogmatic religion or materialist science), since both moves smuggle in a normative "true" vs. "false" distinction that has no place in historical scholarship.

## Contexts Within the Source
This argument opens Chapter 6 ("Practice") as a methodological precondition for surveying esoteric practices, and Hanegraaff returns to it in his closing summary of the chapter: once the artificial "magic" category is discarded, "most of what used to be seen as magic may as well be described as religion (or, alternatively, as science or natural philosophy), whereas what used to be seen as religion appears to be full of the very same types of practice."
