## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that within his "Amplification" category of practice, techniques for training visualization — from Giordano Bruno's mnemonics to the Golden Dawn's Middle Pillar Ritual and astral travel — rest on a distinction, drawn by modern and contemporary esotericists, between mere "fantasy" and "true imagination": the latter is treated as a faculty of perception granting access to realities considered ontologically real, a view he links to Antoine Faivre's characteristic of "imagination/mediation" and to Henry Corbin's concept of the mundus imaginalis.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"It must be noted that modern and contemporary esotericists tend to distinguish between mere fantasy and true imagination: the latter is then considered to be a faculty of perception through which one gains access to realities that are ontologically real in some sense. From such a perspective (also implicit in Antoine Faivre's characteristic of 'imagination/mediation', very close to Henry Corbin's concept of mundus imaginalis), by learning to expand one's powers of visualization one is in fact training one's ability to receive true visions of other worlds." Hanegraaff illustrates the practice-history behind this with Giordano Bruno's mnemonic training of visualization, and with the Golden Dawn's Middle Pillar Ritual (visualizing the kabbalistic Tree of Life and "universal energy" descending through the sefirot) and astral travel (visualizing a hexagram as a "doorway" into "another world of the reified imagination"), drawing on Tanya Luhrmann's anthropological account of "interpretive drift" among London occultists.

## Contexts Within the Source
This claim sits within the "Amplification" category of Hanegraaff's eightfold typology of practice, distinguished from Ficino's astral magic (which manipulates external astral influences) as instead a technique for training an internal faculty. Hanegraaff notes the practice continues into contemporary New Age visualization manuals.
