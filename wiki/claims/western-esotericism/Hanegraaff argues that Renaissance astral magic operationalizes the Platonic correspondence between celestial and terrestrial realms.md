## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that the Platonic "great chain of being" was not merely contemplative but operative: practitioners like Marsilio Ficino believed influences continuously descend from the stars and could be deliberately channelled for benefit, giving rise to the practice of astral magic. Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa's *De occulta philosophia libri tres* (1533) is presented as the most complete synthesis of this "magical" downward movement together with the "mystical" upward return of the soul to God.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Human beings are subject, each second of their lives, to influences that are coming down from the stars according to the constantly changing astrological constellations. We can use these 'gifts' for our own benefit if we know how to channel them" (Ch. 4). Hanegraaff cites the Hermetic *Asclepius*'s account of ancient Egyptians drawing angelic and demonic powers into temple statues, and Thomas Aquinas's distinction between "talismans" (demonic, using linguistic signs) and "amulets" (licit, working by natural causes) — a distinction Ficino exploited to legitimate astral magic for healing. Agrippa's treatise "discussed the entire range of traditional... information about the powers of the elementary, celestial and super-celestial spheres of reality," aiming at a "deification" that recovers Adam's pre-Fall godlike powers rather than dissolving the self into God.

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff connects this Renaissance magical framework to later, secularized descendants: the Renaissance "magus" as prototype for the modern occultist/New Age ideal of "self-realization," "creating one's own reality," and the "fully realized human being" at the center of the universe — themes he says persist in contemporary popular culture (novels, comics, video games).
