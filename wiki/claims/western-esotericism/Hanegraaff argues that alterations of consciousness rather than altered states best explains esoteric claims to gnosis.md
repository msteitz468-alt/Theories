## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that claims of gnosis in Western esotericism are best understood through Imants Barušs's concept of "alterations of consciousness," which he prefers over the more familiar "altered states of consciousness" (ASCs) because ASC terminology falsely implies a stable baseline state and carries a 1960s countercultural/psychedelic bias, whereas consciousness is better understood as a continuum affected by a broad range of psychophysiological factors (sensory input/deprivation, focus, body chemistry), only one of which is drugs.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The better-known terminology of 'altered states of consciousness' (ASCs) carries problematic implications. It suggests that there must be some kind of baseline state of consciousness, whereas the evidence suggests rather that our consciousness is never entirely stable to begin with" (Ch. 5). He argues such experiences are "normal phenomena that are known to occur under specific conditions" (ritual, spiritual technique) rather than "weird anomalies," citing Plato's four types of *mania* (Phaedrus), Jacob Böhme's breakthrough "wrestling with God," the Enthusiasm/Schwärmerei of Pietist groups, and post-Enlightenment developments (Swedenborg's visionary claims, mesmerism/"artificial somnambulism," astral travel, Steiner's "akasha chronicles," New Age "channelling") as a continuous historical through-line of the same underlying mechanism.

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff distinguishes the pre-Enlightenment use of alterations of consciousness — where gnosis remains the "ineffable cherry on the cake" atop reason and faith — from the post-Enlightenment use, where such experiences are promoted as quasi-scientific "empirical proof" of spiritual realities in their own right, gnosis becoming "the whole cake." He calls for a "radical interdisciplinary" study combining anthropology, psychology, neurobiology, and cognitive studies with close textual work.
