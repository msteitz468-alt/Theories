## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that before the Enlightenment, esoteric authors treated gnosis as the capstone of a hierarchy that still required and respected reason and faith; after the Enlightenment, esotericism increasingly presented gnosis as a superior "spiritual" alternative set against reason (recast as reductionism) and faith (recast as blind belief in authority), even though contemporary esotericists in practice remain highly faith-driven and rarely apply the historical-critical scrutiny associated with "reason."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Pre-Enlightenment figures (Ficino, Pico, Agrippa, Bruno, Dee, Fludd, Khunrath, Swedenborg) were "highly erudite scholars" and devout Christians for whom gnosis was "an extra level built on the foundations" of reason and faith, not their replacement — Hanegraaff notes Agrippa "went out of his way to emphasize the absolute superiority of faith in Jesus Christ." After the Enlightenment, "esoteric authors tend to become much more aggressive in presenting their 'higher knowledge' as a superior alternative," exemplified by Blavatsky's *Isis Unveiled* (1877), a "virulent polemic against established 'science'... and 'religion'." Hanegraaff calls it "deeply ironic" that "contemporary esotericists tend to believe with as much devotion in their own traditions and authorities (e.g. inspired scriptures 'channelled' from spiritual entities) as traditional Christians believe in theirs," while "a consistent appeal to reason... is in fact quite rare," since historical criticism is "potentially more damaging to esoteric convictions than anything else."

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff frames this shift as part of the larger post-Enlightenment pattern where esotericism moves from being integrated within existing religious/scientific culture to functioning as a self-styled counterculture (echoing the "grand polemical narrative" discussed in Chapter 3).
