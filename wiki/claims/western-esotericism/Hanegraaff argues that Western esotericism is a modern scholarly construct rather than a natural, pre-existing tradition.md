## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that "Western esotericism" is not a natural, self-evident tradition that historians merely discovered, but a modern, artificial scholarly category retrospectively applied to a range of currents and ideas that were originally known by other names and not necessarily seen as belonging together.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"This means that 'Western esotericism' is not a natural term but an artificial category, applied retrospectively to a range of currents and ideas that were known by other names at least prior to the end of the eighteenth century... In short, 'Western esotericism' is a modern scholarly construct, not an autonomous tradition that already existed out there and merely needed to be discovered by historians." He notes the substantive term itself is recent: coined in German (*Esoterik*) in 1792, in French (*l'ésotérisme*) by 1828, in English by 1883, and that it is "only as recently as the later seventeenth century that we find the first attempts at presenting them as one single, coherent field."

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff immediately qualifies the claim: calling the category a construct "does not mean... that there is nothing 'real' about the field." The category arose because scholars noticed genuine structural similarities among a wide variety of thinkers and movements, similarities explored further in his later chapters on worldviews and knowledge.
