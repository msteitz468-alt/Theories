## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that magic, astrology, and alchemy — often lumped together as "occult sciences," a label he avoids — cannot be understood through the modern religion/science divide, because in pre-Enlightenment contexts the boundaries between the two were "much more ambiguous and permeable." He traces "magic" as split between a demonological Judeo-Christian polemical concept and the medieval concept of *magia naturalis* (natural magic), which tried to withdraw nature's wonders from theological/demonic explanation. Classical astrology he presents as originally "the most comprehensive scientific theory of antiquity," a rigorous causal cosmology later dismissed as pagan superstition and then revived via Arabic-preserved learning after the fall of Toledo (1085). Alchemy he treats as inseparable from what became chemistry, combining laboratory transmutation practice with religious narratives of spiritual rebirth (e.g., Christ as "philosophers' stone").

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"In modern secular culture we are used to delegating these concerns to the separate spheres of 'religion' and 'science', but in pre-Enlightenment contexts the boundaries were much more ambiguous and permeable... Often referred to by the problematic label 'occult sciences', which we will be avoiding here." On magic: "hence magic has always remained a deeply ambiguous category hovering between demonism and natural science." On astrology: it "had been developed into a rigorous causal model of cosmology... described as 'the most comprehensive scientific theory of antiquity'." On alchemy: "it is practically impossible in this period to separate alchemy from what we would now see as chemistry, and hence the contemporary term 'chymistry' has been proposed."

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff situates all three within the broader medieval "scientific revolution" triggered by the translation of Arabic-preserved Greek learning after the Christian conquest of Toledo (1085), and traces each domain's later exile from official science during the eighteenth century and its post-WWII partial rehabilitation (astrology via Jung's "synchronicity"; alchemy via Jungian/Traditionalist reinterpretation, which he calls "esoteric rather than scholarly in nature").
