## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that the incommunicability of gnosis is not accidental obscurantism but a consistent, structural feature of the claim itself: sources describing gnosis characteristically produce "stammering expressions of amazement" and inadequate analogies, and insist that genuine understanding requires having the experience oneself, not merely hearing about it.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
He quotes the Hermetic dialogue *The Ogdoad and the Ennead*: "I see, yes, I see unspeakable depths... I want to speak! Fear holds me back!... I have seen. It is impossible to express this in words." He notes the Islamic Platonist Suhrawardī told Aristotelian rivals that rational knowledge, being restricted to the "lower and secondary realm of 'darkness,'" cannot grasp the "superior and primary reality of 'light'" — one must instead "engage in mystical disciplines" to see for oneself. Corpus Hermeticum X: "the knowledge [gnōsis] of it is divine silence and suppression of all the senses. One who has understood it can understand nothing else." Hanegraaff notes scholarship has largely avoided the question "what is gnosis?" (unlike "what is gnosticism?") precisely because of this claimed incommunicability.

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff situates this within his broader argument that unusual bodily/psychological conditions ("alterations of consciousness") underlie these claims, and that a rich ancient technical vocabulary (ekstasis, enthousiasmos, mania, etc.) existed to describe them, indicating the phenomena were common rather than anomalous.
