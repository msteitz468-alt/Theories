## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that Christian apologists, faced with the pagan taunt that Christianity was "new" and therefore not credible, answered by adapting Platonic Orientalism: claiming that Plato and other gentile sages had ultimately drawn on the ancient wisdom of Moses (later fulfilled in Christ), so that pagan philosophers could be regarded as "unconscious Christians." This apologetic strategy resurfaced in the Renaissance as two distinguishable programmes: Marsilio Ficino's revolutionary prisca theologia ("ancient theology"), which highlighted Zoroaster rather than Moses as the earliest authority and was corrected in a more orthodox direction by Giovanni Pico della Mirandola's Christian kabbalah; and Agostino Steuco's conservative philosophia perennis (1540), which held that universal truth had always been available to humankind through the one Church, with no need for reform. Both were, in Hanegraaff's account, apologetic counter-narratives designed to defend Christian orthodoxy by claiming continuity with, rather than opposition to, ancient pagan wisdom.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Hanegraaff writes that Christian apologists such as "Justin Martyr, Tatian, Clement of Alexandria, Origen or Eusebius of Caesarea argued that all those gentile philosophers had ultimately depended on the ancient wisdom of the Hebrews, codified by Moses at Mount Sinai, which had now attained its final and full splendour in the teachings of Jesus Christ." He quotes Augustine: "The very thing which is now called the Christian religion was with the ancients... from when on the true religion, which already existed, began to be called the Christian." On the Renaissance revival: "This Renaissance revival of Platonic Orientalism became known as prisca theologia ('the ancient theology') or philosophia perennis ('the perennial philosophy'): two terms that have often been conflated but should be clearly distinguished." He notes Ficino followed Gemistos Plethon in "highlighting not Moses but Zoroaster as the earliest authority of ancient wisdom," while Pico claimed Moses received at Sinai a secret Kabbalah "from this supreme wisdom that all the pagan sages had derived anything of enduring value," including "the name of Jesus" — an argument Hanegraaff notes "finds absolutely no support in modern research of Jewish kabbalah." Of Steuco: "he simply stated that the universal and eternal truth had always been available to mankind and would always remain so... Roman Catholicism had preserved the ancient wisdom intact and inviolate." Hanegraaff concludes: "the revival of Platonic Orientalism could have either revolutionary or conservative implications in a Roman Catholic context."
