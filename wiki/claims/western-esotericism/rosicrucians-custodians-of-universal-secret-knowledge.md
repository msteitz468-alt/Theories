## Claim Statement

Hall presents the **Rosicrucians** (the Fraternity of the Rose Cross) as a secret brotherhood of adepts who claimed possession of a "book of universal knowledge" and carried the perennial secret doctrine forward into the modern era — encoding it in ciphers, symbols, and (he argues) the works attributed to Francis Bacon.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He identifies the Tarot as "possibly the very book of universal knowledge which the members of the order claimed to possess," noting that the **Rota Mundi** of the early Rose Cross manifestoes is an anagram of *Taro* (Rota → Taro). He reports W. F. C. Wigston's claim that **Sir Francis Bacon** "employed the Tarot symbolism in his ciphers" — the Tarot numbers 21, 56, and 78 recurring in Bacon's cryptograms, and Bacon's Christian name appearing "21 times on page 56" of the 1623 Shakespearian Folio's Histories — part of Hall's broader treatment of the Rosicrucian/Baconian authorship and cipher question.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hall presents the Bacon-cipher and Rosicrucian-authorship material as the findings of named investigators (Wigston and others) and frames much of it with "possibly"/"undoubtedly"-style esoteric inference rather than documentary proof.
