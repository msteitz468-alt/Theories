## Claim Statement

Hall's unifying thesis: a single "secret doctrine" or "Universal Wisdom" — a transcendental knowledge of the cosmos and its "invisible ultimate cause" — has existed since the earliest civilizations and been preserved and transmitted, in an esoteric current, by the Mystery schools and the secret societies that succeeded them.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He writes that the Mysteries "claimed to be the guardians of a transcendental knowledge so profound as to be incomprehensible save to the most exalted intellect," whose dignity and "claim to possession of Universal Wisdom are attested by the most illustrious philosophers of antiquity, who were themselves initiated." Neo-Platonism, he says, "recognized the existence of a secret and all-important doctrine which from the time of the earliest civilizations had been concealed within the rituals, symbols, and allegories of religions and philosophies." The whole encyclopedia maps this current across Egyptian, Hermetic, Pythagorean, Qabbalistic, Rosicrucian, and Masonic forms.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hall concedes little information survives because the Mysteries enforced secrecy "on pain of death" and never wrote the doctrine plainly — so his case rests on the testimony of initiated philosophers and on symbolic interpretation rather than direct documentation.
