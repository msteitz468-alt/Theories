## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues there is no single "esoteric worldview," but that answers to the basic questions about God, the world, and humanity's place between them fall into a limited number of structural types. He proposes a fundamental distinction between "metaphysical radicals," who think in sharp either/or terms, and "metaphysical mediators" (the more common Western esoteric option), who think in both/and terms.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"On the one hand, we come across 'metaphysical radicals' who think in terms of sharp either/or choices. On the other hand – and much more frequently, in the context of Western esotericism – we find 'metaphysical mediators' who think, rather, in terms of both/and" (Ch. 4). Radicalism itself splits into pantheistic/monistic (e.g. Salinger's Teddy character voicing Advaita Vedanta, quantum mysticism) and dualistic (e.g. Rosicrucians van Rijckenborg and de Petri, gnostic/Manichaean world-rejection) variants, both agreeing that "the World is the problem" but differing on whether the solution is realizing its unreality (pantheist) or fighting to escape it (dualist). Hanegraaff notes radical dualism has historically correlated with militant sectarian violence (Cathars, Bogomils, Order of the Solar Temple, Heaven's Gate), though such "spiritual extremism" is rare in the field as a whole.

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff frames these as heuristic "structural models or ideal types" for organizing the history of ideas, explicitly warning they are "seldom, if ever, encountered in pure or perfect form" and should not be mistaken for a single essential "esoteric worldview," which he says "does not exist."
