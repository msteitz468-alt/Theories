## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that Renaissance intellectuals — chiefly George Gemistos Plethon, Marsilio Ficino, and Giovanni Pico della Mirandola, followed by Christian kabbalists like Johannes Reuchlin and the great synthesizer Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa — created what "has been called the 'basic referential corpus' of Western esotericism" by fusing ancient pagan learning, Platonic Orientalism, and the newly discovered Jewish kabbalah into a Roman Catholic theological and philosophical framework. He frames this intellectual innovation as itself a side effect of geopolitical upheaval: the Christian conquest of Toledo (1085) opening access to Arabic science, the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople (1453) sending Greek manuscripts to Italy, and the 1492 expulsion of Jews from Spain sending kabbalists to Italy.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"During the period of the Renaissance, a range of creative and influential thinkers took up the task of synthesizing ancient 'pagan' learning and religious speculation with Christian thought, along with the newly discovered Jewish kabbalah. In so doing, they created what has been called the 'basic referential corpus' of Western esotericism. This phenomenon of intellectual innovation was essentially a side effect of military conquests and geopolitical changes." Plethon is "a living embodiment of... Platonic Orientalism"; Ficino's translations "laid the groundwork for a large-scale Renaissance revival of Platonism – or more precisely, Platonic Orientalism"; Pico claimed "all the fundamental truths of Christianity were already contained... in the secret tradition of kabbalah"; Agrippa's *De occulta philosophia libri tres* (1533) integrated the three cosmic "worlds" within a Christian kabbalistic framework.

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff traces two offshoots from Pico: speculative number symbolism and Christian kabbalah (via Reuchlin, Postel, Agrippa), and notes that Giordano Bruno's post-Copernican infinite universe eventually destabilized the whole geocentric framework this synthesis assumed. He treats this corpus as distinct from, though preparatory to, the later German-centered Naturphilosophie/Christian theosophy tradition (Paracelsus, Böhme) discussed in the following section of the same chapter.
