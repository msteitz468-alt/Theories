## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that religion, including esotericism, is "much more than belief" — it is something people practice (prayer, ritual, pilgrimage, etc.) — yet scholarship has systematically neglected esoteric practice in favor of esoteric doctrine, for four compounding reasons: the problem of sources, the problem of verbal description, a (crypto-)Protestant bias, and the problem of method.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Religion is much more than belief... religion is something that one does... What do people involved in esotericism actually do? Perhaps no other aspect of the field is so difficult to study and understand." He lists: (1) "the problem of sources" — practices are transmitted orally and rarely written down in detail, so we know beliefs better than practices; (2) "the problem of verbal description" — even simple rituals are "overdetermined with countless details" that resist being captured in words; (3) "the problem of (crypto)Protestant bias" — classical religious studies inherited Protestant polemics against Catholicism as "crypto-'pagan' practice," producing "a structural over-emphasis on doctrine and belief and a corresponding lack of attention to ritual"; (4) "the problem of method" — even anthropologists skilled in participant research have rarely applied such approaches to historical esoteric material. He concludes: "our knowledge of esoteric practice is considerably less developed than that about the history of esoteric ideas or organizations."

## Contexts Within the Source
This is the opening methodological framing of Chapter 6, presented before the eight-category typology of practice, and functions as Hanegraaff's justification for why the chapter can only be a preliminary "first attempt at mapping largely uncharted territory."
