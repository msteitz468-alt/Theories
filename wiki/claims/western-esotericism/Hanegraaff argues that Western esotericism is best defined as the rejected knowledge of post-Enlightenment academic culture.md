## Claim Statement
Rather than adopting any of the three prototype models, Hanegraaff argues that Western esotericism is best understood negatively, as the "chief casualty of academic specialization after the eighteenth century": a domain set apart as "rejected knowledge" because it was judged incompatible with normative post-Enlightenment concepts of religion, rationality, and science. This exclusion was the outcome of centuries of apologetic and polemical battles, not an accident, and it is offered as "the closest we will get to a definition of Western esotericism."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"What initially sets it apart is its modern status as 'rejected knowledge': it contains precisely everything that has been consigned to the dustbin of history by Enlightenment ideologues and their intellectual heirs up to the present, because it is considered incompatible with normative concepts of religion, rationality and science... That Western esotericism is the academy's dustbin of rejected knowledge does not imply that it is just a random collection of discarded materials without any further connection." He defines the field as (1) set apart by mainstream religious and intellectual culture as the "other" by which it defines its own identity, and (2) characterized by worldviews and epistemologies at odds with normative post-Enlightenment intellectual culture.

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff notes this process of exclusion "did not just happen overnight" but was the final outcome of a long history of apologetic and polemical battles beginning in late antiquity — the subject of his Chapter Three — and that the rejected domain nonetheless has recognizable, coherent characteristics, explored in his chapters on worldviews and knowledge (Chapters Four and Five).
