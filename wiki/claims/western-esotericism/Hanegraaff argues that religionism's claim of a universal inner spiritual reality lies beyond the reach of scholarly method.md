## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that "religionism" — the study of esotericism as evidence of one universal, inner spiritual dimension underlying all religions — rests on a claim (that such a hidden absolute reality exists) that scholarly method cannot verify or falsify, and that the empirical-historical study of esotericism must instead practice "methodological agnosticism," while also noting that religionism flattens genuine historical differences between esoteric currents.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Scholarly methods... are 'exoteric' by definition and can only study what is empirically available to observers regardless of their personal convictions: the academy has no instruments for gaining direct access to the true and absolute nature of reality that is claimed to exist according to this model... The Absolute or the Divine is simply not a possible object of research... it is logically more consistent to admit that we simply do not know – and cannot know. This position... is technically known as 'methodological agnosticism'." He adds that "there are in fact enormous differences between various 'esoteric' currents and ideas in different historical periods and social contexts," citing Hermetic texts, Böhme, Swedenborg, Crowley, and contemporary New Agers as examples religionists flatten into illustrations of one eternal truth.

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff traces religionism's influence through Mircea Eliade's postwar American school and names Henry Corbin, Antoine Faivre's earlier work, Arthur Versluis, and Nicholas Goodrick-Clarke as scholars shaped by it, while noting the mainstream of scholarship has moved away from religionism since the 1990s. He uses the same critique to reject conflating "Western esotericism" with a supposed parallel "Oriental Esotericism," arguing comparative religionist study of inner religious experience is an independent field that does not need, and does not coincide with, the esotericism label.
