## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that the legal separation of church and state since the eighteenth century created a "free market" of competing religions and, further, made it possible for esoteric "spiritualities" to exist entirely independent of organized "religions," as individual ad hoc syncretic constructions. Distinguishing "esoteric religion" (all available esoteric ideas/practices), "esoteric religions" (specific organizations, e.g. the Theosophical Society), and "esoteric spiritualities" (individual personal mixtures), he argues this reverses the pre-modern situation where esoteric spiritualities (e.g. Ficino's Platonic Christianity, Böhme's Christian theosophy) had to be embedded within a specific church. He credits Émile Durkheim with foreseeing this trend of "individual religions that the individual institutes for himself," calling the resulting phenomenon the "heretical imperative" (from Greek haeresis, "choice").

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"[T]he legally enforced separation between church and state made it possible for religious minorities to establish themselves as new organizations... The result was a new situation of religious competition in the context of an emerging 'free market' of larger and smaller religions, with citizens in the role of religious consumers." "It became possible for spiritualities to exist as entirely individual forms of syncretism without any organizational structure at all: spiritualities independent from any religions (while still recognizable as forms of religion!)." Quoting Durkheim: "'Some people today pose the question whether such religions are not destined to become the dominant form of religious life – whether a day will not come when the only cult will be the one that each person freely practices in his innermost self'." "This autonomization and individualization of esoteric spiritualities, in contrast to their traditional embeddedness in established religions, is a final innovation that has radically changed the landscape of Western esotericism... referred to as the 'heretical imperative'... for even the most traditional and well-established churches are no longer self-evident foundations."

## Contexts Within the Source
This is the fifth and final of Hanegraaff's modernization transformations ("The religious supermarket"), closing the chapter. He explicitly ties it back to the book's overarching thesis (Chapters One and Three) that what unifies the field of Western esotericism across all this change is its shared status as "rejected knowledge" since the Enlightenment.
