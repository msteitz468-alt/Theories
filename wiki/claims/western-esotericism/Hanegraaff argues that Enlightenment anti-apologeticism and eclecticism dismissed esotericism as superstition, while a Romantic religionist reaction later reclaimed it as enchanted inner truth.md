## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that German Enlightenment eclecticism (Christian Thomasius, Christoph August Heumann, and above all Johann Jacob Brucker's Historia critica philosophiae, 1742–4) inherited Protestant anti-apologetic logic and used it to expel the entire domain of "Western esotericism" from serious philosophical and religious discourse as mere pseudo-philosophy/pseudo-religion — "superstition" rather than a target that was really pagan-derived rather than simply Christian, contra the popular assumption (which he corrects via Voltaire) that Enlightenment reason simply attacked Christian irrationality. This academic dismissal made esotericism "academically homeless" through the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. He then argues that a Romantic and later Jungian/Eranos "religionist" reaction (Mesmerism, somnambulism, C. G. Jung, the Eranos circle of Scholem/Corbin/Eliade, and finally Antoine Faivre) reversed this dismissal, recasting the same rejected material as evidence of a genuine "enchanted" inner spiritual dimension of the soul opposed to rationalist "daylight" disenchantment — a religionist position he treats as equally anti-historical, mirror-imaging rather than correcting the Enlightenment's error.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
On Heumann's 1715 dismissal: "'So adieu, dear Philosophia Chaldaeorum, Persarum, Egyptiorum, &c... superstitious idiocies belong in no better library.'" On Brucker: his Historia critica philosophiae characterized the esoteric domain "as the continuation of pagan religion concealed as Christianity... a false religion, not based on Revelation," and after Brucker these currents "began to vanish from academic textbooks... they became 'academically homeless.'" On the real Enlightenment target: quoting Voltaire, "superstition was born from religion, 'as the very foolish daughter of a wise and intelligent mother'"; "'Born in paganism, adopted by Judaism, superstition infected the Christian church from the very beginning'" — hence Peter Gay's characterization of the Enlightenment as "'The Rise of Modern Paganism' is therefore spectacularly off the mark." On the Romantic reversal: Romantics saw somnambulism as revealing "empirical facts about nature and the soul that were ignored by Enlightenment ideologues," defending "the scientific superiority of an 'enchanted' worldview." Jung's account "traced a continuous spiritual tradition... from Gnosis and Neoplatonism in late antiquity, via medieval alchemy to Renaissance traditions such as Paracelsianism, and from there to German Romantic mesmerism and, finally, modern Jungian psychology"; Eranos "became by far the most influential twentieth-century representative of religionism." Hanegraaff's summary: "Both approaches were based on ultimately anti-historical foundations: the former chose to ignore any historical currents and ideas that were not considered reasonable and scientific enough, whereas the latter claimed that esotericism was really about a supra-historical 'inner' or spiritual dimension, not about any 'external' influences or developments."
