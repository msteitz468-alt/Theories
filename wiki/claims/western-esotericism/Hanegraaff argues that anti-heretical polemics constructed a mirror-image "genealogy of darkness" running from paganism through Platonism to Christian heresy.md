## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff argues that the inclusive apologetic tradition (prisca theologia/philosophia perennis) always had a "reverse mirror image" in an exclusive anti-heretical tradition, which built historical genealogies tracing all heresy back to a single demonic origin. Irenaeus made Simon Magus the original arch-heretic; sixteenth-century anti-witchcraft authors such as Johann Weyer extended this genealogy back through Simon to Noah's son Ham/Misraim, identified with Zoroaster the "inventor of magic" — producing a "Platonic Orientalism-in-reverse" in which Plato and later Platonists are cast as the channel through which demonic paganism, rather than divine wisdom, infiltrated Christianity as gnostic heresy. Hanegraaff frames this construction (citing Karen L. King) as serving internal doctrinal boundary-work rather than responding to a real external enemy, and stresses that such "mnemohistorical" simplifications proved far more socially effective than the real, much more complicated historical relationship between paganism and gnosticism.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"anti-heretical strategies 'were devised not in the face of a clear external enemy, but to deal with an internal crisis of differentiation... The polemicists needed to create sharp lines of differentiation because in practice the boundaries were not so neat'" (quoting Karen L. King). On Weyer: "'From Simon, as though from a seed pod, there sprouted forth in long succession the monstrous Ophites, the shameless gnostics..., the impious Valentinians, Cardonians, Marcionists, Montanists, and many other heretics'"; Simon in turn traced to "Noah's son Ham and his son Misraim, believed to be identical to Zoroaster, the inventor of magic." Hanegraaff: "such 'genealogies of darkness' became a staple of later anti-witchcraft literature, and show how paganism and gnostic heresy could be conflated to a point of virtual identity in the context of a Platonic Orientalism-in-reverse: instead of a narrative of divine wisdom originating with Moses, we now get narratives of demonic infiltration originating with Zoroaster." He adds that "the simplifications of mnemohistorical narrative tend to be much more effective than the careful reconstructions of historians." He also traces the Protestant intensification of this pattern: Jacob Thomasius's "anti-apologeticism" made the "eternity of the world" doctrine the single root error behind all pagan/heretical belief (emanation, dualism, pantheism, materialism), a framework Ehregott Daniel Colberg then used in Platonisch-Hermetisches Christenthum (1690–1) — described by Hanegraaff as effectively the first "history of Western esotericism" — to depict "Platonic-Hermetic Christianity" as vermin crawling from a single "Platonic egg."

## Contexts Within the Source
Covered in "The anti-heretical position" and "Protestantism"/"Anti-apologeticism" sections of Chapter 3; Hanegraaff presents it as the second pole of the paradoxical Roman Catholic situation on the eve of the Reformation, later intensified and systematized by German Protestant anti-apologeticism (Jacob Thomasius, Colberg), which he treats as feeding directly into Enlightenment historiography.
