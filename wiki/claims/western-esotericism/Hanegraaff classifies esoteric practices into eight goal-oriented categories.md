## Claim Statement
Hanegraaff proposes, as a first attempt at mapping the largely uncharted territory of esoteric practice, that the wide range of practices found in Western esotericism can be organized under eight non-mutually-exclusive headings according to their intended goals: (1) Control, (2) Knowledge, (3) Amplification, (4) Healing, (5) Progress, (6) Contact, (7) Unity, and (8) Pleasure.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The wide range of practices in the context of Western esotericism will be discussed here under eight headings, according to their intended goals: (1) Control; (2) Knowledge; (3) Amplification; (4) Healing; (5) Progress; (6) Contact; (7) Unity; (8) Pleasure... it should be emphasized from the outset that they are not meant to be mutually exclusive." Examples: Control covers amulets, talismans, love charms, and Crowley's "Science and Art of causing Change to occur in conformity with Will"; Knowledge covers the medieval ars notoria, John Dee's angel conjuration, and divination (chiromancy, geomancy, astrology, catoptromancy); Amplification covers Ficino's astral magic (De vita coelitus comparanda) and Golden Dawn visualization techniques such as the Middle Pillar Ritual; Healing covers Paracelsus, Mesmer's animal magnetism, and modern holistic-health movements; Progress covers Masonic initiatory ritual and the disciplined "working on oneself" of Gurdjieff and Steiner; Contact covers Neoplatonic theurgy, medieval angelic/demonic "ceremonial magic," Swedenborg, and New Age channeling; Unity covers panentheistic experiences of cosmic oneness, illustrated by Corpus Hermeticum XI–XIII and Jean Houston's account of "cosmic unity"; and Pleasure covers ritual as "serious play," exemplified by Freemasonry and contemporary Wicca.

## Contexts Within the Source
Hanegraaff frames this typology explicitly as provisional ("the present chapter cannot do more than make a first attempt at mapping largely uncharted territory") and stresses the categories overlap: e.g., practices of "knowledge" may serve "control," "healing," or "progress" simultaneously. He presents it after arguing (see the companion claim on the "magic" category) that the practices covered here should not be segregated into a separate "magical" domain apart from religion and science.
