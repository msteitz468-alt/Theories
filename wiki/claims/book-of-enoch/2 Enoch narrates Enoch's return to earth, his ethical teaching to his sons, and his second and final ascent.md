# 2 Enoch narrates Enoch's return to earth, his ethical teaching to his sons, and his second and final ascent

## Claim Statement

2 Enoch narrates that after receiving God's creation-secrets and writing 366 heavenly books, Enoch is sent back to earth for exactly thirty days to instruct his sons and household. He delivers extensive ethical and theological teaching — on judgment, predestination, care for the poor, prohibition on oaths, and monotheism — then ascends permanently before a crowd of two thousand witnesses. The text's closing chapters record his age (365 years), the date of his final ascent (the sixth day of the first month, matching his birth), and the memorial feast established by his sons.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 33–36 (the thirty-day mandate)**: God tells Enoch: "I give you a period of thirty days to spend in your house … so that all may hear from you what was spoken by my face … And after thirty days I shall send my angel for you." Enoch is sent down by night and returns to his bed; Methuselah keeps watch.

**Chapters 39–66 (the ethical discourses)**: Enoch addresses his sons and, later, a large assembly. The teaching includes:
- *No intercessor*: "Do not say: 'Our father is standing before God, and is praying for our sins.' For there is no helper for any man who has sinned." (Ch. 53) — a statement the editor identifies as marking the core pre-Christian Jewish stratum.
- *Predestination of souls*: "Every man who has been in his mother's womb has had a place prepared for the repose of that soul, and a measure predetermined of how much it is intended that a man be tried in this world." (Ch. 49)
- *Moral scales and judgment*: At the great judgment, "every weight, every measure and every makeweight will be as in the market … every one shall learn his own measure." (Ch. 44)
- *Care for the poor, widows, orphans*: Repeated throughout Chs. 50–63, presented as the primary criterion for eternal reward.
- *Prohibition on oaths*: "I do not swear by any oath, neither by heaven nor by earth … let them swear by the words, Yea, yea, or else, Nay, nay." (Ch. 49) — a direct verbal parallel to Matthew 5:37.
- *Books as inheritance*: Enoch charges his sons to copy and distribute the 366 books across all generations and nations.
- *The eschatological age*: After six thousand years and a seventh-thousand-year sabbath, an endless eighth age begins in which the righteous "will live eternally, and there will be no labor, nor sickness, nor humiliation, nor anxiety." (Ch. 65)

**Chapter 64–67 (the final ascent)**: Two thousand people gather to say farewell to Enoch. He blesses them. The Lord sends darkness upon the earth; under cover of it, the angels take Enoch up to the highest heaven. When the darkness lifts, the people find only a scroll inscribed "The God of the Spiritual." Enoch's sons build an altar at the place of his ascent (Achuzan) and hold a three-day feast.

**Chapter 68 (summary and dating)**: Enoch was born on the 6th day of the month Tsivan; lived 365 years; was first taken to heaven on the 1st of Tsivan; remained 60 days; returned for 30 days; and ascended permanently on the 6th of Tsivan — the same day and hour he was born.

## Cross-source connections

The portrait of Enoch as the possessor and transmitter of secret books parallels the role ascribed to him in [[The Gods of Eden (Bramley)]] and implicitly in [[Escaping from Eden (Wallis)]], where pre-diluvian knowledge-keeping figures are read as evidence of hidden custodians of advanced information. The "no intercessor" doctrine and the absence of a redemption figure are used by the scholarly apparatus to distinguish 2 Enoch's Jewish core from later Christianizing layers — relevant context for [[Fallen angels, the Serpent, and Lucifer as misunderstood cosmic forces (Blavatsky)]], which draws on the Enochian tradition to reconstruct a pre-orthodox theology. The ethic of the poor and the moral-scales judgment image are structurally parallel to Egyptian *Ma'at* / weighing-of-the-heart traditions noted in comparative mythology.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

- The "Sermon on the Mount" parallels in Chs. 51–52 are noted by the editor as potentially Christian-influenced additions, in tension with Ch. 53's denial of any intercessor.
- The 365-year lifespan and "ascent without death" motif is drawn directly from Genesis 5:21-24 ("Enoch walked with God; then he was no more, for God took him"), making it the shared foundation of all Enochian literature rather than a distinctive claim.
- The moral teaching in Chs. 40–66 has a repetitive, catechetical character that scholars attribute to multiple editorial hands across the text's long transmission history.
