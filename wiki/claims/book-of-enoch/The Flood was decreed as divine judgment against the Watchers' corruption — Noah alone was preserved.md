## Claim Statement

The Book of Enoch frames Noah's Flood not as an arbitrary divine reset but as a direct, targeted response to the corruption introduced by the Watchers and the devastation of the Nephilim. The Lord's decree (ch. 10) issues simultaneously with the orders to bind Azazel and destroy the giants:

> "Say to him [Noah]: 'Go to Noah and tell him in my name "Hide yourself!" and reveal to him the end that is approaching: that the whole earth will be destroyed, and a flood is about to come on the whole earth, and will destroy everything on it.'" (ch. 10:2)

The earth was to be "cleansed from all oppression, and from all unrighteousness, and from all sin, and from all godlessness, and all the uncleanness that is brought on the earth" (ch. 10:20). The Lord promises after the cleansing that "I will never again send another flood from this generation to all generations and for ever" (ch. 10:22) — the covenant motif also found in Genesis.

### Noah's Vision and Enoch's Intercession (ch. 65–67)

Later in the text, a sub-narrative presents Noah himself in distress: "Noah saw the earth that it had sunk down and its destruction was near" (ch. 65:1). He cried out to his grandfather Enoch, who explained the divine decree: "A command has gone out from the presence of the Lord concerning those who dwell on the earth that their ruin is accomplished because they have learned all the secrets of the angels, and all the violence of the Satans" (ch. 65:5). The knowledge of metallurgy and sorcery specifically — silver from dust, soft metals, lead and tin — is cited as part of what made the earth ripe for destruction.

God then spoke directly to Noah (ch. 67:1–3), declaring his lot "without blame, a lot of love and righteousness," promising to preserve the ark and its contents as "the seed of life," and to establish his seed forever.

The imprisoned Watchers' valley (described in ch. 67:4–7) is located beneath a region where springs of hot, sulfurous water were said to serve both as healing for worldly kings' bodies and as punishment for their spirits — a geological-theological marker connecting the Watchers' imprisonment site to ongoing earthly phenomena.

### The Rainbow Covenant (ch. 55)

In the Parables, the "Head of Days" (the Lord of Spirits) repents of the destruction and swears: "From now on I will not do this to all who dwell on the earth again, and I will set a sign in heaven: and this shall be a covenant of good faith between Me and them for ever" (ch. 55:2). The author's note identifies this as the rainbow.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The text presents God's post-Flood oath never to send another universal deluge as a firm divine commitment — but ch. 55:3 notes that divine punishment will still come through angels on a "day of tribulation, anger, and pain." The Flood is a unique event; final judgment through the Elect One replaces it as the eschatological mechanism.
