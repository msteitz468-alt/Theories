## Claim Statement

The Book of Enoch narrates that the angel Uriel — appointed by the Lord of glory as guide and leader of all the luminaries — personally escorted [[Enoch (antediluvian patriarch)]] through a complete revelation of the laws governing the sun, moon, stars, and winds. Central to this revelation is the teaching that the divinely ordained solar year contains exactly 364 days, that six doors in the east and six in the west govern the sun's seasonal path, and that humans who fail to count correctly will err in their seasons and judgment will follow.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The Astronomical Book opens with Enoch's declaration: "The book of the courses of the luminaries of heaven, the relations of each, according to their name, origin, and months (dominion and seasons) which Uriel, the holy angel who was with me, who is their guide, showed me; and he showed me all their laws exactly as they are, and how it is with each of the years of the world and to eternity" (ch. 72:1, line 2131).

**The six doors of the sun:** The text describes how the sun rises and sets through one of six eastern and western doors, spending approximately thirty mornings in each. Door four is the first-month starting point; in door six (midsummer) "the day becomes double the night, and the day becomes twelve parts, and the night is shortened and becomes six parts" (ch. 72:14). The equinox occurs when "the day becomes equal with the night in length, and the night amounts to nine parts and the day to nine parts" (ch. 72:20).

**The 364-day year stated explicitly:** "On that day the night amounts to nine parts, and the day to nine parts, and the night is equal to the day and the year is exactly as to its days three hundred and sixty-four" (ch. 72:32, line 2223). Chapter 74 reinforces this: "the sun and the stars bring in all the years exactly, so that they do not advance or delay their position by a single day to eternity; but complete the years with perfect justice in 364 days" (ch. 74:12, line 2293).

**The four intercalary days:** Chapter 75 explains that "the leaders of the heads of the (ten) thousands…have also to do with the four days of the year which are not counted in the yearly calendar" — placed at the solstices and equinoxes. "Because of them men go wrong," the text says, because ordinary reckoning misses these days. Chapter 82 repeats: "Men shall be at fault, and not recognize them accurately. For they belong to the calculations of the year and are truly recorded therein for ever, one in the first door and one in the third, and one in the fourth and one in the sixth, and the year is completed in three hundred and sixty-four days" (ch. 82:5–6, lines 2545–2551).

**Uriel's authority confirmed:** "For the signs and the times and the years and the days the angel Uriel showed to me, whom the Lord of glory hath set for ever over all the luminaries of heaven, in heaven and in the world" (ch. 75:3, line 2323). Chapter 79 identifies Uriel as "the archangel, who is their leader" (ch. 79:6, line 2473).

**The moon's deficit:** The moon falls behind the sun-and-stars reckoning by thirty days over five years and eighty days over eight years (ch. 74:10–16), confirming that lunar-based calendars are inferior to the solar 364-day system the text advocates.

**Cosmic consequences of calendar error:** Chapter 80 warns that in the days of the sinners "the years shall be shortened…the moon shall alter her customs…many rulers of the stars shall transgress their customary order…and they shall err and take them to be gods. And evil shall be multiplied on them, and punishment shall come on them so as to destroy all" (ch. 80:2–8, lines 2485–2503).

**Transmission mandate:** The seven holy angels return Enoch to earth for one year to instruct Methuselah. Chapter 82 opens with Enoch's command: "my son Methuselah, preserve the books from your father's hand, and see that you deliver them to the generations of the world" (ch. 82:1, line 2537).

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The text does not directly argue against the lunar calendar but implies it by showing the moon's perpetual deficit relative to the solar reckoning. The critique is directed at unnamed humans who "err" in counting the year, not at a named religious tradition. The four extra days that prevent the year from being a clean 360 are presented as divinely ordained but invisible to ordinary human counting — a built-in asymmetry the text acknowledges without resolving.
