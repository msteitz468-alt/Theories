## Claim Statement

The Book of Enoch narrates that when the daughters of men had multiplied and become beautiful, "the angels, the sons of heaven, saw and lusted after them." Their leader, **Semjaza**, voiced concern that he alone would bear the penalty if others backed out, so all two hundred swore a mutual oath of binding curses to ensure collective commitment. They descended together "in the days of Jared" onto "the summit of Mount Hermon." The text states the mountain was called Hermon because of the oath (*hermon* = curse/oath in the text's etymology) they swore there (ch. 6:6).

The text names twenty leaders: Samlazaz (Semjaza), Araklba, Rameel, Kokablel, Tamlel, Ramiel, Danel, Ezeqeel, Baraqijal, Asael, Armaros, Batarel, Ananel, Zaqiel, Samsapeel, Satarel, Turel, Jomjael, and Sariel — described as "chiefs of tens."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "And they were in all two hundred who descended in the days of Jared in the summit of Mount Hermon, and they called it Mount Hermon, because they had sworn and bound themselves by mutual curses on the act." (ch. 6:6)

> "And Semjaza, who was their leader, said to them: 'I fear you will not agree to do this deed, and I alone shall have to pay the penalty of this great sin.' And they all answered him and said: 'Let us all swear an oath, and all bind ourselves by mutual curses so we will not abandon this plan but to do this thing.' Then they all swore together and bound themselves by mutual curses." (ch. 6:3–5)

The author also provides a second roll-call of Watcher leaders in ch. 69, listing twenty-one names (with some variant spellings), reinforcing the text's emphasis on the deliberate, organised nature of the rebellion.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The text presents the descent as uniformly transgressive — a violation of a divine boundary between the spiritual and physical orders, not an authorised mission. The Lord's response (ch. 10) confirms the Watchers acted without divine sanction and would bear permanent punishment.
