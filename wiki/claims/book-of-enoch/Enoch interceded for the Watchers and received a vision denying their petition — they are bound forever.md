## Claim Statement

The Book of Enoch narrates that after the Watchers' transgression and Enoch's commission by the holy angels, the condemned Watchers sought Enoch's help. They were "all afraid, and fear and trembling seized them" when he approached (ch. 13:4). Unable to pray to heaven themselves — "they had been forbidden to speak with Him nor were they to lift up their eyes to heaven for shame of their sins because they had been condemned" (ch. 13:5) — they asked Enoch to write a petition for them and read it before the Lord.

Enoch wrote the petition and went to the waters of Dan, southwest of Hermon, where he read it "until he fell asleep" (ch. 13:7). In a dream he received a vision of their chastisement. He then found the Watchers gathered at Abelsjail between Lebanon and Seneser, weeping with their faces covered, and told them the vision's verdict.

### The Heavenly Throne-Room Vision (ch. 14)

The denial of the petition comes within the most elaborate heavenly vision in the Book of the Watchers. Enoch is swept up by clouds and wind into heaven, passes through a wall of crystals ringed with tongues of fire, enters a crystal house with floors of snow and a ceiling of lightning and cherubim, and finds a second, greater house of flaming fire. There he sees a high throne of crystal with wheels like the sun and rivers of fire flowing beneath it; upon it sits "He who is Great in Glory" — too bright for any angel or flesh to behold directly.

The Lord addresses Enoch directly, calling him to approach. He delivers the verdict:

> "Your petition will not be granted to you throughout all the days of eternity, and judgment has been finally passed on you: Your petition will not be granted. From here on you shall not ascend into heaven again for all eternity, and you will be bound on earth for all eternity." (ch. 14:4–5)

The Watchers would see their beloved sons (the Nephilim) destroyed by the sword before being themselves imprisoned.

### The Ironic Reversal (ch. 15)

The Lord's reply makes explicit what the Watchers have inverted: "You should intercede for men, and not men for you" (ch. 15:2). The beings whose purpose was to watch over and intercede for humanity have instead required human intercession — and that intercession was useless.

The Lord then explains the nature of their sin: spiritual beings who were eternal and had no need of wives have defiled themselves with mortal blood, generating a hybrid class that belongs neither fully to heaven nor earth (see [[The Nephilim devoured humanity and their disembodied spirits became the demons that afflict the earth]]).

Chapter 16 closes the matter: the Watchers are told they had not received all of heaven's mysteries and what they taught women from "worthless ones" will cause men and women to "work much evil on earth." Their final sentence: "You have no peace."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The text offers no sympathy for the Watchers. Enoch's role as scribe of their petition does not imply moral endorsement — he is the "scribe of righteousness," commissioned to deliver an unfavourable verdict. The Watchers' weeping and fear is presented as appropriate response to their condemnation, not as evidence they deserve mercy.
