## Claim Statement

The Book of Enoch teaches that [[Enoch (antediluvian patriarch)]] assembled Methuselah and his sons to deliver a prophecy dividing all of human history into ten "weeks," each representing a moral-historical era. The Apocalypse of Weeks (embedded in chapters 91–93 of the Epistle of Enoch) traces history from Enoch's own antediluvian time through the Flood, Abraham, Moses, Solomon, a period of apostasy and temple destruction, a final apostate generation, and then three escalating stages of righteous judgment — ending with an Eternal Judgment on the Watchers, the passing away of the First Heaven, and the appearance of an eternal New Heaven. After Week 10, the text promises unlimited weeks of goodness in which sin is never again mentioned.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The editorial context:** The Lumpkin edition notes that the codex was transmitted out of sequence at this point; the ten-week list appears in chapter 93, while its expansions are scattered through chapters 91–92. The editor provides a reconstructed flow that places the weeks in chronological order.

**Enoch assembles his family (ch. 91:1–4):** "And now, my son Methuselah, call to me all your brothers and gather together to me all the sons of your mother; for the word calls me, and the spirit is poured out on me, that I may show you everything that shall befall you for ever" (line 3127). Enoch addresses them: "Love righteousness and walk in it, and draw near to righteousness without a double heart" (ch. 91:4, line 3137).

**The coming tribulation prologue (ch. 91:5–11):** Enoch warns that "violence must increase on the earth, and a great punishment will be executed on the earth…unrighteousness shall again be complete on the earth, and all the deeds of unrighteousness and of violence and sin shall prevail a second time." When blasphemy, violence, and apostasy reach their peak, "a great chastisement shall come from heaven on all these, and the holy Lord will come out with wrath and chastisement to execute judgment on earth" (ch. 91:7, line 3145). Violence will be "cut off from its roots," idols abandoned, and heathen temples burned. Then "the righteous shall arise from their sleep, and wisdom shall arise and be given to them" (ch. 91:10, line 3157).

**The ten weeks as stated in chapter 93:**

- **Week 1 (ch. 93:3):** "I was born the seventh in the first week, able judgment and righteousness still endured." This is Enoch's own time; righteousness characterizes the era.
- **Week 2 (ch. 93:4):** "After me there shall arise in the second week great wickedness, and deceit shall have sprung up; and in it there shall be the first end" — the Flood. One man (Noah) is saved.
- **Week 3 (ch. 93:5):** "In the third week at its close a man shall be elected as the plant of righteous judgment, and his posterity shall become the plant of righteousness for evermore" — Abraham and the patriarchal covenant.
- **Week 4 (ch. 93:6):** "In the fourth week, at its close, visions of the holy and righteous shall be seen, and a law for all generations and an enclosure shall be made for them" — the Mosaic law and the covenant at Sinai.
- **Week 5 (ch. 93:7):** "In the fifth week, at its close, the house of glory and dominion shall be built for ever" — Solomon's Temple.
- **Week 6 (ch. 93:8):** "In the sixth week, all who live in it shall be blinded, and the hearts of all of them shall godlessly forsake wisdom. And in it a man shall ascend; and at its close the house of dominion shall be burned with fire, and the whole race of the chosen root shall be dispersed" — the temple's destruction and the diaspora. The editor's chart identifies this as "Jesus' time," though the text does not name him.
- **Week 7 (ch. 93:9–10):** "In the seventh week shall an apostate generation arise, and many shall be its deeds, and all its deeds shall be apostate. And at its end shall be elected, the elect righteous of the eternal plant of righteousness shall be chosen to receive sevenfold instruction concerning all His creation" — the current era in the editor's reading.
- **Week 8 (ch. 91:12–13):** "There will be another week; the eighth, that of righteousness, and a sword will be given to it so that the Righteous Judgment may be executed on those who do wrong, and the sinners will be handed over into the hands of the righteous. And, at its end, they will acquire Houses because of their righteousness, and a House will be built for the Great King in Glory, forever."
- **Week 9 (ch. 91:14):** "In the ninth week, the Righteous Judgment will be revealed to the whole world. And all the deeds of the impious will vanish from the whole Earth. And the world will be written down for destruction and all men will look to the Path of Uprightness."
- **Week 10 (ch. 91:15–16):** "In the tenth week, in the seventh part, there will be an Eternal Judgment that will be executed on the Watchers and the Great Eternal Heaven that will spring from the midst of the Angels. And the First Heaven will vanish and pass away and a New Heaven will appear, and all the Powers of Heaven will shine forever, with light seven times as bright."
- **After Week 10 (ch. 91:17):** "There will be many weeks without number, forever, in goodness and in righteousness. And from then on sin will never again be mentioned."

**Chapter 92 — the Book of Enoch as wisdom literature:** Enoch describes this entire composition as "The book written by Enoch…for all my children who shall live on the earth…for the future generations who shall observe righteousness and peace" (ch. 92:1, line 3187). He promises that the righteous one shall arise from sleep, receive eternal righteousness, and "walk in eternal light. And sin shall perish in darkness for ever" (ch. 92:4–5, lines 3193–3195).

## Cross-source connections

The ten-week schema shares structural similarities with other periodized-history apocalypses; the schema of weeks culminating in a final judgment and new creation connects to the broader stream of apocalyptic literature discussed in [[The Book of Enoch: Watchers, Nephilim, and apocalyptic revelation]]. The Eternal Judgment on the Watchers in Week 10 ties directly to the Watcher narrative in the earlier portion of 1 Enoch.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The text's sequence is explicitly disordered in the surviving manuscripts, as the Lumpkin editor notes; the reconstructed ten-week order is the editor's proposed restoration, not native to the codex's pagination. The identification of Week 6's "man who ascends" with Jesus is a Christian interpretive overlay; the text itself is entirely non-specific. Similarly, the assignment of Week 7 to "our time" reflects the editor's placement, not an explicit textual claim.
