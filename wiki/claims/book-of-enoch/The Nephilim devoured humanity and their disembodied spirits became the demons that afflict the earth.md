## Claim Statement

The Book of Enoch narrates a three-stage escalation of Nephilim violence after the giants were born to the Watchers and human women:

1. The giants "consumed all the work and toil of men" — devouring their produce and resources.
2. "When men could no longer sustain them, the giants turned against them and devoured mankind."
3. They then "began to sin against birds, and beasts, and reptiles, and fish, and to devour one another's flesh, and drank the blood" (ch. 7:4–5).

The text states "the earth laid accusation against the lawless ones" (ch. 7:6). The archangels, observing "much blood being shed on the earth, and all lawlessness being done on the earth" (ch. 9:1), brought the petition to the Lord of Spirits: "the whole earth has thereby been filled with blood and unrighteousness" (ch. 9:9).

### The Nephilim Spirits as Demons (ch. 15–16)

The most doctrinally distinctive element of the Nephilim narrative is the fate of their spirits after death. The Lord's revelation to Enoch explains:

- The giants' spirits are "born from men and from the holy Watchers" — they are a hybrid class with no proper dwelling in heaven (which is for pure spirits) and no proper mode of human existence either.
- "On earth shall be their dwelling" as evil spirits (ch. 15:10).
- Their function: "The spirits of the giants afflict, oppress, destroy, attack, war, destroy, and cause trouble on the earth. They take no food, but do not hunger or thirst. They cause offences but are not observed" (ch. 15, between v. 10 and 11).
- At the death of the Nephilim body, "spirits will go out and shall destroy without incurring judgment, coming from their bodies their flesh shall be destroy until the day of the consummation, the great judgment in which the age shall be consummated, over the Watchers and the godless" (ch. 16:1).

This constitutes an explicit origin myth for demons: they are the hybrid spirit-offspring of fallen angels and mortal women, condemned to haunt and afflict humanity until the Final Judgment.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The text gives no positive valuation to the Nephilim. The text also notes that archangel Gabriel's mandate was to destroy them by causing them to fight each other — a decree of divinely ordained self-destruction rather than direct divine annihilation, suggesting even their end is characterised by violence.
