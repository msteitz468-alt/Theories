## Claim Statement

Feder argues the Cardiff Giant — an 1869 carved-gypsum "petrified man" secretly buried and then
"discovered" on a New York farm — demonstrates the anatomy of a hoax and the paying public's
eagerness to believe (here, in biblical giants).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Feder recounts how George Hull manufactured and planted the statue, how crowds paid to view it, and
how it was quickly debunked by geologists (gypsum doesn't "petrify"; tool marks were visible) yet kept
drawing believers — even spawning P. T. Barnum's copy.

## What this counters in the wiki

A general lesson in credulity and commercial fakery that underlies the book's umbrella argument; see
[[Anatomy of an archaeological hoax: the Cardiff Giant and Piltdown (Feder)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Feder notes that scientists *did* expose it quickly — the failure was public appetite, not scientific
method.
