## Claim Statement

Feder argues pseudo-archaeology persists not because its evidence is good but because it satisfies
human wants science declines to indulge — simple certainty, cosmic meaning, a flattering or romantic
past — and he urges "a past we deserve," one built on honest evidence.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the closing chapters Feder surveys creationism and New Age "visions," arguing they share the
emotional appeal of meaning-on-demand; he ties this to the motives laid out in Chapter 1 and concludes
that the real past, reached through evidence, is more interesting than the invented one.

## What this counters in the wiki

The capstone counter to the corpus's appeal; converges with Shermer's
[[Why people believe weird things (Shermer's thesis)|Belief Engine]] and Sagan's
[[Science and skeptical inquiry as a candle in the dark (Sagan)|candle in the dark]] on *why* weird
beliefs are attractive.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Feder is sympathetic to the human needs involved; his argument is that meeting them with falsehood
ultimately impoverishes rather than enriches.
