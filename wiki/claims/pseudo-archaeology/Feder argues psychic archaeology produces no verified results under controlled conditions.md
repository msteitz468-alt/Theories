## Claim Statement

Feder argues that "psychic archaeology" — locating sites or reading the past via clairvoyance,
dowsing, or remote viewing — yields impressive anecdotes but **no verifiable results under controlled
conditions**; the apparent hits are vague, retrofitted, or no better than informed guessing.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Feder applies the book's standard evidentiary test: extraordinary claims require controlled,
independently checkable results, which psychic archaeology has not delivered; he examines its
showcase cases and finds them unremarkable on scrutiny.

## What this counters in the wiki

Counters paranormal-knowledge claims generally; compare the documented but inconclusive military
remote-viewing programs in [[The Men Who Stare at Goats (Ronson)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Feder allows that a lucky guess or a researcher's unconscious expertise can produce occasional
"hits" — which is exactly why controlled testing is required.
