## Claim Statement

Feder argues Piltdown Man — a "missing link" announced in 1912, pairing a human cranium with an
ape (orangutan) jaw — was a deliberate forgery, but one that **science's own methods eventually
exposed** (in 1953, via fluorine dating and microscopic tool marks on filed teeth).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Feder uses Piltdown to show both how a fraud can exploit researchers' expectations (it fit hopes for
a British early human) and, crucially, how the self-correcting machinery of science caught it —
contrasting this with pseudoscience's inability to police itself.

## What this counters in the wiki

Often cited by creationists/anti-evolutionists as proof "science can't be trusted"; Feder turns it
around — the exposure is the system *working*. Feeds
[[Anatomy of an archaeological hoax: the Cardiff Giant and Piltdown (Feder)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Feder notes the forger's identity remains debated, and that the fraud did delay recognition of genuine
African fossils for decades.
