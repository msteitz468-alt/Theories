## Claim Statement

Feder argues the past is knowable not through authority, revelation, or the appeal of a story, but
only through **evidence tested by the scientific method** — and that *believing* a thing is different
from *knowing* it.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He illustrates with the stone monument atop Bear Mountain, Connecticut, that confidently but wrongly
marked the state's "highest ground" — a marker can be authoritative and still be mistaken; only
measurement and testing settle it. This epistemology is the yardstick for the entire book.

## What this counters in the wiki

The methodological counter to every revelation- or authority-based claim in the corpus; complements
[[The Demon-Haunted World (Sagan)]] and [[Why People Believe Weird Things (Shermer)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Feder acknowledges archaeological inference is provisional and revisable — that is presented as a
strength, not a weakness.
