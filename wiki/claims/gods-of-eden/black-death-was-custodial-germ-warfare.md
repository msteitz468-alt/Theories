## Claim Statement

Bramley's most distinctive claim: the Black Death was not (only) a flea-borne rodent plague but a campaign of biological warfare waged by the Custodians from the sky. He argues the historical record shows the epidemics were repeatedly preceded by bright lights/"comets" in the sky, by foul-smelling "mists" or "pestilential fogs," and by sightings of "men dressed in black" carrying scythe-like instruments — which he interprets as UFOs and Custodial agents spraying germ/aerosol weapons.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Bramley marshals period accounts (many via Johannes Nohl's *The Black Death*): "The plague years were, in fact, a period of heavy UFO activity"; reports that the plague "did not pass from one man to another, but everyone… got it straight from the air"; and the 1559 Brandenburg account of "horrible men" with "long scythes" whose swishing preceded an outbreak. He argues these "men in black" with scythes are the origin of the popular image of **Death** as a scythe-bearing skeleton — "we may, in fact, be staring into the face of the UFO." He notes the puzzles conventional theory leaves (summer outbreaks, isolated populations, abrupt endings) and likens the mists to modern aerosolized germ weapons.

## Contexts Within the Source

Bramley draws an explicit parallel to the Mesopotamian tablets in which Custodians defoliated land and "commanded that there be a plague," and to the Biblical plagues of Egypt. The plague's social aftermath (the Inquisition, the genocide of Jews falsely blamed for well-poisoning, apocalyptic fervor) feeds his later chapters on the Reformation.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Bramley concedes some reported "comets" were genuine comets/meteors, that crowding and poor hygiene aided the pneumonic form, and that some accounts (e.g., a rotting whale) may be prosaic — he rests the case on the cumulative pattern, not any single report.
