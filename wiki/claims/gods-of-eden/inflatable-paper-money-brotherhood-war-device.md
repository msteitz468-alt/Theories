## Claim Statement

Bramley argues that fractional-reserve "inflatable paper money" — in which bankers issue notes far in excess of their actual gold reserves — was installed by "Brotherhood revolutionaries" and that its "primary reason" was to enable nations "to fight and prolong their wars," while saddling populations with debt and eroding their wealth through inflation. He traces it from medieval goldsmiths (who lent out notes "four to five times in excess" of their gold) to the privately owned Bank of Amsterdam (1609) and the Bank of England (1694), the template for modern central banks.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Using a chicken-farmer parable, Bramley explains how promissory notes invite over-issue, inflation, and devaluation. He states the Bank of Amsterdam "by prior agreement with the new Dutch government… issued notes four times in excess of the Bank's asset base," letting the Dutch finance war against Spain, and that the Bank of England "system was subsequently spread from nation to nation, often on the backs of revolutions led by prominent Brotherhood network members." He proposes replacing it with debt-free, government-issued money tied to gross national product.

## Contexts Within the Source

This is Bramley's economic application of [[War is a commodity used to control populations]] — the money system furthers the Custodial aims of the Garden of Eden (survival as toil) and Babel. The thesis parallels the central-banking accounts in [[Secrets of the Federal Reserve: The London Connection (Mullins)]] and [[Tragedy and Hope (Quigley)]], though Bramley frames the banks as a Brotherhood/Custodial instrument.
