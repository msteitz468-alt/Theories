## Claim Statement

Bramley's starting premise is that "war can be its own valuable commodity": the mere existence of conflict between groups is valuable to a hidden third party regardless of the issues fought over. War enriches arms-makers and war-lenders, maintains social and political control by keeping populations divided and distracted, and consumes resources so that physical survival becomes "an all-consuming chore" — which he argues is precisely a Custodial aim.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "The simple existence of violent conflict between groups of people can, in itself, be valuable to someone regardless of the issues over which people are fighting." (Ch. 1)

He cites 16th-century Italian princes breeding internal conflicts among conquered citizens to prevent "unified action against the conqueror," and Machiavelli's *The Prince* on holding subject towns "distracted by factions." Wars also "tear down and destroy what has already been created," forcing populations into "tedium and repetitious toil."
