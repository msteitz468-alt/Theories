## Claim Statement

Quigley defines **Fascism** in economic terms: "the adoption by the vested interests in a society of an authoritarian form of government in order to maintain their vested interests and prevent the reform of the society." Faced with the long depression and the [[The "deflationary gap" between savings and investment is the key to the twentieth-century economic crisis|deflationary gap]], the propertied classes preferred to close the gap by **rearmament** — government spending on armaments adds purchasing power without putting goods up for sale — rather than by reforming the economy or redistributing income.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quigley surveys the menu of gap-closing public spending — destruction of goods (the early New Deal's crop/livestock destruction), nonproductive monuments, productive investment (the TVA), and **armaments** — and concludes rearmament was "the one most readily and most widely adopted in the twentieth century," appealing not on economic grounds (where "there is no justification") but political ones: it aids heavy industry, reduces unemployment, and is easily sold as national defense. He notes a country need not *consciously* adopt rearmament-as-depression-cure; fear of a neighbor's arms (themselves a response to that neighbor's deflationary gap) suffices. He documents that in Germany, Italy, and Spain the governments "fell into the hands of the possessing classes," reinforcing the normal trend toward greater income disparity. (The Chadwick edition inserts a bracketed "[Money Power]" gloss into the related Chapter 38 passage — that interpolation is the annotator's, not Quigley's.)
