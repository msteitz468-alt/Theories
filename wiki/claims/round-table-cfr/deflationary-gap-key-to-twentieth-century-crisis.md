## Claim Statement

Quigley's signature economic theory. Available purchasing power equals income minus savings; if savings are not fully returned to the market as investment, purchasing power falls short of the aggregate price of goods by exactly that amount — the **"deflationary gap."** When investment chronically lags savings, goods cannot be sold, prices fall, activity declines, and depression results. Quigley calls this gap "the key to the twentieth century economic crisis and one of the three central cores of the whole tragedy of the century."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He derives the gap from nine elements of the modern economy — rising standards of living, industrialism, growth and dispersal of enterprise, separation of ownership from control, **concentration of control**, decline of competition, **increasing disparity in the distribution of income**, and a declining rate of expansion — all of which push savings up while investment lags. He argues the rich save disproportionately, so widening income disparity enlarges the gap; and that the progressive ("lack of purchasing power") and conservative ("lack of confidence") explanations of the 1930s were both right, being "opposite sides of … a single continuous cycle" (purchasing power → demand → confidence → investment → purchasing power). The gap can be closed only by (a) destroying/not-producing goods or (b) producing goods that don't enter the market (public spending).
