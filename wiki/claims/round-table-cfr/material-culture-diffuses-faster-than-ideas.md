## Claim Statement

Quigley argues that the *material* elements of a culture (tools, weapons, vehicles, technology) diffuse far more readily than its *nonmaterial* elements (ideas, religion, social patterns, ideology). Because of this, peripheral and outside societies acquire a civilization's technology without its ideological foundations — which strengthens them in the short run but often "disorganizes and weakens" them in the long run, and explains Western Civilization's destructive impact on other societies.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He defines a civilization's distinctive character as resting on its nonmaterial nexus — for the West, "its Christian heritage, its scientific outlook, its humanitarian elements, and its… rights of the individual" — not on exportable commodities like firearms or plumbing. Western technology, he says, falls under four headings: ability to kill (weapons), to preserve life (sanitation/medicine), to produce (agriculture/industry), and to move (transport/communication). Critically, these "revolutions" diffused at **different speeds and in a different order** outside Europe than within it (e.g., the Transportation Revolution often preceded industrialization in the non-European world) — the root, he says, of "many of the problems which the world faced at the middle of the twentieth century." Example: the horse and firearms reaching Plains and woodland Indians.

## Contexts Within the Source

A corollary of [[Civilizations pass through a seven-stage life cycle]]: material diffusion to the periphery strengthens it against the core. A shattered society survives only if it can integrate the cultural debris around a new ideology/morale — which is how new civilizations are born.
