## Claim Statement

Quigley delivers a blistering critique of John Foster Dulles's "Liberation / Massive Retaliation / New Look" doctrine (1953–60) as "hopelessly irresponsible" — "not a doctrine of action but solely a doctrine of threats" that the U.S. could never actually carry out, alienating allies and neutrals while "going to the brink" of war. He ties it to a broader **neo-isolationism / unilateralism** in the Republican congressional core, which he dissects as a set of provincial beliefs incompatible with any real knowledge of the world.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quigley lists the neo-isolationists' assumptions: **unilateralism**; **national omnipotence**; **utopian "unlimited goals"** (a "final smash" to settle the world forever); the belief that any Soviet success "must be based on treason and espionage" (hence McCarthyism); anti-tax conviction (a supposed Democratic–Soviet plot to "tax America into bankruptcy"); and "pactomania." He argues massive retaliation "could not be used" — Soviet ground forces could overrun NATO, and the U.S. would never launch a suicidal first strike over a local advance. He notes the doctrine "worked" only because it coincided with the post-Stalin Kremlin interregnum, and that the same forces destroyed Robert Oppenheimer's career and reputation in 1953–54.

## Contexts Within the Source

Part of Quigley's even-handed apportioning of Cold War responsibility (see [[The Cold War was an inevitable consequence of WWII, but blame falls chiefly on Stalin]]). Note: this is a *different* John Foster Dulles portrait than the finance-oriented one in the federal-reserve/dulles material — here he is the architect of brinkmanship.
