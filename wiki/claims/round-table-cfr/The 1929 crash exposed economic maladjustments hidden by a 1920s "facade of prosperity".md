## Claim Statement

Quigley argues the 1929 crash and slump did not create the crisis but tore away a "facade of prosperity" that had concealed deep deflation and structural maladjustment since ~1921. The decade's apparent boom rested on rising security prices and on massive U.S. foreign lending (~$10 billion, 1920–1931), which let a fundamentally unsound world economy "live in a fairyland of self-delusions remote from economic realities."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quigley identifies the core contradiction: WWI turned the U.S. into a **creditor that was also an exporter** — an unfeasible combination — yet it refused both remedies (reducing debts owed to it or accepting more imports), instead raising tariffs and papering the gap with foreign loans. He stresses an **insufficiency and maldistribution of gold** (about four-fifths of the world's gold in five countries, over half in the U.S. and France), proving the automatic 1914 gold-standard mechanism had "broken down." He traces the crash sequence: France's franc stabilization and gold purchases → the New York Fed's offsetting open-market operations → credit pouring into speculation rather than production → Fed tightening (April–August 1929) → the Hatry case and a Bank of England rate rise (Sept. 26, 1929) → British funds leaving Wall Street → the October 1929 panic. The 1931 banking crisis (the Austrian Creditanstalt collapse → Germany → Britain off gold, Sept. 1931) then split the world into a **sterling bloc** and a **gold bloc**.
