## Claim Statement

Quigley's "weapons thesis": the nature, cost, and organization of weapons is "the most significant of the numerous factors that determine what happens in political life." When weapons are **cheap and easy to use** ("amateur" weapons), they are widely held, power is dispersed, and democracy tends to result; when weapons are **expensive and complex** ("specialist" weapons), only a few can possess them, and an authoritarian system reflecting that inequality eventually arises. He likewise links the *offensive/defensive* balance to political *scale*: dominant offensive weapons enlarge political units, dominant defensive weapons fragment and decentralize them.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "When weapons are cheap and easy to obtain and to use, almost any man may obtain them, and the organized structure of the society, such as the state, can obtain no better weapons than the ordinary, industrious, private citizen." (Part Twenty)

He cites the rare "amateur weapons system" of ~1880 (and 5th-century-B.C. Greece) as enabling mass citizen armies and majority rule, versus the "specialist" weapons of the nuclear age. On scale: the growing offensive power of weapons from the 9th–20th centuries let states rule ever-larger areas (feudal units → principalities → national states), while a future rise in *defensive* power (e.g., effective guerrilla tactics, decentralized energy/industry) could reverse centralization and restore "small-group freedoms." He even notes biological/chemical weapons as a cheap "non-nuclear deterrence" available to less-advanced nations.
