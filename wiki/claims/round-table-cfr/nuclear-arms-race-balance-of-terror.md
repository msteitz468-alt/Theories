## Claim Statement

Quigley treats the Soviet–American **technological and nuclear rivalry** as "the very foundation and core of the Cold War," passing through four stages — (1) American atomic monopoly (1945–49), (2) a brief balance (1949–50), (3) the H-bomb race (1950–57), and (4) the ICBM race (1957–62) — until a "balance of terror" / **nuclear stalemate** made negotiation possible by 1963. Crucially, he stresses a persistent gap between the **real situation** and **public belief**: e.g., the Soviets had an H-bomb months before the U.S. in 1954, and the widely feared 1960 "missile gap" was a fiction.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quigley explains that mutual deterrence "has nothing to do with the relative size of the numbers of missiles" but on whether a first strike leaves enough for retaliation. He cites 1960 Pentagon computer estimates of a hypothetical 1963 total nuclear war (tens to ~150 million dead on each side, no winner) and argues that the un-targetable Polaris submarine made a disarming first strike impossible. He maps the parallel four-stage sequence of **strategic doctrine**: Great Power Cooperation (1945–46) → Containment (1946–53) → Dulles's "Liberation/Massive Retaliation/New Look" (1953–60) → Graduated Deterrence (from 1960).
