## Claim Statement

Quigley's concluding forecast — the "tragedy and hope" of the title. The **tragedy**: because modern weapons are complex and expensive, armies are professionalizing, which historically "leads, in the long run, to a decline of democracy"; authoritarian regimes are likely to dominate the foreseeable future. The **hope**: a nuclear/Superpower *stalemate* (especially the un-targetable Polaris submarine deterrent) makes total war between the Superpowers nearly unthinkable, pushing rivalry down a continuous "spectrum" toward limited, "controlled conflict" and eventually nonviolent competition.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quigley argues mutual deterrence "has nothing to do with the relative size of the numbers of missiles" but on whether a first strike leaves enough for retaliation — which it always does. He predicts the disintegration of both Super-blocs and growing independence for the neutral "Buffer Fringe." Realistically (not idealistically), he suggests the West could let the Kremlin have low-level, nonviolent "victories" because Soviet leaders prize domestic control over world revolution, and could be pushed back toward "Socialism in one country" — even "non-Socialism in one country."

## Internal Tensions / Editorial Interpolation

The ingested **Chadwick annotated edition** inserts a bracketed editorial denunciation here, calling Quigley's realpolitik suggestion "a call for appeasement and support for a system of tyranny" — **this gloss is the annotator's, not Quigley's**, and is not attributed to Quigley in this wiki (see the caveat on [[Tragedy and Hope (Quigley)]]).
