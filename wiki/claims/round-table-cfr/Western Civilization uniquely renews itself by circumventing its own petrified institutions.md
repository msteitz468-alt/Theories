## Claim Statement

Quigley argues that Western Civilization is unique in that it passed through the Age of Conflict not once but three times, each time **reorganizing itself rather than collapsing into a Universal Empire** — because a new "organization of society" (an *instrument of expansion*) repeatedly circumvented the petrified vested interests of the previous one. Each instrument of expansion eventually "petrified into a structure of vested interests" and became an institution that throttled growth, only to be bypassed by a new one.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quigley's sequence: the **feudal-manorial system** (instrument) petrified into **chivalry** (vested interest); **commercial capitalism** (c. 1440–1690) petrified into **mercantilism**; **industrial capitalism** (c. 1770–1929) began petrifying into **monopoly capitalism** by ~1890, opening the third Age of Conflict. He dates Western Civilization's eight periods (Mixture 350–700; Gestation 700–970; three Expansion/Conflict cycles) and notes core-empire bids by England (1420), France (1810), and Germany (1942), each defeated when a new expansion began.

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the engine of [[Civilizations pass through a seven-stage life cycle]] — the idea that an "instrument" becomes an "institution" is Quigley's central analytical mechanism. He leaves open whether Western Civilization will reorganize into a fourth expansion or pass into a U.S.-led Universal Empire, decay, and invasion.
