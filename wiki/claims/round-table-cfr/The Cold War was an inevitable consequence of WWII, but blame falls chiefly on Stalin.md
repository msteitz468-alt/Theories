## Claim Statement

Quigley argues the Cold War was "an inevitable consequence of the defeats of Germany, Japan, France, and Italy, and the collapse of Nationalist China" — a power vacuum leaving Soviet mass armies facing the American nuclear monopoly — but that, "if blame must be allotted, it may well be placed at the door of Stalin's office in the Kremlin." Stalin's chief error was misjudging Truman and the American people: he expected the U.S. to relapse into isolationism and suffer a 1921-style depression, both of which Truman avoided.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quigley says Stalin, misled by Marxist-Leninist ideology (the "inevitable struggle of capitalism and Communism"), could not believe American offers of cooperation were sincere, and built his western buffer of satellite states "on force and not on consent" — postponing elections in the eastern satellites because free votes in Austria and Hungary (Nov. 1945) had defeated local Communists. He highlights the semantic trap over eastern Europe: Washington (Byrnes) wanted governments both "free/democratic" and "friendly," which the Kremlin knew were "mutually exclusive terms." He dates the Soviet decision for "limited aggression" to ~Feb–March 1946 and marks the Soviet rejection of Marshall Plan aid (July 1947, forcing Czechoslovakia to reverse its acceptance) as the open beginning of the Cold War.

## Contexts Within the Source

Quigley balances the blame: he also faults near-total **American demobilization** (the army fell from 8 million to 1.9 million by mid-1946) as "encouragement to Soviet aggression," and the **neo-isolationism** and Dulles policies that followed — see [[Quigley condemns Dulles's "massive retaliation" and American neo-isolationism as unworkable and dangerous]]. This realist, two-sided framing contrasts with the conspiratorial authors who cite him.
