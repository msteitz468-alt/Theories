## Claim Statement

Quigley opens the book with his philosophy of history: each civilization passes through a recognizable life cycle of **seven stages** — (1) Mixture, (2) Gestation, (3) Expansion, (4) Age of Conflict, (5) Universal Empire, (6) Decay, (7) Invasion. The Age of Expansion brings four kinds of growth (population, geographic area, production, knowledge) and a **core/periphery** structure; the Age of Conflict brings a declining rate of expansion, class struggle, imperialist wars, and "growing irrationality, pessimism, superstitions, and otherworldliness."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quigley tabulates ~16 of the roughly 20 civilizations in history (Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Cretan, Classical, Sinic, Islamic, etc.), noting that twelve to fourteen are "already dead or dying," six of them destroyed by Western Civilization. He observes that the core area enters the Age of Conflict before the periphery, so the periphery often grows wealthier and becomes the seat of the Universal Empire (e.g., peripheral Rome over the Aegean core; the U.S. as Western Civilization's potential Universal Empire).
