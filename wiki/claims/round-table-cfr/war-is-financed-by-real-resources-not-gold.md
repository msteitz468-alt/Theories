## Claim Statement

Quigley argues that the WWI-era financiers fundamentally misunderstood money: they predicted the war could last only months because of "limited financial resources" (gold reserves), whereas "wars… are not fought with gold or even with money, but by the proper organization of real resources." He then sets out the four ways a government can pay for war and their distinct consequences for **inflation** and **public debt**: (a) taxation — no inflation, no debt; (b) fiat money — inflation, no debt; (c) bank credit — inflation *and* debt; (d) bond sales — no inflation but debt.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Quigley judges that the "best" mix minimizes debt while allowing only as much inflation as needed for rapid mobilization (a small amount of inflation spurs production via unusual profits); the worst single method is bank credit. He documents how belligerents concealed collapsing gold-reserve ratios during WWI (Britain 53%→32%, France 60%→11%, Germany 59%→10%, Russia 98%→2%) by suspending some attributes of the gold standard while pretending to maintain it, and how postwar inflation (prices up 200–300%, public debts up ~1,000%) "destroyed capital accumulations and savings" because few leaders had the courage to control it.

## Internal Tensions / Editorial Interpolation

In the ingested Chadwick edition this passage is broken up by inserted bold subheadings (e.g., "The International Bankers Devise a Secret Scheme to Enrich Themselves," "Governments Accept the Secret Plan of the Bankers"). **These sensational subheadings are the annotator's, not Quigley's** — Quigley's own text is the analytical discussion of war finance above.
