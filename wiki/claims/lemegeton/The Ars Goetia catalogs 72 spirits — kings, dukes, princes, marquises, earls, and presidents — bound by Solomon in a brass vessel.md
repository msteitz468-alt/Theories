## Claim Statement
Part I of the Lemegeton presents an enumerated catalog of 72 named spirits — ranked as kings, dukes, princes, marquises, earls (counts), presidents, prelates, and knights — each with a described shape of appearance, an "office" (power or teaching), a number of commanded "legions," and a seal or character to be worn as a lamen. The text states these 72 were originally bound together by Solomon into a vessel of brass and sealed with his secret seal.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
The section opens: "[Of the seventy-two infernal spirits evoked and constrained by King Salomone]." Representative entries (cleaned of OCR noise) include:

- "The first principall spirit is a king ruling in ye East, called Bael. he maketh men goe Invisible, he ruleth over 66 Legions of Inferiour spirits, he appeareth in divers shapes, sometimes like a Catt, sometimes like a Toad, sometimes like a man... This is his Character which is to be worne as a Lamen."
- "The 9th spirit in order is Paimon; a great king, & very Obedient to Lucifer... This spirit can teach all arts and siences... he is of ye order of dominions and hath 200 Legions of spirits under him."
- "The 32d spirit in order is called Asmoday. he is a great king, strong & powerfull, he appeareth wth 3 heads... he is ye first & chifiest under ye power of Amaymon... he governeth 72 Legions of Inferiour spirits."
- "The 68th spirit is called Belial, he is a mighty king and powerfull; he was Created next after Lucifer, & is of his order... his office is to distribute preferments of senatorships... he governeth 80 Legions of spirits. Note this king Belial must have offerings sacrafices & gifts presented to him... or else he will not give True answares."
- The list runs to "The 72d spirit in order is called Andromalius he is a great and Mighty Earle... he ruleth 36 Legions of spirits."

The catalog closes with the binding narrative: "These be the seaventy two Mighty kings or Princes weh King Salomon commanded into a vesall [vessel] of Brasse wth their Legions, of whome Belial Bileth Asmoday & Gaap wear the Cheefest, & it is supposed it was for their pride, for Salomon never declared why he thus bound them; & when he hadd bound them up & sealed ye vesel, he by ye devine power cast Them all into a deep lake or hole in Babilon... [the Babylonians] went wholy into ye lake to brake ye vesel open, suspecting to find a great Treasure; but when they hadd broken it open out flew all ye cheefe spirits Immediately, and their Legions followed them, and they were restored againe to their former places; But only Belial who entered Into a certaine Image, and there gave answares to those whome did offer sacrifice unto him as ye Babilonians did."

The rank system and metal correspondences for the seals are given explicitly: "The seals of those 72 kings are to be made in Mettalls, The cheefest King in [gold], Marquisses in [tin], Dukes in [copper], Prelates in [silver], knights in [mercury], & Presidents in [lead], & Earles in [lead] & [tin] equally alike." Governing authority above the 72 is assigned to four kings: "These 72 kings are under the power of Amaymon, Corson, Ziminiar, & Goap [Gaap] weh are kings ruling in ye 4 quarters East, West, North, & south."

Peterson's editorial footnotes (drawn from Weyer, Scot, Crowley, and manuscript variants Sloane 2731/3648, Harley 6483) show significant instability in names, legion-counts, and details across manuscripts — e.g. Amon "vomiting out of his Mouth fleames of fire," Buer teaching "Phylosophy Morall & Naturall," Vine governing "35" or "36" Legions depending on manuscript — indicating the catalog was actively copied, corrupted, and emended across its transmission.

## Contexts Within the Source
This catalog is the operative core of Book One (Goetia); each entry supplies the practical data (shape, office, legion count, seal) needed for the evocation ritual described in the same Part.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Peterson's footnotes repeatedly flag manuscript disagreement on numbers of legions, names, and even offices (e.g., footnote on spirit 25, "Should probably be 30," on legion counts; footnote disputing whether Marchosias appeared as "Wolfe" or "Ox" in one badly-written 17th-century codex). Peterson also notes the Goetia's order of spirits diverges inexplicably from Weyer's earlier list, and that the reason Solomon bound the spirits is never stated in the source itself ("Salomon never declared why he thus bound them").
