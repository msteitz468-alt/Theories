## Claim Statement
Part II of the Lemegeton, the Theurgia-Goetia, presents 31 named chief aerial spirits (emperors, kings, and princes) who are of mixed good-and-evil nature, each — with the exception of 11 "wandering" princes — assigned a fixed mansion at a point of the compass. Each chief spirit commands a hierarchy of dukes and lesser servient spirits, summarized with names, seals, and rules for the hour in which they may be evoked.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
The source opens Part II: "In this following Treatise you have 31 names of cheife spirits with severall of the ministering spirits which are under them with their seals and characters which are to be worne as a lamin on your breasts." It states their mixed nature directly: "These spirits [being aerial] are by nature good and evill. That is, one part is good, and the other part Evill. They are governed by their princes, and each prince hath his place of abode in the points of the compass — as is shewed in the following figure."

Twenty of the 31 kings have fixed mansions, while eleven are mobile: "20 of these kings have their first mantions and continue in one place, and that ye other 11 are moveable & are sometimes in one place sometime in another... therefore its no matter which way you stand with your face."

The four cardinal emperors are named and quantified with grandiose (often absurd, OCR-garbled) numbers of servants: Carnesiel ("the most great and Cheefe Emporor Ruling in The East who hath 1000 great Dukes and a 100 lesser Dukes... besides 500000000000 [sic] ministering spirits"), Caspiel (Emperor of the South, "200 great Dukes and 400 lesser Dukes"), Amenadiel (Emperor of the West, "300 great Dukes, and 500 lesser Dukes"), and Demoriel (Emperor of the North, "400 great Dukes and 600 lesser Dukes"). Beneath these emperors sit the 31 named princes/kings in strict numbered order — e.g. "Pamersiel is the First and Cheefe spirit in the East, under Carnesiel" through to the sixteenth, "Baruchas... who rulleth as a king in the East and by North" — followed by 11 "wandering dukes/princes" (Geradiel, Buriel, Hydriel, Pirichiel, Emoniel, Icosiel, Soleviel, Menadiel, Macariel, Uriel, Bidiel) "which wander up and down in ye Aire and never continue in one place."

Each prince's entry records his rank, mansion or wandering status, the number of dukes/servants under him (frequently in the thousands or absurdly large OCR-inflated figures), whether his servants are diurnal or nocturnal, and a brief conjuration formula ("Wee Conjure thee 0 thou mighty & potent Prince [Name] who is the... cheife Comander, ruling as King in the dominion of [direction]..."). Some princes are noted as good, some evil, some mixed: e.g. Pamersiel's servants "are by Nature Evill, and verry false... not to be trusted in secrett things," while Padiel's are "all good by nature and may be trusted."

## Contexts Within the Source
Presented as the second of the five books of the Lemegeton, following directly upon the Ars Goetia. The source explicitly continues the same practical apparatus as the Goetia (garments, seals worn as a lamin, crystal stone or circle for evocation) while introducing the compass-direction organizing scheme and the mixed-nature classification not used for the wholly evil 72 spirits of Part I.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
The source itself notes the wandering princes are exceptions to the compass-direction rule, and that individual servants/dukes vary in trustworthiness even within a single prince's court (day servants vs. night servants sometimes differing in nature, as with Aseliel and Cabariel, whose day-dukes are "very good and willing to obey" while night-dukes are "by nature Evill & Disobedient, and will deceive you if They can").
