## Claim Statement
Peterson presents the Lemegeton (Lemegeton Clavicula Salomonis, "The Lesser Key of Solomon") as a composite work assembled from five originally distinct treatises of differing age and origin, unified under a single umbrella title only by a later, linguistically confused compiler, and reconstructed for this edition primarily from a specific set of 17th–18th century British Library manuscripts.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
On the name and compilation: "The name Lemegeton was probably naively invented because of the compiler's ignorance of Latin. He or she was no doubt familiar with the Clavicula Salomonis (Key of Solomon) and wanted to title this work the Little Key of Solomon; this became Lemegeton Clavicula Salomonis." Peterson dates the bulk of the underlying materials to before 1531, since "Weyer included a text closely related to the Goetia," implying pre-existing circulation.

On manuscript sources: "The major texts used for this edition have all been from the British Library Manuscript collection. They include Harley MS 6483, and Sloane MSS 2731, 3825, and 3648." He describes each: Harley 6483 ("Liber Malorum Spirituum"), dated 1712–1713, "handsomely written... among the least reliable of the manuscripts"; Sloane 2731 ("Clavicula Salomonis"), dated January 18, 1686, "incomplete, makes arbitrary rearrangements... omits all of Book 5"; Sloane 3825 ("Treatise on Magic," containing Janua Magica reserata and Clavicula Solomonis), which Peterson calls "the most consistently reliable readings of the available manuscripts" and his base text; Sloane 3648, a "collective codex" from the 17th century, "carelessly written, with poorly executed drawings." Peterson states: "I have followed Sloane 3825 for this edition, except for the Ars Notoria," for which he used Robert Turner's 1657 printed translation as primary source.

On the five constituent books, quoting the source's own introduction: "The little Key of Salomon the King... in 5 Parts, called Books viz: The first part, is a Book of evill spirits, called Goetia... The second part is a Booke of [aerial] spirits, partly good and partly evill, weh is called Theurgia Goetia... The Third part is [a book] of spirits governing ye Planetary houres... called Ars Paulina. The fourth part... is called Ars Almadel Solomonis... The fifth part is a Booke of orations and prayers... which is called Artem Novam [Ars Nova]... also called Ars Notoria."

On relative antiquity: "The oldest book of the collection, Ars Notoria, is a Medieval grimoire of the Solomonic Cycle... the oldest date from the 13th century, and possibly earlier." By contrast, Ars Paulina shows signs of "a late redaction" due to "repeated mention of the year 1641 and guns."

On Weyer as an ancestor text: "The first book, Goetia, corresponds closely with the catalog of demons published by Johann Weyer as Pseudomonarchia daemonum... included as an appendix to his De Praestigiis Daemonum (1563)." Peterson notes Weyer's source, "Liber officiorum spirituum, seu Liber dictus Empto. Salomonis, de principibus & regibus demoniorum," already showed redaction by 1563, "so it evidently dates from long before."

Peterson also surveys prior printed editions — Arthur Edward Waite's *The Book of Black Magic and of Pacts* (1898)/*The Secret Tradition in Goetia* (1911), the Mathers/Crowley *Goetia* (1904), Idries Shah's *The Secret Lore of Magic* (1970), the White edition (1979), and Mitch Henson's *Lemegeton* (1999) — judging all of them uncritical or textually unreliable to varying degrees, and states his own edition as an attempt at a more careful, manuscript-based reconstruction with variants noted "in square brackets."

## Contexts Within the Source
This is Peterson's editorial/textual-history apparatus preceding the primary texts; it frames everything that follows (Parts I–V) as a scholarly reconstruction rather than a single unified original document.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Peterson repeatedly flags manuscript unreliability and disagreement among sources (e.g., Harley 6483 "among the least reliable," Sloane 2731 "incomplete... many careless mistakes"), and states plainly of prior editions that "none of the editions I have reviewed are critical, and most rely only on a single manuscript."
