## Claim Statement
Part III of the Lemegeton, the Ars Paulina ("Art Pauline of King Solomon"), is explicitly structured by the source into two divisions: a first part covering the angels who rule each of the 24 hours of the day and night (whose offices rotate through the seven planets), and a second part covering the angels of the 12 zodiac signs and, further, of each of the 360 individual degrees of the zodiac — termed the "angells of men," since every person's nativity falls under one such degree.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
The source's own division: "This [book] is divided into two parts, the first containing the Angells of the hours of the day and night: The second part [with] the Angells of ye signs of the Zodiac as hereafter followeth." It explains that the 24 hour-angels' powers shift daily: "The Nature of these 24 Angells of the day and night changeth every day: and their offices is to doe all things that are attributed to the 7 planetts. But that changeth every day also," illustrated with the worked example: "Samuel [Samael] The Angell rulleth the first houre of the day beginnig at Sunn Rising, supose it be on a munday in the first hour of ye day (that houre is attributed to the Moon)... But if you call him... on Tuesday Morning at Sunn Riseing... Their offices are to doe all things that are attributed to Mars."

Each of the 24 hours is given its own named angel, chief dukes, and a magic square/seal to be built for a specific date (the source's worked example throughout is "the 10th day of March... in the year 1641... being on a wednesday"): e.g. hour 1, day = Samael, with 8 dukes ("Amenie1, Charpon, Darosiel, Monasie1, Brumiel, Nestoriel, Chremas, Meresyn"); hour 2 = Anael; hour 3 = Vequaniel; running through night-hour 12 = Sarandiel. A long conjuration is given for Samael as the template ("all subsequent conjurations... only differ... in the first part... But from thence forward they are to be all one and the same").

The second part opens: "Which containeth the Mysticall names of the Angells of ye signes in general, and allso the names of the Angells of every degree... who are called the angells of men: because in some one of those signs and degrees, every man is born under. Therefore he that knoweth the moment of his Birth he may know the angel that governeth him." A table gives one angel per zodiac sign by element (e.g. Aries/fire = Aiel, Taurus/earth = Tual, Gemini/air = Giel, Cancer/water = Cael), "attributed... because of those that doe not know the very degree of their nativity." A second table then lists individual angels for each of the first five degrees of each sign (e.g. Aries: Biael, Gesiel, Hael, Vaniel, Zaciel), extending in principle to all 360 degrees. The source also preserves (via manuscript H) a theory of a "threefold Daemon" attending every person — a holy guardian daemon from God, a daemon of the geniture/nativity from the stars, and a daemon of one's profession — attributed to Agrippa's De Occulta Philosophia.

## Contexts Within the Source
Framed as the third of five books, following the Theurgia-Goetia. The Ars Paulina explicitly reuses the crystal-stone/Table-of-Practice apparatus and refers the reader to astrology handbooks for planetary attributions ("as for to know what is attributed to the planetts, I doe referr you to the books of Astrology whereof large volumes have been written").

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
The source notes its own uncertainty at points (e.g., a marginal note on the 7th night-hour seal: "I supose this seal to be wrong and that it must be as the following seal of the 8th houre"), and Peterson's footnotes record extensive manuscript variants in angel/duke names between the base text (T) and comparison manuscripts (S1, S2, H), indicating an unstable transmission rather than a single fixed list.
