## Claim Statement
The Ars Notoria (the "Notary Art") is presented as the oldest and most exalted book bound into the Lemegeton: a system of memorized orations, spoken at specific hours and lunar days, paired with figures ("notae") to be contemplated, which — if performed exactly and devoutly — instantly grants the practitioner supernatural memory, eloquence, and comprehensive knowledge of the liberal arts and sciences, without ordinary study.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
The introduction states its origin and purpose plainly: "It is called the Notary Art because in certain breif notes it teacheth and comprehendeth the knowledge of all arts. This fifth part is a book of orations and prayers that wise Solomon used upon the altar in the temple, called Ars Nova, & was revealed to Solomon by the holy angel of God called Michael... without which notes King Solomon had never obtained his great knowledge."

The main text opens: "In the Name of the holy and undivided Trinity, beginneth this most holy Art of Knowledge, Revealed to SOLOMON, which the Most High Creator by his holy Angel ministred to SOLOMON upon the Altar of the Temple; that thereby in a short time he knew all Arts and Sciences, both Liberal and Mechanick... He had suddenly infused into him, and also was filled with allwisdom." Its opening prayer asks God to "increase my Understanding and Memory, and strengthen the same, to know and receive the Science, Memory, Eloquence, and Perseverance in all manner of Learning."

On its structure: "This art is divided into two parts: The first containeth general Rules, the second special Rules" — general rules being prerequisite faculties like memory and eloquence, special rules being the means to mastery of the seven liberal arts (per Peterson's editorial note citing Claire Fanger). The text repeatedly stresses that its orations are transnational and untranslatable in full: "cannot possible by any means be explicated fully in the poor Thread-bare Scheme of our Language," having "its Original out of the depth and profundity of the Chaldee, Hebrew, and Grecian Languages."

Sample oration text (Latin/Hebrew-derived, given without full translation): "Hely Scematb, Amazaz, Hemel; Sathusteon, hheli Tamazam, &c.," which "Solomon entituled, His first Revelation." Another, "The Oration is, Theos Megale, in tu ymas Eurel, &c.," is partially expounded in English: "Oh God, the Light of the World, Father of Immense Eternity, Giver of all Wisdom and Knowledge... Stretch forth thy Hand, and touch my Mouth, and make my Tongue as a sharp sword; to shew forth these words with Eloquence."

Timing is prescribed with precision: orations are to be "rehearsed four times a day, beginning in the morning once, about the third hour once, once in the ninth hour, and once in the evening," and "spoken secretly," alone, "with a low voyce, so that he scarcely hear himself." Certain lunations are specified: "the fourth and the eighth day of the Moon; and in the twelfth, sixteenth, four and twentieth, eight and twentieth, and thirtieth." Magister Apollonius is quoted promising rapid results: "Whosoever will pronounce these words let him do it in a determinate appointed time... and he shall profit in all Sciences in one Moneth, and attain to them in an extraordinary wonderful manner."

Later orations extend the claimed benefits beyond scholarship to purity and revelation: one, said to "expell all lusts," asks God to "extinguish and repress in me by thy power all the sparks of lust and fornication"; another late in the book instructs the practitioner seeking hidden knowledge to recite "the Oration Theos throughout, and the Psalm Qui Habitat... and take the Figure for this purpose, and put it under the right Ear: and about the second or third hour of the night, thou shalt see thy desires."

## Contexts Within the Source
Presented as the fifth and final book of the Lemegeton proper, prefaced with Robert Turner's 1657 dedicatory epistle and "To the Ingenious Readers," in which Turner (via "Dr. Rudd") calls the work "a flower of the sun... fit for every mans garden" and swears before God that nothing in it is contrary to Christian faith: "quod omnia & singula quae in hoc opere dixi... veris & naturalibus principiis innituntur, fuintque cum Deo & bona Conscientia, sine injuria Christianae fidei." Peterson's textual note records that one manuscript (Sloane 2731, "S1") omits Ars Notoria entirely, substituting other material (see Appendix 1), underscoring that the book's inclusion varied across copies.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
The text repeatedly warns the words cannot be casually understood or used: "Neither think, that all words of the preceding Oration can be translated into the Latin Tongue... these Orations cannot be expounded nor understood by humane sense." Effectiveness is conditioned on strict devotional discipline — fasting, chastity, secrecy, and exact timing ("let him fast the first day wherein he rehearseth it, and let him live chastly and devoutly").
