## Claim Statement
Appendix 2 reproduces (Latin text with the 16th-century English translation by Reginald Scot given in parallel, plus modern bracketed translations where Scot's is missing) Johann Weyer's "Pseudomonarchia Daemonum" — an inventory of demon-kings, dukes, princes, marquises, and presidents, each with a described shape, manner of speaking, granted powers ("offices"), and count of subordinate legions — attributed within the text to "Salomonis" ("Liber... dictus Emptor Salomonis, de principibus & regibus daemoniorum, qui cogi possunt divina virtute & humana"), and framed by the source material as the direct precursor from which the Goetia's 72-spirit catalogue descends.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
The appendix's Latin epigraph frames Weyer's own stated purpose in compiling it: to expose "Ne Sathanicae factionis monopolium usque adeo porro delitescat, hanc Daemonum Pseudomonarchiam, ex Acharonticorum Vasallorum archivo subtractam..." — presented in Scot's English heading as "An inuentarie of the names, shapes, powers, gouernement, and effects of diuels and spirits, of their seuerall segniories and degrees: a strange discourse woorth the reading." Weyer titles his own extraction "Pseudomonarchia Daemonum," noting the underlying magical book was called (in Latin) "Liber officiorum spirituum... Liber dictus Emptor Salomonis, de principibus & regibus daemoniorum."

Representative entries, given in both the original Latin and Scot's English:

"Primus Rex, qui est de potestate Orientis, dicitur Baël, apparens tribus capitibus, quorum unum assimilatur bufoni alterum homini, tertium feli... Huic obediunt sexaginta sex legiones." — Scot's translation: "Their first and principall king (which is of the power of the east) is called Baell, who when he is conjured up, appeareth with three heads; the first, like a tode; the second, like a man; the third, like a cat... he hath under his obedience and rule sixtie and six legions of divels."

"§ 2. Agares Dux primus sub potestate Orientis, apparet benevolus in senioris hominis forma, equitans in crocodilo, & in manu accipitrem portans. Cuncta linguarum genera docet optime... sub sua potestate habens triginta & unam legiones." — "The first duke under the power of the east, is named Agares, he commeth up... in the likenes of a faire old man, riding upon a crocodile, and carrieng a hawke on his fist; hee teacheth presentlie all maner of toongs... having under his regiment thirtie one legions."

"§ 4. Pruflas, alibi invenitur Bufas, magnus Princeps & Dux est, cujus mansio circa turrim Babylonis... Autor est & promotor discordiarum, bellorum, rixarum & mendaciorum." (Translated in the appendix since "Translation missing in Scot": "Pruflas, otherwise found as Bufas, is a great prince and duke, whose abode is around the Tower of Babylon... He is the author and promoter of discord, war, quarrels, and falsehood.") Peterson's editorial note elsewhere identifies Pruflas/Bufas as "the only spirit from Weyer's list" accidentally omitted from a manuscript of Scot dated 1570, underscoring how closely the Goetia's transmission tracks Weyer's list spirit-by-spirit.

The appendix closes with a conjuration invoking a chain of divine names — "Stimulamaton, Espbares, Tetragrammaton, Olioram, Cryon, Esytion, Existion, Eriona, Onela, Brasim, Noym, Messias, Soter, Emanuel, Sabboth, Adonay, I worship thee, I invocate thee... that by thee, my present praiers, consecrations, and conjurations be hallowed... they may come togither from everie coast, and diligentlie fulfill the will of me the exorcist. Fiat, fiat, fiat, Amen" — followed by Weyer's own closing condemnation of the practice: "This kind of blasphemy and cursing constitutes the worst kind of refuse and dregs of the earth, and punishment of these profane magi is well deserved."

## Contexts Within the Source
Peterson appends Weyer's text specifically because of its relationship to the Goetia (Part I of the Lemegeton), which the source elsewhere (in Peterson's introductory apparatus, referenced from within this same edition) frames as descending from the same underlying "Liber officiorum spirituum" tradition Weyer drew on for his 1563 De Praestigiis Daemonum. Appendix 3 ("Comparison of Goetia with Weyer," immediately following this appendix) tabulates the correspondence spirit-by-spirit: e.g. "1. Bael / 1. Baël [Baell]," "2. Agares / 2. =," "5. Marbas / 3. =, Barbas," "6. Valefar / 14. =, Malaphar," noting that "the third spirit in the Goetia, Vassago, is not found in Weyer," and that "the last three spirits in the Goetia, Seere, Dantalion, and Andromalis, are also not found in Weyer" — evidence the Goetia's 72-spirit roster expanded on, rather than simply copied, Weyer's catalogue.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source
Weyer's own framing is denunciatory rather than instructional: his epigraph and closing note present the catalogue as an exposure of "Sathanicae factionis monopolium" (Satanic conspiracy) and brand practitioners' claims as "praestigiae, vanitas, dolus, impostura, deliria" (illusions, vanity, deceit, imposture, delirium) — i.e., within the source, Weyer's own stated intent in publishing the list was critical/exposing, not devotional, unlike the Goetia's operative framing of the same material.
