## Claim Statement

The authors argue overshoot can "ease back down to sustainability" through deliberate choice, and they are emphatic about what such a society would and would not be. A sustainable society, they say, "need not, and probably could not, be centrally controlled or authoritarian"; it "need not leave anyone living in poverty" but must "provide material security to all its people"; and — strikingly — "a global transition to a sustainable society is probably possible without reductions in either population or industrial output."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In World3 Scenario 9, combining technical change with two "definitions of enough" (material consumption and family size) yields a sustainable world of "about eight billion people," all at a well-being "roughly equivalent to the lower-income nations of present-day Europe." They define sustainability via Brundtland ("meets the needs of the present without compromising… future generations") and Herman Daly's throughput conditions, and stress it means *qualitative development*, not the end of all growth, and "certainly not lock the poor permanently in their poverty." The concluding chapter offers five "soft" tools they call essential: **visioning, networking, truth-telling, learning, and loving**. They also affirm they are "not anti-market."
