## Claim Statement

Jacobsen asserts that building the atomic bomb was "the single most expensive engineering project in the history of the United States," and that the bomb's price tag, adjusted for inflation, was $28,000,000,000 (twenty-eight billion dollars).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The claim appears in the book's prologue:

> "Building the bomb was the single most expensive engineering project in the history of the United States. It began in 1942, and by the time the bomb was tested, inside the White Sands Proving Ground in the New Mexico high desert on July 16, 1945, the bomb's price tag, adjusted for inflation, was $28,000,000,000."

The endnotes cite the Brookings Institute, "50 Facts about U.S. Nuclear Weapons," fact no. 1, giving the cost as $20,000,000,000 in 1996 dollars and $28,000,000,000 in 2011 dollars. The 2011-dollar figure is the one Jacobsen uses in the main text.

## Contexts Within the Source

Jacobsen deploys this figure at the opening of her prologue to establish the Manhattan Project as "the mother of all black projects" and the template for all subsequent black operations. The Manhattan Project employed two hundred thousand people, had eighty offices and dozens of production plants across the country, including a sixty-thousand-acre facility in rural Tennessee, and operated without public knowledge.
