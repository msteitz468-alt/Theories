**Claim Statement**
Gest argues that numerous Christian festivals, dates, and symbols were absorbed and "rebranded" from earlier pagan solar worship as Christianity spread through Europe, including December 25th (previously the winter solstice/Sol Invictus festival), Twelfth Night (a Druidic solar-rebirth festival later becoming the Feast of St. John, January 6th), the dating formula for Easter (tied to Sol Invictus worship via the Emperor Constantine), the Celtic cross (combining the Christian cross with pre-Christian sun-wheel and Moon-circle symbols), and the general practice of orienting churches toward the sunrise point associated with a saint's feast day.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Gest states December 25th "became the primary festival for the followers of Sol Invictus, the Roman Sun god" before Christian adoption, and that Twelfth Night "was also celebrated as a festival for the rebirth of the Sun" among Druidic peoples before becoming the Feast of St. John. On Easter, he writes that Emperor Constantine, "although he championed the establishment of Christian acceptance, he still held to a belief in the Roman gods... in particular, worship of the Sun god, Sol Invictus," and that fixing Easter to a Sunday "was therefore appeasing his god, formalizing the festival of Eastre, and bringing together the civil and lunar calendars." On church orientation, Gest states it "was a common practice to build a church... aligned to the point on the eastern horizon where the Sun rose on the day of the festival associated with the saint to whom the church was dedicated." On the Celtic cross, Gest reports that "modern academics consider that the Celtic cross was brought together as a combination of Christian symbolism and the pagan symbols that signified the Moon and the Sun," and separately narrates inventor Crichton E. M. Miller's reconstruction of a portable cross-shaped surveying/navigation instrument (UK Patent GB 2 344 654 A) as a possible practical origin for the Celtic cross form, citing possible prehistoric parallels at Pickaway County and Tygart River, Ohio, and Callanish in the Outer Hebrides.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest frames this material as evidence that "the Sun, the Moon, and the macro-cosmos were key components of all the early religions, even passing into the symbolism of Christianity," supporting his broader thesis that Freemasonry's solar imagery descends from a much older, continuous stream of astronomical religious practice rather than being invented for Masonic ceremony.
