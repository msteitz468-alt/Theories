## Claim Statement

Gest argues that biblical "miracle" narratives can be explained as natural phenomena. He proposes that the Plagues of Egypt were a cascading sequence of natural events triggered by the Santorini volcanic eruption — volcanic ash causing darkness and river pollution, killing fish, driving frogs onto land, breeding disease-carrying flies, and contaminating drinking water, causing boils — and cites a 1999 real-world parallel (a pfiesteria outbreak reddening the Neuse River in North Carolina). He also argues that the story of Sodom and Gomorrah, and Lot's wife becoming a "pillar of salt," records a real earthquake (c. 2500 BCE) near the Dead Sea that caused soil liquefaction (swallowing the towns and Lot's wife) and ignited underground methane pockets (the "fire and brimstone"), a scenario he says was modeled at Cambridge University and supported by satellite surveys locating possible submerged town remains.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

- "One theory is that the darkness was caused by volcanic ash, perhaps from the eruption on the island of Santorini... At the same time, there could have been pollution in the River Nile caused by falling volcanic ash. The pollution would have killed the fish, which in turn would have further polluted the river."
- "A BBC TV documentary about Moses reported an incident that occurred in 1999 at New Burn, North Carolina. The River Neuse turned a red color, as if it had become stained with blood... The cause... was found to have been a mutation of a micro-organism called pfisteria."
- "Current scientific and archeological investigations suggest that about 2500 BCE this once fertile plain and the towns that stood on it were subjected to a violent earthquake that caused a massive landslide... it has been shown that even today pockets of methane gas can be found just beneath the surface of the countryside close to the Dead Sea. In an earthquake, such pockets of gas could escape and ignite, creating a land of fire and brimstone."
- "It could be interpreted that the reference to the pillar of salt was a means of conveying that she was absorbed by the Dead Sea, her body to remain encrusted and disintegrated by the high salt content of the waters."

## Contexts Within the Source

Gest explicitly frames this material as part of a broader argument (chapter epigraph warning: "There are aspects of religion considered in this chapter that those of an intense religious conviction may be uncomfortable with") that archaeological and scientific advances allow previously "miraculous" biblical events to be reinterpreted as natural occurrences later mythologized by scribes and oral tradition.
