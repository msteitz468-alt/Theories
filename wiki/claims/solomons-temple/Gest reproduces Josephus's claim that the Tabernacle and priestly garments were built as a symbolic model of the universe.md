**Claim Statement**
Gest reproduces, as an appendix, a verbatim extract from Flavius Josephus's *Antiquities of the Jews* (Book 3, chapter 7, William Whiston's translation) in which Josephus argues that the Tabernacle's tripartite division, furnishings, and the high priest's garments were all built "in way of imitation and representation of the universe": the Tabernacle's three sections representing land, sea, and the heavens reserved for God; the twelve loaves representing the twelve months; the seventy branches of the candlestick representing the "Decani" (seventy divisions of the planets) and its seven lamps the seven then-known planets; the four colors of the veils and priestly vestment representing the four elements (linen=earth, purple=sea, blue=air, scarlet=fire); the breastplate placed centrally to represent the earth's central position in the cosmos; the girdle representing the encircling ocean; the two sardonyx shoulder-stones representing the sun and moon; the twelve stones representing the months or the zodiac signs; and the blue, gold-crowned mitre representing heaven and divine splendor.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
The appendix is presented as a direct quotation, introduced by Gest as follows: "The above is an extract from Flavius Josephus, *Antiquities of the Jews*, Book 3, chapter 7, relating to the garments worn by the priests and the high priests. Translated by William Whiston." Key quoted lines include: "he will find they were every one made in way of imitation and representation of the universe"; "the ﬁne linen was proper to signify the earth... the purple signiﬁed the sea... the blue is ﬁt to signify the air; and the scarlet will naturally be an indication of ﬁre"; "Each of the sardonyxes declares to us the sun and the moon"; and "the mitre, which was of a blue color, it seems to me to mean heaven."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest presents this appendix without added commentary, as primary-source corroboration for the book's recurring thesis (developed in earlier chapters) that the priestly garments and Tabernacle/Temple furnishings encoded celestial/cosmological symbolism.
