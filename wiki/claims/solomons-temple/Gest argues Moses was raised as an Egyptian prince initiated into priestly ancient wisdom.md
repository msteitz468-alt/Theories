## Claim Statement

Gest asserts that Moses (raised as an Egyptian prince named Mousos, per Josephus and the Catholic Encyclopedia's suggested etymology from "mesh," meaning child) was educated by Egyptian priestly adepts in arithmetic, geometry, meter, rhythm and harmony (per the philosopher Philo), the languages of neighboring nations, and "the Chaldean science of the heavenly bodies" — astronomy and astrology — as well as the secret symbolic meaning of Egyptian sacred inscriptions. Gest argues Moses drew directly on this Egyptian priestly education, rather than divine revelation, when designing the Tabernacle, the Ark of the Covenant, and the priestly vestments, embedding solar orientation and numerological/cosmological symbolism (per Josephus's Antiquities of the Jews) that he claims was later inherited by Solomon's Temple.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

- "Philo, a Jewish philosopher who lived about a thousand years after the first Jerusalem Temple was built, suggested that Moses's education included the study of: Arithmetic, geometry, the lore of metre, rhythm and harmony... Moses was taught the languages of bordering nations and the Chaldean science of the heavenly bodies, what today we call astronomy... To this was added astrology."
- "Philo also indicates that Moses received from the Egyptian priesthood a thorough understanding of the 'symbols displayed in the holy inscriptions.'"
- Quoting Josephus's Antiquities: "consider the fabric of the Tabernacle... he will find they were every one made in way of imitation and representation of the universe... when he [Moses] ordered twelve loaves to be set on the table, he denoted the year... By branching out the candlestick into seventy parts, he secretly intimated the Decani, or seventy divisions of the planets..."
- "I have come to the conclusion that on Mount Sinai, Moses drew on his education and experience as a prince and general in Egypt, and the advice he received from his priestly father-in-law... Hence the reason we find them inculcated in the design of the Ark, the priest's garments, and rituals, as outlined by Josephus. And that ancient wisdom was then transmitted to later generations to turn up subsequently in Solomon's Temple."

## Contexts Within the Source

Gest explicitly acknowledges the speculative/controversial nature of his reasoning about Moses's motives on Mount Sinai ("I appreciate that the following comments will not find favor with all my readers"), and frames the Sinai episode as a deliberately staged act of political theater by Moses — using secrecy (sending Aaron partway and continuing alone) and a mountain setting to lend authority to a legal and religious code he had already devised.
