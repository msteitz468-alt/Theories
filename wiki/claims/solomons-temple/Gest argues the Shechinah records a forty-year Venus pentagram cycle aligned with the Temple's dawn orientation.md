## Claim Statement

Gest argues that the "Shechinah" — a term in the King James Old Testament (later replaced in modern translations by "glory-cloud") describing the visible symbol of God's presence in the Tabernacle and Temple — is a reference to the planet Venus and its roughly forty-year synodic cycle, in which Venus's apparent motion traces a pentagram pattern in the sky. He argues Moses, versed in Egyptian astronomical "ancient wisdom," would have known this cycle and incorporated it into the Tabernacle's dawn-facing orientation, so that once every forty years the light of Venus (not just the Sun) would fall on the Ark near the equinox — a feature he claims was inherited by Solomon's Temple but lost or omitted when the Second Temple was later built by Zerubbabel, which he suggests explains Easton's Bible Dictionary's note that the Shechinah "afterwards disappeared."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

- "There has been the suggestion that it was a reference to the planet Venus, and in particular the forty-year cycle that results in the apparent movement of its orbit to create a pattern of a pentagram progressively described in the sky, with Venus returning to the same point in the sky, creating a forty-year precision clock. This was apparently well understood by ancient cultures, including the Celts and Druids."
- Quoting Easton's Bible Dictionary: "a Chaldee word meaning resting-place... used by the later Jews to designate the visible symbol of God's presence in the tabernacle, and afterwards in Solomon's Temple... It afterwards disappeared."
- "In particular, the ancients are thought to have specifically noted the position of Venus about an hour before sunrise on the day of the equinox as being the start and end of the forty-year cycle... Thus, once every forty years, the light of the planet Venus would fall on the Ark inside the Tabernacle."
- "It did not disappear; they just lost the knowledge, or it was not important anymore."
- "Interestingly, on June 21, 945 (summer solstice) at 4:00 a.m., the Sun-Venus relativity in the sky was almost the same as on the same date in 958 BCE. This was revealed when I used an astronomical software package called Skyglobe."

## Contexts Within the Source

Gest presents this as a corollary to his central sun-orientation thesis for the Tabernacle and Temple, treating Venus/Shechinah alignment as an additional macro-cosmic design layer alongside solar orientation, and using his own software-based astronomical check (Skyglobe) as supporting evidence for the plausibility of the alignment at the traditional dedication dates of the Temple.
