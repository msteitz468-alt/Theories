**Claim Statement**
Kevin L. Gest speculates that "Solomon" may not have been the personal name of the biblical king credited with building the Jerusalem Temple, but rather a word describing his role, position, or function. If so, the temple's name and the king's "wisdom" would reflect the title of an office or duty rather than a personal identity or superior intellect.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
In Chapter One, Gest writes: "suppose it wasn't his name. Suppose it was a word that described his role and position... His supposed wisdom may have then been a reflection of the skills and knowledge he had acquired in relation to his function instead of implying that he was a person of superior intellect." Gest supports the plausibility of this reframing by citing Dr. David Rohl's *A Test of Time*, which quotes Professor Thomas L. Thompson's view that Old Testament narratives are literary rather than historical, and Rohl's own observation that archaeology has produced "no tangible evidence" for the early biblical narratives, including for Saul, David, and Solomon as historical figures, and that "monumental structures once attributed to the building activities of Solomon" at Megiddo, Gezer, and Hazor have been redated away from his reign. Gest also notes the conventional derivation of "Solomon" from Hebrew *shalom* ("peace"), which he treats as one interpretation among others "to which we will come... in due course."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest frames this as the first of a chain of unanswered questions that motivated his personal research quest as a Freemason trying to understand why Solomon's Temple is central to Masonic ceremony despite official assurances that Freemasonry is merely a "gentleman's club."
