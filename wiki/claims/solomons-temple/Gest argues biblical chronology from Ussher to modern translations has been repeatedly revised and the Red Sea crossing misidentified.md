## Claim Statement

Gest argues that biblical chronology has never been a fixed, reliable science: Archbishop James Ussher's 1650s calculation dating creation to October 23, 4004 BCE was treated with near-scriptural authority for centuries (and still underlies some Masonic ceremonial dating) despite being, in Gest's words, "patently ludicrous" by modern standards. He further argues that successive Bible translations (King James 1611, New International Version 1978) have materially altered the text relevant to the Temple's design — e.g., the door "lintel and side posts" of 1 Kings 6:31 becoming "five-sided jambs" — and that the traditional "Red Sea" crossing of the Exodus is likely a mistranslation of the Hebrew "Yam Suph" (Sea of Reeds), an area east of the Nile Delta distinct from the actual Red Sea, possibly linked to a tsunami from the Santorini eruption (c. 1650 BCE).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

- "James Ussher... added together all the time intervals in the Bible and came to the conclusion that the date of creation... was October 23, 4004 BCE... With the sum total of the accumulated knowledge that we have at our disposal today, Ussher's idea is patently ludicrous."
- "the Ussher chronology has been applied to some aspects of Masonic ceremonies where dates and a chronology for the order are based on Ussher's scheme."
- Comparing 1 Kings 6:31: King James version, "the lintel and side posts were the fifth part of the wall," versus New International Version, "doors of Olive wood with five-sided jambs" — Gest notes "There is a great deal of difference in the interpretation."
- "It was suggested that the Red Sea reference was a misspelling and that it should have been the Reed Sea... In the New International version of the text there is a footnote... 'Hebrew term is Yam Suph; that is, Sea of Reeds.'"
- "there are those who have put forward the hypothesis that the Exodus coincided with the eruption of a volcano on the Greek island of Santorini around 1650 BCE... a tsunami... was created just as a group of Israelites reached the banks of a narrow river in the Reed Sea area."

## Contexts Within the Source

Gest presents this chronological instability as evidence that "the religious dogma that has existed and been espoused for centuries is questionable," directly motivating his willingness to propose a revised interpretation of Solomon's Temple's design and purpose in subsequent chapters.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Gest notes the two translation traditions (King James vs. New International Version) give materially different readings of the same verse (1 Kings 6:31) without resolving which is correct, leaving the discrepancy unresolved within the source.
