**Claim Statement**
Gest reproduces, credited to Robert Lomas and Christopher Knight (authors of *Uriel's Machine*, with research data from Alan Butler), a step-by-step method by which Neolithic megalith-builders could have derived and consistently reproduced Professor Alexander Thom's "megalithic yard" (MY, 2 feet 8.64 inches / 82.966 cm) — the unit Thom found underlying over a thousand surveyed megalithic structures from northern Scotland to western France — using only equinox observation, a 366-part division of the horizon, star-transit timing, and pendulum counting, without abstract mathematics, thereby explaining how the unit could be reproduced accurately across hundreds of miles.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
- Gest opens the appendix by crediting Professor Alexander Thom's survey of "over a thousand megalithic structures from northern Scotland through England, Wales, and western France," all built to the same unit, which Thom termed the megalithic yard.
- A quotation from the Book of Enoch is given as an epigraph: "I saw in those days how long cords were given to two angels... 'They have gone to measure.'"
- The reconstructed method (attributed to Lomas, Knight, and Butler) proceeds in five stages: (1) select a flat site with horizon views; (2) prepare rods, cord, plumb line, and a measuring stick divided into five parts; (3) construct a "megalithic degree" by dividing 1/6 of a circle into 61 parts using a 175:3 cord ratio, yielding 1/366 of the horizon (366 being the number of sunrises the builders reportedly counted from equinox to equinox); (4) time a first-magnitude star's transit between two marker rods (calculated at 236 seconds, or 3 minutes 56 seconds, being 1/366 of a day) and tune a pendulum's swing to exactly 366 beats over that interval; (5) mark a stick to the pendulum's swing-width to produce a rod exactly one megalithic yard long.
- Gest notes the unit is based on three astronomical/physical constants: the Earth's orbit around the Sun, the Earth's spin on its axis, and the Earth's mass (which determines gravity and thus pendulum period).
- He notes the British "rod" or "pole" measure equals six megalithic yards "to an accuracy of 1 percent," and speculates the modern 1,760-yard mile may derive from the megalithic yard via rods and chains (4 rods to a chain, 80 chains to a mile).
- The appendix closes with an explicit attribution: "The above has been reproduced by kind permission of Dr Robert Lomas. Further details can be seen at his Web site: www.robertlomas.com."
