**Claim Statement**
Gest argues that the decor of his own lodge room — a forty-foot zodiac painted on the domed ceiling, a black-and-white checkered "Mosaic" pavement bordered by a tessellated (planetary) border, and a geometric symbol on the front step — is not merely decorative but encodes ancient astronomical/astrological knowledge whose original meaning had been forgotten even by senior Freemasons of his own lodge.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Gest describes the Sussex Masonic Temple's "enormous and beautifully decorated zodiac on the ceiling," the "black-and-white checkered pavement encased by a tessellated border," and states that when he asked lodge elders, the deputy provincial grand master, and the temple curator about the meaning of the zodiac, "none of them knew." He quotes the *Lectures of the Three Degrees* (traced to William Preston's 1772 system): the tessellated border "refers us to the planets which, in their various revolutions, form a beautiful border... round that grand luminary, the Sun." Gest personally measured the pavement and found the tiles were exactly eighteen inches (one cubit) square, giving pavement dimensions of 24 x 12 cubits and a perimeter of 72 cubits — a number he later connects (in Chapter Two) to the rate of precessional rotation of one degree per seventy-two years.

**Contexts Within the Source**
This is presented as Gest's own first-person discovery, framed as the origin of his multi-year investigation into whether Freemasonry's Solomon's Temple imagery has astronomical/solar origins predating the 1717 founding of the Grand Lodge of England.
