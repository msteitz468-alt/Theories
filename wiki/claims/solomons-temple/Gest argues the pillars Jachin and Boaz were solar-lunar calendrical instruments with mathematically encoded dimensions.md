**Claim Statement**
Gest argues the two freestanding bronze pillars of Solomon's Temple, named Jachin (south) and Boaz (north) in 1 Kings 7:21, were positioned at the eastern end of the Temple in front of the portico (not the west, as some Masonic lodge layouts imply), that they stood on one-cubit-high stone bases (following the model of Egyptian pillar bases he measured on site visits, contra the Reverend W. Shaw Caldecott's earlier claim of three-cubit bases), and that their height (18 cubits shaft + 5 cubits capital + 4 cubits supra-capital = 27 cubits, or 28 cubits including a 1-cubit base) and 35-cubit combined/inter-center spacing (2 Chronicles 3:15) were deliberately chosen to record lunar and solar cycle numbers (27.5/29.5-day lunar month lengths, 28-day lunar darkness cycle, 18.6-year lunar nodal cycle) and to cast shadows at midday that, at the winter solstice, exactly matched the 40-cubit Temple dimensions — making the pillars a "repository/record of celestial mechanics" rather than mere incense burners, structural supports, or decoration.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
- 1 Kings 7:15-22 is quoted in full describing the pillars' height (18 cubits), circumference (12 cubits), capitals (5 cubits, interwoven chains of 7, 200 pomegranates each), and naming (Jachin south, Boaz north).
- 2 Chronicles 3:15 is quoted giving the pillars as "together... thirty-five cubits long," which Gest reinterprets as the distance between the pillar centers/bases rather than their height, based on his shadow-geometry reconstruction.
- Gest recounts the Reverend W. Shaw Caldecott's early-20th-century book *Solomon's Temple — its history and structure*, which proposed 3-cubit pillar bases to reach a "Babylonian" total of 60 cubits (2 x 30); Gest critiques this as inconsistent with the reconstructed 20-cubit porch height and, based on his own measurements of Egyptian pillar bases at Luxor, Thebes, Karnak, and Petra (using fingertip-to-elbow cubit measurement), proposes 1-cubit bases instead, giving a total pillar height of 28 cubits.
- Using an astronomical simulator set to 955 BCE Jerusalem, Gest calculates solar altitudes at the equinox (54.5°) and solstices (81.5° and 35.3°) and derives shadow lengths and geometry that, he argues, match the Temple's 40-cubit and 60-cubit dimensions and produce the 35-cubit pillar spacing recorded in 2 Chronicles.
- He connects the shadow-marking function to the Masonic office of Junior Warden, whose duty is "to mark the Sun at its meridian."
- A visit to the Jantar Mantar observatory in Delhi (built c. 1725) is offered as a real-world parallel: two pillars there cast shadows whose seasonal movement (contained within the base at summer solstice, touching the other pillar's base at equinox, reaching its top at winter solstice) mirrors Gest's reconstructed pillar-shadow geometry for Jerusalem.
- Gest proposes the "supra-capitals" (the 4-cubit lily/pommel section) were pointed (cone or pyramid, gilded to represent the Sun) rather than rounded, for shadow-marking precision, rejecting the Masonic tradition that they were terrestrial/celestial globes as anachronistic for the era.
- A "secret lozenge" pattern is proposed: shadows cast by a single pillar at sunrise/sunset on both solstices form a diamond/lozenge shape, comparable to a symbol Gest observed at Knossos, Crete, and to patterns Robert Lomas and Christopher Knight (*Uriel's Machine*) associate with the Grooved Ware people at Newgrange; Gest connects this to Lomas's claim in *Turning the Hiram Key* that the solstice angles at Rosslyn's latitude "produce a perfect square," which he links to the Masonic phrase "on the square."
- The chapter closes with a numerological tally connecting pillar dimensions to lunar-cycle numbers (27.5, 28, 29.5, 18.6) and a "golden proportion" calculation showing the Temple's total width (32.5 cubits, including pillar bases) approximates 20 x 1.618 = 32.36 cubits.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest frames this chapter as the culmination of the book's inquiry, stating that once the pillars' geometry and calendrical function were understood, "everything now fits in place" to explain why Solomon's Temple was revered and why the pillars feature so prominently in Masonic ceremony.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**
Gest explicitly rejects Caldecott's 3-cubit base and square-capital hypotheses as internally inconsistent with the Temple's other geometric proportions, while crediting Caldecott with first suggesting the pillars had bases at all.
