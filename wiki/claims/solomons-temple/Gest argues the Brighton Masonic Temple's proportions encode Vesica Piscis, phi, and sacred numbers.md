## Claim Statement

Gest asserts that, having measured the Brighton Masonic Temple, he found its design based on three intersecting circles of forty feet diameter in Vesica Piscis form, that this vesica geometry generates the exact 12-by-24-cubit dimensions of the temple's black-and-white tessellated floor, and that further geometric analysis of the temple's pavement, zodiac ceiling, and ornamental "lozenges" reveals the mathematical constants √2, √3, √5, and the golden proportion (phi = 1.618/0.618), along with symbolic numbers (6, 8, 10, 12) drawn from Pythagorean philosophy.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

- "The basic design of the temple was the first to be easily solved... the length and width of the room was based on three circles, each of forty feet diameter in Vesica Piscis form."
- "I discovered that by drawing the vesica based on two circles, each of forty cubits diameter, the largest rectangular area... was exactly that of the floor, twenty-four cubits long, twelve cubits wide."
- On the pavement circles and golden proportion: "the arc clearly produces points Y and Z at a distance marginally larger than 6 to reflect 0.618. And 10 × 6.18 = 1.618."
- On mathematical constants derived from the room's geometry: "Vesica Piscis has already been mentioned at some length. In addition to creating a rectangle of representative size equating to the dimensions of the floor, it enables the definition of certain mathematical constants, such as the values of: √2 = 1.414; √3 = 1.732; √5 = 2.236."
- Gest's stated conclusion: "The original creator(s) of the design of the Brighton Temple clearly possessed a most awe-inspiring knowledge of sacred geometry and its effect on—or heritage within—Freemasonry."

## Contexts Within the Source

Gest presents this as the empirical starting point of his investigation ("I felt I had accumulated sufficient understanding to begin trying to resolve some of the aspects of the Brighton Temple") and frames the subsequent chapters (Moses, David, Solomon) as testing whether the same geometric principles underlie the design of the original Jerusalem Temple. He explicitly treats the result as an "allegory" consistent with the Masonic teaching that "Freemasonry is a peculiar system of morality veiled in allegory."
