**Claim Statement**
Gest argues that the Temple's narrow clerestory windows (1 Kings 6:4), positioned twelve to a side above the side rooms, cast a beam of sunlight onto the north wall whose position shifted with the seasons (high on the wall in winter, spreading further by the equinox, covering the wall by the summer solstice), and that combined with wall decorations of "gourds and open flowers" (1 Kings 6, cf. 1 Kings 4) this created a functioning agricultural almanac indicating when to sow, cultivate, or harvest. He further argues the five-sided door jambs described for the entrance to the holy of holies (1 Kings 6:31, NIV) were a deliberate optical device — two square blocks, one rotated 45 degrees — that spread the equinoctial sunbeam progressively across the cherubim's wings as the seasons changed, effectively turning the cherubim into a calendar and the "glory of the LORD" that halted the priests' ministrations (1 Kings 8:10-11) into the physical effect of sunlight striking gold and mixing with incense smoke.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
- 1 Kings 6:4 ("He made narrow clerestory windows") and Gest's derived window dimensions (12 windows, 6x3 cubits) are used to model the seasonal light-beam movement across the equinox and both solstices.
- 1 Kings 6:31 and 6:33 are contrasted (five-sided jambs for the inner sanctuary vs. four-sided/conventional jambs for the hall entrance) as the basis for the five-sided-jamb hypothesis.
- Gest describes arriving at the "rotated square" solution in a Durham hotel room using sticky notes, later corroborated (in his account) by two ancient stones arranged the same way near Heliopolis, Egypt, and by angled window reveals he had earlier observed at Durham Cathedral.
- He built and tested a scale cardboard model of the hall and holy of holies with a spotlight to verify that light spread onto the cherubim's wings as predicted at the equinox and both solstices.
- Bede's *De Templo* is quoted: "the equinoctial sunrise could shed its rays directly on the Ark of the covenant through the three doors, namely the portico, the Temple and the oracle."
- 1 Kings 8:10-11 is quoted ("the cloud filled the house of the LORD, so that the priests could not continue ministering") and interpreted as the priests' reaction to an intense reflected light-and-smoke effect at dawn on the equinox.
- A comparative account is given of a palace at Udaipur, Rajasthan, where a removable Sun-disc wall permits an equinox dawn beam to illuminate a Sun-god image, offered by Gest as an independent parallel to the Temple's proposed mechanism.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest presents this as a natural extension of the geometric reconstruction in the same chapter, framing it as filling in gaps left unaddressed by conventional illustrations of the Temple, which "ignore this statement completely and show square windows."

**Opposing Information Within the Source**
None presented; framed as speculative but internally consistent with Gest's broader thesis.
