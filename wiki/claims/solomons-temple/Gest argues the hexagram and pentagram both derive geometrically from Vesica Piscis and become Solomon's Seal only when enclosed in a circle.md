**Claim Statement**
Gest argues that both the hexagram (Star of David) and the pentagram can be constructed directly from the geometry of Vesica Piscis (two overlapping circles), and proposes that the term "Solomon's Seal" specifically denotes either figure only when it is additionally enclosed within an outer circle — a construction method he presents as previously unpublished and offers "to readers" as his own discovery, rather than a claim with any demonstrable historical connection to King Solomon himself.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Gest writes that the Vesica Piscis "has the additional capacity to enable the drawing of an equilateral triangle... It is obvious that the symmetrical geometric relationship that resides within the vesica figure dictates that a Star of David can be accurately produced," and that "the resultant geometric symbol can be fully enclosed by a circle that touches each of the outer points when the circle is drawn from the exact center of the vesica." For the pentagram, he describes a construction method using a circle and inscribed square, and states he found that the point used to construct the pentagram's leg is "very close to the side points of the circles that overlap in the vesica," with a variance of about 1.2 percent, concluding: "It is easy to understand how, and why, the term Solomon's Seal has come to be associated with both figures... Both, when intertwined, are derived from the intertwined circles—Vesica Piscis." He states explicitly: "At the time of writing this chapter... I have not encountered any previous suggestions that claim this solution as being a definition of the term Solomon's Seal. I commend it to readers."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest frames this as the resolution of a puzzle he poses at the start of Chapter Four: how the hexagram and pentagram can both be called "Solomon's Seal" despite being different figures. He explicitly leaves open, as unresolved at the chapter's end, the separate question of "why it is linked to King Solomon" by name.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**
Gest separately reports (without endorsing) that the hexagram's documented, widespread association with Judaism specifically is "as recent as the late nineteenth century," with earlier isolated attestations (a seal tradition from the sixth century BCE, a wall frieze in Capernaum c. 200 BCE, and Encyclopedia Judaica's "oldest undisputed example" from seventh-century BCE Sidon) but "no definitive connection with King Solomon" himself. He also notes the hexagram was used across many other cultures (Vishnu/Shiva shields in India, alchemical fire/water symbolism, general good-luck symbolism) prior to and alongside its Jewish associations.
