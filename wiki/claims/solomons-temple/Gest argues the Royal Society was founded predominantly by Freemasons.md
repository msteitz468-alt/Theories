**Claim Statement**
Gest claims that the Royal Society of London, founded in 1660 in the milieu of the "Invisible College" that had discussed Francis Bacon's ideas since the 1640s, was substantively founded by Freemasons: of the twelve men present at its inaugural meeting, eleven were allegedly Freemasons and the twelfth was sympathetic to Masonic ideals, with the Society's primary motivator being Sir Robert Moray, a close confidant of Charles II and an initiated Freemason.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Gest writes: "The primary motivator was Sir Robert Moray, who was a close confidant of Charles II and initiated as a Freemason. Moray gathered together a group of men, versed in aspects of science. Of the twelve men present at the inaugural meeting, eleven, it is alleged, were Freemasons, and the twelfth was sympathetic to the ideals of this ancient institution. Thus, it could be claimed that the renowned Royal Society was founded by Freemasons." He notes the Royal Society's near-contemporaneous founding (1660) alongside the French Academy of Sciences (also 1660), both devoted to "the research and promotion of science."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Presented in Chapter Two immediately after Gest's discussion of the French Academy of Sciences' 1791 measurement of the Earth's meridian (used to derive the meter), as an aside linking Freemasonry to the institutional history of Western science.
