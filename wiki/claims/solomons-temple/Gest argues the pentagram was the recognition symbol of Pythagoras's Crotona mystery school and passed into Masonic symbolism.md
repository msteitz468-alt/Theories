**Claim Statement**
Gest argues that the pentagram served as a symbol of recognition among the initiated adepts of the mystery school founded by Pythagoras at Crotona, and that Pythagorean doctrine — including this symbol — was subsequently absorbed into Freemasonry, explaining the pentagram's presence in Masonic architecture and regalia (e.g., on the front step of Freemasons' Hall, London) despite its absence from any official Masonic ceremony or lecture text.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Gest writes: "One such school in ancient Greece was that of the Pythagoreans, an apparent secret society that used the pentagram as a symbol of recognition." He speculates the choice was linked to the natural world: cutting an apple through its "equator" reveals a pentagram pattern at the core, providing initiates a wordless means of demonstrating affiliation. He quotes a Grand Lodge of British Columbia and Yukon website noting the pentagram "is not mentioned in, and does not have any connection with, any Masonic ceremony or lecture," yet observes it prominently on the steps of Freemasons' Hall in London and on external lights at the Sussex Masonic Center. Gest reproduces extended passages from William Preston's 1795 *Illustrations of Masonry* stating "Pythagoras was regularly initiated into masonry" and that Druidic philosophy "chiefly derived their system of government from Pythagoras." He concludes in his Chapter Five summary: "The pentagram was the symbol of recognition for the initiates at the school in Crotona. With Pythagorean doctrine being inculcated in Freemasonry, it was logical that the symbol should make its presence in the fraternity."

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest is explicit that this account rests on unverified tradition: "In the absence of any direct evidence that could be established to verify a link with the 'mystery schools' and, in particular, that associated with Pythagoras, I could conclude only that this interpretation arose out of an education system" at classically-oriented private schools (Eton, Harrow) that shaped Victorian and Edwardian Freemasons.
