**Claim Statement**
Gest argues that "Solomon" was not the birth name of David and Bathsheba's son but a title attributed to him after his death. According to 2 Samuel 12:24-25, the child was named Jedidiah at birth. Gest further argues that "Solomon" derives from an original two-word alchemical form, "Sol Amon" (or "Sol Omon"), where "Sol" means the Sun and "Amon" is an ancient word for the hidden/set Sun, i.e. the Moon; thus the Jerusalem Temple was originally the "Temple of Sol and Amon" — a temple dedicated to the Sun and the Moon — and the name was later corrupted into the single word "Solomon."

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
- Gest cites Dr. David Rohl's opinion in *A Test of Time* that "Solomon" was a name attributed to him "sometime after his death" (Ch. 12).
- 2 Samuel 12:24-25 is quoted: "She gave birth to a son, and they named him Solomon... he sent word through Nathan the prophet to name him Jedidiah." Gest reads the two verses together as evidence the birth name was Jedidiah, and that the "Solomon" reference is a later scribal insertion.
- Gest reports an acquaintance raised in East Africa who told him "Solomon" was traditionally rendered as two words, not one — corroborating the "Sol Amon" reading for him.
- Gest concludes in Chapter Twelve: "it would seem logical that the term King Solomon was a reference to a king who was the son of David and Bathsheba, who we now know was named Jedidiah, and who built a temple in Jerusalem dedicated to the worship of the Sun and the Moon, the Sol and Amon Temple."
- The idea recurs as a structural premise of Chapter Thirteen ("Solomon's Temple is... the Sol and Amon Temple—a temple dedicated to the Sun and the Moon") and in the Chapter Sixteen conclusion ("Jedidiah was referred to as the man who built the Sol and Amon Temple, which became corrupted over time to Sol-amon and then to Solomon").

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest presents this as the pivotal discovery of his investigation, arrived at only after building the case (in his account) that Moloch/solar worship was practiced under Solomon and connecting it to the celestial symbolism of the priestly garments discussed in earlier chapters. He states he was initially "very skeptical" of the two-word etymology until the anecdotal corroboration from his East African acquaintance.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**
None presented; Gest treats this as a settled conclusion for the remainder of the book, though he repeatedly flags it as his own subjective interpretation ("my own subjective opinions") in the Chapter Sixteen closing remarks.
