**Claim Statement**
Gest argues that the workforce figures given in 1 Kings for the building of Solomon's Temple (30,000 sent to Tyre, 70,000 "burden-bearers," 80,000 "hewers of stone," 3,300 overseers) are implausibly large for a building roughly the size of a small English parish church, and proposes that the numbers were chosen for their Pythagorean numerological symbolism (reducing, by digit-summation, to the number 6, "the value for harmony") rather than, or in addition to, reflecting a literal historical labor force.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Quoting 1 Kings 5:13–16 and 6:2, Gest calculates the temple's dimensions at roughly 90 x 30 x 45 feet ("It would fit into the rear garden of numerous houses of every town in Britain") against a reported total workforce of 183,300, of which 150,000 cut and hauled stone. He writes: "If we add together the 30,000 who went to Tyre, 70,000 hewers of stone, 80,000 haulers, 3,300 overseers—and forget the noughts... we have 30+70+80+33=213. Taking Pythagoras to his logical conclusion of reducing the answer to a single number, we then have 1+2+3=6, the value for harmony." He further suggests 30,000 could represent the 30-day lunar-religious calendar, that 70,000+80,000 reduces to 15 (the 15-degree hourly rotation of the Earth) and then to 6, and that 3,300 could represent "the three primary positions of the Sun on the horizon." He also compares the workforce figure to Egyptological estimates of 100,000 men over twenty years for the Great Pyramid of Giza, noting the Temple's reported labor force, if applied at pyramid-building efficiency, "may have completed the construction in ten years" rather than the seven years reported in 1 Kings 6:38.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest introduces this immediately after noting the roughly 500-year gap between the traditional construction of the Ark of the Covenant and the building of the Temple, and links it forward to a discussion of the Venerable Bede's numerological treatment of the Tabernacle and Temple.
