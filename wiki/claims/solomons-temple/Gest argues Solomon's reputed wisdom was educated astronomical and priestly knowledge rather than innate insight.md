## Claim Statement

Gest argues that Solomon spent the four years between his accession and the start of Temple construction (per 1 Kings 6:1) deliberately acquiring "ancient wisdom" — the same body of astronomical, geometric, and cosmological knowledge earlier attributed to Moses — rather than being innately or divinely gifted. He treats the famous "two prostitutes" judgment (1 Kings 3:16–28) as merely an example of lateral thinking, and instead points to 1 Kings 4:29–34 ("Solomon's wisdom was greater than the wisdom of the men of the East, and greater than all the wisdom of Egypt") as evidence of formally acquired knowledge, arguing "men of the East" likely refers to Chaldean astronomy/astrology, and that Solomon may have been taught by the Levite priesthood and by Egyptian sources via his marriage to a daughter of the pharaoh Haremheb (following David Rohl's chronology), continuing his father David's project of designing the Temple around solar/macro-cosmic symbolism inherited from the Amarna Heresy period.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

- "In that four-year period, Solomon sought wisdom."
- On the flower-identification story often attributed to Solomon: "The trouble with this story is that it does not appear in any of the defined texts that constitute the Holy Bible."
- "Solomon's wisdom was greater than the wisdom of the men of the East, and greater than all the wisdom of Egypt. —1 Kings 4:30. Could it be that men of the East is a reference to the Chaldean science of the heavenly bodies, the zodiac, precession, the orbits of the planets, the principles of astrology, and celestial mechanics; the same knowledge, we are told, that was taught to Moses while he was a prince in Egypt?"
- "David Rohl suggests that the Egyptian princess whom Solomon married was Haremheb's daughter, as part of what one might call a peace treaty between the two nations... He used his time to gain as much knowledge of the macro-cosmos as he could, being taught by the priests of Levi, and maybe even some from Egypt as a consequence of his marriage to the pharaoh's daughter."
- "He then modified the plans so that it reflected the design his father had left him, but not so overtly as to cause friction with their powerful neighbor, Egypt."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim closes the arc Gest builds across Chapters Nine and Ten — Moses's Egyptian priestly education, David's exposure to the Amarna Heresy's solar monotheism — by positioning Solomon as the final inheritor and refiner of the same body of "ancient wisdom," consciously adapting his father's Temple design during a deliberate period of study rather than executing an unaltered inherited plan.
