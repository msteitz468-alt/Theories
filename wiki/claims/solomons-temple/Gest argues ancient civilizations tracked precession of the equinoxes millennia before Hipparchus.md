**Claim Statement**
Gest argues that while the Greek astronomer Hipparchus (c. 245 BCE) is the first person for whom surviving records document an understanding of the precession of the equinoxes (a roughly 25,920–26,000-year cycle), the phenomenon must actually have been observed and understood by priesthoods thousands of years earlier — potentially as far back as the sixth or seventh millennium BCE — because reliably detecting precession requires on the order of 5,000 years of accumulated, transmitted observational records.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
Gest writes: "the first person to have noted, understood, and explained the precessional cycle was the Greek philosopher, mathematician, and astronomer Hipparchus. He did so around 245 BCE. This doesn't mean to say he was the first, only that he is the first for whom there is evidence." He reasons that "it probably needed some 5,000 years or more of accumulated understanding and observation... before the phenomenon was positively identified," projecting the origin of such knowledge "back to the sixth or seventh millennium BCE—9,000 years ago." He cites the Wooden Henge phase of Stonehenge (Aubrey holes, carbon-dated to 3100 BCE, preceding the stone circle by roughly 1,000 years) as physical evidence of long-term astronomical monitoring apparatus, and notes that "the Babylonians and Mesopotamians also knew and understood this celestial process." He also cites Graham Hancock's *Fingerprints of the Gods* on a priesthood devoted to astronomical observation existing for possibly thousands of years at Heliopolis in Egypt.

**Contexts Within the Source**
This argument is part of Gest's broader case (developed across Chapters One–Three) that Freemasonry's solar and zodiacal symbolism preserves a body of ancient astronomical knowledge accumulated by priesthoods over vastly longer timescales than conventional history credits to named "discoverers" like Hipparchus.
