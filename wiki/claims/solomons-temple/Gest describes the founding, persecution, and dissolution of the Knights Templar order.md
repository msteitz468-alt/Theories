**Claim Statement**
Gest gives a historical account of the Knights Templar: their founding by nine French knights led by Hugues de Payen who, after the 1099 capture of Jerusalem in the First Crusade, established themselves on the site of Solomon's Temple as the "Poor Soldiers of Christ and the Temple of Solomon"; their alleged excavation and discovery of something of great value beneath the Temple site during their first nine years there; their rapid growth in wealth and papal recognition (1128) after Hugues de Payen secured the sponsorship of St. Bernard; their establishment of an early European banking system; their marriage alliance with the Scottish St. Clair family; and their downfall when French King Philip the Fair, seeking to seize their wealth, orchestrated their mass arrest on Friday, October 13, 1307, followed by the order's dissolution by papal authority in 1314 and the execution of Grand Master Jacques de Molay by burning.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
- Gest cites William Preston's *Illustrations of Masonry* (1795) asserting a direct historical link between the Knights Templar and English masons: "During the reign of Henry II the Grand Master of the Knights Templar superintended the masons, and employed them in building their Temple in Fleet-street, A.D. 1155."
- The founding narrative: Pope Urban II's 1095 council at Clermont (reportedly ~225 bishops, ~100 abbots) launching the Crusades; Jerusalem's fall to the crusader army on July 15, 1099; the Blessed Gerard's hospice founding the Knights Hospitaller (papal bull, Pope Paschal II, 1113); and the nine knights led by Hugues de Payen camping on the Temple site "for some nine years," during which Gest reports speculation (including possibly the Ark of the Covenant or genealogical documents) about what they found.
- Gest quotes an unnamed writer describing the Templars' post-1128 rise: "They had gone west with nothing and came back with a Papal Rule, money, precious objects, landed wealth and no less than 300 recruited noblemen."
- Hugues de Payen's marriage to Catherine de St. Clair and the establishment of the first Templar preceptory outside Jerusalem on St. Clair lands in Scotland are noted.
- The persecution narrative: Philip the Fair's alleged murder of two popes and threats against a third (Clement V); the arrest of Jacques de Molay and other Templars on October 13, 1307 (cited as the origin of the "Friday the 13th" superstition); James Orchard Halliwell's *Early History of Masonry in England* (1840) quoted on "a convocation at Vienne in Dauphiny, where the extermination was decided upon in 1307" and describing "the assassination of the Grand Master, Jacques de Molay, his murderers being Philip le Bel, Pope Clement V and Squin de Florion"; charges of heresy, blasphemy, and sodomy which Gest states "are now widely understood to have been false"; the order's dissolution by papal authority in 1314; and de Molay's execution by burning "not far from the Cathedral of Notre Dame."
- Gest notes that Templars in Portugal, protected by King Denis, were reconstituted as the "Chevaliers (Knights) of Christ" after six years of negotiation with Pope John, while English Templars were absorbed into monasteries under the Bishop of York's protection.
- Templar architectural legacy is described: round churches and octagonal buildings (of which "only five now survive" in England), exemplified by the Cambridge "Round Church" (Church of the Holy Sepulchre, c. 1130 CE).

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest presents this history as necessary background before developing his Round Church solar-alignment analysis and his broader argument that the Templars inherited and transmitted suppressed "ancient wisdom," including the geometric knowledge behind Gothic architecture, from contact with Islamic science during the Crusades.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**
None presented as internal to the source; Gest treats the Templar persecution narrative as settled and the heresy charges as established falsehoods.
