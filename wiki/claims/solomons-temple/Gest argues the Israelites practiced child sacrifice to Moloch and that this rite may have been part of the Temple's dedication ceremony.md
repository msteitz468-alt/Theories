**Claim Statement**
Gest argues that the ancient Israelites, from the time of Abraham through the reign of Josiah, practiced child sacrifice in honor of the sun-god Moloch/Molech, that this practice was well established during the reign of Solomon (who built high places for Molech for his foreign wives per 1 Kings 11:7-8), and that the "burnt offerings" and "fellowship offerings" mentioned at the Temple's dedication in 2 Chronicles 7 may be a euphemistic, deliberately obscured biblical reference to child sacrifice performed as part of that dedication ceremony.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**
- 2 Kings 23:10 is quoted: Josiah "desecrated Topheth... so no one could use it to sacrifice their son or daughter in the fire to Molech."
- Genesis 22:2 (Abraham and Isaac at Moriah, the future Temple site) is cited as "the most prominent evidence quoted to support the child sacrifice association with Moloch."
- Exodus 32 (the golden calf built by Aaron) and Acts 7:41-43 ("You have lifted up the shrine of Moloch and the star of Rephan") are cited as further evidence the Israelites worshipped Moloch and the "heavenly bodies."
- Psalm 106:37-38 is quoted: "They sacrificed their sons and daughters to demons... to the idols of Canaan."
- 1 Kings 11:7-8 is quoted showing Solomon built "a high place for Chemosh... and for Molech" for his foreign wives.
- 2 Chronicles 7:1 and 7:7 are cited: "fire came down from heaven and consumed the burnt offerings" and Solomon "offered burnt offerings and the fat of the fellowship offerings," which Gest argues are guarded biblical terms substituted for child sacrifice, given the "abhorrent connotations" and embarrassment to later scribes.
- Gest notes Solomon also sacrificed "42,000 animals" at the dedication (2 Chronicles), which he takes as evidence that most temple sacrifice was animal, while suggesting the "burnt offerings and fellowship offerings" phrase in 2 Chronicles 7:7 describes something distinct from the animal sacrifice.
- Moloch is identified as a Sun god, tying the practice to Gest's broader thesis that the Temple was dedicated to solar/lunar worship.

**Contexts Within the Source**
Gest frames this as a text he had "previously dismissed" as irrelevant to his inquiry (since 2 Kings 23:10 concerns events centuries after the Temple's construction) until he recognized its bearing on the dedication ceremony itself. He presents the identification of the dedication offerings with child sacrifice explicitly as speculation ("Could it be that...").

**Opposing Information Within the Source**
Gest notes the biblical footnotes describe the "burnt offerings and fellowship offerings" as "traditionally peace offerings," a reading he argues is itself a later euphemistic cover-up rather than a genuine contradiction.
