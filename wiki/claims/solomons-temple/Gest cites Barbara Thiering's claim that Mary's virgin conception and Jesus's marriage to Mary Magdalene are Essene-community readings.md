## Claim Statement

Gest reports Dr. Barbara Thiering's (Sydney University) interpretation, drawn from her research on the Dead Sea Scrolls, that the doctrine of the Virgin Birth arose from a misunderstood two-stage marriage custom in Mary's devout community: a betrothal period during which the woman was legally but not necessarily physically a virgin, so that a child conceived before the first marriage ceremony could be described by a "play on words" as conceived by "a Virgin." Gest also reports Thiering's chronology asserting that Jesus and Mary Magdalene were betrothed and married (first wedding September 23, AD 30; second/final wedding March 19, AD 33) and had four children (Tamar, two unnamed/named sons including one called Jesus Justus, and a daughter by a second wife, Lydia).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

- Quoting Thiering: "The New Testament, speaking of an ideal like the Essene one, discusses the case of a man who 'has a virgin,' whose 'passions become strong.' If it happened that during the betrothal period and before the first wedding, the passions became too strong, and a child was conceived, then it could be said by a play on words that 'a Virgin had conceived.'"
- "6:00 p.m. ... Betrothal of Jesus and Mary Magdalene at Ain Feshkha 'Cana.' 'Wedding' under Hellenist rules... on September 23, again at Ain Feshkha, the first wedding ceremony took place with Simon Magus officiating... on Thursday, March 19th, AD 33, we find the second wedding of Jesus and Mary Magdalene."
- "Dr. Thiering, in other works, indicates that Jesus and Mary Magdelene had three children: a daughter named Tamar, a son... named Jesus (Justus), and a second son, whose name is not recorded. In addition, she states that Jesus had a second wife named Lydia and through this marriage he had a fourth child, also a daughter."

## Contexts Within the Source

Gest presents Thiering's work as one of several "new discoveries" (alongside the Nag Hammadi Scrolls and Dead Sea Scrolls) that could overturn traditional religious teaching, explicitly as a lead-in to his broader argument that if the New Testament is open to reinterpretation, so is the Old Testament — including the design history of Solomon's Temple.
