## Claim Statement
Fortune traces Hebraic mysticism's rise from "the nomad star-worshippers of Chaldea," through Abraham's encounter with a mysterious priest-king, through generations of contact between the princes of Israel and the priest-kings of Egypt and Tyre, down to the Essenes who trained the boy Jesus. She holds that the tradition possessed three literatures (Law and Prophets, Talmud, Qabalah — body, rational soul, and immortal spirit respectively), and that it was violently suppressed by the organized Church, which burned the manuscripts of Alexandria, until fifteenth-century initiates dared commit it to paper.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Chapter 1, sections 9–15: Abraham receiving "the first Eucharistic feast of bread and wine" from a priest-king "born without father, without mother, without descent"; "Generation by generation we trace the intercourse of the princes of Israel with the priest-kings of Egypt"; the boy Jesus sent "by them to the Essenian community near the Dead Sea to be trained in the mystical tradition of Israel"; "It is recorded that the baths and bakehouses of Alexandria were fired for six months with the manuscripts from the great library"; "It was not until the fifteenth century... that men dared to commit to paper the traditional Wisdom of Israel."

## Contexts Within the Source
Fortune uses this lineage to answer the objection that the Qabalah is a "medieval forgery," arguing that oral transmission by symbol and "mouth to ear" explains the absence of continuous manuscript evidence.
