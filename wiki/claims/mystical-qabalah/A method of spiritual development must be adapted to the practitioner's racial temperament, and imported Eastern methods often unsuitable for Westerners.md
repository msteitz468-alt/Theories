## Claim Statement
Fortune argues that "the method must fit the temperament" and that it is "injudicious to apply to one type of psycho-physical make-up the developing methods adapted to another." She warns that Eastern systems pursued by Westerners "with no other guide than a book and under unmodified Western conditions" seldom give good results, and that the methods effectual for an Eastern recluse "produce neurotic conditions and breakdowns" in a person compelled to live the "hard-driving life" of a modern Western city-dweller.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Chapter 2, section 2: "Whoever has any practical experience of the different methods of spiritual development knows that the method must fit the temperament, and that it must also be adapted to the grade of development of the student." Section 3: "Experience proves with unfailing regularity that the methods of psychic development which are effectual and satisfactory for the recluse produce neurotic conditions and breakdowns in the person who pursues them while compelled to endure the strain of modern life." Section 6: "It is injudicious to apply to one type of psycho-physical make-up the developing methods adapted to another... one man's meat is another man's poison."

## Contexts Within the Source
This claim opens Chapter 2's argument for choosing a single system and mastering it, and specifically warns against Western aspirants adopting Eastern guru-led Yoga without proper adaptation or supervision.
