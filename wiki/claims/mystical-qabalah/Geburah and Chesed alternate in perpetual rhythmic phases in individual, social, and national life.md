## Claim Statement
Fortune teaches that "rhythm, not stability" is the vital principle of life, and that Geburah (severity, katabolism) and Gedulah/Chesed (mercy, anabolism) alternate in swinging phases in individuals, organised groups, and nations, likening manifested stability to "a man on a bicycle, balanced between two opposing pulls," kept upright by momentum.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
She writes: "In the life of individuals, in the development of any transaction, in the tone of any disciplined or highly organised group-mind, we see the constant alternation of the Geburah and Gedulah influences in a rhythmic swing from one side to the other." Applying this to contemporary national life, she states: "I venture to prophesy that the nation is passing out of a Gedulah phase and entering a Geburah one... Police work is being reorganised; judges are giving stiffer sentences; penal reform has had a set-back." She adds that "for the next cycle... Geburah will be the senior partner, and any mitigations of severity that Gedulah proposes will have to pass the scrutiny of even-handed justice," since "towards the end of a phase extremes tend to develop, and the humanitarianism of Gedulah has been abused."

## Contexts Within the Source
Fortune presents this rhythmic alternation as a corrective to Christianity's static conception of good, which she criticizes for having "no realisation of the principle of equilibrium in space and rhythm in time." She notes the initiate "does not take any phase too seriously," recognizing that extremes signal "the end of the swing" and the pendulum's return toward the centre — except where "vision is completely lost to a people" and civilizations (she names Rome, Carthage, and "more recently... Russia") allow the pendulum "to fly off its hook into self-destruction."
