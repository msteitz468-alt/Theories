## Claim Statement
Fortune states plainly that it is "not necessarily incumbent upon us to do certain things or hold certain ideas because the Rabbis who lived before Christ had certain views," and that "the modern Qabalist is the heir of the ancient Qabalist, but he must re-interpret doctrine and re-formulate method in the light of the present dispensation." She explicitly does not claim identity with pre-Christian teaching, only legitimate descent from it, and treats a practiced esoteric technique as something that "is a growing thing, for the experience of each worker enriches it."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Chapter 1, sections 5–8: "It is not my intention to write a historical study of the sources of the Qabalah, but rather to show the uses that are made of it by modern students of the Mysteries... A technique that is being actually practised is a growing thing." Chapter 4, section 4: "Let it be clearly understood, therefore, that I do not say, This is the teaching of the ancient Rabbis; rather do I say, This is the practice of the modern Qabalists."

## Contexts Within the Source
This is Fortune's explicit methodological disclaimer, repeated in Chapter 4 where she distances her presentation from strict historical scholarship and instead grounds it in ten years of personal practical experience of "the Practical Qabalah."
