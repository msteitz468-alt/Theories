## Claim Statement
Fortune argues that Geburah, "the Destroyer," "the Lord of Fear and Severity," despite being called an "infortune" in astrology (Mars) and widely mistaken for the "evil aspect of the Tree of Life," is in fact holy and "as necessary to the equilibrium of the Tree as Chesed, the Lord of Love, and Netzach, the Lady of Beauty" — because the Qabalistic doctrine holds all Ten Sephiroth to be holy, and evil is simply "misplaced force," not a personal Devil or Adversary.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
She writes: "Were it not that the Qabalistic doctrine explicitly lays it down that all the Ten Sephiroth are holy, there are many who would be inclined to look upon Geburah as the evil aspect of the Tree of Life... Geburah is by no means the Enemy or Adversary described in Scripture, but the king in his chariot going forth to war whose strong right arm protects his people with the sword of righteousness." She defines: "Evil is simply misplaced force; misplaced in time... Misplaced in space... Misplaced in proportion... It is in such things as these that evil lies, not in a personal Devil who acts as Adversary." She calls Geburah "the Celestial Surgeon," "the knight in shining armour, the dragon-slayer," and states that "no Sephirah, therefore, is wholly and solely evil, not even Geburah — which is destruction personified."

## Contexts Within the Source
Fortune frames this as a corrective to what she considers the one-sidedness of Christian ethics, which she says "ignores rhythm" and "balances God with Devil instead of Vishnu with Siva." She argues dynamic energy, sacrifice (understood as "the transmutation of force," not mere renunciation), and the "Spartan virtues" of Geburah are as necessary to psychological and social health as the mercy of Chesed, and that Geburah's Archangel Khamael leads the Seraphim "not in destructive rage, but temperately and impersonally in God's service."
