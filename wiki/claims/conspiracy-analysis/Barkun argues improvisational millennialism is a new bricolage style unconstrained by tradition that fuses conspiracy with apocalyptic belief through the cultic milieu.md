## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that contemporary American millenarian activity is distinguished not only by its volume but by its bewildering diversity, a diversity that the older two-category schema (religious millennialism vs. secular-ideological millennialism) cannot adequately describe. A rapidly growing third variety — which Barkun calls **improvisational millennialism** — has emerged and is the primary vehicle for the culture of conspiracy he documents.

### The three millenarian styles

1. **Religious millennialism** — rooted in canonical sacred texts (pre-eminently the Bible); adherents consciously place themselves within a defined tradition. Conspiracist elements are possible but constrained to vocabulary already present in the tradition (e.g., elaborating the scriptural Antichrist).
2. **Secular millennialism** — rooted in political ideologies (Marxism, nationalism, racism); promises a millennial consummation for a group judged particularly worthy. Similarly constrained to its founding texts. Barkun observes this style has been less vital since the collapse of the Soviet empire and the "end of ideology."
3. **Improvisational millennialism** — characterized by **bricolage**: relentless and seemingly indiscriminate borrowing from Eastern and Western religion, New Age ideas, esotericism, fringe science, and radical politics simultaneously, without any sense that the resulting combination is contradictory. Because it is beholden to no dominant set of ideas, it can incorporate conspiracist motifs of any origin. This gives conspiracy theories an unprecedented mobility across belief systems.

### Conditions for its flourishing

Barkun identifies two structural conditions:

- **Accessibility**: new communication technologies (the Internet, cable television, mass-market paperback distribution, self-published 'zines) have made diverse and unusual ideas easily available and have eliminated the gatekeepers who might filter fringe content. The Internet in particular is "the first mass medium without gatekeepers."
- **Weakened authority**: traditional authority structures — both religious and scientific — have been sufficiently eroded that novel combinations of ideas can be proposed and taken seriously. Paradoxically, science contributed to the decline of religious authority but then itself faced challenges from those claiming nonrational forms of knowledge.

### The cultic milieu as source

Barkun draws on Colin Campbell's concept of the **cultic milieu** — the cultural underground of all deviant belief systems, including occultism, alternative medicine, and fringe science — as the primary reservoir from which improvisational millennialists draw their materials. The milieu is intrinsically hostile to authority, intrinsically receptive to revisionism, and characterized by high fluidity: ideas and individuals migrate rapidly across its constituent subcultures via shared publications, meetings, and websites. This fluidity means that antigovernment, fundamentalist, and UFO subcultures can remain in constant cross-fertilizing contact.

### Self-perpetuating dynamic

Barkun argues the domain of stigmatized knowledge — the raw material of improvisational millennialism — is self-perpetuating. As ideas pass from the stigmatized domain into mainstream popular culture (through conspiracy films, television series), new, more extreme ideas arise to maintain the frisson of the taboo and the sense of elite possession of hidden truth. The result is a ratchet: popularization of one layer of conspiracy belief drives demand for an ever darker, more shocking layer below it.

## What this characterizes in the wiki

This claim provides Barkun's meta-level account of the cultural conditions that produced the wiki's primary conspiracy sources. It explains why works as different as [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] (UFO/government conspiracy), [[The Biggest Secret (Icke, 1999)]] (reptilian shapeshifter superconspiracy), [[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]], and [[The Illuminati (Marrs)]] can draw on the same pool of motifs — Illuminati, Freemasonry, ancient bloodlines, occult ritual — despite coming from different starting points. The cultic milieu Barkun describes is directly visible in the dense cross-citation networks among these sources. See also [[Secret societies through history compared across sources]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun is careful to note that improvisational millennialism has not displaced religious or secular millennialism — both older forms remain active. Improvisationalism is additive, not substitutive. He also notes uncertainty about the long-run cultural impact of conspiracy themes entering mainstream popular culture: they may be destigmatized (and thus defanged) or may serve to recruit new believers.
