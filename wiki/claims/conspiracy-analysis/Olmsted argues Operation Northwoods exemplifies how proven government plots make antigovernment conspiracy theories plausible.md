## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that Operation Northwoods — the 1962 Joint Chiefs of Staff plan to stage terrorist attacks on American soil, shoot down a civilian airliner using a drone substitute, and blame Cuba as a pretext for invasion — functions as the central historical evidence for her book's thesis. Real, documented government conspiracies of this kind give antigovernment conspiracy theories their plausibility. The theorists who claim that 9/11 was an "inside job" are not merely paranoid; they can point to a declassified government document showing that the U.S. military once drew up an almost identical plan.

Olmsted documents that the Northwoods plans — declassified in the late 1990s — were subsequently featured in the opening of the 9/11 documentary *Loose Change*, which had received at least ten million views. Post-9/11 conspiracy theorists cited Northwoods as "the precise template for the remote control and plane-switching theory" used to explain the attacks. The logic is direct: if U.S. officials were willing in 1962 to plan the murder of American civilians to manufacture a pretext for war, they were plausibly capable of doing so in 2001.

Olmsted's framing is neither an endorsement of 9/11 conspiracy theories nor a simple debunking. Her argument is structural: the history of *proven* government conspiracies is inseparable from the history of *unproven* conspiracy theories, because the former supplies the evidential raw material for the latter. "When they charge that the government has plotted, lied, and covered up, they're often right."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Olmsted opens the Introduction with the Northwoods case. The Joint Chiefs planned to: (a) explode bombs in U.S. cities, (b) sink boats of Cuban refugees, (c) kill Cuban dissidents in the U.S., (d) blow up John Glenn's rocket, and (e) use drone aircraft to simulate the shootdown of a civilian airliner. President Kennedy rejected the plan three days after receiving it; it never left the drawing board. The documents were declassified in the late 1990s and became widely circulated after 9/11.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted is careful to note that Kennedy *rejected* Northwoods — it did not happen. Her argument is not that the government routinely executes such plans but that their documented *existence* is sufficient to make antigovernment conspiracy theories structurally reasonable to many citizens. She also acknowledges that conspiracy theorists typically make a "big leap from the undeniable to the unbelievable" (quoting Hofstadter), and that most specific 9/11 conspiracy theories have been effectively rebutted.
