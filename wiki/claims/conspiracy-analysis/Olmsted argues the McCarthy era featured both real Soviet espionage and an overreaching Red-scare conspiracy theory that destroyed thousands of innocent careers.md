## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that Soviet espionage in the United States was real and significant — atomic spies
(Fuchs, Greenglass, Julius Rosenberg), the Bentley network, Hiss, White — but that by the time
McCarthy made his career-defining speech in February 1950, the actual espionage conspiracy had
already been dismantled, largely by the FBI itself. The mass Red Scare that followed was therefore
a response to a *defunct* threat, inflated into a sweeping conspiracy theory by ambitious politicians
and J. Edgar Hoover's institutional interests. Thousands of people lost careers and livelihoods for
associations with communism that posed no live security threat.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Olmsted documents that Elizabeth Bentley's 1945 defection to the FBI — combined with Kim Philby's
simultaneous tip to the KGB — effectively destroyed the Soviet networks she exposed: Moscow
immediately rolled up its spy rings. By 1951 "there were no Soviet spies of any importance remaining
within the government." McCarthy's 1950 Wheeling speech, which launched the Red Scare, used an
outdated 1948 list of alleged subversives; his figure of "205 card-carrying Communists" was "simply
not true." The senator, Olmsted writes, "was not an original thinker" — he plagiarized charges from
Nixon and right-wing papers — and "seemed to focus on communism not because he truly feared it but
because he saw an opportunity for headlines and reelection."

Hoover knew the espionage network was broken but had institutional reasons to exaggerate the threat.
He launched COINTELPRO in 1956 — a "sophisticated vigilante operation aimed squarely at preventing
the exercise of First Amendment rights" (Senate committee finding) — employing 1,500 informants
against an increasingly irrelevant Communist Party that by then had more FBI agents than real members
per capita. Over 400 suspected homosexuals lost State Department jobs in the Truman years — more than
twice as many as suspected Communists.

Hiss and White, Olmsted concedes, did spy for the Soviets; but both were limited in their influence.
White's greatest achievement — the IMF — *strengthened* global capitalism. Historian Harry Magdoff
(himself a communist economist and spy) said of White: "If Harry Dexter White was a Soviet spy…he
wasn't a very good one."

## What this bears on in the wiki

This argument directly undergirds [[Real government conspiracies and the rise of conspiracy theories (Olmsted)]]:
the *genuine* espionage conspiracy gave conspiracists their proof-text, but the overreach of official
countersubversion then "laid the foundation for a new generation of conspiracy theories about the
government" — the left-wing mirror-image that suspected the military-industrial complex of murdering
the president. The self-perpetuating cycle of real-conspiracy → overreach → counter-conspiracy
theory is the book's master mechanism.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted does not exonerate Hiss or White — she calls the evidence of Hiss's espionage "convincing"
and accepts that White "met with Soviet agents and passed them some information." Her argument is
about proportion and consequence, not innocence. She also notes that Hoover's COINTELPRO, though
vastly overreaching, grew from a genuine concern that communist infiltration posed some ongoing risk.
