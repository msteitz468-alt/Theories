## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that when New World Order conspiracy theory migrated into the UFO subculture, it carried with it the residue of nineteenth-century American nativism — intense hostility toward Catholics, Freemasons, and Jews — which had otherwise largely disappeared from mainstream discourse by the postwar period. He characterizes this as a "nativist renascence": the UFO conspiracy subculture functions as a sheltered transmission zone in which old hatreds survive and prosper, insulated from mainstream correction by their embedding in the wider domain of stigmatized knowledge.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun cites John Higham's definition of nativism as "intense opposition to an internal minority on the ground of its foreign (i.e., 'un-American') connections," and historian David Bennett's observation that nativism was effectively dead in mainstream American society by 1950. The paradox Barkun identifies is that this "dead" tradition survives and even flourishes in UFO conspiracy literature, where cheek-by-jowl with speculation about alien beings and interstellar spaceships are "diatribes against the Masons and the Illuminati, the pope and the Jesuits, the Anti-Defamation League and the Jewish banker" — and where *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion* is approvingly cited.

Barkun attributes this survival to the structural properties of the UFO-conspiracy subculture: it transmits content through tracts, fringe publications, oral communication, and websites, bypassing the mainstream editorial and academic gatekeeping that filtered nativist ideas from respectable discourse. He also notes the subculture's characteristic temporal promiscuity — freely citing Barruel (18th century), Hislop (19th century), and Webster (early 20th century) as authoritative — which treats no heterodox idea as ever having been superseded.

Barkun's chapter details how anti-Catholic, anti-Masonic, and proto-anti-Semitic material entered ufology through specific figures. Cooper's [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] reproduces what Barkun identifies as a 19th-century nativist forgery (the supposed Jesuit oath) and incorporates Fatima speculation in which the aliens engineered the apparition. Icke and Branton reproduce Alexander Hislop's *Two Babylons* Babylonian-mystery-religion thesis verbatim. Icke finds no fewer than three Satanic pentagrams in the Washington D.C. street plan. Branton connects Scottish Rite Masonry to a "Reptilian-Bavarian Illuminati" superconspiracy. Barkun observes that the same conspiratorial infrastructure can accommodate Catholics, Masons, Jews, and extraterrestrials because the superconspiracy framework allows unlimited accretion of agents.

## What This Characterizes in the Wiki

Barkun's analysis characterizes the New World Order / secret-society argumentation in [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]], [[The Biggest Secret (Icke, 1999)]], [[Secret Societies and Subversive Movements (Webster)]], and the broad pattern tracked in [[Models of the New World Order Elites Compared]] and [[Secret societies through history compared across sources]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun acknowledges that many UFO conspiracists explicitly disclaim anti-Semitism, racism, and anti-Catholic bias — they publicly assert they have no animus toward individuals from targeted groups. He treats these disclaimers as analytically significant rather than simply dismissing them: they produce what he calls "refracted racism and anti-Semitism," a simultaneous use and repudiation that makes the prejudice more durable and harder to challenge. He also notes that NWO material can be "sanitized" — distributed in versions free of overt bigotry — which helps explain its rapid spread.
