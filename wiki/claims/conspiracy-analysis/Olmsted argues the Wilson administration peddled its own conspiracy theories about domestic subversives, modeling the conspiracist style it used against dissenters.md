## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that the Wilson administration did not merely suppress conspiracy theories — it actively produced and disseminated its own. The government's official conspiracy theory held that Germany had dispatched agents throughout the United States to undermine national unity, corrupt government offices, sabotage industry, and "poison" Americans' loyalty. Wilson proclaimed that Germany had "filled our unsuspecting communities and even our offices of government with spies and set criminal intrigues everywhere afoot against our national unity." Attorney General Palmer told dissenters they could "expect no mercy" from "an avenging Government."

Olmsted's key point is structural: the government wanted to *monopolize* the production of conspiracy theories. It promoted fear of hidden plotters when this served official interests (rallying the country for war, suppressing dissent), while simultaneously prosecuting citizens who proposed alternative conspiracy theories (that bankers and insiders had pushed the country into war for profit). The result was not the elimination of conspiracist thinking but its intensification: the government taught the public to see hidden plotters everywhere, then tried to dictate which plotters citizens were allowed to fear.

This created an inherent contradiction. Once citizens absorbed the conspiracist frame — hidden agents, secret influence, a government infiltrated by enemies — they could and did turn that same logic against the government. The dissenters who charged that a "certain inner circle" of bankers had manipulated Wilson were using exactly the same analytical template the government had taught them.

Olmsted also documents the government's use of extralegal violence through "superpatriot" vigilante groups, which the official rhetoric of treason and disloyalty had effectively licensed. The vice president praised a lynch mob. Montana vigilantes hanged an IWW organizer from a railroad trestle the day after the federal prosecutor refused to bring charges against him.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Wilson's proclamations: Germany filled U.S. communities with spies; "poison of disloyalty"; dissenters would be dealt with by "stern repression." CPI's George Creel described his mission as showing the war was "a just war, a holy war." AG Palmer's statement that dissenters should expect no mercy. The lynching of Frank Little in Butte, Montana, praised by VP Thomas Marshall as having a "salutary effect." J. Edgar Hoover's 450,000-card subversive index by 1921.

The Espionage Act (1917) and Sedition Act (1918) made it a crime to utter "disloyal, profane, scurrilous, contemptuous, or abusive language" about the government — effectively criminalizing any narrative that competed with the official one.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted does not deny that genuine foreign espionage and sabotage existed during WWI; German agents did conduct some operations on U.S. soil. Her argument is not that the official conspiracy theory was entirely fabricated but that the government used it as a pretext for suppressing all political dissent, including dissent with no foreign connection whatsoever, and that this abuse discredited the official narrative in retrospect.
