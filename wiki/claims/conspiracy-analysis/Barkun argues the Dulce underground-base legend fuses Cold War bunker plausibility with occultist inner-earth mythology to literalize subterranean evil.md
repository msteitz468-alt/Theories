## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that the Dulce underground-base legend — the claim that a vast joint alien–CIA installation exists near Dulce, New Mexico — succeeded because it fused two independently plausible bodies of belief: (1) real Cold War secret installations, whose documented existence gave a veneer of credibility to rumors of yet another secret bunker; and (2) long-standing occultist and crank-science traditions about an inhabited inner earth. The result is a literalization of the cultural logic that places evil underground — a subterranean Armageddon in which the underground base becomes the locus of a final apocalyptic conflict between humanity and reptilian alien forces.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun traces the Dulce legend's origin to Paul Bennewitz, an Albuquerque businessman who from 1979 claimed to be intercepting alien electronic communications. The story entered the ufology community by 1982 and gained public traction through John Lear's 1987 statement and 1988 writings by William F. Hamilton III and Jason Bishop III. Barkun explains Dulce's appeal on two grounds: first, the Cold War had produced genuine hardened underground command-and-control sites, so claims of a secret bunker in northern New Mexico carried "superficial plausibility"; second, the region's pre-existing cattle-mutilation reports (centered on New Mexico State Police officer Gabriel Valdez) made it a pre-established paranormal zone. The wilder details — grotesque experiments on abductees, firefights between aliens and Delta Force — lay "well outside even the most far-fetched reports of secret underground bases," but fell within the domain of stigmatized knowledge circulated by Theosophists, pulp fiction writers, and hollow-earth advocates since the 1930s and 1940s (see the genealogy traced in the concept page).

Barkun identifies the occult genealogy: Blavatsky's subterranean Atlantean "net-work," Doreal's Serpent Race in underground caverns, Richard Shaver's "deros," and Raymond Palmer's editorial transformation of both into popularized stigmatized knowledge. The alien underground-base concept — fully elaborated by "Branton" (Bruce Alan Walton) in the 1990s as a "Reptilian-Bavarian Illuminati" conspiracy — was available, "fully formed," when Dulce reports appeared in the late 1980s because occultist and pulp-fiction antecedents had been in circulation for decades.

## What This Characterizes in the Wiki

Barkun's analysis directly characterizes the reptilian underground thesis elaborated in [[The Biggest Secret (Icke, 1999)]] and [[Children of the Matrix (Icke, 2001)]]. Icke absorbed Branton's material as authoritative and linked Dulce-style underground bases to the Babylonian Brotherhood theory. Barkun's tracing of the Dulce legend also informs the broader pattern of UFO cover-up models discussed in [[Why the UFO cover-up exists and how it is maintained models compared]] and [[Competing models of the UFO phenomenon compared across sources]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun consistently frames the Dulce legend as constructed belief, not documented fact. He notes that the "four independent confirmations" Lear cited in 1990 were unverifiable, and that Bennewitz's original claims were evidently delusional. He also acknowledges that the Cold War context gave a rational hook on which irrational elaborations could hang — which explains the legend's spread without validating its content.
