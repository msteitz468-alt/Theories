## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that when UFO conspiracism re-entered radical-right NWO circles in the 1990s, it generated two diametrically opposed syntheses exemplified by Jim Keith (American) and David Icke (British). In Keith's synthesis, UFOs and alien abductions are manufactured by the same human elite that runs the NWO — there are no real aliens, only a false-flag extraterrestrial threat fabricated to enable totalitarian control. In Icke's synthesis, the human NWO conspirators (Illuminati, Rothschilds, Trilateral Commission) are themselves servants of genuinely nonhuman — ultimately other-dimensional reptilian — masters. Both syntheses absorbed the full NWO belief system of the Christian patriot milieu without direct participation in its core organizations.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Jim Keith (1949–1999)**: Barkun describes Keith as "a professional conspiracy theorist" writing for a conspiracist audience who came to UFOs late, after deep immersion in post-JFK assassination conspiracism. Keith's 1994 *Black Helicopters over America: Strikeforce for the New World Order* became the "central work" on that theme. By the mid-1990s Keith had adopted FEMA concentration-camp lists, mind-control implants (including believing Timothy McVeigh's account of being implanted), and UN-occupation coded road signs. On UFOs, Keith "seems never to have seriously suggested that UFOs did not exist" but attributed them to elite human technology and government mind-control programs. Abduction "victims" were, in Keith's analysis, CIA mind-control subjects made to believe they had been seized by aliens. Keith explicitly drew on *Alternative 3*: "How better for the elite to exert total control over the world than by counterfeiting an extraterrestrial invasion?" Even acknowledging *Alternative 3* as a hoax, Keith concluded "the conspiracy was behind it either way." By 1998–1999, Keith moved toward multiple epistemologies — speculating that the Illuminati had discovered magical means to produce UFO appearances, and that "men in black" could be "occultists or denizens of some supernatural realm."

**David Icke (1952– )**: Barkun tracks Icke from BBC sportscaster and British Green Party spokesperson through a 1990 psychic-healing experience that launched his conspiratorial career. *The Robots' Rebellion* (1994) offered standard NWO theory (Illuminati-controlled Brotherhood, world government plan, *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion* attributed to the Illuminati rather than Jews) with minimal UFO content. *...And the Truth Shall Set You Free* (1995) introduced "Prison Warders" — extraterrestrial masters above the Global Elite — exercising control through "negative energy" and a "frequency 'net' thrown around this planet." [[The Biggest Secret (Icke, 1999)]] identified the Prison Warders as reptilians from Draco who crossbreed with humans to create "hybrid" bloodlines that control the Brotherhood. [[Children of the Matrix (Icke, 2001)]] elaborated the reptilian thesis and located the beings in other dimensions — conferring unlimited concealment and physics-defying powers, while allowing Icke to "castigate his critics as stodgy materialists unable to grasp a new paradigm."

Barkun observes that "there is no more complete codification of New World Order ideas about mind control and black helicopters than Keith's, and no fuller explication of its beliefs about ruling elites than Icke's" — yet both were outsiders to the radical right's organizational core. Both also exemplify "improvisational millennialism": end-of-history scenarios assembled eclectically from Babylonian mystery religion, Masonic magic, the Protocols, UFOs, and the Illuminati. The greater a source's stigma, Barkun notes, the more attractive it becomes to such thinkers, because "the intensity of rejection is taken to be a measure of its truthfulness."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun notes Icke's own acknowledgment that the reptilian thesis "has not gained general acceptance even among fellow conspiracy researchers." He also flags the tension in Icke's relationship with the American radical right: Icke admires Christian patriots as the "only Americans who know the truth," but is put off by their rejection of New Age spirituality and their fundamentalism. Barkun is explicit that Icke's anti-Semitism, while technically displaced onto the Illuminati or reptilians, crosses into "dangerous terrain" that parallels classical anti-Semitic templates.
