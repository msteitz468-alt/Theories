## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that the enduring JFK conspiracy industry was produced not by the strength of the
conspiracy evidence but by a deliberate decision by senior officials — Lyndon Johnson, J. Edgar
Hoover, Allen Dulles, and Earl Warren — to hide politically dangerous truths about U.S. policy
toward Cuba and about institutional failures. The "officials were not trying to protect the 'real
killers'"; they were "statists trying to maintain Americans' trust in their system of governance.
Ironically, their lies would shatter that trust."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Olmsted identifies the specific secrets the government chose to protect:

1. **The CIA's Castro assassination plots.** The Eisenhower and Kennedy administrations had run at
   least eight plots against Castro, enlisting the mafia (Sam Giancana, Santo Trafficante) through
   mob fixer Robert Maheu. On the very day of Kennedy's assassination, a CIA officer was delivering
   a poison-filled pen to a Cuban agent in Paris. These plots raised the obvious possibility that
   Castro had retaliated — a possibility that both Johnson ("I never believed Oswald acted alone")
   and Robert Kennedy suspected immediately. Allen Dulles, placed on the Warren Commission, never
   told fellow commissioners about the plots. Former commission counsel Burt Griffin later said
   knowledge of the plots "lead not only to the issue of possible conspirators with Oswald, but also
   his motive."

2. **The FBI's surveillance failure.** Hoover knew before Dallas that Oswald was violent,
   politically extreme, and employed on the motorcade route. Oswald had even hand-delivered a
   threatening note to the Dallas FBI office. After the assassination, the Dallas special agent
   destroyed the note in a bathroom, tearing it into pieces and flushing it down the toilet, on
   orders from his supervisor. Seventeen agents were internally censured — but the punishments were
   kept secret so Americans would not interpret them as "a direct admission that we are responsible
   for negligence which might have resulted in the assassination of the President."

3. **Nuclear-war logic.** Both Hoover and Johnson concluded that a rigorous investigation into
   possible communist or Cuban involvement could push the American public to demand war — and "the
   D.A. … yelled … 'What the hell are you trying to do, start World War III?'" when an assistant
   wanted to charge Oswald with a "communist conspiracy." The logic of nuclear deterrence drove
   officials to suppress precisely the information that would most have mattered.

The grassroots Warren Report critics — Sylvia Meagher, Shirley Martin, Harold Weisberg, Linus
Pauling — were, Olmsted notes, partly right: the government *was* hiding things. Many were direct
victims of McCarthyism, with deep personal reasons to distrust the FBI. The critics were also
"partly wrong": they built their case around suppressed evidence that pointed to institutional
self-protection, not a conspiracy to cover up the real killers.

Jim Garrison — the New Orleans D.A. who brought the only criminal prosecution related to the
assassination — Olmsted compares explicitly to Joe McCarthy: a demagogue who exploited legitimate
public suspicion for his own ends, using "inchoate fears" to "organiz[e] a popular flight from
reality." Garrison's core theory (CIA killed Kennedy to keep Vietnam going) was, Olmsted reveals,
partly inspired by a KGB disinformation campaign that planted the CIA-link story in European
newspapers three days after Garrison arrested Clay Shaw.

## What this bears on in the wiki

This claim is the primary Olmsted contribution to the wiki's JFK cluster. It directly engages
[[Controversy: Was Allen Dulles complicit in the Kennedy assassination?]]: Olmsted's position is
that Dulles was complicit in *suppressing* the Castro-plot context from the Commission, rather than
in arranging the murder itself. Compare the thesis of [[The Devil's Chessboard (Talbot)]] and
[[Hidden History (Jeffries)]], which argue for deeper institutional agency in the killing; Olmsted
offers a more restrained reading: the cover-up was real, the covered-up truth was about Cuba policy
and FBI incompetence, not about a CIA hit.

The chapter also directly generates the later claim that Jeffries makes in
[[Jeffries alleges 9-11 was an inside job or allowed to happen, and the official account is a cover-up]]:
Olmsted traces the template — secret government failures → rushed official narrative → grassroots
skeptic community → permanent distrust — that recurs at every subsequent crisis.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted does not conclude that Oswald was innocent or that another gunman fired. She accepts that
the evidence ties Oswald to the shooting. She specifically states: "Oswald may have been the real
assassin, but the FBI's refusal to consider alternatives ensured that conspiracy theories would
flourish." She also notes that the Warren Commission's single-bullet theory, while "unpersuasive
even to the men who came up with it," does not mean the conclusion of a lone gunman was necessarily
wrong. The point is that the process was irredeemably compromised by institutional self-interest.
