## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that conspiracy theories exist on a spectrum of ascending scope and that there is an inherent dynamic pushing them toward greater inclusiveness. He identifies three types in ascending order: **event conspiracies** (a limited, discrete plot), **systemic conspiracies** (sweeping goals but simple machinery — a single evil organization infiltrating institutions), and **superconspiracies** (multiple conspiracies hierarchically nested, with an all-powerful invisible master force at the summit). Superconspiracies grew particularly prominent from the 1980s onward in works such as [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] and in the writings of David Icke.

The escalation dynamic is structurally driven: as a conspiracy theory expands to explain more, two things happen simultaneously. First, the domain of evil enlarges — more malevolent agents must be incorporated to account for the expanded range of events. Second, and more importantly, the theory becomes progressively less falsifiable. Skeptics and their evidence are increasingly identified as instruments of the conspiracy itself, so that every attempt at disproof becomes further confirmation. Barkun states this directly in Ch. 2: "the more inclusive the conspiracy theory, the less susceptible it is to disproof, for skeptics and their evidence are increasingly identified with the powers of evil."

The terminal point of this escalation is the superconspiracy, in which the master conspirators are always of the most secret type (both invisible and acting secretly), and the entire history of the world is reinterpreted as the product of their design.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

*A Culture of Conspiracy*, Ch. 1 (pp. 7–8, raw lines 1168–1244) for the typology; Ch. 2 (pp. 37–38, raw lines 4019–4041) for the explicit statement of the escalation dynamic and falsifiability collapse.

## What this characterizes in the wiki

This claim provides the analytical backbone for understanding the trajectory from works such as [[None Dare Call It Conspiracy (Allen & Abraham, 1971)]] and [[Secret Societies and Subversive Movements (Webster)]] (systemic type) to the comprehensive superconspiracy architectures of [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]], [[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]], [[The Illuminati (Marrs)]], and [[The Biggest Secret (Icke, 1999)]]. It also connects to the comparative syntheses [[Is history steered by a deliberate cabal or an emergent network? Models compared]] and [[Models of the New World Order Elites Compared]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun notes that escalation is not inevitable — event conspiracies can remain event conspiracies. The dynamic is probabilistic rather than mechanical. He also observes that some conspiracists, like Cooper, revised their theories radically mid-career (Cooper eventually concluded UFO revelations were themselves disinformation), suggesting the superconspiracy framework can turn inward and consume its own prior commitments.
