## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that New World Order conspiracy theory is not a single unified tradition but a convergence of two historically distinct streams of thought: (1) Protestant millenarian Christianity, centered on the figure of the Antichrist who would impose global tyranny in the end-times; and (2) secular Illuminati conspiracy literature, which attributed the French Revolution and all subsequent upheaval to a secret society of plotters. These streams evolved separately over roughly two centuries before merging around 1990 into the single overarching NWO framework.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun traces the Antichrist tradition from the sparse New Testament references (1 John 2:18) through dispensational premillennialism as systematized by John Nelson Darby (1800–1882), in which the Antichrist would rule a global dictatorship for three and a half years during the Tribulation. In the twentieth century, successive international institutions — the League of Nations, the United Nations, the European Common Market — were identified as the Antichrist's organizational vehicle, while modern technology (computers, bar codes, microchips) was read as the mechanism for his "mark of the beast" economic control.

The Illuminati stream is traced from Robison (1798) and Barruel (1803) through Nesta Webster and Lady Queenborough in the interwar period, who expanded the framework to implicate Jews as the driving force. By the early 1990s, Barkun observes, "New World Order theory came to constitute a common ground for religious and secular conspiracy theorists." A figure like Pat Robertson could move fluidly between Antichrist analysis and Illuminati theory. Barkun notes: "The two literatures had also begun partially to overlap, so that religious students of the Antichrist like Robertson experienced no difficulty in drawing the Illuminati into their speculations."

## What This Characterizes in the Wiki

This analysis is directly relevant to [[Secret Societies and Subversive Movements (Webster)]], which Barkun identifies as the foundational interwar synthesis that linked Illuminism to Jewish power — a move that made the Antichrist and Illuminati traditions compatible. Barkun also discusses [[None Dare Call It Conspiracy (Allen & Abraham, 1971)]] as a post-1965 node in this convergence, positioning the CFR-and-Illuminati framework for American right-wing audiences.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun notes that the convergence of these two streams did not erase their differences: religious believers saw the NWO through the lens of divine prophecy, while secular conspiracists had no need of scriptural narrative. The "ecumenical" quality of NWO theory, Barkun emphasizes, was its structural advantage — it could be shared across a religious-secular divide without requiring identical theological commitments.
