## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion* operates in UFO and NWO conspiracy literature not simply as an anti-Semitic text but as a transferable template: its conspiratorial architecture (a hidden global council with a multigenerational plan, exercising control through finance, media, and the engineering of apparent conflict between nominal enemies) has been detached from its original Jewish-conspiracy context and re-applied to Masons, Jesuits, aliens, reptilians, and the New World Order. Conspiracy writers cite the *Protocols* approvingly while often disavowing anti-Semitism, and the same structural logic — secret council, manufactured opposition, world-domination plan — recurs in their accounts of non-Jewish targets.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun observes that in UFO and NWO conspiracy writing, *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion* is cited approvingly alongside diatribes against the Masons and Illuminati, the Vatican and Jesuits, the Anti-Defamation League, and "the Jewish banker." This co-presence of the *Protocols* with non-Jewish conspiracy material indicates, Barkun argues, that the text's structural logic has been generalized.

The key architectural feature Barkun identifies as transferable is the "manufactured-opposition" motif: the idea that apparent enemies (Jesuits and Masons, communists and capitalists, the US and the Soviet Union, even Jews and Nazis) are secretly allied arms of a single plot. This motif — which Barkun traces to early nineteenth-century anti-Masonry — is structurally identical to the *Protocols'* claim that all political factions are secretly managed by the same hidden council. Branton claims Jesuits created Scottish Rite Masonry; Commander X blames Jesuits for Nazism; Griffin claims Illuminati agents created the state of Israel; an Internet posting Barkun cites cannot determine whether multiple Masonic-alien alliances are fighting each other or are arms of one plot. The *Protocols* template makes all such arguments internally consistent: apparent conflict is always manufactured cover.

Barkun notes that Cooper's [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] exemplifies this dynamic: it moves from the Illuminati and Trilateral Commission to a supposed Jesuit oath and then to Fatima-alien speculation, never specifying which group is ultimately in charge — an indeterminacy the *Protocols* template accommodates because the hidden council is always one level deeper than the identified actors.

The chapter's closing argument is transitional: because alien-conspiracy literature is structured around secrecy and duplicity, it is a "perfect site for anti-Semitism," and the *Protocols* template makes anti-Semitism structurally available even for authors who publicly repudiate it. Barkun describes the result as "refracted racism and anti-Semitism that allows those who use them simultaneously to repudiate them."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun does not claim that all UFO conspiracists consciously model their arguments on the *Protocols*. His argument is structural rather than intentional: the conspiratorial architecture recurs because it solves the same logical problems (explaining all-encompassing hidden control, accounting for apparent contradictions, foreclosing falsification) for which the *Protocols* template was originally designed.
