## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that while [[The paranoid style in politics (Hofstadter)]] linked the paranoid political style to millennialism, the relationship between the two is more complex than a simple equivalence. He contends that **conspiracism is neither a necessary nor a sufficient condition for millennialism**, and vice versa — but that the two are in practice **mutually reinforcing** and are frequently found together.

### Not necessary

Conspiracism is not a necessary condition for millennialism because some millenarian movements lack significant conspiracist components. Barkun's example is Millerite Second Adventism in the 1840s, which coalesced around William Miller's biblical chronology of Christ's return and attempted to maintain harmonious relations with established Protestant churches rather than constructing a conspiracist explanation for the world's condition.

### Not sufficient

Conspiracism is not sufficient for millennialism because all it does is impose a sharply dualistic vision on the world. It does not guarantee that good will triumph or that such a triumph will perfect the world. Barkun notes that conspiracism can even produce an anti-millenarian conclusion in which the evil cabal is depicted as virtually invincible — some conspiracy-minded survivalists retreated into the wilderness partly because they feared imminent defeat, not imminent redemption.

### Symbiotic relationship

Despite the absence of a logical entailment, the two are in fact often linked in practice. Barkun quotes Stephen O'Leary: "The discourses of conspiracy and apocalypse are linked by a common function: each develops symbolic resources that enable societies to address and define the problem of evil." In Barkun's formulation, conspiracy theories **locate and describe evil** while millennialism **explains the mechanism for its ultimate defeat**. The two therefore exist in a symbiotic relationship — conspiracism predisposes believers to be millennialists and vice versa, each reinforcing the other, though each can also exist independently.

### The role of improvisational millennialism

Barkun argues that conspiracy theories are now more common elements of millennialism than they were in the past. He attributes this to the rise of what he calls **improvisational millennialism** — a third millenarian style that joins the older religious and secular varieties. Unlike religious millennialism (anchored in canonical scripture) and secular millennialism (anchored in ideological texts such as Marx), improvisational millennialism is characterized by **bricolage**: the indiscriminate borrowing of elements from Eastern and Western religion, New Age ideas, esotericism, fringe science, and radical politics. Because it is unconstrained by any single tradition, it can incorporate conspiracist motifs of any origin, giving conspiracy theories an unprecedented mobility across millenarian systems.

Barkun identifies the conditions for improvisational millennialism's flourishing as (1) easy accessibility of diverse belief material, enabled by new communications technologies and weakened gatekeepers, and (2) the erosion of traditional authority structures in both religion and science.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun notes that secular millennialism, while less vital after the Cold War, has not disappeared — it persists in ethnic nationalism, xenophobic movements, and some antiglobalization rhetoric. He is therefore careful not to reduce all contemporary millennialism to the improvisational type, even though the improvisational variety is his primary focus throughout the book.
