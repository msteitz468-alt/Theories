## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that the U.S. government's counter-terrorism response to 9/11 produced significant unintended consequences for conspiracist culture. Regardless of their actual intent, the language and institutional arrangements of "homeland defense" — in particular the elevation of FEMA into a cabinet-level agency — mapped precisely onto the New World Order fears that conspiracists had been articulating for years, functioning as inadvertent confirmation rather than reassurance. For believers who already viewed FEMA's emergency powers as the template for martial law, these measures read as proof that the prophesied totalitarian takeover was imminent.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun notes: "Both the proposals and the rhetoric used to describe them inadvertently evoked parallels with the New World Order ideas that undergird contemporary American conspiracism. For example, conspiracists took *homeland defense* to mean that the imposition of martial law was imminent. Proposals to make FEMA part of a new, cabinet-level homeland-security agency raised even greater conspiracist anxieties. As already described in chapter 4, the demonization of FEMA had become a virtual article of faith in conspiracist circles, whether among militia members, fundamentalists, or ufologists."

Barkun's structural observation: "For those who already believed that history is a plot, the post-September 11 developments were read as validation. Given the closed, self-perpetuating character of conspiracy ideas, there was little anyone could have done to eradicate such beliefs."

He closes the chapter with a warning: as long as believers hold that the New World Order is almost but not quite a reality, they will confine themselves to propagandising. But should they conclude that the prophesied evil day has actually arrived — a conclusion that post-9/11 developments could plausibly trigger — "their behavior would become far more difficult to predict." Apocalypticists, he notes, are more likely to engage in violence when they believe themselves to be trapped or under attack.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun is careful to note that "there is no reason to think that any of the measures proposed or taken had any connection with conspiracist fantasies." The validation is entirely the product of the conspiracists' pre-existing interpretive framework, not of any actual convergence between government intent and conspiracist expectation. He also acknowledges that predictions of violence based on beliefs alone are "notoriously unreliable" and that inflammatory rhetoric can come from otherwise peaceable individuals.
