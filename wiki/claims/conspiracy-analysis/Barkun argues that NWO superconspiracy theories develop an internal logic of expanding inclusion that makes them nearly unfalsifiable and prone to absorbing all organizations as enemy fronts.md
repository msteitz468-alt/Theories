## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that the internal logic of NWO conspiracy theory — its premise that "everything is connected to everything else" — drives a relentless expansion toward superconspiracies in which the enemy swallows virtually every institution. "Modest" theories concentrating on a single organization (the CFR, the Trilateral Commission) are driven out or absorbed by "ambitious" superconspiracies that nest organizations inside organizations, Russian-doll fashion, until barely anything lies outside the conspiracy. This expansion reinforces the theory's unfalsifiability: when everyone outside the conspiracist circle is potentially complicit, no external information can be trusted and conspiracy theorists inevitably turn on one another.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun traces the escalation from "modest" to superconspiracy approaches in Chapter 4. The modest version — identifying the CFR or Trilateral Commission as the locus of elite control — is exemplified by John McManus (John Birch Society) and Larry Abraham ([[None Dare Call It Conspiracy (Allen & Abraham, 1971)]]). Abraham's "World Supra-Government" diagram centers on the CFR with spokes extending to international banks, the Royal Institute of International Affairs, major foundations, the executive branch, think tanks, media, and corporations.

The superconspiracy version goes much further. John Coleman's *Conspirators' Hierarchy: The Story of the Committee of 300* (1992) connects an international elite to the UN, Trilateralists, the drug trade, Gulf royal families, Freemasonry, and a "One World Government Church." Texe Marrs (author of [[The Illuminati (Marrs)]] and [[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]]) adds a coded list including "Skull & Bones Society, Aspen Institute, Knights of Malta, Opus Dei, Club of Rome, Bohemian Grove, World Economic Forum, World Federalists, and many others."

David Icke (author of works analyzed in chapters Barkun devotes to him) achieves what Barkun regards as the logical extreme — a conspiracy that includes "mind manipulation, tax-exempt foundations, elite military, the drugs and arms trades, religion, and politics — dragging in, along with the Bilderbergers, Illuminati, and other standard players, the Vatican, Christianity, Judaism, Islam, capitalism, fascism, communism, and Zionism." UFO writer Val Valerian "includes so many organizations in the conspiracy that he requires six pages merely to provide a condensed, diagrammatic representation."

The reductio ad absurdum of this logic, Barkun notes, is that conspiracy theorists turn on each other: William Cooper accused fellow Patriot celebrity James "Bo" Gritz of being "a 32nd Degree Freemason, . . . an FBI Informant, and . . . an active undercover agent."

## What This Characterizes in the Wiki

This analysis directly characterizes [[None Dare Call It Conspiracy (Allen & Abraham, 1971)]] (the "modest" pole), [[The Illuminati (Marrs)]] and [[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]] (mid-level superconspiracy), and [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] (radical superconspiracy). It is also relevant to [[Models of the New World Order Elites Compared]] — Barkun's taxonomy here illuminates why different sources identify such different primary enemies while all claiming to expose the same NWO.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun observes that even within the NWO milieu, the John Birch Society acts as a partial corrective, dismissing black helicopter stories as "naiveté, rashness, and hysteria" that embarrass the cause. This shows that there are internal standards of evidence — but Barkun's point is that these are overridden by the structural incentives toward ever-more-inclusive theories, which "appear to explain everything."
