## Claim Statement

Hofstadter argues the paranoid style is "an old and recurrent" mode in American public life whose
"content remains much the same even when it is adopted by men of distinctly different purposes" — the
*form* persists while the *villain* changes.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He traces it through the anti-Masonic movement, anti-Catholic nativism, alarms over Mormonism, the
Populist "international bankers" conspiracy, the WWI "munitions makers' conspiracy," McCarthyism, and
the John Birch Society — successive waves with the same structure but different enemies (Masons →
Catholics → bankers → Communists). He also stresses it is international (Jesuits, Freemasons,
"international Jews," Communists) and notes its "consummatory triumph" was Nazi Germany.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Hofstadter declines to claim the style is uniquely American or constant; it comes "in successive
episodic waves" of differing intensity.
