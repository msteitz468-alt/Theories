## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that the Church Committee's fifteen-month investigation — which confirmed COINTELPRO, MKULTRA, and CIA assassination plots against foreign leaders — had a paradoxical effect: it told Americans more about government crimes than any people in history had known about their own government, yet simultaneously made them trust it less. Disclosure without justice produced cynicism rather than redemption. The senator who sought to confess and redeem government sins instead vindicated the "paranoids" and accelerated the erosion of democratic trust.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Olmsted documents what the Committee revealed:

- **COINTELPRO:** The FBI had infiltrated, sabotaged, and provoked to violence dozens of American political groups. Most shockingly, it had sent Martin Luther King Jr. a composite tape of alleged sexual encounters with a letter urging him to kill himself before accepting the Nobel Peace Prize ("King, there is only one thing left for you to do. You know what it is…. You are done"). The committee condemned COINTELPRO as "intolerable in a democratic society."
- **CIA assassination plots:** The committee's interim report documented more than a dozen CIA-sponsored murder attempts against Castro and other foreign leaders, revealing CIA ties to organized crime and the fact that Kennedy had shared a "close friend" with mobster Sam Giancana while the CIA was using Giancana to target Castro.
- **MKULTRA:** The Rockefeller Commission first exposed the program; 1977 Senate hearings on 5,000 rediscovered documents revealed that forty-four universities, research foundations, hospitals, and prisons participated. The CIA dosed random citizens in "Operation Midnight Climax" and paid Dr. Ewen Cameron to conduct devastating "depatterning" experiments on psychiatric patients.
- **Operation CHAOS:** The CIA had compiled illegal files on at least ten thousand U.S. citizens.

Olmsted documents the paradox of disclosure: "The percentage of Americans who said they distrusted the government actually increased during and after Church's investigation." There was "confession, but no expiation." No one went to jail. Nixon told David Frost in 1977, "Well, when the President does it, that means that it is not illegal." The result was "discovery without justice, a (cold) war crimes trial with criminals but no convicts."

The revelations also directly revived JFK and MLK conspiracy theories. By late 1976, the lone-gunman theory in the Kennedy assassination was left "almost totally without adherents" by Gallup; 70 percent believed King's assassin was part of a conspiracy; only 5 percent accepted the government's version of both killings.

Olmsted quotes journalist Rod MacLeish: "American society has gone buggy on conspiracy theories of late because so many nasty demonstrations of the real thing have turned up."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted notes Church's own limitation: he refused to blame Kennedy personally for CIA abuses and called the CIA a "rogue elephant," though most of his committee quietly disagreed. The Pike Committee reached the opposite conclusion — that CIA was the president's "lapdog," not a rogue agency. These competing framings left the question of institutional vs. individual responsibility unresolved, further feeding conspiricism.
