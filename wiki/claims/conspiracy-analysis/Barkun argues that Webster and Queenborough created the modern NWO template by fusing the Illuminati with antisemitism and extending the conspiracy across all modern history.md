## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that it is scarcely hyperbole to say, as Nicholas Goodrick-Clarke does, that without Nesta Webster "few Americans today would have heard of the Illuminati." Webster (1876–1960) and Lady Queenborough (Edith Starr Miller, d. 1933) performed a decisive transformation of the Illuminati legend by adding two ideas: first, that world history is correctly understood only as the product of secret-society machinations; and second, that Jews are central to those machinations. These moves turned a relatively narrow anti-Illuminati tradition into a sweeping antisemitic theory of history that could absorb virtually all modern upheaval under a single conspiratorial design.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun explains that Webster and Queenborough needed to explain how an organization that ceased to exist around 1787 could still be operating in the twentieth century. Their solution was to sweep in a "whole raft of other organizations as ancestors, successors, affiliates, or subsidiaries of the Illuminati" — Knights Templar, kabbalists, Rosicrucians, Carbonari — creating what Barkun calls "a kind of interlocking directorate of conspirators who operate through a network of secret societies."

Webster's 1924 synthesis attributed the world's ills to anti-Christian Illuminati, Pan-German power, and "the Jewish power," though she was uncertain of the precise interrelation. Queenborough was more definitive: "Illuminism represented the efforts of the heads of the powerful Jewish Kahal... which has ever striven for the attainment of political, financial, economic and moral world domination." These ideas quickly crossed the Atlantic through Gerald Winrod, who concluded in 1935 that "the real conspirators behind the Illuminati were Jews" and that communism was merely "Illuminism's most recent manifestation."

Barkun further traces how *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion* — disseminated in the United States through Henry Ford's *Dearborn Independent* — reinforced this fusion. Although the *Protocols* was quickly exposed as a forgery plagiarized from Maurice Joly's political satire and a German antisemitic novel, it provided a ready-made template for Jewish-Illuminati conspiracy theory that subsequent writers like Des Griffin and William Cooper would repurpose by substituting "Illuminati" for "Jews" in the text.

## What This Characterizes in the Wiki

This analysis directly characterizes [[Secret Societies and Subversive Movements (Webster)]], identifying it as the decisive interwar synthesis that shaped all subsequent NWO literature. Barkun also discusses [[None Dare Call It Conspiracy (Allen & Abraham, 1971)]] as one node in the lineage from Webster: Abraham's work draws on the Illuminati-CFR framework, treads close to antisemitism by centralizing the Rothschilds, while nominally disclaiming it. [[The Illuminati (Marrs)]] and [[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]] belong to the same derivative tradition.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun notes that some post-WWII authors in this tradition made efforts to distance themselves from overt antisemitism, citing Abraham as an example. However, Barkun observes that structural antisemitism remained embedded in the framework (Rothschilds at the center, attacks on the Anti-Defamation League, etc.) even when authors explicitly disclaimed it. He also points out that Pat Robertson's *The New World Order* (1991) — the most mainstream entry in this tradition — was found by critics to be recycling Webster's and Mullins's antisemitic history, though Robertson denied antisemitic intent.
