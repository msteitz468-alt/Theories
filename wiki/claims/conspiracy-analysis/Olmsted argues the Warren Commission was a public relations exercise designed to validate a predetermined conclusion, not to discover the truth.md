## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that the Warren Commission was structured from the outset to ratify Hoover's lone-
gunman conclusion rather than to conduct an open investigation. President Johnson appointed it to
suppress politically dangerous conspiracy theories while concealing secrets about U.S. Cuba policy
and the FBI's competence. The commission's most important forensic finding — the "single-bullet
theory" — was disbelieved by six of its seven members, including by its nominal chairman Earl
Warren himself, yet was issued as a unanimous conclusion. Robert Kennedy called it "a public
relations exercise."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Olmsted documents the commission's design constraints step by step:

- **Johnson's framing**: "All you're gonna do is evaluate a Hoover report that he's already made."
  Johnson told Richard Russell directly. He used the threat of nuclear war — "if they don't,
  why, [if] Khrushchev moved on us, he could kill 39 million in an hour" — to coerce Warren into
  chairing an investigation he did not want.
- **Membership**: The commission included former CIA director Allen Dulles (who concealed the
  Castro plots) and John McCloy, both described as "charter members of the Washington establishment
  who could be trusted to stick to the script." Dulles was the lone commissioner to accept the
  single-bullet theory without expressed doubt.
- **The Katzenbach memo**: Within hours of Jack Ruby killing Oswald, deputy attorney general
  Nicholas Katzenbach wrote that the public must be satisfied "that Oswald was the assassin; that
  he did not have confederates who are still at large." Olmsted calls this the core of the
  government's goal — to convince the public of something Johnson "personally did not believe."
- **The single-bullet theory**: Required by the Zapruder film's timing (Oswald could not fire
  twice in the 2.3 seconds available), but internally unconvincing. Governor Connally maintained
  until death that he was hit by a separate shot. Senator Russell recorded his dissent and then
  watched Warren suppress it. Johnson told Russell privately: "I don't either" believe the same
  bullet hit both men.
- **The FBI's obstruction**: Hoover actively worked to thwart the commission, which FBI officials
  "perceived as an adversary." The bureau withheld the Oswald threatening note (which was
  destroyed), failed to investigate the Tague bystander wound for months, and produced a bullet-
  path account contradicting the autopsy doctors — providing decades of material for skeptics.
- **The autopsy**: The rushed Bethesda post-mortem, pressured by the Kennedy family to finish
  quickly (partly to conceal JFK's venereal disease and Addison's disease), failed to dissect the
  wounds, trace the bullet path, or take several routine steps. The head pathologist later admitted
  the family interference "cause[d] difficulty — of course it did."

## What this bears on in the wiki

This claim is the evidentiary core supporting the concept page
[[The JFK assassination and the collapse of public trust (Olmsted)]]. It directly complicates
[[Controversy: Was Allen Dulles complicit in the Kennedy assassination?]] — Olmsted's reading is
that Dulles's complicity was in suppression (hiding the Castro plots from his fellow commissioners)
rather than in orchestrating the murder, a more modest but documented form of culpability than
argued in [[The Devil's Chessboard (Talbot)]]. It also provides the historical template for the
pattern described in [[Hidden History (Jeffries)]]: official investigation structured to reach a
predetermined conclusion while hiding what the state is actually doing.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted is careful to note that "this does not mean that their conclusion of a single bullet or a
lone gunman was wrong." The commission's process was compromised, but process failure does not
prove the conclusion false. She also acknowledges that Johnson's motivation in suppressing a real
investigation was partly protective of humanity: a rigorous probe into Soviet or Cuban involvement
genuinely risked "catastrophic nuclear exchange," and "one scholar has argued that Johnson's
determination to avoid an investigation into a possible communist conspiracy might have saved the
world."
