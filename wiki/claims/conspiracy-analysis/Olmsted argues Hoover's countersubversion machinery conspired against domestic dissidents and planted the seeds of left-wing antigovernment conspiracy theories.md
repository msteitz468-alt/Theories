## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that J. Edgar Hoover — a committed official conspiracy theorist about communism —
used the FBI's secret powers to run genuine covert conspiracies *against* law-abiding American
citizens: leaking classified files to partisan politicians, attempting to defeat Truman at the polls,
deploying informants and "snitch jackets" against leftists and civil rights activists, and monitoring
hundreds of thousands of people who had no live security connection. These official conspiracies
were real; and they directly produced the victims who, in the 1960s, became the founding generation
of antigovernment conspiracy theorists — including many of the first Warren Report critics.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Olmsted documents Hoover's pattern of covert political action step by step:

- **Against Truman**: Hoover "began using his power to ensure that Truman was not reelected" by
  leaking his secret file on the president to Republican opponent Thomas Dewey in 1948 — a file
  consisting mostly of Missouri corruption rumors. He also fed congressional Republicans classified
  FBI information to embarrass the White House.
- **Against scientists**: Linus Pauling — one of the century's greatest chemists — was prevented
  from attending a London conference held in his honor, urged to be prosecuted for perjury after
  denying Party membership under oath (though the FBI "could never prove that Pauling had even
  attended a Communist Party meeting"), and had his mail monitored into the Nixon administration.
- **COINTELPRO**: From 1956, the bureau ran a "sophisticated vigilante operation aimed squarely at
  preventing the exercise of First Amendment rights" (Senate committee). Tactics included "snitch
  jackets" (false letters identifying targets as informers), anonymous poison-pen letters, and
  rumor-spreading about marital infidelities. By 1960, the FBI maintained 432,000 headquarters
  files on dissident groups and individuals.

The key structural irony Olmsted identifies: the most prominent victims of this McCarthy-era official
repression — people like Sylvia Meagher (WHO analyst, loyalty board target), Harold Weisberg (State
Department analyst, fired on vague "indirect association" grounds), John Henry Faulk (radio career
destroyed), and Linus Pauling (monitored for years) — became the *founding researchers of the JFK
assassination conspiracy industry*. The right-wing antigovernment conspiracy theories of the 1950s
"helped to spawn left-wing antigovernment conspiracy theories of the 1960s." If the "masters of
deceit" were actually Hoover's agents, left-wing conspiracists reasoned, there was no limit to what
they might do — "They might even try to kill the president."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted does not argue that Hoover was a cynical fraud. He "sincerely" believed communism was "the
most evil, monstrous conspiracy against man since time began." His covert operations were extreme,
but they grew from a genuine — if grotesquely inflated — security worldview. Furthermore, the
COINTELPRO revelations came only in the 1970s; the McCarthyism victims who became Warren Report
critics did not yet know the full extent of what had been done to them.
