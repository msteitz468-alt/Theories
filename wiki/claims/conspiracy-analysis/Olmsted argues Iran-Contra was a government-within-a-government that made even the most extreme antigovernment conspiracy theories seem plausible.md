## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that Iran-Contra represented the most alarming form of the conspiratorial executive: a secret government operating entirely within the White House, contemptuous of democratic oversight, willing to sell arms to a regime it publicly called terrorist, and connected to drug traffickers. It was not the subversion of the government; it was the government. The congressional majority report called it the work of a "cabal of zealots" who "plotted and conspired … lied, misrepresented and deceived" and planned "to superimpose upon our government a layer outside of our government, shrouded in secrecy."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Olmsted documents the structure of the conspiracy:

- Reagan directed his national security adviser to keep the contras "together body and soul" despite the Boland Amendment's prohibition of contra funding; this was delegated to Marine Lt. Col. Oliver North.
- North built "the Enterprise" — private businessmen, mercenaries, arms dealers, and drug runners on the U.S. government payroll, with its own planes, funding mechanism, and bank accounts. Senator Daniel Inouye called it "a shadowy government with its own air force, its own navy, its own fund-raising mechanism, and the ability to pursue its own ideas of the national interest, free from all checks and balances."
- Simultaneously, the Reagan administration secretly sold arms to Iran — the nation Reagan publicly called part of "a new, international version of Murder, Incorporated" — in exchange for hostage releases.
- North diverted profits from the arms-for-hostages deals to the contras, merging the two operations.
- CIA Director William Casey reportedly discussed plans for a permanent "off-the-shelf, self-sustaining, standalone entity" — a covert agency entirely outside democratic oversight.
- North held a "shredding party" to destroy evidence; the documentary record was fragmentary. Key figures were convicted but never served prison time — pardoned by George H. W. Bush.
- The congressional minority report, signed by eight Republicans including Dick Cheney, argued that the real problem was Congress's failure to accept the president's power.
- Reagan said privately: "If such a story gets out, we'll all be hanging by our thumbs in front of the White House."

Iran-Contra directly fueled 9/11-era conspiracy theories: William Cooper used the hearings and Church Committee reports to "prove" that aliens controlled the government; the Roswell MJ-12 documents appeared in May 1987, at the height of Iran-Contra testimony, and were modeled explicitly on the Enterprise's structure; Gary Webb's "Dark Alliance" story (CIA-contra-crack) drew its credibility from the Kerry Committee's documented findings of CIA knowledge of contra drug trafficking.

Olmsted quotes political scientist Theodore Draper: "We usually think of a junta as planning to overthrow a president. This junta came into being to overthrow an established constitutional rule of law, with the help of a president."

## What this bears on in the wiki

It also directly shapes the conspiratorial worldview in [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] — Cooper used Iran-Contra as evidence for his alien-government thesis. The "fusion paranoia" across left and right that Olmsted documents here underlies the analysis in [[Models of the New World Order Elites Compared]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted notes that Reagan's approval recovered somewhat after his willingness to negotiate with the Soviets at the end of his term — suggesting the public's cynicism about government was not absolute. She also notes that many core Iran-Contra facts were confirmed: the Kerry Committee's findings of CIA-contra drug links were validated by an internal CIA investigation, but the mainstream press largely ignored the confirmation.
