## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that in the 1980s and 1990s antigovernment conspiracy theories transcended ideology and fused left and right into what journalist Michael Kelly called "fusion paranoia." Real conspiracies — MKULTRA, Iran-Contra, contra-drug links, Tuskegee, and the FBI's COINTELPRO — made the most extreme antigovernment theories seem credible to millions across the political spectrum. The Patriot movement and its militia wing drew on neofascist doctrine but marketed a secular, libertarian antigovernment message. Both Timothy McVeigh and leftist intellectuals like Gore Vidal found common ground in contempt for what they called the "secret government."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Olmsted documents the components of fusion paranoia:

- **African American conspiracy belief and Tuskegee:** The 1972 revelation of the Tuskegee syphilis experiment (40 years of withholding treatment from Black men to study the disease) made government anti-Black conspiracies viscerally credible. By 1990, 60 percent of African Americans told pollsters it was "likely or possible" that the government deliberately ensured drugs were available in poor Black neighborhoods; only 16 percent of whites agreed.
- **Gary Webb and "Dark Alliance":** Webb's 1996 San Jose Mercury News series linking CIA agents and contra-drug dealing to the crack epidemic reached millions via the early Internet — "the first online interactive exposé." An internal CIA investigation later confirmed his core point. Major papers attacked Webb, he lost his job, and he died by suicide in 2004; conspiracists across the spectrum mourned him.
- **Ruby Ridge and Waco:** Federal sieges of Randy Weaver's family (1991–92) and the Branch Davidians (1993) — in which an FBI sniper killed Weaver's wife holding their infant, and at least 80 people died in the Waco fire — convinced millions beyond the militia movement that the government targeted nonconformists. A CBS TV movie portrayed the Weavers sympathetically; 61 percent of Americans believed federal officials (not the Davidians) started the Waco fire.
- **Timothy McVeigh and Oklahoma City:** McVeigh, a militia sympathizer saturated in antigovernment conspiracy theories, killed 168 people on April 19, 1995 — the second anniversary of the Waco fire. Gore Vidal, in a *Vanity Fair* essay, tried to justify McVeigh's terrorism; the Unabomber Theodore Kaczynski told McVeigh in prison that "certain rebellious elements on the American right and left respectively had more in common with one another than is commonly realized."
- **William Cooper and *Behold a Pale Horse*:** Cooper used the Iran-Contra hearings and Church Committee reports as "proof" that aliens controlled the government and warned that "the wolf is at the door." The X-Files (1993–2002) gave fusion paranoia its most influential popular form; 90 percent of Americans doubted the Warren Report at the show's height; only 9 percent expressed "a great deal" of confidence in the presidency.

Olmsted concludes that this conspiracism "bent the political spectrum and fused its extremes into an endless circle of paranoia."

## What this bears on in the wiki

This is Olmsted's direct account of the milieu that produced [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] — Cooper appears explicitly in Olmsted's analysis. The fusion paranoia concept also contextualizes the NWO synthesis documented in [[Models of the New World Order Elites Compared]] and the Barkun analysis of how UFO conspiracism absorbed militia antigovernment subculture.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted is careful to distinguish the conspiracists who were responding to real abuses (Webb documented genuine CIA-contra drug links) from those who leaped to false conclusions (MIHOP 9/11 theories). She does not endorse "fusion paranoia" as rational but explains it as the predictable result of governments that genuinely conspired.
