## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that World War I and its aftermath marked the birth of the modern American antigovernment conspiracy theory. Before 1914, the federal government was too small (budget under $1 billion; Bureau of Investigation had fewer than 100 agents) to be credibly feared as a conspiratorial actor. By 1918, the federal budget was nearly $13 billion, the government employed over 800,000 civilians, and it had acquired the power to draft men, commandeer industries, control the press, and imprison dissidents.

The Wilson administration used this new power to pursue three mutually reinforcing policies that Olmsted argues directly generated antigovernment conspiracy thinking:

**1. The propaganda apparatus.** The Committee on Public Information (CPI), directed by George Creel, conducted an unprecedented campaign to manufacture consent for the war by portraying Germany as a "sinister power" plotting world conquest and by depicting domestic dissenters as enemy agents. The government blanketed the country with posters, pamphlets, and magazine advertisements advancing this official narrative.

**2. The suppression of dissent.** The Espionage Act (1917) and Sedition Act (1918) effectively criminalized criticism of the president, the government, and the war effort. Charles Lindbergh Sr.'s book plates were seized and smashed by federal agents. Eugene Debs went to prison for saying the war was fought by the poor for the rich. Vigilante violence — encouraged by the government's official rhetoric — included lynchings, tar-and-feathering, and mobs attacking antiwar speakers.

**3. Presidential secrecy about war aims.** The Nye Committee (1934–36) later proved that Wilson had known since 1917 of the secret Allied treaties dividing up conquered territory, yet had told the American people the war was fought for democracy and open covenants. This documented presidential lie became the foundational evidence for a generation of revisionists — Harry Elmer Barnes, Charles Beard, John T. Flynn — who concluded that the government had deceived the country into an unnecessary war for the benefit of bankers and anglophile insiders.

Olmsted argues the long-term consequence was a transfer of the locus of American conspiratorial fear: from alien forces trying to *capture* the government, to the government *itself* as the conspiratorial actor. The Nye Committee's final conclusion — that the growing secrecy and power of the presidency was the real danger — Olmsted calls "the taproot of modern conspiracism."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Key textual evidence from Ch1: the destruction of Lindbergh's book plates by order of Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer; the five-year budget explosion from under $1 billion to $13 billion; J. Edgar Hoover's 450,000 index cards by 1921 cataloguing suspected subversives; the lynching of IWW organizer Frank Little in Montana encouraged by the VP; the Senate Munitions Inquiry proof that Wilson lied about the secret treaties; Stephen Raushenbush's private memo calling for "open wars openly arrived at with the consent of the people."

The Nye Committee's narrative arc is central: beginning as an investigation of arms merchants, it evolved into a confrontation with presidential secrecy and the undemocratic concentration of war-making power in the executive.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted acknowledges that the revisionist historians — Barnes in particular — overstated the case against Wilson and the bankers, blaming "a few conspirators for complicated events." The Nye Committee genuinely could not prove that the House of Morgan forced U.S. intervention; Jack Morgan produced a cable showing his bank had tried to *prevent* the sterling exchange crisis. Olmsted's point is not that the revisionists were right about the conspiracy but that the government's real secrecy and propaganda gave the conspiratorial interpretation its rational foothold.

She also notes that Barnes eventually descended into Holocaust denial — the endpoint of his unhinged revisionism — which illustrates how the conspiracist impulse, once triggered by legitimate grievance, can "leap from the undeniable to the unbelievable."
