## Claim Statement

Hofstadter presents the late-1790s panic over the Bavarian Illuminati — sparked by John Robison's
*Proofs of a Conspiracy* (1797) and the Abbé Barruel's *Mémoires* — as the archetypal case of the
paranoid style: a real, modest movement (Weishaupt's Enlightenment-rationalist order) inflated into
the secret author of the French Revolution and a plot to root out all religion and government.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He recounts how Robison's "conscientious account" suddenly makes "the characteristic paranoid leap
into fantasy," and how the American clergy (Jedidiah Morse, Timothy Dwight of Yale) amplified it into
a Jacobin-Illuminati plot against the young republic.

## What this counters in the wiki

This is the *origin point* of the wiki's Illuminati conspiracy lineage — Hofstadter reframes as
"paranoid leap" the very continuity asserted as fact by
[[Adam Weishaupt founded the Bavarian Illuminati on May 1, 1776]],
[[A continuous secret force of revolution runs through history (Webster)]], and
[[An unbroken chain of secret societies connects modern elites to the ancient mysteries (Marrs)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Hofstadter (and the foreword) note there was "something to be said for the anti-Masons" — a secret
society of influential men *could* conceivably pose some threat; the pathology is the leap from that
to authorship of the Revolution.
