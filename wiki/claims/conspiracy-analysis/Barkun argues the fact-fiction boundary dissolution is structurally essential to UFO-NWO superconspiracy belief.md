## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that UFO-NWO superconspiracy depends on a systematic dissolution of the boundary between fiction and fact — what he elsewhere describes as the "fact/fiction reversal" — in which deliberately fabricated or acknowledged-fictional texts are treated as disguised truths. This is not incidental but structurally necessary: the very unavailability of confirming evidence is reinterpreted as proof of suppression, making fictional, forged, or anonymous texts as epistemically useful as genuine documents. The pattern appears in the careers of *Alternative 3*, the MJ-12 papers, and comparable texts, and is reproduced in the improvisational millennialism of both Keith and Icke.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun's central case is *Alternative 3*, the 1977 Anglia TV broadcast that "was clearly a hoax — and not only because it was broadcast on April Fool's Day." Actors were named in the closing credits; the production was admittedly fictional. Yet television and newspaper switchboards were "swamped after the broadcast," Anglia sold the book rights, and the 1978 Leslie Watkins novelization continued "the pretense of factuality." In the United States, where the broadcast had not aired, Barkun notes that "whenever the book was unavailable, believers attributed its absence to the conspirators' attempts at suppression." The belief mechanism here is self-reinforcing: unavailability (whether due to distribution decisions or genuine suppression) becomes evidence of reality. "Even those willing to acknowledge that *Alternative 3* was trumped up insisted that its core argument might very well be true — another instance of the demolition of the fact-fiction boundary."

The same logic operates with MJ-12. Though Jacques Vallee and Philip Klass considered the documents probable forgeries, their very existence proved to believers that a secret government had something to hide. Additional "related documents" continued to surface through 1998, proliferating in the way Barkun associates with superconspiracy: "just as with the Kennedy assassination, MJ-12 has generated a cottage industry of commentators, authenticators, and critics."

Jim Keith explicitly demonstrated the unfalsifiability of this structure: even knowing *Alternative 3* "had all the earmarks of a hoax," he concluded that if the broadcast was genuine, it confirmed elite control of space colonization; if it was a hoax, "it was a way of discrediting ufologists and muddying the waters" — meaning the elite was behind it either way. This is a perfect closed epistemic loop.

Barkun connects this specifically to stigmatized knowledge: in the conspiracist framework, the greater the rejection of a claim by official culture, the more it confirms the claim's truth. Icke's embrace of the reptilian thesis — explicitly "bizarre even when viewed in the larger context of contemporary conspiracy theories" — is taken by Icke himself as a warrant of its validity: "the intensity of rejection is taken to be a measure of its truthfulness."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun does not claim that all UFO believers or even all UFO conspiracists engage in this reversal. The fact-fiction dissolution is, in his account, a property of superconspiracy belief specifically — the most extreme end of the spectrum he defines. Many ufologists, including those skeptical of MJ-12, operated with conventional evidential standards. Barkun also notes that the very instability of conspiracy texts on the Internet (different versions of Cooper's documents; impossible to verify dates or authorship) may itself be a product of the same open-ended, improvised, unfalsifiable system rather than deliberate deception.
