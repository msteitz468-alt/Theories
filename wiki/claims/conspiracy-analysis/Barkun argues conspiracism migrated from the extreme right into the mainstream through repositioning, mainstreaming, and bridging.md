## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that the New World Order superconspiracy framework, once confined to the extreme anti-Semitic right and Christian fundamentalist end-times circles, underwent a dramatic cultural migration in the 1990s through three interrelated processes — repositioning, mainstreaming, and bridging — that brought it to an unprecedentedly large and diverse audience. This migration constitutes what Barkun calls "fusion paranoia": the blending of left- and right-wing, religious and secular, political and apolitical forms of suspicion into a shared conspiratorial milieu.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Repositioning**: By diffusing into ufology, conspiracism shed its most toxic political associations. Instead of being primarily linked to anti-Semites, racists, and militia members, it now cohabited with Atlantis believers, alien channellers, and alternative-medicine devotees. Barkun cites *Nexus* magazine as exemplary: its American edition professes no link to any religious, philosophical, or political ideology, yet nestles political conspiracy articles about Bush and Cheney alongside pieces on UFOs, the inner earth, and food irradiation.

**Mainstreaming**: Prior to the early 1990s, NWO conspiracism reached only two niche subcultures. Its migration into ufology connected it to a vast potential audience — survey data showed consistently high levels of public acceptance of UFO and alien-visitation beliefs across half a century. Then popular culture vehicles — *The X-Files*, the film *Conspiracy Theory* — mainstreamed conspiracist motifs (mind control, polar bases, FEMA dictatorship) to mass audiences, conferring an implied stamp of legitimacy: "If major organs of popular entertainment presented such ideas, they must be, at the least, respectable."

**Bridging mechanisms**: Three devices link the stigmatized knowledge domain to broader audiences: (a) crossover authors who cultivate multiple audience segments simultaneously (Cooper to antigovernment militants and ufologists; Icke to New Age and antigovernment right, speaking at both New Age events and "preparedness expos" for gun owners, survivalists, and militia members); (b) an alternative communications infrastructure (niche magazines, specialist publishers, Web sites) that does not respect subject-area divisions and introduces UFO-seekers to antigravity, free energy, Atlantis, and conspiracy material in a single undifferentiated stream; (c) the indivisibility of the stigmatized knowledge domain itself, which communicates to any entrant that all these rejected ideas constitute a unified alternative worldview.

Barkun's conclusion: "New World Order ideas have consequently become the ideological glue binding together an ever more disparate array of elements, some political, some spiritual, some pseudoscientific."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun is explicit that these developments did not constitute "full mainstreaming" — even so, they brought NWO beliefs to a vastly larger audience. He also notes that the farther conspiracism has reached, the more diluted the ideas have become: outreach to mass audiences takes the form of fragmentary references rather than sustained exposition. Belief in superconspiracies in its complete, systematic form is still held by a relatively small segment of the population.
