## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that the MJ-12 documents (made public 1987) were decisive in UFO conspiracism's turn toward a fully developed superconspiracy structure. By positing a supersecret twelve-member government group with total knowledge of an alien presence since at least 1947, MJ-12 licensed the claim that any absence of public evidence simply reflected the group's success at concealment — creating an epistemic structure where the conspiracy could not be falsified. This structure was then populated with the standard elements of radical-right NWO theory (mind control, concentration camps, secret government security forces, Constitution under threat) by Lear, Cooper, and others, producing what Barkun calls ufology's "dark side."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun describes the MJ-12 papers as purporting to brief President Eisenhower on a secret twelve-member group — the "Magestic-12" or "Majic-12" — allegedly established by President Truman, consisting of senior military and scientific figures, which had discovered crashed UFOs and alien bodies confirming extraterrestrial origin. The documents circulated from 1984 but were made public at a Washington UFO conference in June 1987 and reproduced in Timothy Good's *Above Top Secret*.

Barkun identifies the key structural consequence: "It mattered little whether publicly available evidence confirmed a claim; its author could always respond, 'The government knows it, but won't tell you.'" This is the classic conspiracist proof-by-rejection logic, but now embedded in an institutional fiction (the "secret government") that could generate unlimited subsidiary claims.

John Lear's elaboration (December 1987), building on MJ-12, alleged a government-alien treaty (negotiated beginning April 30, 1964, formalized by 1971) under which the U.S. acquiesced in cattle mutilations and the abduction and implanting of American citizens in exchange for alien technology. When the aliens violated the agreement — abducting far more people than agreed, creating a "Grand Deception" — the U.S. government's response was the Strategic Defense Initiative, disguised as a Cold War project. Cooper's 1988–1989 variations introduced black helicopters, the CIA's centrality, the Trilateral insignia found on alien spacecraft, concentration camps, the Kennedy assassination, and a plan to declare martial law under cover of a "terrorist nuclear threat."

Barkun observes that Cooper's "Petition to Indict" (1989), which demanded Congressional action and warned that inaction made all legislators "accessories to the conspiracy," bore structural similarities to Christian Identity preacher William Potter Gale's "Constructive Notices" of 1986 — shadow legal instruments that implied authority to try and punish government officials. The similarity demonstrates the convergent logic of the two milieus even without proven direct contact.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun notes that MJ-12 "immediately polarized the UFO community into believers and skeptics." Jacques Vallee compared it to Watergate disinformation; Philip Klass argued forgery from format and language analysis. Barkun thus treats MJ-12 not as evidence but as a document whose social function — generating an unfalsifiable framework for NWO-style claims — mattered regardless of authenticity. He also notes the impossibility of verifying Cooper's Internet documents, as versions differ and no authoritative text exists.
