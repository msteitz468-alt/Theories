## Claim Statement

Hofstadter argues the paranoid style is marked not by an absence of facts but by an *obsessive* show
of them — "heroic strivings for 'evidence'" — followed by "a curious leap in imagination … from the
undeniable to the unbelievable." The apparatus of scholarship (footnotes, bibliographies) lends
spurious plausibility to fantastic conclusions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

His examples: McCarthy's 96-page pamphlet with "313 footnote references," Welch's *The Politician*
"weighed down by a hundred pages of bibliography and notes." The paranoid mentality "is far more
coherent than the real world, since it leaves no room for mistakes, failures, or ambiguities," and
the evidence-amassing is defensive — "he is not a receiver, he is a transmitter."

## What this counters in the wiki

This characterizes the "meticulously researched and sourced" self-presentation of the wiki's
conspiracy authors — e.g. the heavily-footnoted style praised in [[Hidden History (Jeffries)]] and the
dense citation of [[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]]; compare the skeptical demarcation tools of
[[The Baloney Detection Kit (Sagan)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Hofstadter allows that paranoid writing "begins with certain defensible judgments" — the leap comes
later; and that there are "highbrow, lowbrow, and middlebrow" practitioners.
