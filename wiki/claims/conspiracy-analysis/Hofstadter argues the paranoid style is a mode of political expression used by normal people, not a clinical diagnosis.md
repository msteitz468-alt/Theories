## Claim Statement

Hofstadter argues that the "paranoid style" names a *style of expression* — heated exaggeration,
suspiciousness, conspiratorial fantasy — not a clinical illness, and that its significance lies
precisely in being used by "more or less normal people." He borrows the clinical term only as a
metaphor, as an art historian speaks of the "baroque."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He stresses style "has to do with the way in which ideas are believed and advocated rather than with
the truth or falsity of their content," and distinguishes the paranoid spokesman (who sees the plot
aimed at "a nation, a culture, a way of life") from the clinical paranoiac (who feels personally
persecuted).

## What this counters in the wiki

A neutral analytic frame for the wiki's conspiracy authors — it characterizes the *mode* of
[[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]], [[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]],
and [[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]] without diagnosing anyone.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Hofstadter concedes "nothing entirely prevents a sound program … from being advocated in the paranoid
style," and that the style's pejorative label cannot by itself settle a claim's merits.
