## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that the believers examined throughout *A Culture of Conspiracy* practise a distinctive mode of end-time belief construction he calls "improvisational millennialism": operating outside any single religious or secular tradition, they freely select and recombine elements from the open-access domain of stigmatized knowledge — alien abduction narratives, underground-base lore, Mayan calendar prophecy, Revelation imagery, earth-changes predictions, channelled messages, Masonic and Illuminati templates — into idiosyncratic but recognisably family-resemblant apocalyptic worldviews. Because every practitioner becomes their own "millenarian entrepreneur," there is no authoritative canon; yet the recurrent components produce a shared vocabulary that gives the subculture unexpected coherence.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun develops the concept through several strands in Chapter 11:

**The bricolage mechanism**: "This combination of eclectic materials, hidden knowledge, and distrust of authority is the essence of improvisational millennialism. Because the disparate elements can be endlessly recombined, and because traditional religious authority is deemed to have been co-opted by the forces of evil, every practitioner of improvisational millennialism becomes his or her own millenarian entrepreneur. The bricolage required by the improvisational style permits anyone to try his or her hand at rearranging the blocks, so that, like Legos, they may be combined into new structures."

**Communal vocabulary despite individual idiosyncrasy**: "The components keep reappearing from one structure to another, albeit in different roles — Vatican inner circles, malevolent aliens, secret councils of plutocrats, subterranean installations, and so on. In effect, as New World Order ideas have spread and have attached themselves to ufology, the result has been the development of a common millenarian vocabulary — a set of ideas held jointly by a large if ill-defined subculture."

**Omnivorous appropriation illustrated**: Icke's adoption of José Arguelles's neo-Mayan 2012 millennialism alongside Revelation imagery exemplifies the style. Icke derives the date December 12, 2012 from Arguelles's "harmonic convergence" framework — itself a fusion of Mayan calendrical speculation with the 144,000 saved of Revelation — while simultaneously maintaining his reptilian-Illuminati superconspiracy framework. Barkun describes Arguelles's fusion as "epitomiz[ing] the hybridizing tendencies of improvisational millennialism."

**The structural problem of an omnipotent enemy**: Barkun notes that the logic of elaborating an ever more powerful conspiracy pushes believers toward hopeless pessimism. Citing Hofstadter ("The apocalypticism of the paranoid style runs dangerously near to hopeless pessimism"), Barkun argues this is why conspiracists often embrace millenarian resolution — only the expectation of ultimate triumph rescues the narrative from despair. Cooper's late-career recantation of his ET stories, for instance, was structurally driven: malevolent extraterrestrials cannot in principle be defeated, but human conspirators can.

**The warning**: Barkun closes by noting that improvisational millennialists inhabit "a different epistemic universe, where the usual rules for determining truth and falsity do not apply." Their "Trust no one" epistemology, if generalised, implies a culture war "far more extreme than anything seen previously," because the status quo's supporters are not merely misguided but are deemed the conspirators' dupes or accomplices.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun is careful not to conflate improvisation with violence: "Popular stereotypes to the contrary, millennialists rarely employ violence, and those few who do rarely initiate it against outsiders." Aum Shinrikyo is the sole recent case of an improvisational millennialist group initiating mass violence against outsiders, and Barkun regards it as an aberration, not a template. He also resists the most radical dissolution of the mainstream/fringe boundary (Jodi Dean's claim that there is no longer a "consensus reality"), maintaining that while the boundary has become more permeable it has not disappeared.
