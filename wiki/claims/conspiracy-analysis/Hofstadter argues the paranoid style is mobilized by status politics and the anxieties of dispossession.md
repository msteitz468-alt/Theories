## Claim Statement

In the collection's Part I, Hofstadter argues the paranoid/pseudo-conservative revolt is driven less
by material "interest politics" than by **"status politics"** — projected rationalizations arising
from status anxiety and "the social rootlessness and insecurity of American life" — and is mobilized
chiefly in times of acute political anxiety, by conflicts felt to be over ultimate values rather than
negotiable interests.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He locates the 1950s revolt (McCarthyism) among two status-stressed groups: declining old-family
Anglo-Saxon Protestants and old-stock German/Irish Catholics straining for middle-class respectability
— mobilized against the "international communist conspiracy" and the post–New Deal federal government.
In the title essay he adds that the paranoid disposition is "mobilized into action chiefly by social
conflicts that involve ultimate schemes of values," and that those who feel shut out of normal
political bargaining have their sense of a "sinister and malicious" power "fully confirmed."

## What this counters in the wiki

Supplies a social-psychological *cause* for the conspiracism the wiki catalogs — the "dispossession"
behind movements that produce sources like [[None Dare Call It Conspiracy (Allen & Abraham, 1971)]]
and [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]; relates to
[[Ideological Genealogy of Alternate History and Conspiracy Theories]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The foreword notes Hofstadter himself later saw limits in the thesis — it leaned on sociology over
history and underrated long-running strains (1790s Federalists, Coughlin) and Protestant
fundamentalism within the new Right.
