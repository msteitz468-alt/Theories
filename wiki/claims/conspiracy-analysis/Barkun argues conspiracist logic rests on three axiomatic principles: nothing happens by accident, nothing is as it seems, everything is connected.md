## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that a conspiracist worldview implies a universe governed by design rather than randomness, and that this commitment to design manifests in three principles found in virtually every conspiracy theory:

1. **Nothing happens by accident.** Conspiracy implies a world based entirely on intentionality, from which accident and coincidence have been removed. Anything that happens occurs because it has been willed. At its most extreme, Barkun observes, the result is a "fantasy [world] far more coherent than the real world."
2. **Nothing is as it seems.** Appearances are deceptive, because conspirators wish to disguise their identities and activities. The appearance of innocence is therefore deemed no guarantee that an individual or group is benign.
3. **Everything is connected.** Because the conspiracist's world has no room for accident, pattern is believed to be everywhere — hidden from plain view. The conspiracy theorist must therefore engage in constant linkage and correlation in order to map these hidden connections.

Barkun notes an important double valence to this worldview: it is frightening because it magnifies the power of evil (in extreme cases producing an outright dualism in which light and darkness struggle for cosmic supremacy), but it is simultaneously reassuring because it promises a world that is meaningful rather than arbitrary. The clear identification of evil gives the conspiracist a definable enemy against which to struggle, endowing life with purpose.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

*A Culture of Conspiracy*, Ch. 1, pp. 3–4 (lines 945–1013 in the raw text). Barkun introduces the three principles as a structural definition underlying all conspiracy belief, prior to distinguishing between event, systemic, and superconspiracy types.

## What this characterizes in the wiki

These three principles operate as the deep grammar of the conspiracy worldview articulated across multiple wiki sources. Barkun's formulation provides an analytical frame for claims in [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] and the broader NWO literature, where interlocking "everything is connected" arguments are pervasive.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun is careful not to treat conspiracism as simply irrational or pathological. He notes that the quest for pattern and coherence is understandable, and he distances himself from Hofstadter's more pejorative clinical language (see [[The paranoid style in politics (Hofstadter)]]). The three principles describe the internal logic of conspiracism, not a diagnosis of its adherents.
