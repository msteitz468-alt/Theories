## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that while NWO ideas had existed for decades in both Antichrist and Illuminati versions, they "always been a subsidiary motif" and "did not appear as the central organizing concept for significant numbers of fundamentalists and right-wing ideologues until about 1990." The key catalyst was not George H. W. Bush's use of the phrase "new world order" (which conspiracists interpreted as brazen signaling to fellow plotters), but the collapse of the Soviet Union between 1989 and 1991 — which suddenly removed the defining moral enemy from American millenarian and antigovernment thought, creating a vacuum that NWO theory was structurally suited to fill.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun explains that the Soviet Union functioned not merely as an enemy but as an organizing moral principle, imposing a "Manichaean moral order on the American consciousness. The world was a battleground between light and darkness." Its disappearance was "felt most acutely by millennialists," particularly dispensational premillennialists who had built elaborate end-time scenarios around Soviet involvement in a Middle East war. The Gulf War briefly reinvigorated such scenarios, but its rapid conclusion deflated them.

Barkun identifies three structural advantages that made NWO theory well-suited to fill the vacuum:

1. **Deep historical roots:** The NWO idea could be presented as a legitimate, long-established moral vision rather than an innovation — millenarians, as Boyer notes, have "a long history of bringing formerly secondary themes to the fore when circumstances require."

2. **Inherent unfalsifiability:** The NWO is "by its very nature invisible, always cloaked in garb that disguises its true nature." Nothing can count as disconfirming evidence because "nothing is as it seems." This gives the theory "a resiliency that makes New World Order theory virtually unfalsifiable."

3. **Domestic refocusing:** NWO theory allowed those previously focused on international communist threats to redirect their Manichaean anxieties toward domestic politics — federal gun confiscation, FEMA camps, UN troops on American soil — "rebuilding their Manichaean worldview in a new venue."

Barkun also notes that Bush's own patrician associations (Skull and Bones, CIA, UN) made his use of the phrase easy for conspiracists to read as deliberate signaling: the cabal had grown brazen enough to speak openly.

## What This Characterizes in the Wiki

This structural analysis of NWO's rise is relevant background for understanding the appeal documented in [[None Dare Call It Conspiracy (Allen & Abraham, 1971)]] and [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]], both of which gained large audiences in this post-Cold War milieu. It also contextualizes [[Models of the New World Order Elites Compared]] and [[Is history steered by a deliberate cabal or an emergent network? Models compared]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun does not suggest that the Cold War's end was the sole explanation — he acknowledges the deep historical roots in both Antichrist and Illuminati literatures. His argument is that the post-Cold War context was the precipitating event that elevated what had been a "subsidiary motif" to central prominence. He also acknowledges that Bush's rhetoric provided an occasion (though not the cause) for the explosion of NWO belief.
