## Claim Statement

Olmsted argues that Watergate was not merely an abuse of power by one president but the definitive proof that a secret, parallel government could operate within the executive branch — organized around paranoia, criminal conspiracy, and the fabrication of counter-conspiracies. Nixon simultaneously believed in conspiracies against him, ran real criminal conspiracies, and cynically promoted false conspiracy theories to deflect attention from his crimes. Olmsted writes: "In the Nixon administration, paranoia, conspiracy, and conspiracy theory became fundamental operating principles of the executive branch."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Olmsted documents the interlocking layers of the Nixon conspiracy:

- Nixon created the Plumbers — a secret, ad hoc unit outside the FBI and CIA, unknown beyond a select circle — to spy on and punish enemies, beginning with Daniel Ellsberg.
- E. Howard Hunt fabricated State Department cables implicating Kennedy in the murder of South Vietnamese president Diem, and attempted to plant evidence connecting would-be assassin Arthur Bremer to Democratic campaigns.
- Nixon ran an Enemies List, directing the IRS and Justice Department to harass targets.
- He ordered secret, illegal wiretaps on journalists and administration staff; expanded Operation CHAOS (CIA domestic spying) even when the CIA reported finding no foreign links to American dissenters; and secretly bombed Cambodia while filing false military reports.
- The Oval Office tapes revealed Nixon explicitly ordering obstruction of justice ("Of course, this Hunt, that will uncover a lot of things. You open that scab, there's a hell of a lot of things") and instructing the CIA to block the FBI's Watergate investigation.
- The cover-up required hush money, repeated public lies, and ultimately the attempt to use multiple federal agencies to obstruct justice — a conspiracy involving too many people to hold together.
- Even the Joint Chiefs secretly spied on the president; Adm. Elmo Zumwalt later explained this as the "logical consequence of the atmosphere of fear" Nixon created.
- Nixon resigned August 9, 1974, after the Supreme Court ordered him to release the tapes. Gerald Ford's blanket pardon — opposed by 62 percent of the public — convinced many that the nightmare was not over.

Olmsted frames the pardon as deepening rather than curing cynicism: the columnist Anthony Lewis concluded it produced "more rancor, more division, more cynicism about government and the law."

## What this bears on in the wiki

Olmsted's documented account of Watergate as a genuine presidential conspiracy vindicates the broader Olmsted thesis in [[Real government conspiracies and the rise of conspiracy theories (Olmsted)]] that proven government conspiracies are the seedbed of conspiracy culture. It also provides historical grounding for the conspiratorial worldview examined in [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] and in [[Rule by Secrecy (Marrs)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Olmsted does not argue that Watergate was part of a larger permanent conspiracy; she treats it as the product of one president's extreme paranoia and ambition. She also notes that the system ultimately worked — courts, Congress, and the press exposed the crimes — though justice was incomplete (Nixon was pardoned; no prison time for the president himself).
