## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that UFO belief occupied a strategically advantageous position in the spectrum of stigmatized knowledge: though rejected by science and government, UFO ideas retained access to cable television, mainstream bookstores, and popular culture, and were accepted (in polls) by roughly half the American public. Radical-right NWO ideas, by contrast, were subject to far stronger informal self-censorship by mainstream media, being regarded as not merely false but socially dangerous. When NWO ideas were fused with UFO ideas, ufology became "the vehicle for the New World Order to reach audiences otherwise unavailable to it," providing a reduction in stigma that gave the previously hermetic extremist worldview a mass-market distribution channel.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun marshals polling data to establish the sheer scale of UFO belief: within months of the first modern flying-saucer sighting (June 1947), 90 percent of Americans had heard of UFOs; by 1978, 57 percent believed they were real (the historical high); by 1996, 48 percent still did. In the 1996 Gallup survey, 71 percent believed "the U.S. government knows more about UFOs than they are telling us." Surveys of the UFO community found its members "anything but 'fringe'" — predominantly white, male, middle-class college graduates. This mass base meant UFO ideas "cannot be stigmatized . . . so completely that they are banned from public discussion."

By contrast, Barkun observes, "beliefs about secret Illuminati conspiracies, imminent UN invasions, and Jewish, Masonic, or Jesuit plots . . . have been informally banned from media, classrooms, and other mechanisms of knowledge distribution." Pat Robertson's *The New World Order* (1991) and Pat Buchanan's speeches offered only "highly diluted versions" of NWO theory, and Robertson's "even his weak version produced significant political problems."

Barkun's structural argument: "The linkage of New World Order ideas with UFOs gave the former a bridge to the territory of semirespectable beliefs." The migration was bidirectional — first NWO ideas entered ufology, then in the 1990s NWO-UFO combinations returned to the right-wing milieu, producing both the Cooper/Keith human-conspiracy-faking-alien-invasion synthesis and the Icke extraterrestrial-reptilian-masters synthesis.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun does not claim the UFO-NWO fusion was strategically planned: "It does not seem to have been consciously undertaken by conspiracists or done for opportunistic reasons . . . it began in a disorganized, piecemeal fashion." He treats it as an unintended consequence of parallel development in adjacent stigmatized milieus. He also notes that ufology's adoption of NWO ideas "was by no means universal" — many ufologists rejected the political turn.
