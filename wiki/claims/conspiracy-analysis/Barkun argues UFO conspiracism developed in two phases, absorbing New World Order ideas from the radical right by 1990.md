## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that UFO conspiracism developed through two distinct phases between 1975 and 1990. The first phase (roughly 1975–1986) was characterized by fragmented, internally generated conspiracist themes within ufology — implants, cattle mutilations, Nazi-saucer legends, underground bases — that developed independently of the radical right's New World Order ideology. The second phase (1986–1990), initiated by George Andrews's *Extra-Terrestrials among Us* and greatly accelerated by the MJ-12 documents (1987) and the statements of John Lear and William Cooper (1987–1989), brought explicit convergence between UFO speculation and the fully developed NWO conspiracism of Christian patriot and militia circles.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun locates the first phase's seeds in several disparate developments: abductee implant stories (a small 5% of 270 abduction accounts, per Thomas Bullard's analysis) that could be read as validating both biblical "mark of the beast" fears and secular mind-control fears; Ernst Zundel's neo-Nazi 1976 publications linking flying saucers to a Nazi underground; and Linda Moulton Howe's 1980 documentary *A Strange Harvest* alleging a UFO–cattle mutilation connection with government complicity. The British broadcast *Alternative 3* (Anglia TV, 1977) was "clearly a hoax" but was widely treated as factual, establishing — in Barkun's words — "the demolition of the fact-fiction boundary."

The second phase opens with Andrews (1986), whose conspiracy theory placed extraterrestrials at the pinnacle of a structure otherwise indistinguishable from militia-era NWO theory (CIA "government within a government," Rex 84 concentration camps, imminent martial law). The MJ-12 papers (made public June 1987) posited a secret Truman-era group of twelve senior military and scientific figures who had covered up alien crash retrievals. Barkun observes that MJ-12 "allowed great latitude in what might be 'revealed'" because the alleged secret government could be said to have hidden all confirming evidence — a self-sealing epistemic structure. John Lear's December 1987 statement elaborated MJ-12 into a government-alien treaty in which the U.S. agreed to permit cattle mutilations and abductions in exchange for alien technology, which "turned out to be only partially usable." William Cooper's 1988–1989 Internet documents and MUFON symposium presentation introduced the CIA, the Trilateral Commission insignia, black helicopters, concentration camps, and mind-control implants as NWO elements within a UFO frame.

Barkun emphasizes that throughout this period "any link between [UFO conspiracists and the militia/antigovernment subculture] in the 1980s appears circumstantial" — the convergence was unplanned, piecemeal, and driven by parallel structural logic rather than direct coordination.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun notes that UFO belief was never uniformly conspiracist: early ufologists "did not generally advance a broader political agenda" and the convergence was adopted only by some segments of the community. He also notes significant skepticism within ufology toward MJ-12 (e.g., Jacques Vallee suspected disinformation; Philip Klass argued forgery), demonstrating that UFO conspiracism was internally contested.
