## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that Milton William Cooper was "the most conspicuous link between UFO conspiracists and militia circles" — the single figure who most fully embodied and catalyzed the UFO-NWO fusion. Cooper began in the late 1980s as a ufologist claiming access to classified navy documents about alien-government relations, and by the late 1990s had migrated entirely to militia and antigovernment activism, taking the UFO-derived conspiracy framework with him. His 1991 book *Behold a Pale Horse* remains widely circulated in both ufology and antigovernment circles.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun describes Cooper's trajectory in detail. Cooper served in the U.S. Air Force and later the Navy (discharged 1975). His 1988–1989 Internet documents — beginning with "Top Secret/Majic," posted December 18, 1988 — claimed he had seen classified UFO documents during his 1972 naval service, and elaborated on the MJ-12 framework with distinctly NWO-compatible elements: the CIA as "central to the enterprise," black helicopters accompanying test flights of recovered alien craft, a "trilateral insignia" on alien spacecraft linking the Delta security teams to the Trilateral Commission, mass implanting (one in forty Americans), and concentration camps to be activated under martial law declared on pretext of a "terrorist nuclear threat."

Cooper's text, Barkun notes, introduced the Kennedy assassination, Rockefeller family, and explicit charges of CIA involvement in the international drug trade (via the "Petition to Indict," 1989). By framing inaction as making legislators "accessories to the conspiracy," the petition resembled the "shadow legal institutions" of Christian Identity and common-law court movements of the antigovernment right.

By the late 1990s Cooper had "moved away from the ufology community . . . to the subculture of militias and other antigovernment groups." He targeted Bill Clinton as "The Illuminati Socialist President," took on the IRS, claimed to lead the "Second Continental Army of the Republic (Militia)," and was named a "major fugitive" by the U.S. Marshals Service. He was shot and killed by sheriff's deputies in November 2001. Barkun describes this as "a bizarre conclusion to a strange life."

Barkun also traces Cooper's eventual reversal on aliens: whereas his 1988–1989 documents accepted the reality of extraterrestrials, by the end of his life Cooper had concluded there were "in fact no aliens at all" — the appearance of alien assault was manufactured by human conspirators to justify global dictatorship. This places Cooper in the *Alternative 3* tradition and in direct opposition to the Icke synthesis.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun notes that Cooper's documents present serious verification problems: different versions of his texts circulate on the Internet with no way to determine if changes have been made, and Cooper attributed textual differences to having undergone "hypnotic regression" — a claim that undermines rather than strengthens his credibility as an eyewitness. Barkun is explicit that Cooper's claims are based on the "paucity of evidence" and that his "insider's view" rested entirely on unverifiable personal assertion.
