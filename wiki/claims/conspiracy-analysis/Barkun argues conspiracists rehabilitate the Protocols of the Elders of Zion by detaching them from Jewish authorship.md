## Claim Statement

Barkun argues that the thoroughly discredited anti-Semitic forgery *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion* has been systematically reintroduced into contemporary conspiracist literature through a characteristic manoeuvre: authors validate the document's supposed prophetic accuracy while simultaneously denying its Jewish authorship, re-attributing it to the Illuminati, the Rothschilds, or other superconspiracy actors. This allows the Protocols' anti-Jewish content to circulate in a nominally de-Judaised form, providing the rhetorical template of a secret plan for world domination without exposing authors to the most obvious charge of anti-Semitism.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Barkun traces a spectrum of strategies:

- **Stan Deyo** insists "The Protocols are real; they do exist" but claims Jews are merely the scapegoat, not the authors.
- **Milton William Cooper** reprints the *Protocols* in full as an appendix to *Behold a Pale Horse* while attaching word-substitution instructions: replace "Jews" with "Illuminati" and "goyim" with "cattle." Barkun notes this is "somewhat disingenuous."
- **David Icke** called them "the Illuminati Protocols" in 1995, wanting "to get away from the Jewish emphasis." By 1999 his account had crystallised: the *Protocols* were created by "the Rothschilds and the reptile-Aryans" — meaning Jews (through the Rothschild banking family and Alfred Rosenberg, recast as "a Rothschild agent of Khazar descent") remain implicated even in Icke's nominally non-anti-Semitic re-attribution.
- **Jim Marrs** in *Rule by Secrecy* (HarperCollins, 2000) acknowledges the forgery's czarist origins but concludes they "may indeed reflect a deeper conspiracy beyond its intended use to encourage anti-Semitism, one hidden within the secret upper ranks of the Illuminati and Freemasonry."

Barkun traces the Illuminati-authorship thesis back to Nesta Webster (1924) and Des Griffin (1976), demonstrating that the rhetorical device has a long genealogy in the conspiracy literature.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Barkun acknowledges that some conspiracists are more explicit than others. The spectrum runs from outright Phoenix-publications anti-Semitism (Hatonn transmissions, Father Coughlin reprints, Henry Ford's *International Jew*) through the oblique Icke approach to the relatively less virulent mainstreamed version in Marrs. The mainstreaming of the *Protocols* into a HarperCollins trade title is, for Barkun, particularly significant as evidence of the deteriorating boundary between fringe and mainstream.
