## Claim Statement

David Bohm holds that the everyday tangible world (the "explicate order") is an illusion-like surface unfolded from a deeper, **implicate (enfolded) order** — an undivided wholeness in which, at the subquantum level, separateness and location cease to be meaningful (**nonlocality**). Particles are not separate objects but momentary unfoldings of a totality "enfolded throughout the whole of space."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot traces Bohm's path: his dissatisfaction with the Copenhagen interpretation (sharpened by six months of talks with Einstein), his concept of the **quantum potential** guiding particles via information about the whole environment, and nonlocal interconnectedness shown by the EPR paradox, Bell's theorem (1964), the **Aharonov–Bohm effect**, and — decisively — Alain **Aspect's 1982 experiment** in Paris, which Talbot says demonstrated "the very fabric of reality itself possesses what appears to be an undeniable 'holographic' property." Reflecting on the hologram, Bohm concluded "the universe actually employed holographic principles… was itself a kind of giant, flowing hologram," published in *Wholeness and the Implicate Order* (1980).
