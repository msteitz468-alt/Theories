## Claim Statement

Talbot argues that the holographic model "was foreshadowed" in the mystical and religious traditions of cultures worldwide, and that their descriptions of reality — the Tibetan Buddhist *void*, the Hindu *Brahman* and *maya*, the Kabbalist's illusory creation, and the cosmologies of shamans — are pre-scientific descriptions of the same holographic, undivided, "omnijective" reality that Bohm and Pribram arrived at by other means. In a holographic universe, he suggests, "the dream is dreaming itself."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot assembles a perennialist catalogue (ch. 9) of traditions he reads as holographic:

- **Tibetan Buddhism** — reality as *maya*, "an illusion," and the world as "really a vast, resonating symphony of wave forms, a 'frequency domain'"; the *Tibetan Book of the Dead* "repeatedly stresses the dreamlike nature of the afterlife realm"; the Tantric mystics held thought-stuff (*tsal*) animates "the entire universe… a product of the mind," with the average mind likened to "a small puddle isolated from the great ocean."
- **Hinduism** — "The Hindu Vedas and yogic texts assert again and again that the universe is God's dream"; reality is *maya*.
- **Kabbalah / shamanism / Sufism** — shamanic "dreamtime" journeys, ayahuasca visions, kahuna lore (the "high self" *aumakua* and "shadowy body stuff" *kino mea*), and Sufi "imaginal" realms are presented as repeated, cross-culturally consistent encounters with the implicate order; the Australian aboriginal **dreamtime** is "almost identical to the afterlife planes of existence described in Western sources."

He frames the whole as the **"omnijective universe"** — neither subjective nor objective — and converges the traditions on a single question and answer: most traditions say reality "is the dream of a single divine intelligence, of God," closing with the Kalahari Bushmen's saying, "The dream is dreaming itself."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Consistent with his stated caveats, Talbot presents these as suggestive resonances rather than proof; he notes the difficulty that "in an imaginal realm such descriptions don't mean very much," that visionary cities and beings "can never be sure whether… realities or just allegorical phantasms," and cites Sri Aurobindo's "warning against turning spiritual understandings into religious ones."
