## Claim Statement

Talbot's core thesis: combining David Bohm's physics with Karl Pribram's neuroscience yields the view that **the brain is a hologram interpreting a holographic universe**. Objective reality as we perceive it is a projection — "a splendidly detailed illusion no more or less real than the image of Princess Leia" — unfolded from a deeper level of reality "literally beyond both space and time."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot stresses that Bohm (a physicist dissatisfied with quantum theory) and Pribram (a neuroscientist confronting the failure of localized-memory models) "arrived at their conclusions independently and while working from two very different directions," lending the convergence weight. He marshals the men's stature and integrity (both defied McCarthyism / lobotomy orthodoxy), experimental support (Pribram's corroborated predictions; Aspect's 1982 nonlocality result), and the explanatory reach over the paranormal. He repeatedly hedges: it is a "model/paradigm/analogy/metaphor," "highly controversial and by no means accepted by a majority of scientists," and Bohm and Pribram "do not embrace all of the views… put forward in this book."
