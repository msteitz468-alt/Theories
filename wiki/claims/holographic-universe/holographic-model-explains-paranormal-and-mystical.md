## Claim Statement

Talbot argues the holographic model's great virtue is that it "suddenly made sense of a wide range of phenomena" science has placed "outside the province of scientific understanding" — telepathy, precognition, psychokinesis, near-death and out-of-body experiences, lucid dreams, synchronicity, and "mystical feelings of oneness with the universe." If each part of reality contains the whole and all things are nonlocally connected, such phenomena become intelligible rather than impossible.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot reports the extensions made by other researchers: **Kenneth Ring** — near-death experiences (and death) are "the shifting of a person's consciousness from one level of the hologram of reality to another"; **Stanislav Grof** — only a holographic model can explain archetypal and collective-unconscious experiences in altered states; **Fred Alan Wolf** — lucid dreams are "real images" (vs. ordinary dreams' "virtual images") and may be "visits to parallel universes," pointing to a future "physics of consciousness"; **F. David Peat** — Jungian synchronicities are "flaws in the fabric of reality," brief glimpses of the implicate order's unity of mind and matter; and **Robert Jahn & Brenda Dunne** (Princeton PEAR) — a decade of experiments showing minds can affect machines, "unequivocal evidence that the mind can psychically interact with physical reality."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Talbot concedes the model "is highly controversial and is by no means accepted by a majority of scientists," that psychic functioning "is often very difficult to pin down in the lab," and presents these extensions as the (sometimes speculative) interpretations of various researchers rather than established fact.
