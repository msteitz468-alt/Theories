## Claim Statement

Karl Pribram argues memory and perception are not stored in localized brain cells ("engrams") but **distributed** across the brain on holographic principles, so that — as in a hologram — every portion of the relevant tissue contains the whole. In its strong form: the brain constructs perceived "hard" reality by mathematically interpreting frequencies from a domain beyond space and time.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot recounts the puzzle that started Pribram: Wilder Penfield's experiments suggested specific memories had specific locations, yet Karl Lashley found that **no matter what portion of a trained rat's brain he removed, he could not eradicate its memories**. The holographic analogy resolved this: cut a piece of holographic film in half and each half still reconstructs the whole image (hazier as it shrinks). Pribram's model also accounts for the brain's vast storage capacity, associative recall, and the way we recognize a face changed by years. Talbot notes "numerous studies have corroborated Pribram's various predictions about the holographic nature of memory and perception."
