## Claim Statement

Pasulka argues that the dominant institutional framing of UFO contact — as a future possibility still awaiting proof — contradicts both the testimony of scientists embedded in government UFO research programs (including [[Garry Nolan (Stanford immunologist)]] and Tyler D.) and the knowledge systems of indigenous cultures worldwide, which record contact with nonhuman intelligence as an ongoing, lived reality. The gap between these framings is not a data gap but a product of confirmation bias shaped by media and colonial assumptions about what counts as legitimate knowledge.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Pasulka describes a 2018 exchange between scholars of indigenous studies and SETI's Breakthrough Listen Initiative. SETI asked indigenous scholars what they would want SETI scientists to know about potentially making contact. During collaboration, SETI instructed the scholars to keep their statement to two pages "to avoid all commentary about whether contact had already begun." The indigenous scholars, who represent communities for whom contact has been an ongoing reality, refused to misrepresent their cultural knowledge.

Pasulka writes: "It appears that SETI and many people have a specific idea of contact that is informed by media. If data emerges that contradicts this belief or idea, they dismiss it. This is called 'confirmation bias' and it plays a huge role in the public perception of UFO events."

She cites Oglála Lakȟóta researcher Kite (Suzanne Kite): "The colonial sense of progress is indelibly fixed to an unrelenting linear timeline towards a settler future, where Indigenous peoples are the uncivilized past, American white-superiority the present, and Mars colonization and extraterrestrials the future."

Regarding the scientists: "The scientists who studied what they believed are UFOs do not have a military scenario of UFO contact, and, like the indigenous scholars who spoke with SETI, believe that we are already in contact. They do not posit contact as a future possibility, but as a lived reality."

Pasulka also notes that Lakota are among many indigenous cultures that report contact with members of "Star Nations," and that Harvard researcher John Mack found most human societies recognize people have contact experiences and do not pathologize them.

## Contexts Within the Source

This is one of the book's central structuring arguments, established in the Introduction and sustained throughout. It provides the intellectual rationale for Pasulka's choice of experiencers as primary data sources.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Pasulka acknowledges she is making a comparative claim across disparate traditions and does not equate indigenous Star Nations frameworks with extraterrestrial spacecraft hypotheses. She explicitly remains agnostic about what the nonhuman intelligence actually is. The fear that contact disclosure would eradicate religious belief is noted as a real social concern — one Pasulka argues is unfounded, since major religious traditions already have metaphysical categories for nonhuman beings.
