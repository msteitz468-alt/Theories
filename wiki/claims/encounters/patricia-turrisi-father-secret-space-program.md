## Claim Statement

Patricia Turrisi, a scholar of Charles S. Peirce and former colleague of Pasulka who was also recruited by the NSA, reports that her father held a top-secret clearance and worked on classified aerospace and space programs (at Kollsman Instrument Company, Sperry, and Grumman, among others) from at least the 1950s until his death in 1977. She states that her childhood was characterized by extreme secrecy, intense surveillance, financial opacity, and social isolation consistent with what Pasulka calls "the Invisibles" — a community of scientists and aerospace workers embedded in intelligence-linked programs, whose family lives were subordinated to operational security.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Pasulka conducts a January 2022 interview with Turrisi (published in Ch. 8). Key reported details:

- **Father's employment**: Worked at Kollsman Instrument Company (navigation instruments), Sperry, and Grumman; held a top-secret clearance; participated in the Lunar Excursion Module program (put his handprint on one of the modules); delivered UNIVAC computing equipment; was involved in something Turrisi's brother described as "a navigational matrix that used starlight as its basis."
- **Secrecy and the word "simulacrum"**: Father and his colleagues used the word "simulacrum" as a coded reminder to maintain their cover stories.
- **Surveillance**: Turrisi describes never being allowed to talk on the phone, having no sleepovers, being monitored "every minute," with fifteen-minute windows to walk home from school. She interprets the constraints as possibly related to kidnapping fears tied to her father's security status.
- **Financial opacity**: After her mother's death, Turrisi found "dozens of bank accounts (empty after being very full)" she could not account for, and notes the family lived as if "scraping by" despite what appeared to be significant wealth.
- **Father's social network**: His wake was attended by approximately a hundred people Turrisi had never met, who knew her but would not say how. She writes, "It's as if our lives were being erased even as we lived them."
- **The accelerated program (EAP)**: At age eight or nine, Turrisi was enrolled in an "Extra Accelerated Program" for children with IQs of 140+, which she describes as oriented toward space and which she can find no record of on the internet. Many cohorts of EAP children died young; she speculates her own survival may be linked to her habit of "speaking to power in the face of social censure."
- **NSA interest in Turrisi herself**: NSA recruited Turrisi as an adult and sought her help creating a "thinking machine" based on her Peirce scholarship; she declined because NSA would not share real-time problems for investigation.

Pasulka's framing:

> "It became clear that, in reference at least with the 'secret' programs related to UFOs, much of the data was stored by people and passed on as oral tradition."

She connects the Turrisi case to Plato's critique of writing in *Phaedrus*, arguing that the "oral tradition" of classified UFO knowledge is transmitted through people — "stored in human bodies and minds."

## Contexts Within the Source

Pasulka uses the Turrisi interview to illustrate the broader thesis that intelligence-linked "Invisible" communities form a parallel world to public science, and that UFO-related data is preserved in oral tradition rather than written record. She also argues that "children of the Invisibles" like Turrisi often possess the download capacity — Turrisi "just knew the answers" in math without being able to explain her method — suggesting this ability may be cultivated or inherited within these communities.

The chapter also includes Pasulka's encounter with a new colleague at the London West Hollywood Hotel, where [[Jacques Vallée]] warned her that "people in intelligence communities are generally very charming. They meet you. Then they meet your friends. Then they meet your family." Pasulka uses this as the framing context for understanding the Turrisi case and the broader "Children of the Invisibles" thesis.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

- Turrisi herself notes that "the corroborating evidence is sketchy and mostly by my own inference."
- She acknowledges that "memory is a trickster, and many of the things I'm about to tell you happened almost half a century ago."
- She cannot verify the precise nature of the classified programs and her accounts rest primarily on childhood inference and fragments of adult discovery.
- Pasulka is aware that the "secret space program" terminology has a highly mythologized form in popular and entertainment media, and she explicitly distances Turrisi's account from that mythology, noting it "corresponded to the lived realities of the people I had come to know."
