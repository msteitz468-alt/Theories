## Claim Statement

Pasulka argues, drawing on [[Jacques Vallée]]'s observations, that public and researcher perception of UFO contact has been decisively shaped by entertainment media and popular culture — particularly the iconic alien image on the cover of [[Whitley Strieber]]'s [[Communion (Strieber)]] — to the point where witness reports that do not conform to this template are actively discounted by researchers. This media-driven template produces a feedback loop in which only culturally pre-approved contact scenarios are taken seriously, suppressing the far stranger and more varied reality of what witnesses actually report.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Pasulka writes: "Their version of contact envisions a scenario in which nonhuman and extraterrestrial intelligence contact humans through beings that look humanlike and drive spaceships that resemble flying sports cars." She contrasts this with what witnesses actually report, noting the gap is wide.

Regarding [[Communion (Strieber)]]: "The cover of the book features the image of what has come to be the iconic look of an alien being, with large almond-shaped eyes and greenish reptilian skin. The image pervaded culture to the extent that Jacques noticed that if witness stories did not conform to the look of Strieber's alien, researchers discounted their reports. Media shapes how people view contact and determines how they envision UFO contact scenarios."

She also notes: "UFOs are, as Carl Jung famously wrote, 'technological angels.' They are symbols and signs of a new epoch in human history."

Pasulka applies this point reflexively: she states that she herself inherited the flying-saucer-from-another-planet template before her research, and that her immersion in witness testimony forced a wholesale revision of that view.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim anchors the Introduction's argument for returning to witness testimony and for adopting a religiously and technologically informed interpretive framework rather than the media-derived extraterrestrial-spacecraft default.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Pasulka is describing the bias phenomenologically — she does not claim that the Communion-template alien is false, only that it functions as an a priori filter that distorts research. She acknowledges that experiencers' own prior media exposure may shape how they narrate and interpret their encounters.
