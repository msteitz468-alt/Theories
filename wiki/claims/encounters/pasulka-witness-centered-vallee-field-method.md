## Claim Statement

Pasulka argues that [[Jacques Vallée]]'s method — conducting long-term, quiet field research by interviewing first-hand witnesses before their cases enter press or UFO-community circulation — is the most sound methodology for studying UFO phenomena, yet it has not dominated official or academic studies. Pasulka adopts and extends this approach in *Encounters*, centering the book on witness and experiencer testimony rather than on remote analysis or theoretical debate.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Pasulka quotes Vallée directly:

> "The only way we're going to understand it is to stop talking to each other, and go back and talk to the witnesses. So, I put the highest priority on first-hand cases that had not been reported to the press or to the UFO community, because the moment the cases become part of ongoing discussion, they get polarized: the witnesses are bombarded with all kinds of questions; there are biases; the ego gets into it. I wanted to do a quiet kind of long-term research."

Pasulka notes that Vallée "maintains a career as a well-known and successful venture capitalist and calls his work in the field of UFOs a hobby," and that his "recognition of potential disinformation is invaluable in the assessment of UFO cases." She identifies him as a close associate of [[J. Allen Hynek]], the longtime USAF consultant on UFOs and a key figure in most secret UFO research programs. Pasulka also notes that academics generally avoid the topic of disinformation related to UFOs, but she argues it is unavoidable: "Responsible research into this topic must address disinformation."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim appears in the Introduction's subsection "The Witnesses" and frames the epistemological stance of the entire book. It serves as the methodological justification for Pasulka's choice to foreground personal testimony over institutional data.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Pasulka acknowledges that the book remains phenomenological — she does not adjudicate the truth or falsity of contact claims, but documents the social and experiential realities of witnesses. Vallée himself is quoted emphasizing the risk of bias and polarization once cases enter public circulation, which means even this method has integrity limits once testimony is mediated.
