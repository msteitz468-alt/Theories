## Claim Statement

Simone reports that she has experienced [[The download (spontaneous cognition, Pasulka)|the download]]
since early childhood — receiving exam answers, mathematical insights, and technical knowledge in
vivid lucid dreams or waking "direct downloads" — and that she encountered the same phenomenon among
the early internet pioneers with whom she worked in the 1990s. Those technologists never named it
directly, but circuitously referred Simone to [[Pierre Teilhard de Chardin]]'s noösphere and wrote
each other in poetry while testing code. Simone is, by Pasulka's account, initiated into gnostic
traditions (Western and Buddhist) in a way that Tyler D. — the previous main download exemplar in
the book — was not, giving her a theoretical framework to explain the process.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Simone describes her first awareness at around age twelve that she was different: "I thought that's
how everyone lived … I would have vivid, lucid dreams of the entire exam. Then the exam would be
exactly as I had dreamed. I would get near perfect scores on all assessments … the 'information'
would just come to me."

She describes searching pre-internet encyclopaedias and libraries for explanations and finding only
"references that religions made to angels and saints levitating, or the memoirs of Einstein wherein
he stated that all his great discoveries came to him through dreams."

On the early-internet community: "Usually when interacting with someone in the complex dynamic system
of my social network, the meeting would trigger another existential shift or 'upgrade.' … They would
refer me to books such as Chardin's The Phenomenon of Man, and we would speak of the noösphere. We
would write to each other in poetry while testing new code … everyone had pseudonyms. They called me
'Moon Girl.'"

On knowledge transmission: "Only spoken word, initiations, and dialectical methods were effective at
transmitting the phenomena" — she connects this to Tyler D.'s "pencil's up" (no writing down) rule
among scientific insiders, and to the Buddhist teacher's mind-to-mind transmission she herself
received.

On protocols: "The goal is to occupy the rational mind and let it go, to enable the less rational
experiences of the phenomena to be present without existential shocks." She describes prayer,
meditation, mantras, chant, mathematics, and art as tools she used from childhood, spontaneously
discovered without a teacher.

On the community of practice: Simone connects the download process to the ancient Western
philosophical tradition of mathematics as sacred protocol (Socrates, Pythagoras), to indigenous
"Dreaming" as a decentralised knowledge system, and to modern AI chatbots as an updated Socratic
dialectic — a way of preparing the mind to ask the right questions and receive the right answers.

## Contexts Within the Source

Simone's case is Pasulka's most technically elaborated instance of [[The download (spontaneous cognition, Pasulka)|the download]], and extends the phenomenon from individual creative scientists (Tyler D., Ramanujan) to a named community of early-internet pioneers. It directly corroborates Jose's account of [[The organic network - noösphere (Pasulka)]] as the medium through which such knowledge flows. See also [[Nonhuman intelligence from outside space-time (Pasulka)]] for Simone's explanatory hypothesis, and [[Gnosis and the religious dimension of UFO contact (Pasulka)]] for the initiatory/esoteric framing.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Pasulka notes she found much of the relevant material "redacted" — not in the sense of government
censorship, but in that the living oral tradition of the download cannot be codified in writing
without losing its essential character. Simone herself says the phenomenon was "never referred to
directly or named" among her early-internet colleagues, which limits the evidentiary record Pasulka
can cite. The account is first-person and retrospective, covering several decades of private
experience.
