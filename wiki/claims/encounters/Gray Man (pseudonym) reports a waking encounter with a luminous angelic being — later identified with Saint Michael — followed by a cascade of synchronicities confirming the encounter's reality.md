## Claim Statement

A pseudonymous government scientist ("Gray Man") reports waking to find a luminous being hovering inches above his face: tall, with shoulder-length golden hair "made of light," wearing a white sleeveless gown, carrying a concealed sword, and radiating a presence Gray Man describes as pure righteousness and overwhelming power. The being transported him to a dark realm, gave him armor and weapons, and directed him to fight and defeat a dark, smoke-like evil entity. Gray Man insists this was not a dream. He had no prior awareness of the iconography of Saint Michael the Archangel when this event occurred. In the week following Gray Man's disclosure to Pasulka, she independently encountered Saint Michael imagery three times (paintings sent by an unconnected acquaintance, a garden statue, a therapist's correspondence with the archangel). Gray Man then independently discovered that a prayer card featuring Saint Michael — given to him two decades earlier by a severely troubled friend he had prayed over in a maximum-security psychiatric hospital — had appeared at the bottom of a box he opened in preparation for travel.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Gray Man is quoted extensively in Chapter 3, including on the initial encounter:

> "I woke up to see a being hovering above me. His face was about four or five inches from my face. His eyes pierced me, I felt, and he seemed to look directly into my heart, as if he was assessing me, but it wasn't unpleasant."

> "He gave me a sword. It made no sense to me. How was this going to help me? A dark, shadowy, three-dimensional black figure appeared. It was like thick dark smoke but completely opaque... I felt it was evil."

> "I then woke up at some point soon after this. It was like waking up from being knocked out or unconsciousness, not waking from sleep."

On the prayer card discovery twenty years later: "I have no idea how it got there... This is honestly freaking me out."

Pasulka notes she had assumed Gray Man knew about Saint Michael and was surprised he did not: "Apparently he didn't. He thought that I was trying to placate him about this experience, that I was 'being nice' because I had not outright dismissed it."

Pasulka frames the synchronicities through Nietzsche's concept of "personal providence" and through Tyson Yunkaporta's description of "extra-cognitive" knowledge processes in indigenous Australian Lore ("a coincidence that highlights a deep meaning or revelation").

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter 3 establishes the Gray Man as Pasulka's primary case study for the religious and supernatural dimensions of UFO-proximate contact. The Saint Michael synchronicity cluster serves as the chapter's narrative spine, leading into Gray Man's UFO sighting and his broader interpretive framework (Chapter 4). See [[The Gray Man (Pasulka)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Pasulka is careful to note she remained "of two minds" about the synchronicities. She considers a naturalistic explanation — that the convergence was "latent within his unconscious" or coincidental — but finds it insufficient for her own autonomous experiences. She does not claim to verify the ontological reality of the angelic being; she records it as Gray Man's report.
