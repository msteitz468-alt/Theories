## Claim Statement

The modern cattle-mutilation phenomenon is traced to a horse — named **"Lady," popularly "Snippy"** — killed and surgically excised near **Alamosa, Colorado, in 1967**, the first such case to receive wide publicity, and exhibiting the same signature seen in later incidents.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mezrich: "The very first well-publicized mutilation had occurred very close to Glenda's ranch, more than fifty years earlier. Back in 1967, a horse named Lady had been killed and similarly excised in nearby Alamosa, Colorado. Lady's head and neck had been skinned, and her hide appeared to have been cauterized. As with Buck and Princess, the wounds had been bloodless."

Elsewhere the book records that Zukowski held "photos, diagrams, even vet autopsy reports about Snippy's case": the animal "found lying on one side, some of the hide cut away to expose the body cavity," with "heart and brain ... missing, along with other organs," bones "bleached a strange white color," and a visiting pathologist reporting the injuries "appeared to be surgical in nature" with "no blood found in or around the carcass."

## Contexts Within the Source

Used to establish that the bloodless-surgical pattern is decades old and geographically tied to the same Colorado region (near the 37th parallel) as later cases.
