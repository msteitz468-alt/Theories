## Claim Statement

Elizondo asserts that the UAP Disclosure Act, signed by President Biden in late December 2023 as part of the FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act, was specifically designed to give the US government eminent domain authority over nonhuman biological evidence and recovered technology of nonhuman origin held by defense contractors — acknowledging in its legislative text that such materials exist.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 24, Elizondo describes the UAP Disclosure Act provisions:

- **Eminent domain**: The US government can exercise eminent domain to take possession of "biological evidence of nonhuman intelligence and any type of recovered technology of nonhuman origin" held by government elements or defense contractors
- **National Archives direction**: The National Archives is directed to collect all government UAP records under the categories "unidentified anomalous phenomena, technologies of unknown origin, and nonhuman intelligence"
- **Disclosure timeline**: UAP records must be made public within 25 years of creation unless the president certifies a national security exception
- **Congressional funding control**: Funding for any UAP-related activity not approved by relevant Congressional committees is prohibited — cutting off Legacy Program contractors from unauthorized taxpayer-funded UAP work
- **Presidential review board**: Proposed but stripped by House opposition led by Rep. Mike Turner

Elizondo quotes Senator Schumer: "The American public has a right to learn about technologies of unknown origins, non-human intelligence, and unexplainable phenomena."

## Contexts Within the Source

Elizondo frames the eminent domain provision as the legislative acknowledgment that the Legacy Program is real — Congress would not write eminent domain authority for materials it did not believe to exist. He identifies House opposition by Rep. Mike Turner as having been influenced by defense contractors involved in the Legacy Program.
