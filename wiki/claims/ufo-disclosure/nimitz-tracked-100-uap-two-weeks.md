## Claim Statement

Elizondo asserts that in the weeks preceding the November 14, 2004, visual encounter by Cmdr. Dave Fravor, the guided-missile cruiser USS Princeton — part of Carrier Strike Group 11 built around the USS Nimitz — tracked more than 100 UAP on its newly upgraded SPY-1 Aegis radar system. The objects were observed repeatedly descending from altitudes above 80,000 feet to approximately 50 feet above the ocean surface before ascending again.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 7, Elizondo describes the Nimitz carrier strike group as comprising five vessels: USS Nimitz (CVN-68), USS Princeton (CG-59), USS Higgins (DDG-76), USS Chafee (DDG-90), and USS Louisville (SSN-724). The Princeton's newly upgraded radar detected more than 100 unidentified objects over two or more weeks of pre-exercise training operations. The pattern of rapid descent from high altitude (above 80,000 feet) to near sea level and back was consistent across the encounters. Elizondo notes that the objects showed no sonic boom, no heat signature, and no radio emissions.

Elizondo states that the Nimitz case was among the most thoroughly documented UAP encounters reviewed by AATIP, with multiple sensor modalities (radar, FLIR) and multiple credible military witnesses.

## Contexts Within the Source

The extended surveillance period established that the encounters were not isolated anomalies. Elizondo uses the two-week baseline to argue that the UAP were operating in the area deliberately and repeatedly — behavior inconsistent with conventional aircraft, balloons, or atmospheric phenomena. The visual encounter on November 14, 2004, occurred as a direct result of the Princeton tasking Fravor's flight to investigate one of these ongoing contacts.
