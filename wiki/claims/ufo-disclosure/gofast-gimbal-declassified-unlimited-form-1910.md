## Claim Statement

Elizondo asserts that he personally filed DoD Form 1910 to request declassification of three UAP videos — the FLIR video (Tic Tac, 2004 USS Nimitz), the GoFast video, and the GIMBAL video — and that the DoD's Office of Prepublication and Security Review (DoDOPSR) granted UNLIMITED DISTRIBUTION status, making the videos legally releasable to the public and to any partner. The DoDOPSR actively encouraged the UNLIMITED DISTRIBUTION designation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 19, Elizondo describes the Form 1910 process in detail. He selected three unclassified videos (he excluded the Aguadilla, Puerto Rico, DHS helicopter video as too sensitive because it was a sister agency's material). He submitted the form listing the purpose as research, analysis, and sharing with government and industry partners for threat identification.

The DoDOPSR responded that they could not declassify for release to a restricted audience and recommended switching to unrestricted public release. Elizondo states this was unexpected but welcomed. DoDOPSR stamped the form: **UNLIMITED DISTRIBUTION**. He received the approval by email.

Elizondo notes that Pentagon spokesperson Christopher Sherwood later falsely claimed the videos had been cleared "for research purposes only, not public dissemination" — a claim directly contradicted by the UNLIMITED DISTRIBUTION stamp.

## Contexts Within the Source

The declassification action was the operational mechanism that allowed the December 2017 New York Times and Politico stories to be published with actual government-released footage. Elizondo frames the UNLIMITED DISTRIBUTION approval as surprising evidence that some DoD officials were willing — or even eager — to allow the videos into the public domain.
