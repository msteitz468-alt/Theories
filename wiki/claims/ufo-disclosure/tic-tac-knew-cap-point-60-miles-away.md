## Claim Statement

During the November 2004 USS Nimitz Tic Tac encounter, after Cmdr. Dave Fravor's direct visual confrontation with the Tic Tac object caused it to depart at high speed, the object reappeared at the Combat Air Patrol (CAP) rendezvous point approximately 60 miles away — a classified coordinate that Fravor had been assigned only minutes earlier and that was not broadcast on any open channel. Elizondo identifies this as evidence of real-time intelligence awareness by the UAP.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 7, Elizondo describes the Tic Tac's departure from the engagement area and its subsequent appearance at the CAP point — the prearranged "bullseye" rendezvous coordinate assigned to Fravor's flight by the USS Princeton's Combat Information Center. This coordinate was classified and communicated on secure channels. Elizondo presents the UAP's anticipation of the CAP point as one of the most operationally significant aspects of the encounter, suggesting the object had either intercepted classified communications or possessed independent intelligence-gathering capability.

## Contexts Within the Source

Elizondo frames this detail within his AATIP investigation review of the Nimitz case. He uses it to argue that the Tic Tac was not a passive phenomenon (e.g., an atmospheric anomaly or misidentified balloon) but demonstrated what the intelligence community would recognize as active situational awareness — behavior consistent with an adversary or non-human intelligence monitoring US military operations.
