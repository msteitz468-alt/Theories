## Claim Statement

Elizondo asserts that in March 1967, a UAP was observed near Malmstrom Air Force Base, Montana, during which multiple Minuteman ICBMs in their silos went offline simultaneously — their nuclear launch systems disabled without human input or equipment failure.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 4, Elizondo cites the Malmstrom 1967 incident as a documented AATIP case — one of three major UAP-nuclear interference events (alongside Rendlesham 1980 and Ukraine 1982). He describes the ICBMs as having been disabled while a UAP was in the vicinity of the launch control facility. This event is presented as evidence that UAP possess the technical capability to interact directly with nuclear command-and-control electronics.

## Contexts Within the Source

Elizondo uses this incident to argue that UAP are not passive or indifferent to human technological development — that they specifically seek out and interact with nuclear weapons infrastructure. He describes a pattern: UAP appearing in the vicinity of nuclear facilities, demonstrating the ability to disable or activate weapons systems, and departing without other engagement.
