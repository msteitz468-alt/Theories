## Claim Statement

Elizondo states that Dr. Garry Nolan — immunologist at Stanford University School of Medicine, using MRI brain imaging — found that 105 individuals with reported UAP encounters all shared an anatomical commonality: an enlarged and hyperconnected caudate-putamen region in the brain. Elizondo further states that remote viewers used in government programs (CIA Project Stargate) share the identical neurological feature, and that virtually every member of the AATIP team exhibits it as well.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 10, Elizondo describes Nolan's study of 105 UAP experiencers. All subjects were described as high-IQ. MRI imaging of all 105 showed enlarged caudate-putamen — a region involved in pattern recognition, intuition, and rapid information integration. Nolan reportedly told Elizondo that the subjects were not damaged by UAP exposure but that they appeared to have been selected because of pre-existing neurological characteristics.

In Chapter 14, Elizondo extends this finding: the same brain structure is present in remote viewers who participated in government programs (Project Stargate), and the caudate-putamen enlargement is correlated with a specific genetic profile. Elizondo states that virtually all AATIP team members share Cherokee DNA, and that this genetic marker appears to be associated with the caudate-putamen feature.

Elizondo further states that key Senate staffers involved in UAP legislation — those who took the topic seriously and moved it forward — also share this genetic marker.

## Contexts Within the Source

Elizondo frames the Nolan finding as potentially explaining UAP interaction selection — suggesting that NHI may preferentially interact with humans who have this neurological profile. He stops short of claiming causation but presents the correlation as sufficiently consistent across 105+ individuals to warrant serious investigation.
