## Claim Statement

Elizondo cites the Wilson/Davis memo as contemporaneous documentation that a Legacy Program contractor, in a 2002 meeting with astrophysicist Eric Davis, acknowledged the existence of a classified crash-retrieval and reverse-engineering Special Access Program — one inaccessible even to Admiral Thomas Wilson, then-Deputy Director of the Defense Intelligence Agency, who had attempted to gain access.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 4, Elizondo describes the Wilson/Davis memo as evidence that the Legacy Program exists and is operating within contractor-level Special Access Programs. The memo documents a meeting in which Eric Davis interviewed Admiral Thomas Wilson about Wilson's attempt to access a UAP-related SAP. Wilson told Davis that he had found the program in DoD records, requested access, and been denied by the program contractors themselves — who told a sitting Deputy Director of the DIA that he did not have need-to-know.

The memo was entered into the Congressional Record in May 2022 during the public UAP hearing. The appendix provides a direct URL to the Congressional Record entry.

Eric Davis is quoted in the appendix: "We couldn't make it ourselves. Off-world vehicles not made on this earth."

## Contexts Within the Source

Elizondo uses the Wilson/Davis memo as institutional corroboration for his Legacy Program claims: it is not merely his assertion but a documented account from a senior US military officer (Wilson) and a credentialed physicist (Davis) that a contractor-held UAP program existed and was actively shielding itself from government oversight.
