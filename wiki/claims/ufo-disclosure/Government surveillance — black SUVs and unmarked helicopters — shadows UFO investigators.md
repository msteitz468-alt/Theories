## Claim Statement

Unmarked **black helicopters** and tinted-window **black SUVs** (and government-plated cars) recur throughout the narrative as a sign that a covert government interest is monitoring animal-mutilation and UFO sites — and shadowing the investigators who probe them.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Mezrich presents the motif at two levels. First, as a feature of the mutilation phenomenon itself: "It was not uncommon for ranchers and other witnesses to come forward after a police report, telling stories of seeing black, unmarked helicopters near mutilation sites." The famous Gomez-ranch case near Dulce, New Mexico (≈50 cattle lost 1975–1983) turned up "evidence of some sort of aircraft landing in the vicinity, traces of radiation ... and reports of unknown craft as well as unmarked black helicopters in the area."

Second, as something the investigators experience directly. The book notes "black helicopters had become such a cultural meme connected with all types of paranormal phenomena," yet repeatedly invokes them — an Agilent lab tech "mentioned 'black helicopters' more than once," and on a streambed mutilation mission Zukowski's assistant Joe Fex jokes about "one of those black helicopters we keep hearing about." For Chuck, "it wasn't the black helicopters that bothered Chuck, it was the SUVs": driving from Denver he spots "a black SUV" trailing one to two hundred yards back. In the closing chapters, rancher Glenda watches "an unmarked dark helicopter circle the exact spot" where her animals died.

## Contexts Within the Source

Mezrich uses the motif to convey a pervasive sense of covert official surveillance — "unmarked helicopters" treated as "a sign of military interest, if not involvement" — that drives Zukowski to deliberately go public, reasoning "the more people who knew who he was ... the less dangerous his work became," and to conclude "it was important to be paranoid as well."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The book itself flags the trope's unreliability: Chuck "didn't like heading down that route without some sort of evidence beyond a 'whooshing' sound," and acknowledges that staying "up late reading about UFOs and military cover-ups, you started to see things behind every rock and tree." The Denver-highway sighting is hedged — "he couldn't be sure ... it could have been a black SUV."
