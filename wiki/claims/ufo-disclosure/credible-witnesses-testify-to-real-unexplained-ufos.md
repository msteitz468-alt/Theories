## Claim Statement

Kean argues that the reality of an unexplained physical UFO phenomenon is established not by anonymous reports but by the firsthand, on-the-record testimony of unusually credible witnesses — generals, fighter and airline pilots, air-traffic controllers, and senior officials — whose accounts withstand scrutiny.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Her flagship case is the **1989–90 Belgian wave**: Major General Wilfried De Brouwer reports that on 29 November 1989 some 143 sightings (≥250 witnesses, including federal police) described a silent triangular craft with beaming spotlights; the Air Force scrambled F-16s, and over two years "all mundane, conventional explanations were ruled out." A FOIA-released U.S. memo ("Belgium and the UFO Issue," 1990) confirmed "no USAF stealth aircraft were operating in the Ardennes area," and Belgium's defense minister stated "the nature and origin of the phenomenon remain unknown." The book adds equally credentialed witnesses to cases such as JAL Flight 1628 (1986), Tehran (1976), and the Phoenix Lights (Governor Fife Symington).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Kean and her witnesses are pointedly restrained: De Brouwer avoids "extrapolations to possible extraterrestrial activities" while urging research that "should not exclude the extraterrestrial option," and Kean concedes most reports are explicable.
