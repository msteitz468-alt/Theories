## Claim Statement

Chuck Zukowski investigates two Southwestern energy anomalies — the **Taos Hum** (a persistent low-frequency rumble only some people can hear) and the Sedona **energy "vortices"** — and on a desert road near Sedona his brand-new jeep suffers a total, temporary electrical power-loss of the kind he associates with close UFO encounters.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Taos Hum:** "a persistent, low-frequency rumble that only certain people could hear," reportedly audible to "around 2 percent of people who visited Taos." Mezrich notes that "by 1993, residents of New Mexico had joined together to petition the U.S. Congress to investigate," with theories tying it "to nearby underground military installations or to the nuclear research going on in nearby Los Alamos"; a commissioned university study "conclude[d] that the phenomenon was not detectable by any scientific means." Chuck favored "an explanation involving an underground tunneling device" linking secret bases (Area 51, Cheyenne Mountain, White Sands, Los Alamos).

**Sedona vortices:** "Descendants of the Apaches ... had first spoken of an energy vortex," described as resembling "electrically charged tornadoes that spiraled up and down with spiritual energy," used by Native Americans "to communicate with spiritual beings, and to commune with their ancestors in the stars." Chuck "filled three pages with notes," feeling "certain he was documenting something real," whether linked to UFOs "or whether it was just some sort of natural phenomenon."

**Vehicle power-loss:** On a desert road, "without warning, the jeep's headlights blinked off ... The vehicle had somehow just died. No engine, no headlights, no taillights, nothing on the dash." The jeep rolled in silence; once Chuck shut the door after considering stepping out, "the headlights blinked on ... The engine growled to life." Chuck judged the episode "something much closer to what happened near a UFO sighting" than to the vortex accounts — the jeep was "brand new ... no smell of electrical burning or any signs of engine damage."

## Contexts Within the Source

These investigations exemplify Zukowski's method (most cases ending in "a lot of notes in a notebook ... a lot of hearsay") while introducing the vehicle-power-loss signature that recurs at the book's climax in the Ute Mountains.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The Taos study found the Hum "not detectable by any scientific means," and no family member could hear it. Chuck "had no evidence" the jeep's failure "was due to an extraordinary cause"; his skeptical wife Tammy's "mind was analyzing the moment and edging back toward a rational interpretation."
