## Claim Statement

Elizondo asserts that in 1982, a UAP hovering over a Soviet ICBM launch site in Ukraine caused the unauthorized activation of ten ICBMs — meaning the missiles were moved to a launch-ready state without human command. Unlike the Malmstrom 1967 incident (where ICBMs were shut down), this event represents UAP causing missiles to come online.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 4, Elizondo lists three major UAP-nuclear interference incidents reviewed by AATIP. The Ukraine 1982 event is distinguished from Malmstrom by its polarity: rather than disabling the weapons, the UAP activated them — a far more alarming demonstration of capability. Elizondo states that ten Soviet ICBMs were brought to a launch-ready state by the UAP interaction.

Elizondo uses all three nuclear interference incidents (Malmstrom 1967, Rendlesham 1980, Ukraine 1982) to argue that UAP are specifically drawn to nuclear weapons sites and are capable of interfering with nuclear command-and-control systems in both directions — disabling and activating.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim, if accurate, would represent the most dangerous documented UAP-nuclear interaction — unauthorized activation of strategic nuclear weapons. Elizondo presents it as classified intelligence sourced through AATIP's inter-agency access. He notes that UAP monitoring nuclear sites is consistent with Puthoff's later theory (Chapter 16) that UAP are monitoring humanity's approach to a dangerous energy threshold.
