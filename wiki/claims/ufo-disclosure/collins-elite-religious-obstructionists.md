## Claim Statement

Elizondo asserts that a group he identifies as the Collins Elite — composed of religious individuals within the US government and intelligence community — actively worked to obstruct UAP research and disclosure on theological grounds, believing that UAP represent demonic entities and that acknowledging them publicly would cause religious and social catastrophe.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 4, Elizondo introduces the Collins Elite as an internal obstacle to AATIP's work. In Chapter 8, he elaborates: Devon Woods (described as a DIA employee and former CIA officer) explicitly told Elizondo that UAP are "demonic" — reflecting the Collins Elite perspective. Elizondo describes the Collins Elite as real but deliberately elusive, operating through informal networks rather than formal organizational structure.

The group's opposition was theological rather than scientific — they did not dispute the reality of UAP but interpreted NHI through a religious framework that made engagement or disclosure anathema.

## Contexts Within the Source

Elizondo frames the Collins Elite as one of three competing internal factions regarding UAP: (1) those who believe UAP are a national security threat requiring military response, (2) those who believe UAP are benign and should be engaged diplomatically/scientifically, and (3) the Collins Elite, who believe UAP are demonic and must be suppressed. He credits Collins Elite influence with some of the institutional resistance AATIP encountered in getting briefings accepted and programs funded.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Elizondo acknowledges that identifying the Collins Elite precisely is difficult — they are described as a loosely affiliated ideological network, not a formal program with membership rolls.
