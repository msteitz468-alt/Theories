## Claim Statement

Elizondo asserts that a sample of molten metal recovered in Council Bluffs, Iowa, in 1977 and subsequently analyzed by Dr. Garry Nolan (Stanford) and Dr. Jacques Vallée showed isotope ratios of magnesium and iron that cannot occur through natural geological or industrial processes. The material also exhibited a fractal internal matrix and anomalous antenna-like electromagnetic properties suggesting it was engineered for a specific electromagnetic function.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 14, Elizondo describes the Nolan/Vallée analysis of the Council Bluffs 1977 sample:

- **Isotopic ratios**: The magnesium and iron isotope ratios in the sample deviate from naturally occurring ratios in ways that can only result from deliberate isotope enrichment — a process far beyond any industrial capability known to exist in 1977
- **Fractal matrix structure**: The internal structure of the material is fractal at multiple scales — not consistent with any known manufacturing technique
- **Antenna properties**: The material exhibits anomalous electromagnetic behavior consistent with a tuned antenna, suggesting it was designed to interact with specific electromagnetic frequencies

Elizondo connects this material to Puthoff's warp bubble theory (Chapter 16), where bismuth-magnesium layered meta-material is identified as a candidate structural component of a warp bubble generator.

## Contexts Within the Source

This physical evidence claim is one of the few in the book where Elizondo points to material that can, in principle, be independently tested. Nolan's isotopic analysis is presented as peer-reviewable science, not merely testimony. Elizondo does not specify how the Council Bluffs material entered AATIP's possession or what event in 1977 produced the molten metal sample.
