## Claim Statement

Deace and Erzen assert that the gravest lesson of COVID-19 is the danger of an **unaccountable, unelected administrative state**: vesting "too much power in the hands of one man who is unaccountable to the will of the people" — exemplified by [[Anthony Fauci]] — is "anathema to liberty." Fauci is "not the disease but the symptom"; the system "requires multitudes of figureheads such as a Fauci," and "if Fauci weren't there... it would've just been someone else."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Introduction & Conclusion**: The book is framed by Benjamin Franklin's "A republic, if you can keep it." It cites the Book of Esther (Haman's bureaucratic plot), Tolkien, and Dwight Eisenhower's 1961 farewell warning that "public policy could itself become the captive of a scientific-technological elite." The authors argue the Founders built "checks and balances" and "layers of liberty insurance," not "an administrative state of perpetual unelected bureaucracy," and that "all it takes for 'checks and balances' to... devolve into an all-powerful and self-perpetuating administrative state, is for self-governing men and women to do nothing."

They stress "the burden of proof is always on the bureaucrats. They are to serve us, not us serve them," and that even Sweden's comparatively successful response (under [[Anders Tegnell]]) does not vindicate concentrating power in one unelected official, "any more than you fight a revolution to replace a King George III with a King George Washington."

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the book's organizing thesis, named in its title and bookending the text. It motivates the repeated call for a "9/11-style tribunal" and the chapter 10 prescription that "we the people," not "them the experts," must govern.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The authors explicitly **do not** advocate "no government whatsoever," acknowledging "vital roles a civil society needs" (military, law enforcement, border security) and granting that "one day an unprecedented contagion could... [require] drastic government action." They also concede the initial lockdown was "hardly... an unforgivable sin" given unreliable early data.
