## Claim Statement

Deace and Erzen assert that the demand to "trust the experts" was hollow because **highly credentialed experts disagreed with [[Anthony Fauci]]** — so the public deserved to see those dissenting views debated rather than suppressed. "Experts have expertise you and I don't have, but they're not necessarily wiser, nor are they any less sinful."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 3** cites: **David Katz** (Yale) warning the response could be "graver than the direct toll of the virus itself"; **John Ioannidis** (Stanford) on "the harms of exaggerated information"; **Sunetra Gupta** (Oxford) contradicting the Imperial College model; **Wesley Pegden** (Carnegie Mellon) that "the duration of containment does not matter"; and **Scott Atlas** (Stanford). It quotes [[The Great Barrington Declaration]] (44 experts calling for "Focused Protection") in full, and notes Fauci himself opposed "draconian" quarantines during the far more lethal 2014 Ebola outbreak.

The authors stress they are "not qualified to determine" who is correct, "however, we do believe the American people are constitutionally qualified to benefit from such a debate," and fault the media for ignoring or humiliating "anyone who dares defy Fauci."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter underwrites the book's central distinction between deferring to *a* bureaucrat and "seeking wisdom in a multitude of counsel," and supports the case against lockdowns and masks.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The authors explicitly caution against the symmetrical error: "Just because the narrative of those who signed 'The Great Barrington Declaration' is calling for a more preferable way of life than lockdowns doesn't mean they're right." They repeatedly disclaim their own expertise ("we're not doctors or scientists").
