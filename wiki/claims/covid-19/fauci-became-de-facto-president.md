## Claim Statement

Deace and Erzen assert that when "15 days to flatten the curve" became "30 days to slow the spread," President Trump "effectively handed over the reins of his presidency to [[Anthony Fauci]] indefinitely," making Fauci "the most powerful bureaucrat in American history" — "in the minds of much of America, Fauci was now president." They further contend that "the main reason Trump was not reelected is he never made the decision he should've made — firing Fauci."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 4** opens with Trump's strong pre-COVID economy (stock-market, wage, and unemployment gains) and argues that without COVID "it likely wouldn't have been" a one-term presidency. It uses the **Lincoln/McClellan** analogy: Lincoln eventually fired the slow general McClellan and won with Grant, whereas "Trump is now out of office while Fauci remains." The chapter notes Fauci is "the highest-paid employee in the entire U.S. federal government," and quotes Ben Shapiro ("Fauci should be fired") and Fauci's own late claim that the "U.S. response to coronavirus is among the worst in the world" — "the very response he was... in charge of."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter develops the book's thesis that an unelected official wielded unaccountable power ([[Unaccountable, unelected bureaucrats like Fauci threaten the constitutional republic]]). It also addresses Bob Woodward's *Rage* (Trump "diminished the severity... to avoid panic"), arguing this was "really the trial of President Fauci, not President Trump," since Trump's "bad flu" framing came from Fauci's own *NEJM* article.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The authors assign Trump real blame ("this wasn't Trump's finest hour by a long shot"; he was "more accomplice than point man"), and concede the initial lockdown decision was forgivable. Their charge is specifically that he deferred to Fauci too long.
