## Claim Statement

Deace and Erzen assert that [[Anthony Fauci]] "felt strongly about every side of every coronavirus issue," shifting positions so often that "if you don't like a current take Fauci has on the virus... stick around because it's likely to change."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 1** assembles a cited chronicle:
- **January 2020**: "It's a very low risk to the United States. It isn't something the American people need to be frightened about."
- **Asymptomatic spread**: "in all the history of respiratory-borne viruses... asymptomatic transmission has never been the driver of outbreaks" — later reversed to claim it drove a "new phase."
- **Feb 28, 2020 (*NEJM*)**: COVID's consequences "may ultimately be more akin to those of a severe seasonal influenza."
- **March 8, 2020**: "There's no reason to be walking around with a mask" — later reversed to recommend double-masking.
- **Models**: relied on the Imperial College and IHME models, then told Fox News he had "always been skeptical of them."
- **Herd immunity**: per Marco Rubio, Fauci "selectively lied," shifting his stated threshold from 60–70% to 75–90% because he felt "the country wasn't ready to hear it."

The book also notes Fauci admitted (to InStyle) he lied about masks "for the greater good."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter establishes the pattern the rest of the book builds on, asking "At what point here was Dr. Fauci right about anything? When was he wrong?" It frames the contradictions as either deliberate ("gloms onto whichever narrative will expand... his authority") or incompetent — "both possible explanations are bad."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The authors concede there were times "Fauci has said things the science agrees with," and acknowledge that early caution was defensible "given how unreliable the native data from China likely was" amid "an ongoing media onslaught demanding action."
