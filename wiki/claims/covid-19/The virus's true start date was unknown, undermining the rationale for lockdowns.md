## Claim Statement

Deace and Erzen assert that because the United States never established a true "patient zero" or arrival date for the virus, the premise of "flattening the curve" was incoherent: "How can we measure the rate of increase to something that lacks a starting point?" If the virus was circulating widely before the panic, "lockdowns were never going to work."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapter 2**: The earliest U.S. case the book says was acknowledged is November 17, 2019, in China, with China not notifying the WHO for nearly two months. It cites ~370,000 Chinese students in the U.S. who could have carried the virus home over the 2019 holidays; Patricia Dowd's February 6, 2020 death (later identified as an early U.S. COVID death); and a USC antibody study suggesting Los Angeles County had "28–55 times more" infections than recorded by April 2020. It also notes Italian antibody studies indicating infections "going all the way back to September 2019," and that late-January/early-March 2020 flu-and-pneumonia death rates were near the CDC epidemic threshold, possibly masking early COVID.

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the second of the book's two "great unanswered questions" requiring a "9/11-style tribunal" (the first being Fauci's unexplained reversal). The authors argue "knowing the accurate arrival of the coronavirus is a vital component to accurately judge the efficacy" of mitigation policy.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The authors frame this as an open empirical question — "There's no way of knowing, but these are things from a public policy perspective we must know" — rather than a settled conclusion, and acknowledge it "maybe wasn't true worldwide" that lockdowns came too late.
