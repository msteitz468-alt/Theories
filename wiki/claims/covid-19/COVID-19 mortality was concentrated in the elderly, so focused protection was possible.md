## Claim Statement

Deace and Erzen assert that COVID-19 mortality was "concentrated in the elderly… by an order of magnitude," so the country could have protected the vulnerable while keeping society open — making the lockdown of low-risk people (workers and schoolchildren) unjustified.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Chapters 2 & 6**: Cited figures include the Kaiser Family Foundation ("8 in 10 People Who Have Died of COVID-19 Were Age 65 or Older"), the CDC's median age of COVID death (~78, "the average American life span") versus the median worker age (~42), and an August 2020 breakdown that "54 and younger are 70% of the U.S. population but make up just 8% of all COVID deaths," with deaths "not even register[ing]" for school-age children. The Conclusion cites that 95% of U.S. COVID deaths were age 50+.

The book argues this known age skew should have produced **Focused Protection** (see [[The Great Barrington Declaration]]) rather than mass lockdowns, and notes that despite locking down the healthy, ~45% of U.S. COVID deaths still came from nursing homes and long-term-care facilities — "frankly indefensible."

## Contexts Within the Source

The claim is the empirical backbone of the lockdown critique ([[Lockdowns were "the dumbest management decision in American history"]]) and the focused-protection alternative. It parallels [[COVID-19 severity was overstated and driven by comorbidities and metabolic ill-health]] from another source in this wiki.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The authors are careful to add that the elderly who died "are [not] a triviality" — "each and every one of those lives were fearfully and wonderfully made" — and that focused protection "doesn't mean isolating vulnerable demographics should be abandoned, and you just let the virus run roughshod."
