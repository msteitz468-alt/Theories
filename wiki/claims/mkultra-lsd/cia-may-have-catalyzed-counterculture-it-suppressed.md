## Claim Statement

The book's central, carefully hedged irony: the CIA — which first imported LSD to the U.S., funded its early research, ordered it in mass quantity, synthesized it domestically (Eli Lilly), and tested it on thousands — may have helped "let loose the whole LSD Fad on the U.S. & the World" (Ginsberg's phrase), unleashing the very counterculture it then sought to suppress. "The metaphysical window that LSD opened for so many of us may have unwittingly been opened by those whose interests lay in keeping it shut."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Lee and Shlain marshal the convergences: CIA-linked figures (Hubbard, the funders, Hollingshead's supply, the Sandoz/Lilly stockpiles) seeded the drug; Henry and Clare Boothe Luce took LSD and gave it favorable press; CIA personnel themselves became enchanted with acid as a visionary experience even while studying it as a weapon. The authors are careful not to assert deliberate design — they present it as an unresolved question ("by conscious plan or inadvertent Pandora's Box"), and Leary's own remark ("It was all planned and scripted by the Central Intelligence") is quoted as provocation, not proof.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

The authors explicitly leave the "conscious plan vs. inadvertent" question open and note the CIA's scientists were themselves confused about LSD; the introduction frames the book as useful to both believers in conspiracy and skeptics.
