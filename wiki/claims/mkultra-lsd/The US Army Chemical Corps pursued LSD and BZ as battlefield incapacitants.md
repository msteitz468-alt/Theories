## Claim Statement

Parallel to the CIA, the US Army Chemical Corps pursued hallucinogens as "madness gas" — a non-lethal "war without death." Major General William Creasy championed spraying LSD (code EA-1729) over cities or battlefields to incapacitate without killing. Some 1,500 military personnel were tested with LSD at Edgewood Arsenal, Fort Bragg, and elsewhere; the Army ultimately favored the more powerful, longer-acting **BZ** (EA-2277, ~2,800 soldiers exposed, 1959–1975) because it could be dispersed as an aerosol and rendered subjects "off the rocker" and "on the floor."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The authors quote Creasy's congressional testimony (LSD could have congressmen "dancing on the desks, or shouting Communist speeches") and document operational LSD interrogations (Operation DERBY HAT in the Far East, 1962) and the death of tennis pro Harold Blauer (1953) from an army MDA injection ("We didn't know if it was dog piss or what it was we were giving him"). BZ was reportedly weaponized in grenades and cluster bombs and "employed by American troops as a counterinsurgency weapon in Vietnam," with possible contingency plans for civilian insurrection.

## Contexts Within the Source

The military and CIA programs ran in parallel and shared contractors (e.g., Hoch). Like the CIA, the Army found LSD acted "primarily as a catalyst" that transformed what its strategists thought possible.
