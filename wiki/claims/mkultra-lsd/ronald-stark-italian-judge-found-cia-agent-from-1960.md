## Claim Statement

Lee and Shlain recount the saga of **Ronald Hadley Stark**, the most prolific underground LSD chemist of the era — who supplied the Brotherhood of Eternal Love, produced perhaps twenty kilos (some fifty million doses) of acid between 1969 and 1972, and moved among Italian terrorists, Mafiosi, Arab and Libyan contacts, and US embassy personnel — and report that in **April 1979 an Italian judge, Giorgio Floridia of Bologna, ordered Stark released on the explicit finding that he had been a CIA agent**, declaring that "from 1960 onwards Stark belonged to the American secret services." This is the book's sharpest documented point at which the covert and grassroots histories of LSD converge.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The authors (Ch. 10, "The Great LSD Conspiracy") describe Stark's modus operandi as "not unlike that of an intelligence operative" — compartmentalized, multilingual (he "spoke ten languages fluently"), and a "genius con artist." He communicated "on a regular basis with American embassy personnel" (letters from foreign service officer Charles C. Adams to his Brussels drug lab were found among his belongings) and "to some of his friends he even boasted of working for the CIA," claiming a CIA tip prompted him to shut his French lab in 1971.

After his 1975 arrest in Bologna under a false British passport, Stark cultivated Italy's most notorious terrorists — befriending Red Brigades leader Renato Curcio in prison and accurately predicting the murder of Judge Francesco Coco — and met with Italian secret-service personnel including General Vito Miceli, "who received $800,000 from the CIA." Sentenced to fourteen years for drug trafficking, Stark was nonetheless freed in April 1979 by Judge Floridia, whose decision Lee and Shlain call "extraordinary": he released Stark because of "an impressive series of scrupulously enumerated proofs" that Stark "was actually a CIA agent," stating that "many circumstances suggest that from 1960 onwards Stark belonged to the American secret services."

## Contexts Within the Source

Stark's story closes Part Two and is positioned as the climactic illustration of the book's thesis that the covert (CIA) and grassroots histories of LSD intertwine. The afterword returns to it, drawing a parallel to the Noriega case: "U.S. intelligence officials will dutifully ignore evidence of dope smuggling when they deem it expedient." Connects to [[The CIA may have inadvertently catalyzed the counterculture it later suppressed]] and [[LSD has a covert CIA history that converges with the 1960s counterculture]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Lee and Shlain explicitly refuse to adjudicate the case: "The facts about Ronald Stark raise more questions than they answer. Was he a CIA operative throughout his drug dealing days? Or was the espionage link merely the work of a brilliant con artist who played both ends off the middle?" They note an Italian parliamentary commission concluded only that Stark "was an adventurer who was used by the CIA," that "proof as to exactly when his espionage exploits began is hard to pin down," and quote Brotherhood chemist Tim Scully's two-sided verdict: he "might have tricked the CIA, just like he fooled everyone else." Their summing-up: "How does one distinguish between an international confidence trickster and a deep-cover spy when both professions are based on pretense and deception?"
