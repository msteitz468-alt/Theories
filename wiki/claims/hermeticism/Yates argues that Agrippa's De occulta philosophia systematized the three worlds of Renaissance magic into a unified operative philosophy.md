## Claim Statement

Yates argues that Cornelius Agrippa's De occulta philosophia (completed c. 1510, published 1533) provided the first comprehensive survey of the whole field of Renaissance magic by organizing it according to three corresponding worlds and three corresponding types of magical practice. In doing so, Agrippa carried the Ficino-Pico synthesis to its logical extreme: a Magus who mounts through all three worlds — elemental, celestial, intellectual — to become co-operator with the divine Creator, doing "marvellous works" that include both demonic-angelic magic and, within the same framework, genuine mathematical and mechanical operations.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates summarizes Agrippa's opening framework: "The universe is to be divided into three worlds, the elemental world, the celestial world, the intellectual world. Each world receives influences from the one above it, so that the virtue of the Creator descends through the angels in the intellectual world, to the stars in the celestial world, and thence to the elements and to all things composed of them in the elemental world." Magicians work this chain in reverse, drawing virtues downward through the upper worlds (Ch. VII).

Agrippa's three books correspond: natural magic (elemental), celestial/mathematical magic (celestial), ceremonial/religious magic (intellectual). In Book II he emphasizes that a magician must know mathematics, for by mathematics one can produce "without any natural virtue" — by purely mechanical means — wonderful operations such as the flying pigeon of Architas, the moving statues of Daedalus, and the speaking statues of Mercurius (the Asclepius animated statues recast as marvels of applied mechanics).

At the summit of Book III, Agrippa describes a Magus who "has cohabited with the elements, vanquished nature, mounted higher than the heavens, elevating himself above the angels to the archetype itself, with whom he then becomes co-operator and can do all things" — language Yates contrasts sharply with Ficino's caution and Pico's piety.

Yates quotes Agrippa's Book III to show that in the intellectual world the Magus performs religious magic: ceremonies, Cabalist angel-summoning, religious miracles — so that "what we are arriving at here is something which is really very like the ideal Egyptian, or pseudo-Egyptian, society as presented in the Hermetic Asclepius, a theocracy governed by priests who know the secrets of a magical religion."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates notes that Cardanus, a "really deep magician," despised the De occulta philosophia as "a trivial affair," and she herself admits it is not a profound philosophical work and does not fully give technical procedures. She also warns that Agrippa is "an irresponsible magician," and that reading his third book one feels the magics Ficino and Pico had placed in lofty philosophical contexts "slipping back towards the old necromancy and conjuring."
