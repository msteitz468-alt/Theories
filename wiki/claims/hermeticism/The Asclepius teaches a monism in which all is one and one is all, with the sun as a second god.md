## Claim Statement

The Asclepius frames a **monist cosmology**: "all are one and one all," all things were in the creator
before creation, the whole consists of the four elements with "one matter, one soul and one god"; and the
visible **sun is "a second god,"** governor of the sensible world.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Did I not say that all are one and one all inasmuch as all were in the creator before he created them
all?" (Asclepius 2); "The elements by which the whole of matter has been formed… are four: fire, water,
earth, air. One matter, one soul and one god" (Asclepius 3); "The sun is indeed a second god, Asclepius,
believe it, governing all things and shedding light on all that are in the world" (Asclepius 29).

## Cross-source connections

The "all is one" formula is the ancestor of the monism the wiki meets in the Kybalion ("THE ALL") and in
Blavatsky's [[The Secret Doctrine as the one wisdom-religion underlying all faiths (Blavatsky)|wisdom-religion]];
the sun-as-second-god motif feeds later solar theology and the Hermetic strand in Renaissance science.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The Asclepius is not consistently monist — it also speaks dualistically of matter, demons, and the
descent into evil; Copenhaver's introduction places it among the "mixed" treatises.
