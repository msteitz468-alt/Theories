## Claim Statement

The Kybalion teaches that deliberate secrecy and selective transmission of wisdom has always been a
defining characteristic of Hermetic teaching. The doctrine is not to be broadcast publicly but
given "from Master to Student; from Initiate to Hierophant; from lip to ear," and only to those
who are prepared. This principle is encoded in the book's opening maxim and justified by historical
examples of traditions that were lost when they became public creeds.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "The lips of wisdom are closed, except to the ears of Understanding."
> — THE KYBALION (opening maxim, Ch. I)

> "Where fall the footsteps of the Master, the ears of those ready for his Teaching open wide."
> — THE KYBALION

> "When the ears of the student are ready to hear, then cometh the lips to fill them with Wisdom."
> — THE KYBALION

The Three Initiates explain: "Even to this day, we use the term 'hermetic' in the sense of 'secret';
'sealed so that nothing can escape'…by reason of the fact that the followers of Hermes always
observed the principle of secrecy in their teachings. They did not believe in 'casting pearls before
swine,' but rather held to the teaching 'milk for babes; meat for strong men.'"

The Hermetic Teachings were never identified with any country or religious sect — this was
deliberate, "because of the warning of the ancient teachers against allowing the Secret Doctrine to
become crystallized into a creed." Historical counter-examples: the occultism of India and Persia
"degenerated, and was largely lost, owing to the fact that the teachers became priests, and so
mixed theology with the philosophy." The same fate befell the Gnostics and Early Christians "at the
time of Constantine."

When the teachings were written down, "its meaning was veiled in terms of alchemy and astrology,
so that only those possessing the key could read it aright" — necessary to avoid "the persecutions
of the theologians of the Middle Ages, who fought the Secret Doctrine with fire and sword; stake,
gibbet and cross."

## Contexts Within the Source

This principle is the stated transmission-rationale of the book itself. The Kybalion is framed as a
partial disclosure: the Three Initiates offer maxims with explanations to those "ready" to receive
them, while asserting that the full tradition continues in an unbroken oral lineage. The principle
intersects with [[The Principle of Mentalism (Kybalion)]] through the Hermetic Principle of Cause
and Effect / Law of Attraction: "the passage of this book to those ready for the instruction will
attract the attention of such as are prepared to receive the Teaching."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The book itself is an act of partial disclosure, which the Three Initiates treat as consistent with
the principle: they are providing the foundational maxims and explanations while acknowledging the
deeper teaching continues elsewhere. The secrecy principle is thus a regulator of depth and
audience, not an absolute prohibition on writing.
