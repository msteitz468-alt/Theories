## Claim Statement

Yates argues that the foundational error driving the entire Renaissance Hermetic revival was a radical misdating: Renaissance scholars, following the Church Fathers Lactantius and Augustine, believed [[Hermes Trismegistus]] to be a real Egyptian priest of extreme antiquity — older than Plato, older than Pythagoras, roughly contemporaneous with or slightly later than Moses — who had personally written [[The Corpus Hermeticum]] and the Asclepius. This error gave those texts an authority they could never have commanded if correctly dated to the second and third centuries A.D. The resulting reverence for Hermes as the fountainhead of all philosophy — the first *priscus theologus*, ahead of Orpheus, Pythagoras, and Plato — constituted what Ficino called the *prisca theologia*: a single ancient theology flowing from primordial Egypt to culminate in Plato and (so Ficino argued) in Christianity.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates opens Chapter I with the statement that "the return to a pure golden age of magic was based on a radical error in dating. The works which inspired the Renaissance Magus, and which he believed to be of profound antiquity, were really written in the second to the third centuries A.D." She characterises this as "a huge historical error" that "was to have amazing results."

The guarantee for the error, Yates shows, came from authoritative Church Fathers:

- **Lactantius** (*Div. Inst.* I.vi) described Hermes as "of great antiquity, and most fully imbued with every kind of learning" and placed him "much more ancient than Plato and Pythagoras" (*De ira Dei*). He cited Hermetic passages as Gentile prophecies of Christ, bracketing Hermes with the Sibyls.
- **Augustine** (*De Civitate Dei* XVIII.xxix) gave Hermes a genealogy three generations removed from a contemporary of Moses, confirming extreme antiquity while condemning the magic in the Asclepius. This created a dilemma Renaissance Hermetists had to navigate: how to revere Hermes while avoiding Augustine's condemnation of his "god-making."

Yates also notes that what made the Hermetica so convincing as primordial Egyptian wisdom was their content: passages closely resembling Genesis, language about God the Father and a "Son of God," and descriptions of ecstatic soul-ascent through the planetary spheres. These features confirmed for Renaissance readers what the Fathers had told them — that Hermes was an Egyptian Moses, perhaps even earlier than Moses. Ficino in his *Theologia Platonica* openly wondered whether Hermes and Moses were the same person.

The *prisca theologia* chain — Hermes → Orpheus → Aglaophemus → Pythagoras → Plato — was first articulated by Ficino in the *argumentum* before his *Pimander* (1463) and repeated throughout his subsequent writings. It established Hermes as *fons et origo* of a wisdom that reached Plato, meaning Platonism itself was derivative of and confirmatory to Hermetic revelation.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates is clear that the error was genuine and not the result of bad faith: the Church Fathers had established the legend and it would have been unthinkable for Renaissance scholars to doubt them. She notes that modern scholarship (Festugière, Nock) dates the Corpus Hermeticum to A.D. 100–300, composed by various unknown Greek authors with Platonic, Stoic, Jewish, and possibly Persian elements. The "genuinely Egyptian elements" in the Hermetica are, she notes, disputed among modern scholars, though she allows for some Egyptian influence.

Yates also notes that Hermes was not the only misdated ancient authority: the Chaldean Oracles (attributed to Zoroaster) and the Orphic Hymns similarly dated from the second or third century A.D., not from remote antiquity.
