## Claim Statement

Yates argues that after Casaubon's redating (1614) and the decisive intellectual defeats sustained in the Fludd controversies of the 1620s–1630s, Renaissance Hermetism did not simply cease to exist but rather went underground — transforming from "an outlook associated with dominant philosophies into a preoccupation of secret societies and minority groups." The Rosicrucians, early Freemasonry, and related esoteric channels became the vehicles through which the Hermetic-Cabalist tradition was carried forward, no longer as a basis for mainstream philosophy or science but as an underground current persisting into the eighteenth century and beyond. Yates traces the thread tentatively from Bruno's reform mission in Germany through the Rosicrucian manifestoes, Fludd, the Rosicrucians' visit to Paris (1623), and Elias Ashmole's Freemasonry (1646), to Mozart's *Magic Flute*.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates traces the going-underground of Hermetism through several interlocking threads:

**The Rosicrucians** emerge in Germany in 1614 — the same year as Casaubon's exposure. Their manifestoes (*Fama Fraternitatis*, 1614; *Confessio Fraternitatis*, 1615; *Chymische Hochzeit*, 1616) represent, Yates argues, "the Hermetic-Cabalist tradition of the Renaissance in some form, and strongly associated with religious ideas." Mersenne described them as "magicians and charlatans who boast of understanding Trismegistus and all the ancient Cabalists." Yates raises the possibility of Brunian influence — noting Bruno's preaching in Germany and the sequence of events linking his mission, Boccalini, and the Fama — while acknowledging it cannot be proved. She is more confident of the Campanellan link: Tobias Adami's journey to Tübingen (c.1611–13) with Campanella's manuscripts placed them with the circles from which the Rosicrucian manifestoes emerged.

**[[Robert Fludd (1574–1637)]]** as Rosicrucian defender: Fludd's self-identification with the Rosicrucians, Yates argues, placed him within this movement of Hermetism going underground. "His late revival of the outlook of the Renaissance Magus comes at a time when the Renaissance Magus — driven by the dominant thought of the seventeenth century from the high position in which Ficino and Pico had placed him — is going underground and turning into something like a Rosicrucian."

**The Rosicrucians in Paris (1623)**: In 1623, a detachment of Rosicrucians visited Paris and posted placards announcing themselves as "Invisible Ones" in possession of deep secrets. Their visit coincided with the height of Mersenne's intellectual campaign against everything they represented. Yates notes the historical irony: at the moment when Hermetism was in retreat before Mersenne and Casaubon's discovery, Campanella was at the French court prophesying that the infant Louis XIV would build the Egyptian City of the Sun.

**Freemasonry**: Yates treats the transition from Rosicrucianism to Freemasonry as murky but plausible. Elias Ashmole — made a Freemason at Warrington in 1646, a fervent royalist with strong interests in the history of chivalry — could plausibly represent a stream of influence reaching from Bruno's courtly Elizabethan Hermetism through the Rosicrucian movement to early speculative Freemasonry. Yates had earlier suggested in the book that Bruno's mission to Elizabethan England — his attachment of Hermetism to royalism and the cult of Elizabeth — might have something in common with Freemasonic attitudes. She does not claim this as proved but as "not impossible."

**Mozart's Magic Flute**: Yates concludes this speculation by noting that if the *Magic Flute* embodies Masonic beliefs, it might represent "a translation into poetic and musical imagery of the theme of the good religion of the Egyptians, of the mysteries of Isis and Osiris … of the magical atmosphere through which human souls make their way to a Hermetic-Egyptian salvation." The character Zarastro, she suggests, reflects the Renaissance equation of Zoroaster with Hermes Trismegistus.

The E. Garin quotation Yates uses to frame this entire movement describes Renaissance Hermetism in this period as descending "sul terreno dell'occultismo e delle confraternite ed associazioni variamente caratterizzate" — into occultism and variously characterized brotherhoods and associations.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates is careful to maintain uncertainty throughout this section. She explicitly describes the subject as "difficult and misty." The connection between the Rosicrucians and Bruno remains speculative; the connection with Freemasonry is "not impossible" rather than proven. She does not claim that Hermetism is the sole or even dominant source of Rosicrucianism or Freemasonry — only that it is "one of those esoteric channels" through which the tradition was carried.

On the Rosicrucians specifically: "Even whether they had an organisation or were actually formed into a sect" is uncertain. The evidence about their views is "tantalisingly vague." If Fludd is taken as characteristic of their views — which Yates says "is by no means certain" — their Magia and Cabala were more conservative and Lutheran than the Italian Hermetic tradition of Bruno and Campanella.
