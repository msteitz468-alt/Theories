## Claim Statement

Yates argues that Tommaso Campanella — Dominican friar, philosopher, and author of the *City of the Sun* (*Città del Sole*) — was the direct heir of [[Giordano Bruno]]'s Hermetic-magical programme and extended it deep into the seventeenth century. Campanella practised the Ficinian astral magic derived from [[Hermes Trismegistus]] throughout his life, actually performing it with Pope Urban VIII in Rome in 1628 to ward off a potentially fatal eclipse. His utopian *City of the Sun* was not, Yates contends, a progressive rational state: it was a Hermetic-Egyptian city-state ruled by a Magus-priest and kept in permanent health and virtue by celestial magic, modelled ultimately on the magical city of Adocentyn in *Picatrix* and on the prophecy of a restored Egyptian city in the *Asclepius*.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates describes how Campanella, working from the same Ficinian magical tradition as Bruno, developed in his *Metaphysica* (Paris, 1638) a full account of Ficino's astral magic and explicitly stated that "all this doctrine" derived from Hermes Trismegistus. He quoted the *Asclepius* on Egyptian statue-animation and referred to decan images. Campanella's own summary of Ficino's magic detailed "what odours, tastes, colours, temperature, air, water, wine, clothes, conversations, music, sky, and stars are to be used for breathing in the Spirit of the World."

The anti-eclipse magic with Urban VIII in 1628 is documented by Campanella himself in an appendix to his *Astrologica* (Lyons, 1629): a sealed room hung with white cloths, herbs burned, two lamps and five torches lit for the planets, the signs of the zodiac imitated, Jovial and Venereal music played, and astrologically distilled liquors drunk. Yates quotes Walker's interpretation: the magic "aimed at artificially creating favourable heavens as a substitute for the real heavens which are going wrong in the eclipse."

On the *City of the Sun*: "The City is arranged so as to be right with the stars, and thence flows all its happiness, health, and virtue. … the head priest, and his assistants, who governed the City of the Sun were Magi, who understood the world, knew how to 'draw down the life of heaven', to use Ficino's phrase, for the benefit of mankind." Yates identifies the closest parallel as the city of Adocentyn in *Picatrix*, built by Hermes Trismegistus with a lighthouse flashing planetary colours, magic images that kept inhabitants virtuous, and control of generation — all features mirrored in Campanella's City. The *Asclepius* prophesies an Egyptian city "towards the setting sun" into which "will hasten, by land and sea, the whole race of mortal men" — which Yates reads as the direct textual source of Campanella's vision.

Yates: "Campanella's City thus takes its place as one among the infinitely rich and varied products of Renaissance religious Hermetism. … owing to the pervasive Christianising of the Hermetic writings, Campanella still believes that their 'natural' religion and laws are close to Christianity, can easily be completed with the Christian sacraments, and, with Christ revered as a Magus, can form the new universal religion and ethic for which the world is waiting."

## Cross-source connections

[[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]] cites Campanella as Bruno's "spiritual heir" and makes the same argument about the Hermetic foundations of the *City of the Sun*. The FU book draws on Yates's analysis (and on Walker's work on Campanella's magic) to establish that the Hermetic tradition did not die with Bruno but continued as a living force, informing both political utopias and direct magical practice within the Catholic hierarchy well into the seventeenth century. This supports the broader FU thesis about the persistence of Hermeticism: see [[The Hermetic origins of modern science (Forbidden Universe thesis)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates is careful to note that Campanella's "success" with Urban VIII was "partial and fleeting"; strong opponents within the Dominican Order exposed his magic by arranging for the account in the *Astrologica* to be published without his knowledge, deliberately to discredit him. His credit in Rome sank rapidly after 1630 and he was forced to flee to Paris.

Yates also acknowledges that Campanella consciously distanced himself from Bruno in later life, naming Bruno only once (in a 1622 apology for Galileo) and adding the phrase "whom heresy forbids me to name." She interprets this as deliberate self-protection, not a real disavowal of intellectual kinship.

The waning of the tradition is given its due: Mersenne and Descartes dismissed Campanella as irrelevant to the new science. By the time Campanella died in 1639, the Casaubon redating of the Hermetica (1614) had destroyed the foundation of the tradition's authority, and Cartesian mechanism had rendered Renaissance animism obsolete.
