## Claim Statement

Yates argues that [[Robert Fludd (1574–1637)]] represents the most significant case of a thinker who completely ignored [[Isaac Casaubon (1559–1614)]]'s 1614 redating of the Hermetica and reconstructed the full Renaissance Hermetic-Cabalist worldview as though Casaubon had never existed. Writing in seventeenth-century England, Fludd treats [[Hermes Trismegistus]] as a priscus theologus of canonical authority equal to Scripture — citing Ficino's Latin *Pimander* on nearly every page — at the very moment when the tradition he represents was being demolished by Mersenne's rationalism and Kepler's mathematical science.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates draws a "total contrast" between Casaubon's 1614 work and Fludd's *Utriusque cosmi … historia* (Vol. I, 1617), both dedicated to James I within three years of each other: "Casaubon, using humanistic tools on Greek scholarship, had convincingly demolished the early dating of the Hermetica … Fludd, totally ignoring the new dating both in this work and in all his other voluminous writings, lives in a world in which Casaubon might never have been born."

She observes that "on nearly every page of Fludd's works there will be found a quotation from Ficino's Latin translation of the Corpus Hermeticum," often quoted from memory, comparing Hermes favourably to Moses and treating the *Pimander* as foreshadowing the Christian Trinity. Fludd adds Cabala to Ficinian Hermetism in the manner of Pico della Mirandola, mapping the Sephiroth onto Pseudo-Dionysian angelic hierarchies.

Yates locates Fludd as a "reactionary towards the origins, towards Ficino and Pico" compared with Bruno and Campanella, who had developed the Italian tradition further. Unlike Bruno, Fludd is not heliocentric and maintains a Trinitarian framework. His enthusiasm for the Hermetica as canonical books "knows no bounds"; Yates describes him as "giving expression, by a kind of delayed action, to sixteenth-century religious Hermetism of the most intense kind."

Fludd's self-identification with the Rosicrucians places him within what Yates calls the movement of Renaissance Hermetism going underground — the Renaissance Magus, driven from his dominant intellectual position by the new seventeenth-century thought, "is going underground and turning into something like a Rosicrucian."

Mersenne accused Fludd of being a magician; Yates considers Fludd's heavy use of Agrippa's *De occulta philosophia* "makes it pretty certain, to my mind, that he was."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates notes that Fludd differs significantly from Bruno and Campanella: he lacks the Italian naturalist tradition's intensity, avoids Bruno's anti-Trinitarianism and radical Egyptianism, and is not heliocentric. She also acknowledges that he knew later influences including Agrippa, sixteenth-century religious Hermetism, and possibly Bruno's memory system. He is therefore not a simple replica of Ficino but a seventeenth-century reconstruction of the Renaissance outlook with later accretions.
