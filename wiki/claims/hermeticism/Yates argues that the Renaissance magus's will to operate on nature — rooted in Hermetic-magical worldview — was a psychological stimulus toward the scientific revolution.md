## Claim Statement

Yates argues — in what has become known as the "Yates thesis" — that Renaissance Hermetic magic played an absolutely basic role in bringing about the fundamental changes in the human outlook that enabled the scientific revolution, not by contributing working scientific methods directly, but by changing the psychological orientation of western man toward the act of operating on the natural world. The emotional and religious excitement of the Hermetic-Cabalist worldview made it newly dignified, pious, and urgent for man to operate — to apply knowledge to produce results — for the first time breaking decisively with the Greek-medieval prejudice that pure contemplation was the only worthy goal and that all operative desire was base or diabolical.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates's core statement from Chapter VIII: "Quite apart from the question of whether Renaissance magic could, or could not, lead on to genuinely scientific procedures, the real function of the Renaissance Magus in relation to the modern period (or so I see it) is that he changed the will. It was now dignified and important for man to operate; it was also religious and not contrary to the will of God that man, the great miracle, should exert his powers. It was this basic psychological reorientation towards a direction of the will which was neither Greek nor mediaeval in spirit, which made all the difference."

She identifies the emotional sources: "They lie, it may be suggested, in the religious excitement caused by the rediscovery of the Hermetica, and their attendant Magia; in the overwhelming emotions aroused by Cabala and its magico-religious techniques. It is magic as an aid to gnosis which begins to turn the will in the new direction."

On heliocentricity: Copernicus's De revolutionibus was framed within the atmosphere of the religion of the world; his heliocentric diagram was immediately followed by a quotation from Hermes Trismegistus calling the sun "the visible god." Yates argues this suggests "the intense emphasis on the sun in this new world-view was the emotional driving force which induced Copernicus to undertake his mathematical calculations on the hypothesis that the sun is indeed at the centre."

On John Dee: "he is a very clear example of how the will to operate, stimulated by Renaissance magic, could pass into, and stimulate, the will to operate in genuine applied science. Or of how operating with number in the higher sphere of religious magic could belong with, and stimulate, operating with number in the lower sphere of 'real artificial magic'."

Yates's summary: "'Hermes Trismegistus' and the Neoplatonism and Cabalism associated with him, may have played during his period of glorious ascendance over the mind of western man a strangely important rôle in the shaping of human destiny."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates is explicit about what the thesis does not claim. Neither Pythagorean number-mysticism nor Cabalist letter-conjuring would of themselves lead to working mathematics. The thesis is about psychological preconditions and emotional stimulus, not causal mechanism. She also acknowledges that Bruno is a complicating case: rather than moving forward from Copernicus toward science, he moved back from Copernicus into gnostic religious mystery — "Bruno pushes Copernicus' scientific work back into a prescientific stage, back into Hermetism, interpreting the Copernican diagram as a hieroglyph of divine mysteries."

Chapter IX of the same book also documents the substantial theological and humanist opposition to Renaissance magic — Catholic and Protestant theologians from Garcia and Del Rio to Wier and Erastus, as well as the Erasmian humanist tradition — suggesting that the magical tradition did not proceed unopposed, and that the humanist critical apparatus (which would eventually enable Casaubon's redating of the Hermetica) was itself a significant counterforce.
