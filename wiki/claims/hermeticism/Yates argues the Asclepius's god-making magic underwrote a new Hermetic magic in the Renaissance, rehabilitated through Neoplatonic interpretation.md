## Claim Statement

Yates argues that the most magically potent section of the Hermetic corpus was the *Asclepius* — specifically its description of how Egyptian priests "made gods" by drawing spiritual powers from the cosmos into the statues of their deities. This passage, condemned by Augustine as wicked demon-worship, was simultaneously the most exciting and most dangerous text in the Ficinian Hermetic revival. Yates shows that Ficino's great achievement in *De vita coelitus comparanda* was to rehabilitate the *Asclepius* magic by re-reading it through Neoplatonic philosophy: the Egyptian priests were not invoking demons, Ficino argued, but using their understanding of the soul of the world to draw down *mundana numina* — world-forces — in a purely natural operation. This Neoplatonic reinterpretation of the Asclepius underwrote the entire tradition of Renaissance Hermetic magic.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates summarises the *Asclepius*'s god-making passage (treated at length in Chapters II and III) as follows: "Hermes the Egyptian" praises the Egyptian priests for their magic by which they drew "spirits or demons" into the statues of their gods, thus animating the statues. Augustine quoted this passage at length and condemned it as proof that Hermes was a devil's confederate. Yet Yates argues that this very passage exercised a powerful fascination on the Renaissance Magus who "found in this description of the magic of the Asclepius the most attractive part of the Hermetic writings."

The key tension, Yates shows, was Augustine's condemnation. Ficino resolved it by a two-step move:

1. **Emphasis on Lactantius.** Ficino's *argumentum* to the *Pimander* stresses the Lactantian tradition — Hermes as holy Gentile prophet of Christianity — while tactfully skirting Augustine's condemnation. He "quotes Augustine's condemnation, and then ignores it, though timidly, by practising magic."

2. **Neoplatonic reinterpretation via Plotinus.** In *De vita coelitus comparanda*, Ficino builds on a passage in Plotinus (*Ennead* IV.3.xi) in which Plotinus praises ancient sages who drew divine beings into shrines by attracting portions of the world-soul. At the end of the work, Ficino reveals that Plotinus was himself "imitating Mercurius" — i.e., that the *De vita coelitus comparanda* is ultimately a commentary on the Asclepius's god-making magic. The Egyptian priests, Ficino now argues (following Plotinus), did not invoke demons separated from matter but *mundana numina*, world-forces operating through "seminal reasons" in the soul of the world.

Yates quotes Ficino's crucial statement: "Mercurius himself, whom Plotinus follows, says that he composed through aerial demons, not through celestial or higher demons, statues from herbs, trees, stones, aromatics having within them a natural divine power." On this reading, the Hermetic magic of the Asclepius becomes a kind of natural philosophy — the intelligent manipulation of the Neoplatonic hierarchy of intellect, world-soul, and world-body — rather than commerce with evil spirits.

Yates also discusses the Hermetic two-branch tradition (Chapter III): on one side, the philosophical Hermetica (Corpus Hermeticum, Asclepius); on the other, the astrological, alchemical, and talismanic literature of practical magic also attributed to Hermes. She argues these cannot be kept separate: even the most exalted philosophical Hermetica presuppose an astrological cosmos, and the Asclepius itself bridges the philosophical and magical by describing the sympathetic magic by which stellar influxes are drawn into material objects. The *Picatrix*, an Arabic talismanic compendium influential in fifteenth- and sixteenth-century Italy, provided the practical techniques whose philosophical framework closely resembled that of the Hermetic treatises — reinforcing for Ficino's readers the unity of philosophical Hermetism and operative magic.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates is careful to note Ficino's anxiety. He was "nervously aware of possible dangers" in advocating talismanic magic, and in his *Apologia* for the *Libri de vita* he stressed the distinction between demonic magic (illicit) and natural magic (licit). She also shows that his preferred planet images — rather than the more "demonic" decan images favoured in some traditions — may reflect a genuine effort to avoid demonic magic by staying at the level of "world" forces.

Yates acknowledges that the distinction between natural and demonic magic was theologically fraught and that Ficino was walking a tightrope. Thomas Aquinas had explicitly condemned the statue-magic in the Asclepius as demonic; Ficino had previously agreed with Aquinas but was revising his view under the influence of Plotinus and the prestige of Hermes.
