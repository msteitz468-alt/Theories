## Claim Statement

The Three Initiates teach that Hermetic Philosophy occupies a uniquely central position among all
esoteric systems: it is "the only Master Key which will open all the doors of the Occult Teachings."
All other philosophical and religious traditions are described as derivative of, or indebted to,
Hermetic wisdom originating in ancient Egypt through [[Hermes Trismegistus]].

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "The Hermetic Philosophy is the only Master Key which will open all the doors of the Occult
> Teachings!"
> — The Kybalion, Ch. I

> "The Principles of Truth are Seven; he who knows these, understandingly, possesses the Magic Key
> before whose touch all the Doors of the Temple fly open."
> — THE KYBALION

The text asserts that India, Persia, Chaldea, Media, China, Japan, Assyria, Greece, and Rome all
"partook liberally at the feast of knowledge" furnished by the Hermetic Masters of Egypt. The
Hermetic Teachings are said to be "found in all lands, among all religions, but never identified
with any particular country, nor with any particular religious sect."

Hermes Trismegistus is styled "The Master of Masters" — "the father of the Occult Wisdom; the
founder of Astrology; the discoverer of Alchemy." Modern astronomy descended from Hermetic
astrology; modern chemistry from alchemy; modern psychology from Hermetic mystic psychology.

With respect to [[The Principle of Mentalism (Kybalion)]] specifically: "He who grasps the truth
of the Mental Nature of the Universe is well advanced on The Path to Mastery. Without this
Master-Key, Mastery is impossible."

## Contexts Within the Source

Ch. I frames this claim as the rationale for the book's publication. The Kybalion was previously
an oral tradition of "maxims, axioms, and precepts" passed from master to student. Its teachings
were "veiled in terms of alchemy and astrology, so that only those possessing the key could read it
aright" — a necessity born of persecution "by the theologians of the Middle Ages, who fought the
Secret Doctrine with fire and sword."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The Three Initiates present this as asserted truth, not argued proof. They acknowledge that the
details of Hermes Trismegistus's historical life "are lost to history" and that various countries
disputed his birthplace. The claim of universal derivation is stated as belief, not documented
history.
