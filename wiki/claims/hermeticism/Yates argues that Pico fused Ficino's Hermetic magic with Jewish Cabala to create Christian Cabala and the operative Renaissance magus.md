## Claim Statement

Yates argues that Pico della Mirandola was the founder and instigator of the Hermetic-Cabalist union — the single most consequential synthesis in the history of Renaissance occult thought. He took [[Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499)]]'s magia naturalis (which drew on the Hermetica and worked through planetary spiritus and sympathies) and yoked it to practical Cabala — magic using Hebrew divine names, letter combinations, and angelic invocations — declaring that no magical operation has any efficacy without Cabala annexed to it. The result was Christian Cabala: a system in which the gnostic-Hermetic elevation of Man as divine operator was confirmed and amplified by the Mosaic Hebrew tradition, and both were used to prove the truth of Christianity.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates quotes Pico's fifteenth magical Conclusio directly: "No magical operation can have any efficacy, unless the work of Cabala, explicit or implicit, is annexed to it" (Ch. V). The twenty-sixth magical Conclusio states that pure Cabala, going immediately to the First Cause, "can do works to which no natural magic can attain." Yates also quotes the Oration on the Dignity of Man: Pico opens it with Hermes Trismegistus — "Magnum, o Asclepi, miraculum est homo" — thereby grounding Man's dignity on a gnostic text rather than a patristic authority, and describes the Magus as one who "marries earth to heaven, that is to say the forces of inferior things to the gifts and properties of supernal things."

The most controversial claim of Pico's magical Conclusiones was that "there is no science which gives us more assurance of Christ's divinity than magic and the Cabala" — a statement condemned by papal commission but which Yates treats as the defining expression of his synthesis.

On the deep structural parallel: Yates argues that Pico's tenth Hermetic conclusion compares the ten "ultores" (punishments) of Corpus Hermeticum XIII with the evil opposites of the Sephiroth in Cabala, "as though the fundamental experience of the Cabalist, when the ten Sephiroth or Powers and Names of God, take up their abode in his soul… is the same as the experience of the Hermetist when the Powers… come to dwell in him and sing within him the 'ogdoadic' hymn of regeneration."

Yates concludes: "it was not only on the level of their magics that Pico married together Hermetism and Cabalism, but on the very deep level of the actual structure of their religious experience."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates acknowledges that Pico's practical Cabala was probably chiefly subjective — used mainly on himself in a devotional and mystical way, not in crude operative fashion. She notes that his magic was "intensely religious and mystical", probably not involving literal external magical operations so much as inner imaginative-spiritual experience. She also notes that details of Pico's actual Cabalist procedures "only the initiated can explain" and that she is not a Hebrew specialist able to elucidate them fully.
