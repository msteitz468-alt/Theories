## Claim Statement

Yates argues that [[Giordano Bruno]]'s celebrated conception of an infinite universe populated with innumerable animated worlds — long celebrated as a proto-modern scientific leap — is in fact a vast extension of Hermetic gnosis. Bruno transformed the Lucretian infinite and the Copernican earth-movement by infusing both with magical animation drawn from the [[The Corpus Hermeticum]] and the Asclepius, producing not a modern cosmology but an animist, vitalist picture of the All as One divine organism — an image of the infinite divinity in which innumerable worlds move as great animated beings, like animals in infinite space.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates quotes the key cosmological passage from the *Cena de le ceneri*: "He made his affirmation that the universe is infinite... the moon, the sun, and innumerable other bodies are in this ethereal region, and the earth also... it is not to be believed that there is any firmament, base, or foundation to which are fixed these great animals which form the constitution of the universe, the infinite material of the infinite divine potency." The worlds are animals, not mechanisms.

From the *De l'infinito universo e mondi*: "Needs must indeed that there should be an infinite image of the inaccessible divine countenance and that there should be in this image as infinite members thereof, innumerable worlds." The infinite universe is needed not for scientific reasons but because God is infinite, and the universe is the infinite image of the divine.

Yates locates the turning point in the *De immenso et innumerabilibus*, where the opening chapter on the ascent of the *mens* leads to this commentary: "Miraculum magnum a Trismegisto appellabitur homo, qui in deum transeat quasi ipse sit deus, qui conatur omnia fieri, sicut deus est omnia; ad objectum sine fine ... contendit, sicut infinitus est deus, immensus, ubique totus." Bruno has extended the famous Hermetic "man as great miracle" passage into a capacity to know an infinite God in an infinite universe. The Hermetic spring upward, the gnostic ascent, literally lands Bruno in the infinite.

Yates argues that Bruno found the raw materials for infinite space and innumerable worlds in Lucretius, but "he absolutely transforms the Lucretian notions by imparting to the innumerable worlds magical animation, totally absent from Lucretius' cold universe, and to the infinite and its contents the function of being an image of the infinite divinity." The godless universe of Lucretius becomes "a vast extension of Hermetic gnosis, a new revelation of God as magician, informing innumerable worlds with magical animation."

Koyré's concession that "Bruno's world-view is vitalistic, magical; his planets are animated beings that move freely through space of their own accord... Bruno's is not a modern mind by any means" is cited as independent scholarly support for Yates's reading.

The unity of the All in the One is the philosophical basis of Bruno's magic: "it is by one and the same ladder that nature descends to the production of things and the intellect ascends to the knowledge of them; and that the one and the other proceeds from unity and returns to unity." This is, Yates comments, "the philosophy conducive to magic, that the All is One, and that the Magus can depend on the ladders of occult sympathies running through all nature."

Finally, Bruno placed both Lucretius and Copernicus in his genealogy of *prisca magia* — the Temple of Wisdom built by Egyptians and Chaldeans and inherited down to modern times. "To my mind," Yates writes, "this genealogy shows that — just as he interpreted Copernicanism as heralding the return of 'Egyptianism', so, to him, the Lucretian universe seemed a kind of extended Egyptian wisdom, whence he adopted the Lucretian infinite universe and innumerable worlds into his Copernicanism as all part of an expanded Hermetic vision."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates acknowledges that Bruno's conception of infinite space and innumerable worlds did have real cosmological implications that were picked up by later thinkers. She recognises the influence of Nicholas of Cusa's Hermetic-inflected geometry on Bruno's idea that God is "a sphere whose centre is everywhere and circumference nowhere." She does not deny that Bruno's ideas mattered to the history of cosmology; she argues only that the spirit in which they were held was Hermetic and magical, and that Bruno himself understood them in that spirit.

She also notes that Bruno's misreading of both Copernicus and Lucretius was a "misunderstanding" in intellectual-historical terms, though a creative one.
