## Claim Statement

Yates argues that the nineteenth-century liberal portrait of [[Giordano Bruno]] as a heroic martyr for modern science and free thought fundamentally misidentifies his position. Bruno was, in her reading, a Hermetic magus of the most extreme type — what she calls a "religious Hermetist" who had abandoned Christian Hermetism and proclaimed a full return to the magical religion of the pseudo-Egyptians described in the Asclepius. His published works from first to last are pervaded by magic; his "philosophy" is inseparable from his "Egyptian" religious mission; and those who followed or were attracted to his teaching might properly be called "Giordanisti" — adherents of a new/renewed pagan dispensation centred on the Hermetic sun-god and the magical ethics of the ancient Egyptians.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates opens Chapter XI by stating her aim: "to place Bruno within the history of Renaissance Hermetism and magic." She notes that Bruno's De umbris idearum (Paris, 1582), the work that attracted the attention of Henri III, was "in fact, written by Hermes — that is to say it is a book about magic, about a very strong solar magic." She identifies the dialogue figure Hermes as the instructor not of the fictional Philothimus but of Bruno himself.

The English ambassador Henry Cobham warned Francis Walsingham about Bruno's impending arrival in England (March 1583): "Doctor Jordano Bruno Nolano, a professor in philosophy, intends to pass into England, whose religion I cannot commend." Yates comments: "Note that it is Bruno's religion, not his philosophy, which the ambassador feels that he cannot commend."

In the Spaccio della bestia trionfante (1584), Bruno quotes the full Lament from the Asclepius in Italian, praising the magical religion of Egypt as "the religion of the mind", deploring its suppression by the Christians ("those senseless and foolish idolaters"), and prophesying its return. Yates writes: "He is taking Renaissance magic back to its pagan source, abandoning the feeble efforts of Ficino to do a little harmless magic whilst disguising its main source in the Asclepius, utterly flouting the religious Hermetists who tried to have a Christian Hermetism without the Asclepius, proclaiming himself a full Egyptian."

The Spaccio is, Yates argues, not merely a satire but a piece of genuine celestial magic: by manipulating the constellation images in the gods' reform of the heavens, the Magus makes the moral and religious reform come in the lower world. "If we thus renew our heaven... the constellations and influences shall be new, the impressions and fortunes shall be new, for all things depend on this upper world" (Jupiter's speech in the Spaccio).

Bruno's own definition of his "new cabala" — "a cabala of theological philosophy, a philosophy of cabalistic theology, a theology of philosophical cabala" — and his explicit identification of his position with the Hermetic Egyptianism of the Spaccio against all forms of Christian theology make clear that he was preaching what Yates calls "an Egyptian Counter Reformation."

She concludes Chapter XIV: "Thus Ficino's whole effort of Christian Theologia Platonica... was as nothing in the eyes of Giordano Bruno, who, fully and boldly accepting the magical Egyptian religion of the Asclepius... saw the Egyptian magical religion as really Neoplatonic theurgy and ecstasy, the ascent to the One."

She further notes: "Where is there such a combination as this of religious toleration, emotional linkage with the mediaeval past, emphasis on good works for others, and imaginative attachment to the religion and the symbolism of the Egyptians? The only answer to this question that I can think of is — in Freemasonry."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates acknowledges that Italian scholars had partially recognised the role of magic in Bruno's thought (Corsano, Firpo) but notes that none had grasped its full Hermetic character or realised that the magic was present from the start of his career, not merely in its late phase. She is careful to note that Bruno thought his "Egyptianism", though unchristian, could still develop within a Catholic framework and that he regarded Christ as a "good and beneficent Magus" — he is thus not simply an atheist or anti-Catholic polemicist.

Yates also stresses that Bruno, like Ficino and Pico, is a syncretist: elements of Cabalism (from Agrippa), Pseudo-Dionysian mysticism, Lullism, Epicurean ethics (from Palingenius and Lucretius), and Dominican scholastic philosophy all enter his synthesis. The Egyptian-Hermetic core is primary, but the other ingredients give his position its particular character and complexity.
