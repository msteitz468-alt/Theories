## Claim Statement

Yates argues that [[Giordano Bruno]]'s acceptance and passionate advocacy of the Copernican heliocentric system had nothing to do with astronomy or mathematics as disciplines. Bruno explicitly said that Copernicus was "more a student of mathematics than of nature" and had not understood the profound meaning of his own discovery. For Bruno, the Copernican diagram was a hieroglyph — a Hermetic seal, read "more Hermetico" — whose true meaning was that the sun is the Hermetic God, the visible divine intellect, and that the heliocentric order heralds the return of the ancient Egyptian solar religion prophesied in the Asclepius.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The decisive piece of evidence comes from George Abbot's 1604 account of Bruno's Oxford lectures (1583), discovered by Robert McNulty: a grave Oxford don recognized that Bruno's lectures on the Copernican theory were "taken almost verbatim out of the workes of Marsilius Ficinus (De vita coelitus comparanda)." Yates comments: "There is the Magus announcing the Copernican theory in the context of the astral magic and sunworship of the De vita coelitus comparanda."

Yates identifies the structural clue: Copernicus himself had quoted [[Hermes Trismegistus]] in the Asclepius — "the sun as the visible god" — near his diagram of the heliocentric system. This, she argues, was what Bruno seized on. "This is the clue; the vision which the Nolan is developing is a new Hermetic insight into the divinity of the universe, an expanded gnosis."

In the *Cena de le ceneri* (1584), Bruno praises Copernicus as the "dawn" that preceded the full sunrise of the ancient and true philosophy: the Copernican sun heralds the return of magical, Egyptian truth that had been suppressed by the "false Mercuries" (Christians). Bruno describes himself as "one who has pierced the air and penetrated the sky, wended his way amongst the stars" — language Yates traces directly to Agrippa's De occulta philosophia on the gnostic ascent that is the prerequisite for magical power.

Yates quotes Bruno's rejection of purely mathematical Copernicanism: Copernicus was only a mathematician and "was not able to penetrate deeply enough to remove the roots of false and misleading principles." The Nolan understood what the mathematician had not. Bruno reads the Copernican diagram as Kepler would describe Fludd's diagrams: "Tu tractas Mathematica more Hermetico."

Bruno's acceptance of earth-movement is grounded in Corpus Hermeticum XII (*De communi*), which affirms that the earth is alive and in motion as part of the universal animation. Agrippa had quoted this passage; Bruno reformulated it as a first principle. Going "far beyond the merely mathematical arguments by which Copernicus put forward the hypothesis of earth-movement, Bruno has seen that it confirms Hermes Trismegistus and Cornelius Agrippa, or, in other words, it confirms the magical philosophy of universal animation."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates does not deny that Bruno's ideas about infinite space and innumerable worlds had some real cosmological implications that later thinkers could use. She acknowledges that antecedents for Bruno's ideas of infinity have been found in Nicholas of Cusa, in Thomas Digges, and in the Lucretian principle of plenitude. But she insists that the spirit in which Bruno formulated these conceptions was Hermetic and magical rather than mathematical or proto-scientific. Alexandre Koyré's own admission that "Bruno's world-view is vitalistic, magical... Bruno's is not a modern mind by any means" is cited approvingly.

Yates is also careful to note that Bruno's misreading of Copernicus was in part a misreading of Lucretius as well: he interpreted Lucretius's universe as a kind of extended Egyptian wisdom, which was historically mistaken.
