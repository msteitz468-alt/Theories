## Claim Statement

In C.H. XIII Hermes leads his son Tat through a **rebirth (palingenesia)**: **twelve torments** of the
body are driven out as **ten powers of God** enter, producing "a birth of mind" by which the initiate is
**divinized** and no longer perceives himself as a three-dimensional body.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"This ignorance, my child, is the first torment; the second is grief; the third is incontinence… the
twelfth, malice" (C.H. XIII.7); "the arrival of the decad sets in order a birth of mind that expels the
twelve; we have been divinized by this birth" (XIII.10); "this, my child, is rebirth: no longer picturing
things in three bodily dimensions" (XIII.13). It closes with a secret "hymn of rebirth."

## Cross-source connections

Rebirth is the in-life form of the death-time
[[The Corpus Hermeticum teaches that at death the soul ascends through the seven planetary zones to be made god|ascent]];
its scheme of expelling vices by acquired virtues prefigures later initiatory and "mental transmutation"
systems, including the [[The Kybalion (Three Initiates, 1908)|Kybalion]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Copenhaver's apparatus notes the heavy scholarly debate over whether *palingenesia* here shows Christian
influence; the term appears eleven times in this treatise but almost nowhere else in the Greek corpus.
