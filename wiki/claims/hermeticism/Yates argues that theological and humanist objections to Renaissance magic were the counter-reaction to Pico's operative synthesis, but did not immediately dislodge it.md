## Claim Statement

Yates argues in Chapters IX and X that the Pico-Agrippa synthesis of Hermetic magic and Cabala provoked two distinct counter-reactions: a theological objection from both Catholic and Protestant quarters, and a humanist objection rooted in critical philology and rational literary culture. Both opposed the magical world of the Renaissance Magus, but neither immediately dislodged it. Instead, religious Hermetism — purged of its explicitly magical elements — flourished even more widely in the sixteenth century, reaching what Yates (quoting J. Dagens) calls "l'âge d'or de l'hermétisme religieux" in the late sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

On theological objections (Ch. IX): Yates documents a growing chorus of anti-magical opinion throughout the sixteenth century. Pico's own nephew Giovanni Francesco Pico attacked his uncle's magic and Ficino's talismans, treating the prisca theologia as pagan idolatry. Protestant writers — Johann Wier (1583) and Erastus — demanded that religion be entirely freed from magic and identified Ficino's Magia with "Egyptian abominations." The Catholic Jesuit Martin Del Rio (1599–1600) condemned Ficino's talismans and denied that Hebrew has special power, while treating Agrippa as an absolutely black magician. Yet Pope Alexander VI had given Pico his blessing, creating a Catholic legitimation that Garcia's earlier attacks had failed to prevent.

On the humanist tradition (Ch. IX): Yates contrasts the Hermetic-Cabalist tradition with Latin humanism. Humanists like Petrarch, Valla, Poggio, and Erasmus had correct chronology, critical philological tools, and a bent toward literature and history rather than philosophy and operative magic. Erasmus's dismissal of Chaldean and Egyptian traditions as the dreams of "some little man, not to say impostor" represents the critical-humanist atmosphere that was inhospitable to magical claims. Erasmus also doubted the authorship of Pseudo-Dionysius — the Christian theological prop on which Ficino and Pico had partly built their synthesis.

On religious Hermetism in the sixteenth century (Ch. X): Yates shows that, despite this opposition, the Hermetic tradition flourished by splitting off its magic. French writers from Lefèvre d'Etaples onward offered a "purified" Hermetism — excising the idol-making passage of the Asclepius (attributed to the interpolating magician Apuleius), retaining the Hermetic religious philosophy and Christian parallels. François de Foix de Candale elevated Hermes almost to the level of inspired Scripture. The Protestant Du Plessis Mornay used Hermetic material extensively in his De la vérité de la religion chrétienne (1581). The Italian Francesco Patrizi proposed to the Pope that Hermetic philosophy replace Aristotle in Jesuit schools as a means of peacefully attracting Protestant converts.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates notes that the anti-magical humanist tradition and the Hermetic-Cabalist tradition were never entirely separate: hieroglyphs, for instance, were taken up enthusiastically by humanists including Erasmus, providing a point of "Egyptian" infiltration into humanist culture. She also notes that Bruno's visit to Oxford in 1583 represents the clash in its sharpest form — the extreme Hermetic Magus confronting a Reformation-Erasmian university — and that this clash ultimately contributed to Bruno's trial and execution in Counter-Reformation Rome.
