## Claim Statement

Yates argues that [[Giordano Bruno]]'s second Parisian period (1585–86) and his wandering German years (Wittenberg, Prague, Helmstedt, Frankfurt, 1586–91), with the Latin poems and late magic treatises produced during them, confirm that Bruno's philosophy of the infinite universe and innumerable worlds was not a step toward modern science but a Hermetic-religious programme. The Latin poems reproduce the Hermetic gnosis of his Italian dialogues in a less attractive form. The *Lampas triginta statuarum* and the *De magia* treatises reveal an intensifying inner magical practice. The diagrams in his published works are "mathesis" — not mathematics, but talismanic figures used "with Pythagorean intentions." Bruno's mission throughout is the return of the world to the Egyptian natural religion.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter XVI, Yates analyses Bruno's second Paris stay (1585–86), showing that his provocative debate at the Collège de Cambrai (May 1586), his dialogues about Fabrizio Mordente's compass, and his *Figuratio Aristotelici Physici Auditus* all carry the same Hermetic message as his English works — but in more crabbed form. Mordente's compass, in Bruno's hands, becomes a divine "mathesis": in the *Idiota Triumphans*, Mordente is cast as Balaam's ass who has spoken a divine truth without understanding it, and the theme of the worship of the Egyptians is introduced at once. Yates: "Mathesis, as we know from the Thirty Seals, is not mathematics, but one of the four 'guides in religion' of which the others are Love, Art, and Magic."

In Chapter XVII, Yates treats the *Lampas triginta statuarum* (written c. Wittenberg, 1586–88) as the key inner document of the period: thirty inner "statues" modelled on the demon-animated statues of the Asclepius, transferred into the imaginative life as a technique for forming a Magus personality. "The thirty statues are thirty imaginative links with demons through which the Magus is forming a Magus personality." The diagrams in the Prague *Articuli adversus mathematicos* (1588) display the Hermetic trinity (mens, intellectus, amor) as intersecting-circle figures, with the word "MAGIC" written on one. Yates concludes these figures may constitute a coded message to initiates, possibly of a sect ("Giordanisti") of which informers spoke at his Venetian trial.

The three Frankfurt Latin poems (*De immenso*, *De triplici minimo*, *De monade*, all 1591), though ostensibly repeating Bruno's philosophy of the infinite universe, carry the same Hermetic content. The *De immenso* contains a violent attack on those who destroyed the Egyptian religion. The *De monade* strips Agrippa's Christian-Trinitarian numerology away and replaces it with purely "Egyptian" or Hermetic number meanings, and revives the demonic magic of Cecco d'Ascoli (burned 1327) — a move even more radical than Agrippa.

Yates: "Bruno is not at all in the line of the advance of mathematical and mechanical science. Rather he is a reactionary who would push the Copernican diagram or a compass invention back towards 'mathesis.' … the conviction grows ever stronger that the new philosophy was a religious message."

In Chapter XVIII, the *De imaginum, signorum et idearum compositione* (Frankfurt, 1591) — Bruno's last published work, written for the occultist Hainzell at Elgg — is analysed as a magic memory system in which twelve "principles" (planetary and solar gods) are to be absorbed into the personality through imaginative concentration on talismanic images. The aim is to "become a Solar, Jovial and Venereal Magus, the leader of the magical reformation." Yates shows that the same gods and same ethical epithets as in the *Spaccio della bestia trionfante* reappear: the reform is still in Bruno's mind, more or less as he had conceived it in England. "Bruno is presenting to the eccentric owner of the castle of Elgg the same panacea for the times, the same magical reformation, as he had presented to Philip Sidney in London six years earlier."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates does not deny that Bruno's works contributed ideas (infinite universe, plurality of worlds, non-Aristotelian cosmology) that later scientists engaged with. She is emphatic, however, that Bruno himself did not think in scientific terms and that any forward-looking scientific implications of his work were incidental to his Hermetic programme.

She also acknowledges the works of this period are among the most difficult and obscure Bruno produced, and that she cannot fully solve the mysteries of some of the diagrams or the possible cipher in some texts. Her claim that the diagrams are "mathesis" rather than mathematics is an interpretive hypothesis, though she supports it with extensive cross-reference to Bruno's own definitions in the *Thirty Seals*.
