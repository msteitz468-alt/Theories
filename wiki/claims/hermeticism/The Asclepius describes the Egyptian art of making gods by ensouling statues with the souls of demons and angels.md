## Claim Statement

The Asclepius teaches that the Egyptians "**discovered the art of making gods**": unable to make souls,
they **called down the souls of demons or angels** and implanted them in statues, so that the resulting
idols — "ensouled and conscious, filled with spirit" — could foreknow the future and bring sickness or
healing.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"They discovered the art of making gods… Because they could not make souls, they mixed this power in and
called up the souls of demons or angels and implanted them in likenesses through holy and divine
mysteries, whence the idols could have the power to do good and evil" (Asclepius 37). Earlier: "Statues,
Asclepius, yes… statues ensouled and conscious, filled with spirit and doing great deeds; statues that
foreknow the future and predict it… that make people ill and cure them" (Asclepius 24).

## Cross-source connections

This passage is the principal ancient warrant for **theurgy** and statue-animation in Western magic, and
it informs the ritual frameworks the wiki catalogs elsewhere (ceremonial magic, the made-god as a
focus). It is paired in the source with the [[The Lament: the prophesied decline of Egyptian religion (Asclepius)|Lament]],
which mourns the loss of exactly this practice.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

It was this "god-making" passage that later Christian readers (e.g. Augustine) cited to condemn the
Asclepius as idolatry — a reception Copenhaver's apparatus discusses.
