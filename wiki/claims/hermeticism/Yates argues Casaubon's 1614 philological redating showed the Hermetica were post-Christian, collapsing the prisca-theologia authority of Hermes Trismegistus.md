## Claim Statement

Yates argues that [[Isaac Casaubon (1559–1614)]]'s 1614 demonstration that the *Corpus Hermeticum* was composed in post-Christian times — not by a primordial Egyptian sage — was a watershed event separating the Renaissance world from the modern world. By destroying the supposed extreme antiquity of [[Hermes Trismegistus]], Casaubon "shattered at one blow" the entire edifice of Renaissance Neoplatonism and magic that rested on the *prisca theologia* chain: the claim that Hermes was older than Moses and Plato, and therefore his writings carried supreme philosophical and religious authority. The redating discredited the Renaissance Magus's Hermetic-Cabalist foundation, the Christian Hermetic movement, and Bruno's project of a return to "Egyptian" pre-Christian religion.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates opens Chapter XXI with this statement: "No one speaks of the 'pre-Casaubon era' or of the 'post-Casaubon era' and yet the dating by Isaac Casaubon in 1614 of the Hermetic writings as not the work of a very ancient Egyptian priest but written in post-Christian times, is a watershed separating the Renaissance world from the modern world."

She catalogues what the redating "shattered":

- "the build-up of Renaissance Neoplatonism with its basis in the prisci theologi of whom Hermes Trismegistus was the chief"
- "the whole position of the Renaissance Magus and Renaissance magic with its Hermetic-Cabalist foundation"
- "the non-magical Christian Hermetic movement of the sixteenth century"
- Bruno's platform of a return to "Egyptian" pre-Judaic and pre-Christian philosophy and magical religion
- Campanella's attempt to build a natural theology on Hermetism

Casaubon's method, Yates explains, was convergent philological analysis: no mention of Hermes in Plato, Aristotle, or the main classical authors; content derived from Plato's *Timaeus*, Genesis, St John's Gospel, and the Pauline epistles; references to Phidias and the Pythian games; and a late Greek stylistic vocabulary inconsistent with ancient composition. His annotated copy of Ficino's 1554 Greek *Pimander* (now in the British Museum) is described by Yates as representing "the death of the Hermes Trismegistus of the Renaissance."

Yates notes the astonishing delay: humanist textual-critical tools for dating by content and style had long been available but had never been applied to the Hermetic texts — suggesting the religious authority of "Hermes" had insulated him from scholarly scrutiny.

She is careful to qualify: "Casaubon's bomb-shell did not immediately take effect and there were many who ignored it." Nevertheless she maintains it must be "reckoned as one of the factors, and an important one, in releasing seventeenth-century thinkers from magic."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates herself notes that "the Hermetica were not, and are not, invalidated as profoundly important documents of religious experience by being at last correctly dated." Modern scholars, she adds, do not regard them as Christian forgeries (as Casaubon and Cudworth did) and "see little or no Christian influence in them." Casaubon and Cudworth were so immersed in the Christian-Hermetic tradition that genuine parallels appeared to them as evidence of deliberate fraud.

She also notes that Casaubon's identification of the Hermetica as Christian forgeries was itself a product of the Christian-Hermetic tradition — he was reacting from within it, not from outside it.
