## Claim Statement

Yates argues that the crucial, symbolically charged event that launched the Renaissance Hermetic revival was Cosimo de' Medici's 1463 order to [[Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499)]] to stop translating Plato — whose complete works were already assembled and waiting — and to translate instead the newly arrived Greek manuscript of [[The Corpus Hermeticum]]. Ficino completed this translation in a few months, giving it the title *Pimander*, while the dying Cosimo was still alive. The episode, Yates argues, speaks volumes about the extraordinary authority of [[Hermes Trismegistus]] in the Renaissance imagination: even Plato had to wait for the Egyptian sage who was believed to be his source.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Yates quotes Ficino's own account from the dedication of his Plotinus commentaries to Lorenzo de' Medici: in 1463 word came from Cosimo that Ficino "must translate Hermes first, at once, and go on afterwards to Plato" (*"mihi Mercurium primo Termaximum, mox Platonem mandavit interpretandum"*). Ficino completed the *Pimander* in a few months while Cosimo was still alive, then began on Plato.

Yates characterises this as "an extraordinary situation": the complete Platonic corpus waits while Ficino hurriedly produces his Hermetic translation, "probably because Cosimo wants to read him before he dies." The explanation, she argues, is that Cosimo and Ficino shared the belief — grounded in the Church Fathers — that Hermes Trismegistus was far older than Plato and the original source of whatever wisdom Plato had: "Egypt was before Greece; Hermes was earlier than Plato. Renaissance respect for the old, the primary, the far-away, as nearest to divine truth, demanded that the Corpus Hermeticum should be translated before Plato's Republic or Symposium."

The *argumentum* (preface) Ficino wrote for his *Pimander* reveals his "state of profound awe and wonder." Opening with a garbled Augustinian genealogy placing Hermes at the time of Moses, it proceeds to construct the *prisca theologia* chain (Hermes → Orpheus → Aglaophemus → Pythagoras → Plato) and describes Hermes as "the first author of theology." The dedicatory preface ends with an ecstatic description of how the *Pimander*'s divine illumination teaches one to turn the mind to the Divine Mind — as "the moon turns to the sun."

Yates also documents the *Pimander*'s extraordinary diffusion: more manuscripts than of any other Ficino work, first printed in 1471, sixteen editions by the end of the sixteenth century. She quotes Kristeller's observation of its "immense diffusion," arguing that this testifies to the "profound and enthusiastic interest aroused by Hermes Trismegistus throughout the Renaissance."

Ficino's commentary on the first treatise revealed his awestruck sense that Hermes was "treating of the Mosaic mysteries." He found striking parallels: as Moses saw darkness over the abyss and the Spirit brooding over the waters, Hermes saw a darkness and the Word of God warming the humid nature; as Moses commanded species to multiply, Hermes uses almost the same words. Ficino even speculated in the *Theologia Platonica* that "if this Mercurius was the same man as Moses, as Artapanus the historian shows with many conjectures," that would explain why Hermes knew so much.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Yates notes that while Ficino's awestruck reading of the Pimander emphasised parallels with Moses, he "significantly fails to point out" the radical differences between the Mosaic and Hermetic accounts of creation — especially in their understanding of Man's nature and his Fall. The Egyptian Genesis presents Man as a divine creative being who falls into matter through desire; the Mosaic Genesis does not grant Adam divine creative power. Yates frames this silence as indicative of the "huge illusion" under which Ficino was operating.

She also notes that Ficino's genealogy was derived mainly from the Fathers (Augustine, Lactantius, Clement), not from Gemistus Pletho, who does not mention Trismegistus in his own genealogy of wisdom.
