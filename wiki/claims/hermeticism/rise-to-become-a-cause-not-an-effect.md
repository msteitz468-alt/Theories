## Claim Statement

The Kybalion teaches that while the Principle of Cause and Effect is universal and inescapable, there are many planes of Causation. By mastering their own mental states and rising to a higher plane of mental action, Hermetic adepts cease to be passive Effects of lower-plane causes and become active Causes — players in the game of life rather than pawns. They do not transcend Causation itself but operate it consciously from a higher level.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "The wise ones serve on the higher, but rule on the lower. They obey the laws coming from above them, but on their own plane, and those below them, they rule and give orders. And, yet, in so doing, they form a part of the Principle, instead of opposing it. The wise man falls in with the Law, and by understanding its movements he operates it instead of being its blind slave. Just as does the skilled swimmer turn this way and that way, going and coming as he will, instead of being as the log which is carried here and there—so is the wise man as compared to the ordinary man—and yet both swimmer and log; wise man and fool, are subject to Law. He who understands this is well on the road to Mastery." — The Kybalion.

The Three Initiates contrast two types of people: "The majority of people are carried along like the falling stone, obedient to environment, outside influences and internal moods, desires, etc. ... Moved like the pawns on the checkerboard of life, they play their parts and are laid aside after the game is over. But the Masters, knowing the rules of the game, rise above the plane of material life, and placing themselves in touch with the higher powers of their nature, dominate their own moods, characters, qualities, and polarity, as well as the environment surrounding them and thus become Movers in the game, instead of Pawns—Causes instead of Effects."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim appears in Ch. XII as the practical and ethical conclusion of the doctrine of universal Causation. It is reinforced in Ch. XV. The Kybalion uses the chess metaphor and the swimmer metaphor to distinguish between unconscious subjection to law and conscious cooperation with law. The teaching integrates all previous principles: the Master achieves Causal status by mastering Polarity and Rhythm (Chs. X–XI), which then enables mastery of Causation (Ch. XII).

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The Kybalion is explicit that Masters do not escape Causation: "The Masters do not escape the Causation of the higher planes, but fall in with the higher laws." On those higher planes they are still servants. The freedom claimed is relative and graduated, not absolute. The text also warns against reading this as pure determinism that negates human agency: the swimmer is still subject to the river's laws, yet still swims.
