## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that ministers' vehement preaching against consulting cunning folk was partly self-interested professional rivalry, since the cunning man offered practical, actionable help with concrete results (recovered goods, apparent cures) that stern clerical counsel to trust solely in God's inscrutable providence could not match, making the wizard a genuine competitor for the laity's loyalty and trust.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Sir Thomas More: "In such wise witches have many fools more faith a great deal than in God," and a Jacobean minister asking rhetorically, "Hath not the blesser more proselytes and patients than the physician... yea than the conscionable preacher?" As late as 1680 a Staffordshire gentleman said white witches had "as many followers as the greatest divines." He records the 1570 Canterbury gaol keeper who let an imprisoned wise woman out because "the witch did more good by her physic than Mr Pundall and Mr Wood, being preachers of God's word," and a character in George Gifford's 1593 *Dialogue concerning Witches* praising a wise woman: "she doth more good in one year than all these scripture men will do so long as they live."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas frames this rivalry in terms of the Reformation having removed the Church's own counter-magic (holy water, exorcism, the cross) without providing an equally satisfying substitute; ministers could only urge that "better lose clothes or coin, than recover either by Satan" (Thomas Gataker), advice which Thomas presents as understandably unattractive set against a cunning man's practical results.
