## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that popular devotion treated saints as specialists rather than general practitioners of supernatural aid: individual saints, images, and relics were each assigned particular diseases, occupations, or dangers, and were believed capable of both bestowing and relieving misfortune.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Reginald Scot's satirical catalogue: "S. John and S. Valentine excelled at the falling evil... S. Roch was good at the plague, S. Petronill at the ague... S. Apolline for teeth, S. Job for the pox." He notes over 500 miracles were associated with Becket's shrine, that the image of St Christopher offered "a day's preservation from illness or death," and that William Tyndale observed people feared saints "lest they should be displeased and angry with us, and plague us."

## Contexts Within the Source
Follows Thomas's discussion of miracle-working saints' lives (The Golden Legend) and precedes his treatment of ecclesiastical blessings and sacramentals; illustrates how supernatural power was fragmented across a specialist pantheon much as in polytheistic religions.
