## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that astrology's core appeal was intellectual: it offered the only comprehensive, systematic explanation of human differences, historical change, and natural misfortune available before the development of sociology, social anthropology, social psychology, and modern medicine and meteorology.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"In the absence of any rival system of scientific explanation, and in particular of the social sciences... there was no other existing body of thought, religion apart, which even began to offer so all-embracing an explanation for the baffling variousness of human affairs... This was the intellectual vacuum which astrology moved in to fill, bringing with it the earliest attempts at a universal natural law." Thomas cites astrology's role in explaining individual differences in temperament (calling it "the first known attempt at a complete system of human typology") and in shaping early historiography, including Newton's use of astronomical data in *The Chronology of Ancient Kingdoms Amended*.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas draws a comparison to Auguste Comte, suggesting "in their confident assumption that the principles underlying the development of human society were capable of human explanation, we can detect the germ of modern sociology." He also discusses John Goad's *Astro-Meteorologica* (1686) as an example of genuinely empirical, if ultimately misguided, astrological research.
