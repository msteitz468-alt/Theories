## Claim Statement
Thomas documents a wide range of magical healing techniques used by village cunning men and wise women, including the recitation of debased Catholic prayers, narrative charms describing episodes in the life of Christ, written charms worn on the body or burned, and Hebrew-sounding pseudo-magical words, all deployed against a huge range of ailments in people and animals.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Named practitioners include Margaret Hunt (1528), who prescribed Paternosters, Aves and Creeds along with herbs; Goodwife Veazy, who cured "tetter-worm" with a formula invoking the Trinity plus honey and pepper; and the Hawkshead bleeding-charm invoking Christ's baptism in the Jordan. Thomas quotes a toothache charm recorded by the astrologer William Lilly ("Jesus Christ for mercy sake / Take away this toothache") and a magic-square-style ague charm ("Arataly, Rataly, Ataly, taly, aly, ly"). He notes charms were often kept deliberately secret from the patient, as in the case of Alice Prabury of Barnsley, Gloucestershire (1563), who "will have nobody privy of her sayings."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas argues these charms represent debris from multiple historical systems — classical, Anglo-Saxon, and medieval Catholic — whose original meanings had by the sixteenth century become "hidden from those who used them," with "the very impenetrability of the formula" itself contributing to its perceived power. He identifies three underlying constituents of primitive healing across these examples: the spell, the medicine, and the special condition of the healer.
