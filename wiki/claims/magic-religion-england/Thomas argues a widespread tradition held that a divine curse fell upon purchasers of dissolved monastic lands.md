## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that, building on the ancient belief that "ill-gotten goods" and sacrilege never prospered, a specific and influential tradition developed from the reign of James I onward holding that a divine curse afflicted the families who had acquired monastic property at the Dissolution — a belief endorsed by leading Anglican clergy and antiquarians, not only Catholic sympathizers, and used to argue against the impropriation of Church tithes.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas details Sir Henry Spelman's systematic study of all ex-monastic estates within twelve miles of Rougham, Norfolk, finding "the monasteries had flung out their owners with their names and families... thrice at least, and some of them four or five or six times... very often by grievous accidents or misfortunes" — published posthumously as The History and Fate of Sacrilege (1698). He cites Sir Simon Degge's parallel 1669 study of Staffordshire, and notes figures including William Cecil, Lord Burghley, and General George Monk warned their heirs against purchasing church lands.

## Contexts Within the Source
Opens section 3 ("Sacrilege") of Chapter 4. Thomas presents this belief as functionally explaining, in contemporaries' eyes, the "unprecedented mobility of land" and the "remarkable number of noblemen who had come to an untimely end on the scaffold" during the period.
