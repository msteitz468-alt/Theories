## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the abolition of compulsory auricular confession at the Reformation removed a system of routine personal counsel, moral discipline and dispute-resolution which the Anglican clergy — through casuistry, spiritual diaries, informal pastoral advice, and exhortations in the Prayer Book — never adequately replaced, leaving many laypeople without a "living guide" for personal troubles.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Jeremy Taylor's admission that "men will for ever need a living guide," and cites Catholic polemicist Benjamin Carier's claim that without confession "neither can inferiors be kept in awe, but by the gallows... nor superiors be ever told of their errors but by rebellion." He notes the correlation contemporaries drew between Protestantism and higher illegitimacy rates (George Hickes's 1677 comparison of parish registers), and records that even the magician John Dee "handed over cases of hysterical illness to the ministrations of godly preachers," while a "skilled casuist" like John Rainolds could become "an oracle" resolving doubts.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas presents this institutional gap as one of the chapter's chief explanations for the persistence of magic: with pluralism, non-residence, and clerical remoteness common, and no comparable confessional structure, "some of the laity should have turned for guidance to those who were less hesitant about claiming supernatural gifts or special access to some occult source of wisdom."
