## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that of the many medieval processions held to secure fertility and good weather, the Reformation abolished all but one — the annual Rogation-week perambulation of parish boundaries — and stripped even that survivor of banners, surplices, and wayside-cross ceremonies in an attempt to convert a rite believed to have "mechanical efficacy" into a purely commemorative, boundary-marking custom.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Bishop Grindal insisting the ceremony was "not a procession but a perambulation," and cites Richard Taverner's 1540 Postils explaining the older belief that reading gospels "in the wide field amongst the corn and grass" would, "by the virtue and operation of God's word," drive off the evil spirits that caused pestilence and crop failure. He records the Puritan incumbent William Brudenell of Deddington refusing in 1631 to wear his surplice or read a gospel at a customary wayside-cross spot, protesting "it was Popery to observe old customs."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas frames the perambulation's eventual decline as owing less to growing rationalism than to social change — enclosure destroying the open-field landmarks the ritual existed to mark, and wealthy villagers tiring of subsidizing communal drinking — a caveat that qualifies his broader Reformation narrative.
