## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that apparent cures effected by cunning men, royal touch, and other forms of magical healing can be substantially explained by the spontaneous remission of certain diseases, the misclassification of superficial or hysterical ailments under labels like "the King's Evil," and the power of patient expectation and imagination, rather than by any genuinely supernatural mechanism.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes that most "cures" of scrofula by the royal touch occurred after a delay and "may be reasonably attributed to the tendency of some kinds of glandular tuberculosis to heal spontaneously," and that many admitted patients suffered only superficial conditions like "sore eyes" loosely grouped under the King's Evil label. He quotes a contemporary pamphleteer: "Physicians do attribute the cause more to the parties' imagination than to the virtue of the touch." He also cites Samuel Butler's observation that patients treat physicians as "a kind of conjurer," reflecting a general lay conflation of professional medicine with magic since both worked by means beyond the patient's comprehension.

## Contexts Within the Source
This claim provides the analytical bridge from Thomas's descriptive catalogue of healing techniques (§1–2) to his broader thesis about the social function of magic: cures need not be literally efficacious to sustain belief, since imagination, the ceremony's psychological weight, and confirmation bias in reporting successes were sufficient to maintain the reputations of both royal healers and village wizards.
