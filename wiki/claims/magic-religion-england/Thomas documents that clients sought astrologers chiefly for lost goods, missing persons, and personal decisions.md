## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the recurring, dominant categories of astrological inquiry recorded in practitioner case-books were personal relationships, business and journey prospects, sickness, lost property, and missing persons — reflecting two underlying needs: for information no other agency could supply, and for advice/reassurance in making difficult decisions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes typical case-book entries: wives asking "if have the man now wooing?"; a client asking whether he would "ever have justice"; questions about husbands lost at sea during the Civil War, such as one from July 1649 answered by Lilly that a soldier husband "had died five years previously." He identifies the recurring formula "Quid agendum?" ("what is to be done?") as the essential question behind most consultations, concluding "the astrologer's essential task was to help the client make up his own mind and to give him confidence in his relations with other people."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates this pattern within a broader anthropological argument that astrological consultation, like other forms of divination, functioned less as objective prediction than as a socially sanctioned mechanism for resolving anxiety and taking decisions under uncertainty — comparable to the role of oracles among the Azande as studied by Evans-Pritchard.
