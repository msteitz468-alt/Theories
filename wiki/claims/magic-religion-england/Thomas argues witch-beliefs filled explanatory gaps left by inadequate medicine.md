## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that witch-belief was especially seductive in medicine because contemporaries lacked natural explanations for many diseases now readily diagnosed (cancer, heart disease, stroke, infection). Even qualified physicians, including members of the Royal College of Physicians, sometimes endorsed a diagnosis of witchcraft when they could identify no natural cause, and Thomas cites Thomas Ady's charge that "ignorantiae pallium maleficium et incantatio" — witchcraft is a cloak for medical ignorance.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"There was, for example, no satisfactory contemporary explanation for the sudden deaths which are today ascribed to cancer or heart disease... Lacking any natural explanation, men turned to a supernatural one." "Seldom goeth any man or woman to a physician for cure of any disease [wrote Thomas Ady], but one question they ask the physician is, 'Sir, do you not think this party is in an ill handling, or under an ill tongue?'"

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes witchcraft was invoked selectively — rarely for commercial failure or bad weather, unlike on the Continent — because English legal statutes emphasized killing and injuring persons or animals, giving the belief "a stylized character" bounded by convention rather than universal applicability.
