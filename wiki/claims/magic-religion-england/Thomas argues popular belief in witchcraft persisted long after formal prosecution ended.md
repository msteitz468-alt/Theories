## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the elite, judicial decline of witch-belief documented through statute and case law did not correspond to a parallel decline among ordinary villagers and husbandmen; the dwindling number of prosecutions "is not evidence that allegations were no longer levied," only that courts no longer entertained them. Informal violence, "swimming," and occasional lynching (such as that of Ruth Osborne in Hertfordshire in 1751, fifteen years after the Witchcraft Act's repeal) continued into the later eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, and Thomas states outright that "if there was a decline in the belief in witchcraft among seventeenth-century cottagers and husbandmen it has yet to be demonstrated."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"But although popular feeling against witches survived the repeal of the Act in 1736, it is possible that the volume of accusations had begun to dwindle... After 1736 when the possibility of formal prosecution was no longer open, villagers turned to informal violence, counter-magic and the occasional lynching."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas contrasts Conyers Middleton's mid-eighteenth-century claim that "the belief in witches is now utterly extinct" — true only of educated opinion — with the persistence of the classic charity-refused accusation pattern into the eighteenth, nineteenth, and even twentieth centuries.
