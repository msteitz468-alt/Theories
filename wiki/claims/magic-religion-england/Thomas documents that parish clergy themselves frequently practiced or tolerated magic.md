## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that, despite official ecclesiastical condemnation of magic, ordinary parish clergy themselves frequently practiced, tolerated, or were popularly credited with magical powers — reading books of spells, using written charms for healing, and, in one extreme case, being executed for maleficent witchcraft — undermining any clean popular distinction between priest and magician.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Cases include John Betson, ordered by the Northern Court of High Commission in 1564 to surrender the Sphere books of "Plato" and "Pythagoras" he had used to recover stolen goods; the Reverend John Bell, stopped by the Royal College of Physicians in 1606 from treating fevers by writing words on paper; Miles Blomfield, a prominent Essex alchemist and cunning man who was made churchwarden at Chelmsford in 1582; and John Lowes, vicar of Brandeston, Suffolk, executed for witchcraft in 1645 after decades of accusations, "no Catholic" but a man with earlier Puritan tendencies. As late as 1804 the Reverend William Ettrick of Dorset cured his supposedly bewitched child with "a phylactery inscribed with sacred words."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas argues that clerical involvement in magic diminished only gradually after the Reformation, driven less by doctrinal change than by the general decline of clerical educational monopoly and the reduced mystique of the priesthood following the end of transubstantiation, clerical vestments, and mandatory celibacy — factors independent of, though reinforced by, official anti-magic teaching.
