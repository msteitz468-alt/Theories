## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that although the formal distinction between a prayer (non-coercive supplication that God may refuse) and a spell (a mechanical technique that works automatically unless ritually flawed) is theoretically clear, medieval Church practice — requiring set numbers of repeated Paternosters and Aves, endowing chantry prayers with quantitative value, and disseminating ritual charms derived from ecclesiastical formulae — repeatedly blurred this line in the popular mind, so that village magicians of the following centuries "inherited" their charms directly from Catholic teaching.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas cites the historian's verdict on chantries that they gave "almost a magical value to mere repetition of formulae." He quotes the commonplace-book of Robert Reynys promising seven supernatural benefits (protection from sword, sudden death, poison, etc.) to anyone bearing an image of Christ's three nails and reciting five Paternosters, five Aves, and a psalter daily. He records the 1543 case of Joanna Meriwether of Canterbury, who used a holy candle dropped on dung in an attempted curse against a young maid.

## Contexts Within the Source
Concludes the main body of Chapter 2's argument before Thomas turns to the Church's own ambivalence and self-justifications (the doctrine of ex opere operato vs. ex opere operantis, and the elastic concept of "superstition"), setting up the sharper Protestant redefinition of the prayer/spell boundary discussed in Chapter 3.
