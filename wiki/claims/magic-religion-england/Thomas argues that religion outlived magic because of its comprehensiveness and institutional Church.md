## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that religion ultimately survived the decline of magic — wizards and astrologers lost prestige during the seventeenth century while the Church continued to provide a framework for society's activities — because religion possessed comprehensive doctrinal scope and, crucially, an institutional Church and communion binding believers together, resources magic entirely lacked.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "Of course, religion ultimately outlived its magical competitors. The wizards and astrologers lost their prestige during the seventeenth century, whereas the Church has continued into modern times to provide a framework for many of society's activities." He states plainly: "It was the general social importance of religion which enabled it to outlive magic. For magic had no Church, no communion symbolizing the unity of believers."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas immediately qualifies this triumph in the following claim: the religion that survived was not the religion of Tudor England but one purged of its own magical elements, so the "victory" of religion over magic was accompanied by religion's own internal transformation.
