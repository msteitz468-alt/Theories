## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that sixteenth- and seventeenth-century England operated with two overlapping but distinct concepts of witchcraft: the ancient popular belief in *maleficium* (the occult infliction of harm), and the theological, continental doctrine that the essence of witchcraft was a heretical pact of allegiance with the Devil. The second concept was a late-medieval Catholic import, codified in the Papal Bull of 1484 and the *Malleus Maleficarum* (1486), which England — lacking an Inquisition or Roman Law — received only partially and late, chiefly through Elizabethan Protestant demonological treatises.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Sixteenth-and seventeenth-century England thus knew not one concept of witchcraft but two. On top of the popular belief in the power of maleficent magic was imposed the theological notion that the essence of witchcraft was adherence to the Devil... it was the fear of maleficium which underlay most of the accusations and trials."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas frames this as the key to understanding the peculiar chronology and character of English witch-prosecution, contrasting England's late and incomplete reception of continental demonology with the Continent's earlier, judicially-driven persecution.
