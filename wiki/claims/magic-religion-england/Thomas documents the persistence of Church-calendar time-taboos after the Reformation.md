## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that although the medieval Church had itself fought against pagan and folk beliefs in unlucky days, its own ecclesiastical calendar reinforced the conviction that time was uneven in quality — and that this legacy of taboo days, unlucky feast-days, and forbidden marriage seasons survived largely intact into post-Reformation England despite Protestant hostility to "superstitious" calendar observance.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes Friday, "as the day of the Crucifixion, was thought unlucky for any venture" and that "we dread to do business on Childermas Day" (John Aubrey). He documents the Church of England's continued enforcement of the medieval prohibited seasons for marriage (Advent to Epiphany Octave, Septuagesima to Easter Octave, Ascension to Pentecost Octave) via bishops' visitation articles — "still being enforced at the beginning of the eighteenth century" — despite Puritan campaigns in Convocation and Parliament to abolish them. He also documents Henry Best's Yorkshire farming calendar keyed entirely to saints' days (Michaelmas, Candlemas, Andrewmas, St Luke, Martinmas, Lady Day) and Thomas Tusser's versified agricultural maxims tied to saints' days.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas argues this persistence was partly rational (the Church year and agrarian year were "intimately related") but that the underlying attitude was "notably less rational" than mere convenience, since medieval clergy had themselves taught that a sin committed on a holy day was worse than one committed at another time, cultivating the sense that saints' days carried a "supernatural aura."
