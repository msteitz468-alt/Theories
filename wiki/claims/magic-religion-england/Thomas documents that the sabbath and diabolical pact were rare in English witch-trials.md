## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that the witches' sabbath, sexual assault by incubi/succubi, flying, and shape-changing — staples of continental demonology — appear only sporadically and unconvincingly in English trial records, and that no reference to an oral compact with the Devil is recorded before 1612. The one genuinely English addition to the popular concept of witchcraft was the belief in the witch's mark and the animal familiar.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Even more foreign to the general run of English accusations was the notion of the witches' sabbath... Nothing approaching a sabbath is to be found in the trial records before 1612... the broomstick, made famous by subsequent children's fiction, occurs only once in an English witch-trial." Familiars, by contrast, "gained a recognized place in witch-accusations at an early stage," appearing in Essex trials from 1566.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas uses this evidence to argue against seeing English witchcraft as a pale copy of continental demonology, insisting on its rootedness in native, pre-existing popular belief about maleficium and animal familiars rather than imported theological doctrine.
