## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the crucial seventeenth-century change was less a matter of actual technical progress than of a new aspiration and expectation of progress: contemporaries became willing to tolerate present ignorance or impotence while believing a rational, human solution would eventually be found, replacing the older reflex of invoking supernatural causes for the unexplained.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "The difference between the eighteenth and sixteenth centuries lies not in achievement but in aspiration." He notes medical therapy showed "negligible" real progress despite advances in anatomy and physiology, quoting Sydenham's hope that men would one day value "him that taught to cure disease" as much as those who merely discoursed about it. He quotes Sir Robert Filmer (1653): "There be daily many things found out and daily more may be which our fore-fathers never knew to be possible." He describes contemporaries developing "that ability to tolerate ignorance which has been defined as an essential characteristic of the scientific attitude." He documents Francis Bacon's list of aspirational goals (prolonging life, curing incurable disease, controlling weather) as paralleling the ambitions of "the astrologers, the magicians and the alchemists, even if the methods he envisaged were different," and cites the fantastical seventeenth-century patents (perpetual motion machines, horseless ploughs, weather control) as evidence of "a breath-taking faith in the potentialities of human ingenuity."

## Contexts Within the Source
This is Thomas's resolution of the puzzle he raises against Malinowski: since technology had not yet caught up when magic declined, the true cause must be located in a shift of mentality and expectation rather than in demonstrated technical mastery — a shift he traces across agriculture, industry, radical Interregnum politics, and above all the new science.
