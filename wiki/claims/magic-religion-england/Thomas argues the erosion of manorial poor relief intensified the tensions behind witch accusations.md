## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the decay of the old manorial system's built-in provisions for widows and the elderly — freebench succession rights, gleaning privileges, permission to sleep in the church — under the pressure of population growth, enclosure, and competitive rents, worsened the position of dependent old women precisely as the new, ambiguous national Poor Law was simultaneously undermining the older moral sense that private charity was a personal duty. This combination of resentment and unresolved obligation, Thomas argues, was "fertile ground for witchcraft accusations."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"This deterioration in the position of the dependent and elderly helps to explain why witches were primarily women, and probably old ones, many of them widowed." "This uneasy conjunction of public and private charity exacerbated the uncertainty with which contemporaries viewed the poor... The conflict between resentment and a sense of obligation produced the ambivalence which made it possible for men to turn begging women brusquely from the door, and yet suffer torments of conscience after having done so."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas cites Robert Burton's complaint about the decay of "charity, love, friendship... alliance, affinity, consanguinity" as contemporary testimony to this perceived social disintegration, while cautioning that fuller research on the decline of manorial custom is still needed.
