## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that astrology survived repeated predictive failure because it was structurally insulated from falsification: astrologers claimed only that the stars "inclined, but did not compel" (Lilly's frontispiece motto was *non cogunt*), predictions were phrased ambiguously, unforeseen miracles or censorship could always be blamed, and clients who received an unwelcome answer simply consulted a rival practitioner rather than abandoning the system.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Jonathan Swift's parody of the technique of vague, always-true prognostication, and notes that Lilly repeatedly invoked divine miracle to excuse failed predictions — over Charles I's execution ("Providence rather acted by Miracle than Nature"), over Richard Cromwell's abdication, and over the Restoration itself. "All this may strike us as the crudest subterfuge, but there is no reason to believe that Lilly was not sincere or that his readers rejected his explanation."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas connects this to the astrologer's role as adviser rather than pure predictor: much of the business "consisted in giving advice and in helping men to resolve personal problems," which was inherently hard to discredit since "there was no telling whether some other day might not have proved even worse." He draws an explicit parallel to Evans-Pritchard's account of Azande oracle consultation.
