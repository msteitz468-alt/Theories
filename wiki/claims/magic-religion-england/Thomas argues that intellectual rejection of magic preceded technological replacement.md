## Claim Statement
Thomas argues, against Malinowski's functionalist theory that magic fills technological gaps and withers as technology advances, that in England magic lost its social appeal well before adequate technical solutions existed to replace it — meaning "it was the abandonment of magic which made possible the upsurge of technology, not the other way round." He cites the Lollards' rejection of Church magic without new material remedies, the Reformation's abandonment of supernatural protection without any accompanying technological revolution, and the decline of witch-belief occurring before medical therapy had meaningfully advanced.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas summarizes Malinowski's view that "magic is dominant when control of the environment is weak," then counters: "the paradox is that in England magic lost its appeal before the appropriate technical solutions had been devised to take its place." He notes: "the fourteenth-century Lollards who renounced the Church's supernatural protection against disease and infertility had no effective alternative to put in its place... Their doctrines gave them spiritual security, but no new means of material aid." On medicine: "in England [witch-beliefs] declined before medical therapy had made much of an advance," and quotes a contemporary on Harvey's discovery of circulation: it "seemed to illustrate the theory of medicine... yet it made no improvement in the practice thereof." He notes expectation of life at birth was actually lower in the late seventeenth century than under Elizabeth I. He invokes Max Weber: magic was "one of the most serious obstructions to the rationalisation of economic life," and that Western technological primacy owed a debt to Europe's comparatively early "disenchantment of the world."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas extends the argument (section 3, "New aspirations") to conclude the change was "thus not so much technological as mental" — a new seventeenth-century "faith in the potentialities of human initiative" (Bacon's New Atlantis aspirations, patents for fantastical inventions, agricultural experimentation) preceded and enabled, rather than followed from, real technical mastery.
