## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that, contrary to the common Protestant polemical claim that magic was purely a "Popish" survival, the medieval Church had already battled popular sorcery just as vigorously as its post-Reformation successor, and that the church courts' methods and severity toward cunning folk remained "substantially unchanged" between the later Middle Ages and the seventeenth century.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes several pre-Reformation charmers uncovered by the courts had Lollard, not Catholic-orthodox, sympathies, complicating the "Popish survival" narrative; that the Counter-Reformation Church "sometimes explicitly associated sorcery with the rise of Protestantism"; and that Neoplatonic learned magic (as practiced by John Dee, "himself a beneficed Anglican clergyman") was "not a medieval survival but a Renaissance discovery," flourishing more under Protestantism than before it. He shows Protestant, Presbyterian, Independent, Huguenot, Baptist and Quaker authorities were all equally hostile: a 1648 Presbyterian ordinance suspended from the Lord's Supper anyone who consulted witches or fortune-tellers.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas argues the real distinguishing feature between medieval Catholicism and Protestantism was not attitude toward lay magic (equally hostile in both) but the range of the Church's own sanctioned magical repertoire — the medieval Mass, relics and saints' healing power gave Catholicism a far wider magical armoury than the stripped-down Anglican Church could offer, which paradoxically made lay magic more, not less, necessary after the Reformation.
