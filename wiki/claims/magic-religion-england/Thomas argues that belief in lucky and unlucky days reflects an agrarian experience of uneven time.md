## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that belief in "unlucky days" — Egyptian/dismal days, climacteric years, taboo Mondays — rested on the essentially inherited, unexplained conviction that time itself varied in quality, with "no one knew why they were unlucky." He distinguishes this from rational seasonal or lunar scheduling of agricultural tasks, and traces the belief to the fundamentally agrarian, pre-mechanical-clock character of early modern society, in which time's uneven value reflected the seasonal, weather-dependent rhythms of rural labour.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "The essential feature of the belief in unlucky days was that no one knew why they were unlucky. The rules for their observance were inherited ones, resting on no discernible foundation." He documents Lord Burghley's three taboo Mondays (traced to the deaths of Abel, the destruction of Sodom, and Judas's birthday) and the climacteric-years doctrine based on multiples of seven. He concludes: "these beliefs about the unevenness of time were the natural product of a society which was fundamentally agrarian in character... The old Church calendar was based on the needs of a people living close to the soil," quoting a fifteenth-century writer: "In cities and towns, men rule them by the clock."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates the belief within a wider comparative frame (Roman dies nefasti, Chinese and other pre-industrial calendars) before narrowing to England, and links its eventual decline to the invention of the pendulum clock (1657) and the Newtonian conception of time as continuous and uniform.
