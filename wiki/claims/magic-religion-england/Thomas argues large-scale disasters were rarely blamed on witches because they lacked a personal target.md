## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that plague, citywide fires, and land-based storms were almost never blamed on individual witches, unlike personal misfortunes, because the required relationship of one-to-one presumed hostility could not plausibly extend to a whole community. Such disasters instead tended to be attributed to national scapegoats (Catholics, the French) or, most commonly, to divine judgement on the collective sins of the people, rather than to maleficium.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"But the victim of an epidemic or a flood was not an individual or a family, but a whole community. To be plausibly suspected of bringing about such a disaster, it would be necessary to stand in a relationship of hostility, not to this or that individual, but to the community as a whole." Cites the 1639 rumor that Scots poisoned Newcastle's wells and the Catholic bookseller blamed for the 1577 Oxford "Black Assize" gaol-fever deaths as examples of alternative, non-witchcraft scapegoating for mass disaster.

## Contexts Within the Source
This claim reinforces Thomas's broader "relationship of hostility" model by showing its limits: only where witches were imagined in the fully continental fashion, as an organized devil-worshipping sect, could they plausibly be blamed for storms or plagues — and Thomas has already argued this conception never took hold in England.
