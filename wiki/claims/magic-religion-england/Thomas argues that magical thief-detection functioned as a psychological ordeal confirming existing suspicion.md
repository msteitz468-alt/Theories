## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the cunning man's divinatory séance for identifying thieves rarely established guilt from a genuine void; instead, it typically confirmed a suspicion the client already held, provided a form of independent-seeming validation, and often operated as a psychological ordeal that pressured the actually guilty party into confession or restitution out of fear.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Thomas Gataker's contemporary analysis that when using the sieve and shears "the strong imagination of those... that hold it... may be a means sufficient to work an insensible motion in the hand of the holder... for the turning of the sieve and shears upon the naming of the parties by him or them formerly suspected." He cites the case of Thomas Harding, a Hertfordshire cunning man exposed as a fraud in 1590 for refusing to accuse people his own client suspected, and Thomas Nottingham, a fifteenth-century priest-magician who initially misidentified a thief until his client whispered the correct name in his ear.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas draws an explicit anthropological comparison with modern African diviners, quoting the observation that a diviner "is successful because he reveals what his inquirer hopes he will reveal," and traces the intimidation function to the medieval judicial ordeal (hot iron, boiling water, cold water, the "holy morsel"), arguing the ordeal was "not usually invoked until the suspect has already been identified" but served instead as confirmation and social deterrent.
