## Claim Statement
Thomas argues, drawing on the surviving case-books of Simon Forman, John Booker, William Lilly and Richard Napier, that professional London astrologers handled an extraordinary volume of consultations across the entire social spectrum, from maidservants to aristocrats and the King himself.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Between 1597 and 1601 Forman set an average of over 1,000 figures a year... Lilly's case-books... reveal a rapidly rising practice which, at its peak, was approaching 2,000 cases a year. John Booker... averaged roughly 1,000 cases a year from 1648 to 1665; he dealt with approximately 16,500 inquiries over the whole period." Clients ranged from servant girls ("Over a third of Lilly's clients in one sample are simply labelled ancilla") to the nobility and Charles II himself, who "wanted astrological advice about his future relations with Parliament."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas treats the case-books as an unparalleled documentary record — "a figure which must surely dwarf the corresponding total of divining séances which have been observed by anthropologists" — though he notes they rarely record what advice was actually given, limiting comparison with anthropological studies of African divination (e.g. Evans-Pritchard on the Azande).
