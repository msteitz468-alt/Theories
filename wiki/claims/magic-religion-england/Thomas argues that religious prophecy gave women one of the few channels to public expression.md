## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the prominence of women among early modern religious prophets is explained by the fact that claiming direct divine revelation was one of the very few socially acceptable means by which women, excluded from pulpit, printed sermon, church office and university, could gain a hearing for their opinions on religious or public affairs.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas's chief example is [[Lady Eleanor Davies (1590-1652)]], whose prophetic career (from an 1625 vision to her death in 1652) was repeatedly suppressed by imprisonment and by her husbands' burning of her books; the High Commission's stated objection was that she "took upon her (which much unbeseemed her sex) not only to interpret the Scriptures... but also to be a prophetess." Other examples include Jane Hawkins of Huntingdonshire (1629), who preached in verse on the downfall of the bishops before a large congregation, and Grace Cary, a Bristol widow whose 1639 vision was later read as foreshadowing the Civil War.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas compares Lady Eleanor to Margaret Cavendish, Duchess of Newcastle, as another woman driven toward "a bizarre exterior... as a form of self-defence" by the social obstacles facing female public expression. He notes the collapse of censorship during the Civil War gave women somewhat more scope, but before that "recourse to prophecy was the only means by which most women could hope to disseminate their opinions on public events."
