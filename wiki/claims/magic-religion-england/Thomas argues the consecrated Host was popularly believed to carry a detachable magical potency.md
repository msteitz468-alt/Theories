## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that late medieval theology's shift of emphasis from communal participation to the priest's formal act of consecration gave the sacrament of the altar "a mechanical efficacy" in the popular mind, so that communicants who carried the Host away from church unswallowed were believed to possess a powerful, detachable, and morally neutral source of magic usable for healing, love-charms, fertilizing crops, or maleficence.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes the ritual was described in "a notorious phrase" as working "like a charm upon an adder." He documents that stolen or retained Hosts could allegedly cure blindness or fever, be "beat... up into a powder and sprinkle it over his garden as a charm against caterpillars," or be used by a thief to become immune from discovery. He cites John Bale's complaint that the Mass had become a remedy used by "witches... sorcerers, charmers, enchanters," and the 1612 Lancashire witch case in which Old Demdike told her grandson James Device to bring home the communion bread.

## Contexts Within the Source
This is Thomas's most developed single example in Chapter 2, illustrating how a doctrine intended as spiritual (transubstantiation via the "instrumental cause" of the consecration formula) generated an extensive "plethora of sub-superstitions" the Church itself struggled to contain, including the 1215 Lateran Council's rule requiring the eucharist to be locked up against theft.
