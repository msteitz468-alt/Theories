## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that "the overwhelming majority of fully documented witch cases" follow the same pattern: an old, poor woman comes to a neighbour's door asking for food, drink, or the loan of household equipment; she is refused, perhaps mumbling a curse; and shortly afterward a misfortune befalls the household, which is immediately attributed to her malice. He quotes Thomas Ady's vivid summary of the householder's guilty reasoning and documents the pattern exhaustively through the 1579 Chelmsford trial of Margery Stanton of Wimbish and the 1585 execution of Margaret Harkett of Stanmore, each of whom accumulated a long list of parties who had denied her requests and then suffered misfortune.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Presently he cryeth out of some poor innocent neighbour that he or she hath bewitched him. For, saith he, such an old man or woman came lately to my door and desired some relief, and I denied it, and, God forgive me, my heart did rise against her…" (Thomas Ady). Margery Stanton's case lists nine separate households who refused her requests, each subsequently suffering a specific, matching misfortune.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas explicitly frames this as illustrating "the essential conflict between neighbourliness and individualism which generated the tensions from which the accusations of witchcraft were most likely to arise," and extends the pattern to disputes over gleaning, common land, and exclusion from communal celebrations like sheep-shearings and funerals.
