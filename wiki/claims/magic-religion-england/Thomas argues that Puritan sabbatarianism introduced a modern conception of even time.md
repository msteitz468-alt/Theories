## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the Puritan campaign against the traditional Church calendar's saints' days, replacing it with a regular six-days-work-one-day-rest weekly rhythm, marked "an important step towards the social acceptance of the modern notion of time as even in quality, as opposed to the primitive sense of time's unevenness and irregularity" — even though the Puritan Sunday itself initially retained the character of a strict taboo day.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes that the Puritans "castigated the observance of Lent, the St Swithin's type of divination, and the celebration of saints' days in general," quoting a Jacobean satirist's Puritan: "it was a passing folly / To think one day more than another holy." He notes their weekly routine was accepted "by the end of the seventeenth century... by society as a whole." Yet he observes the Puritan Sunday was "originally regarded by its supporters as a strict rule to be observed to the letter," citing a 1651 funeral sermon praising a dying man's refusal to send for a doctor because it was a Sunday, and an Anglo-Saxon magical charm requiring total abstention from labour (even face-washing) on the sabbath for protection from danger.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas connects this shift to the rise of mechanical clock-time and urban/commercial life, contrasting the Church's seasonal calendar (rooted in "a people living close to the soil") with the Puritan demand for a weekly rhythm, which he says "emanated from the towns, not the countryside."
