## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that, despite medieval and Reformation-era theological condemnation of "tempting God," it remained common practice for pious individuals — clergy, gentry, and ordinary people alike — to open the Bible or Psalter at random after prayer (sortes sanctorum) or to cast lots in order to receive what they took as divine guidance on personal or political decisions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Examples include Edwin Sandys choosing his sermon text before the Duke of Northumberland by opening his Bible at random; the tailor John Dane consulting scripture before deciding whether to emigrate (c.1636); and the Council of the Army in 1649 seeking God by prayer and then casting lots to decide which regiments would go to Ireland. Thomas quotes Thomas Gataker's 1619 treatise defining a lot as "a casualty or casual event purposely applied to the deciding of some doubt," and William Perkins's warning that lots should be used "with great reverence." He also records folk uses: Mrs Joan Drake of Amersham repeatedly opened her Bible at random for reassurance, and the itinerant preacher Laurence Clarkson chose his road by which way his upright cane fell.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas traces three successive theological attitudes to lots: (1) medieval acceptance of ordeals and lots as a direct appeal to providence; (2) growing condemnation of "lusory" lots as irreverent; and (3) Gataker's early move, in the seventeenth century, toward treating chance as genuinely random rather than providential — a step Thomas presents as anticipating the modern secular concept of chance.
