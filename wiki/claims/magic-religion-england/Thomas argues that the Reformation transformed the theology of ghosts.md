## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that medieval Catholic teaching, which explained ghosts as souls trapped in Purgatory returning for specific purposes, was fundamentally undermined by the Reformation's denial of Purgatory. Since Protestant doctrine held that souls proceeded directly to Heaven or Hell at death, genuine ghosts of the dead became theologically impossible; apparitions that continued to be seen had to be reinterpreted as good or (more often) evil spirits sent by the Devil to deceive men.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes that in medieval England "it was fully accepted that dead men might sometimes return to haunt the living," rationalized by the Church as souls in Purgatory. After the Reformation, "the reformers denied the existence of Purgatory, asserting that at the moment of death all men proceeded inexorably to Heaven or Hell... from neither world could they ever return." He notes belief in ghosts became "a shibboleth which distinguished Protestant from Catholic almost as effectively as belief in the Mass or the Papal Supremacy." Protestants held that apparitions were "not to be mistaken for the souls of the departed, but were to be recognized as spirits; very rarely good ones, more usually evil ones, sent by the Devil."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas contrasts this with Catholic theologians of the Counter-Reformation, who continued to allow that souls in Purgatory might be sent back. He notes the doctrine's strictness eroded over subsequent generations — citing Dr Johnson's eighteenth-century uncertainty about ghosts as evidence of "the shortness of human memory" regarding how firm early Protestant teaching had been. He also discusses the theological uncertainty dramatized in Hamlet's ghost.
