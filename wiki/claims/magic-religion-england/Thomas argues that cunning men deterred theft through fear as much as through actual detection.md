## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the practical social utility of the cunning man's thief-detection lay significantly in deterrence: the mere threat or rumour of consulting a wizard often frightened a guilty party into secretly returning stolen goods before any actual divination took place, functioning much like modern anti-theft devices.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes an 1609 almanac-maker describing how clients would loudly (but falsely) announce within earshot of the household that a cunning man had "shew[n] me in a glass the party that had my ring," prompting the actual thief to quietly return it out of fear. He cites a 1788 Sheffield case where a workman recovered stolen savings simply after a town crier announced he had "gone to a conjurer to find the thief," and quotes John Selden's approving judgment: "There was never a merry world since the fairies left dancing and the parson left conjuring... The opinion of the latter kept thieves in awe and did as much good in a country as a Justice of the Peace."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes the mathematician William Oughtred, Rector of Albury, was content to let country folk regard him as a conjurer, illustrating how educated men sometimes deliberately cultivated a magical reputation for its genuine social utility as an informal crime-control mechanism in a society without organized policing.
