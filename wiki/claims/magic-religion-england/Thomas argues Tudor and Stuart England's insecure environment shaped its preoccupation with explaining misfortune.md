## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the beliefs examined in the book — magical, religious, and providential — were centrally concerned with explaining and relieving human misfortune, and that this preoccupation reflected (without being caused by) the extreme insecurity of the sixteenth- and seventeenth-century English environment: short life expectancy, chronic disease, unreliable harvests, uncontrollable fire, and an almost powerless medical profession.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes that "one of their central features was a preoccupation with the explanation and relief of human misfortune," adding "there can be no doubt that this concern reflected the hazards of an intensely insecure environment," while cautioning that "this is not to suggest that it was these hazards which brought the beliefs into being," since most beliefs were inherited from earlier generations. He documents that aristocratic male life expectancy at birth in the later 17th century was 29.6 years, that a third of aristocratic infants died before five, and that the London mortality data collected by John Graunt in 1662 was comparable to India in the 1911–21 influenza pandemic.

## Contexts Within the Source
This is Thomas's opening methodological framing for the whole book, presented in Chapter 1, "The Environment," which surveys demography, disease, medicine, fire risk, and popular anaesthetics (drink, tobacco, gambling) as the backdrop against which magical and religious "systems for the relief of misfortune" must be understood.
