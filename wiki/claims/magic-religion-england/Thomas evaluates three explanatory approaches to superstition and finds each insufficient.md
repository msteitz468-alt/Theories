## Claim Statement
Thomas evaluates three modern scholarly approaches to explaining superstitious omens and prohibitions — the utilitarian (magical importance tracks genuine social/economic value, e.g. salt as a scarce commodity), the functional (beliefs persist because of their useful social side-effects, e.g. standardizing careful behaviour), and the symbolic/structuralist (Lévi-Strauss's method of decoding beliefs within a total system of classification) — and concludes that none is fully convincing for a culturally heterogeneous society like early modern England, since its beliefs derive from an unsystematized "amalgam of the cultural debris of many different ways of thinking, Christian and pagan, Teutonic and classical."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes of the utilitarian approach: "many of the omens, prohibitions and magically useful commodities seem to have had no utilitarian significance at all, while many of those which were socially useful lacked any magical meaning." Of the functional approach, he notes it "has nothing to say about their origins as such" and fails for beliefs "lacking functional consequences of any apparent value" (e.g. holding one's left thumb in one's right hand when hiccuping). Of the structuralist/symbolic approach he concludes: "it is doubtful whether much progress can be made with it when dealing with the beliefs of a sophisticated and heterogeneous society... the number of 'codes'... is too great for the constituent elements to be easily disentangled." He gives the example of the hare, associated both with the goddess Freya and with harelip deformity, asking "who is to say which of these associations gave it its inauspicious character?"

## Contexts Within the Source
This is Thomas's own methodological intervention, closing chapter 20 and setting up his argument (developed in ch. 22) that magical belief resists a single tidy causal explanation and that superstitions persisted "because they had been brought up to do so," their original symbolic meaning having been "lost or distorted" by the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.
