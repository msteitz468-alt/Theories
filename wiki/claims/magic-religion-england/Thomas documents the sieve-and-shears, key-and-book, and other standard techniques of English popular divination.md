## Claim Statement
Thomas documents a standard repertoire of thief-detection divination widely practised in England, most commonly the "sieve and shears" and "key and book" methods, alongside more elaborate techniques (astrology, geomancy, crystal-gazing, familiar spirits) used by more sophisticated cunning men.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
In the sieve-and-shears method, two people balanced a sieve on the point of shears while suspects' names were read out; the sieve's turning indicated guilt. William Hasylwoode, a cleric, confessed in 1554 to using the technique, learned from his mother, invoking "By Peter and Paul, he hath it." In key-and-book divination, a key placed in a psalter or Bible would cause the book to "wag" at the name of the guilty party; William Newport, vicar of St Owen's, Gloucester, used this method in 1551 to accuse Margaret Greenhill. More sophisticated cunning men, like John Dee (who owned a crystal "as big as an egg: most bright, clear and glorious"), used crystal-gazing with a child "scryer," while John Walsh (1566) confessed to consulting a familiar spirit that appeared as a pigeon, a dog, or a cloven-footed man.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas presents this catalogue to show these techniques required no special equipment or exclusive expertise (William Hasylwoode's mother had taught it to him as a household technique) and were "widely dispersed throughout Europe" and used at every social level, from ordinary villagers to clergymen like Newport, blurring any strict social distinction between "wizard" and "layman."
