## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that Elizabethan and Stuart Protestants habitually described the entire apparatus of Catholic ritual — consecration, exorcism, the agnus dei, St Agatha's letters, and eventually formal Anglican prayer itself — as witchcraft, sorcery, and conjuration, and that "conjurer" became a standard synonym for "recusant priest."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes James Calfhill asking who were "the vilest witches and sorcerers of the earth" if not priests who "consecrate crosses and ashes... christen bells... conjure worms." He cites Daniel Defoe calling Popery "one entire system of anti-Christian magic," and William Lambarde dubbing the Pope the "witch of the world." He records that by 1645 an Essex Anabaptist could declare "none but witches and sorcerers use to say the Lord's Prayer," and that Gerrard Winstanley's Digger utopia would execute any professional preacher "as a witch."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas traces this rhetoric from the Henrician Reformation through Elizabeth, noting it eventually became double-edged: radical sectaries turned the same "witchcraft" charge against the established Anglican Church itself, embarrassing moderate Anglican bishops like Jewel who had defended the Church's "scenic apparatus."
