## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that beyond doctrinal disputes, clergy resented astrologers as practical, competing agencies for advice and comfort — clients brought astrologers the very personal and moral problems the Church expected to resolve itself, which the clergy experienced as a direct threat to their pastoral authority.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"That they should turn to star-gazers in their hour of need, rather than to the traditional pastoral agencies of the Church, seemed a direct threat to the moral supremacy of the clergy, whose privilege it had always been to resolve disputes and to give advice." Thomas quotes contemporary complaints that people gave "more credit to judiciary astrologers than to God's word," and "put more confidence in Lilly than they did in God." He quotes Joseph Blagrave's admission that clerical denunciation actually increased his business: "after the ministers had preached against me and my art, I had twice so much custom as I had before."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes the parallel rivalry over prayer: astrological doctrines of propitious timing seemed to make prayer's efficacy conditional on planetary alignment, though most astrologers (echoing Archbishop Laud) insisted fervent prayer could "frustrate the stars."
