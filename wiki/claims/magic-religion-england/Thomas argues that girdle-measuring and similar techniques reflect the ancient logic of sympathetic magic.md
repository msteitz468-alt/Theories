## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that widely-dispersed European techniques such as girdle-measuring — diagnosing fairy-affliction or witchcraft by whether a patient's belt appeared to change length — along with practices like transferring sickness by taking on the patient's symptoms, exemplify an underlying folk assumption that disease is a physical, transferable "foreign presence" in the body that can be manipulated through sympathetic correspondence rather than treated medically.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Elizabeth Mortlock's 1566 Cambridgeshire confession describing how she measured a sick child's girdle "from her elbow to her thumb" to determine fairy affliction, and notes this "almost exactly reproduces" the 1438 confession of Agnes Hancock in Somerset, showing remarkable continuity of technique over a century. He also cites Elizabeth Page of Blagdon, Somerset (1555), who claimed she had to "cause herself to be in as ill a case as the said child" to effect a cure, and Henry Baggilie of Lancashire (1634), whose "blessing" always left him afflicted with symptoms matching those of his patient.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates girdle-measuring within a broader Neoplatonist-adjacent worldview of "correspondences and analogies between different parts of creation," also invoked to explain contemporaries' use of the rattling eagle-stone (aetites) for labour pains and the doctrine of signatures in herbal medicine, arguing that the boundary between "natural" and "magical" remedy was often unclear even to contemporaries.
