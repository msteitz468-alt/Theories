## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that although the Protestant Church officially abandoned formal ritual cursing (the medieval quarterly "general sentence" of excommunication was abolished in 1534), popular belief continued to hold that a curse pronounced by a genuinely wronged party — especially the poor, the aged, or an injured parent — carried real supernatural force, on the theological premise that God would avenge the oppressed. Church court records document numerous ritualized public curses, often delivered kneeling in the street, and contemporaries like Selden argued that such curses "worked" only because the curser had a legitimate grievance, not through any inherent magical power of the words themselves.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"It was above all the poor and the injured whose curses were believed likely to take effect." "'The curses that come from the poor,' agreed Selden, '[do not] hurt me because they come from them, but because I do something ill against them,… [it] deserves God should hurt me for it.'" Cites cases such as Joanna Nurden of Much Marcle (1616) cursing on her knees, and the legendary curse behind Thomas Arden of Faversham's 1551 murder.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas presents cursing as the direct psychological precursor to witchcraft accusation: a reputation for effective cursing (as with Mary Glover's accuser Elizabeth Jackson, 1602) routinely escalated into a formal charge of witchcraft.
