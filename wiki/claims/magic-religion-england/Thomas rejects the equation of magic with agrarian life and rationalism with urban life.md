## Claim Statement
Thomas rejects the common assumption — voiced by Christopher Hill ("Magic is agrarian"), H. T. Buckle, and David Hume — that magic is inherently a rural, agricultural phenomenon destined to give way to urban, industrial rationalism. He argues instead that crafts and manufacturing (mining, weaving, masonry) generated their own rich magical traditions, that seventeenth-century London was full of astrologers, sectarian prophets, and witchcraft accusations, and that magic's real vulnerability to urban life came from anonymity and social scale, not rationality as such.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Christopher Hill: "There is less magic in twentieth- than in sixteenth-century England, because there is more industry. Magic is agrarian," and Buckle's claim that agriculturists are "naturally... more superstitious than manufacturers." He counters: "it is not obvious that magic was essentially agrarian... any specialized occupation can assume a magical charisma: smiths and metal-workers, in particular, have magical associations among many primitive peoples." He documents mining's belief in subterranean "knockers," taboos on whistling underground, the divining rod (introduced from Germany mid-sixteenth century), and the secret fraternal culture of masons that gave rise to Freemasonry. He notes: "it was in London that the sects, with their prophecies and healing miracles, were most successful; and it was there that the busiest astrologers had their practices." He concludes the real urban effect was that "large urban concentrations of population proved themselves inherently unsuitable for the practice of much of the traditional magic of the past," since thief-magic and witchcraft accusation required the personal, tightly-knit relationships of village life which cities like London (population half a million) no longer provided.

## Contexts Within the Source
This qualifies Thomas's broader search (section 3–4) for a sociological explanation of magic's decline, concluding that "the connection is only approximate and a more precise sociological genealogy cannot at present be constructed."
