## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the revival of Neoplatonism in Renaissance Europe gave educated natural philosophers a coherent intellectual framework — treating the universe as an organic unity of "occult influences and sympathies" rather than inert matter — within which the possibility of natural, celestial and ceremonial magic became a serious, respectable subject of inquiry rather than mere vulgar superstition.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas identifies three categories of learned magical activity this framework supported: "natural magic," exploiting occult properties of the elemental world; "celestial magic," involving stellar influence; and "ceremonial magic," invoking spiritual beings. He quotes Cornelius Agrippa on "the harmoniacal correspondency of all the parts of the body" as the theoretical basis for palmistry and physiognomy, and notes the doctrine of signatures (that a herb's appearance indicated its medicinal use) rested on the same correspondence theory. He explicitly contrasts this Neoplatonist openness with the more restrictive Aristotelian view of matter that conservative clergy and philosophers upheld.

## Contexts Within the Source
This claim situates the practice of village cunning men within a much larger intellectual current, arguing that the plausibility of divination like geomancy or palmistry, and later of learned magic such as John Dee's and Robert Fludd's, depended on this contemporary cosmology being taken seriously by "most scientists and philosophers" of the period, not merely by uneducated laypeople.
