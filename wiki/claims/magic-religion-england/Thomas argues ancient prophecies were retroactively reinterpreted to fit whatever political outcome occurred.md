## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the durability of ancient prophecy, like that of astrology, depended on its capacity to be reinterpreted after events had already occurred, so that virtually any outcome could be presented as having been foretold — a pattern he traces from the Reformation through the Fire of London and the Restoration.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
When Charles II returned in 1660, "Lilly did a rapid volte-face, declaring that the King's return had been predicted nearly a thousand years earlier by Ambrose Merlin." After the Great Fire of London in 1666, Prince Rupert reportedly declared "now Shipton's prophecy was out," and one of Lilly's own 1651 prophetic illustrations, depicting a burning town, "seemed to indicate suspicious foreknowledge of the Fire of London" and brought him under suspicion. Thomas notes the "When Hempe is spun" prophecy was reinterpreted after Elizabeth I's death to mean the realm would be renamed from England to Britain.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas treats this retrospective, endlessly flexible reinterpretation as the central mechanism explaining why prophecy, like astrology, was never discredited by its manifest failures — each new crisis simply generated a fresh application of the old texts rather than testing their validity.
