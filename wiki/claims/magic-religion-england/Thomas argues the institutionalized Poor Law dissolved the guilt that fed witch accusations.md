## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that as the Tudor Poor Law shifted from an emergency expedient to a regular parish-administered system of relief over the seventeenth century, the support of the indigent became a legal obligation rather than a personal moral duty, and the volume of money raised for relief increased more than tenfold. This allowed householders who refused a beggar to do so "with a clearer conscience," since they could tell themselves that other institutional provision now existed — dissolving the specific guilt dynamic that had previously fed the charity-refused pattern of witch accusation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"'I am certain,' wrote a late-seventeenth-century commentator, 'that now, care being taken by overseers publicly chosen in every parish, a great many that have compassionate hearts do not much in that kind as they would do otherwise... Many people not only think it needless but foolish to do that which is parish business.'" "He need no longer feel the torments of remorse which would once have culminated in an accusation of witchcraft."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes this as a distinctly social (rather than purely intellectual) explanation for declining accusation, and observes that belief persisted longest precisely in village communities where informal mutual aid and personal-scale misfortune still predominated — citing the 1751 lynching of Ruth Osborne, refused buttermilk shortly before her accuser's illness.
