## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the doctrine of divine providence, once accepted, was structurally impossible to disconfirm by experience: adversity befalling the wicked was punishment, adversity befalling the godly was a trial, prosperity of the godly was a blessing, and prosperity of the wicked was no threat to faith because it might itself be a sign of God's abandonment — so that "religion was positively reinforced by the hardships of life."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "there was no way in which the theory once accepted could be faulted. If the wicked man encountered adversity this was clearly a punishment from God; if a godly man was smitten then he was being tested and tried." He cites the case of Richard Greenham consoling a father whose son had drowned by offering several possible sins (excessive love of the son, insufficient prayer) any of which could explain the tragedy without requiring a specific one. He notes the farmer Robert Loder thanked God in good harvest years and accepted loss stoically in bad ones.

## Contexts Within the Source
This is Thomas's key analytic move in Chapter 4: having established that providence explained everything, he shows how pastoral practice (Greenham, Josselin, Rees Prichard) applied this elastic framework to console individual believers facing personal tragedy.
