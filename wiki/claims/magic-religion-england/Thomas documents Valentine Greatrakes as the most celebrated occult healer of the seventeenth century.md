## Claim Statement
Thomas documents [[Valentine Greatrakes (1629-1683)]] — an Irish gentleman and former Cromwellian officer who believed he was divinely instructed to heal the King's Evil, and later other ailments, by touch — as "the most famous occult healer of the seventeenth century," whose brief but sensational 1666 English tour attracted the endorsement of leading intellectuals despite his eventual failure to convince Charles II.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Greatrakes made no charge beyond travel expenses and was championed by the Cambridge Platonists Henry More, Ralph Cudworth and Benjamin Whichcote, and the scientists Robert Boyle and John Wilkins. George Rust, Dean of Connor, sought a physiological explanation, convincing himself "that Greatrake's urine smelled of violets." A Cambridge correspondent recalled in 1676: "However it looks at London, it was laughed at in the University." Thomas suggests Greatrakes's healing career may have been "a veiled sectarian protest against the Restoration and the miraculous powers claimed by Charles II," noting his Cromwellian past and admiration for Jacob Boehme.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas uses Greatrakes to exemplify the theory of "stroking" as a magnetic means of "easing the evil humours down through the limbs," a partial rationalization within Galenic medical theory, contrasted with cases where no such physiological mechanism was even proposed and the healer's power was simply attributed to inexplicable personal "mana."
