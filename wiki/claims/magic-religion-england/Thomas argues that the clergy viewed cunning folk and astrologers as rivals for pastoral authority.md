## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the contemporary clergy treated cunning folk and astrologers as "deadly rivals," resenting them both as competing pastoral agencies and as purveyors of non-theological explanations of disaster (plague, fire) that had to be refuted in favour of divine providence. He also notes that Civil War-era sectarian religion itself absorbed much of the magical function that Reformed Protestantism had originally suppressed.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes that the clergy "did so because they resented a competing pastoral agency, and because they were anxious to replace a magical explanation of misfortune by a theological one." He quotes a 1609 Paul's Cross preacher denying that "the Fortune of the Epicureans... the Destiny of the Stoics... the mystical numbers of the Pythagoreans, nor the stars of the astrologers" could explain the fall of commonwealths — only "the sins of the people." He documents that during the Interregnum "the sectarians brought back much of the magic which their early Tudor predecessors had so energetically cast out," healing and prophesying "in a way unsurpassed... since the days of the early Christian saints," to the point that a contemporary judged the cunning woman Anne Bodenham "was either a witch or a woman of God."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates this rivalry within his larger argument that the Reformation, by stripping magic out of official religion, created a vacuum which cunning folk and astrologers (and later, enthusiastic sectarians) rushed to fill — a dynamic he treats as central to understanding why magic persisted so long after the Reformation.
