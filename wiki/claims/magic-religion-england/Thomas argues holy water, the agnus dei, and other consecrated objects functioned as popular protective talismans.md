## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that holy water, the agnus dei (a wax amulet blessed by the Pope), rosaries, holy relics, the sign of the cross, and consecrated church bells were widely used as talismans against disease, plague, fire, lightning, and evil spirits — a use theologians permitted so long as it proceeded from "genuine Christian faith," a line Thomas says was "impossibly fine."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Jeremy Taylor's later complaint that the Irish used holy water on "sick cows' horns" and "them that are blasted." He recounts the 1543 Canterbury storm when inhabitants sprinkled holy water to ward off lightning, and the story of Widow Wiseman throwing holy water at her persecutor Topcliffe, whose horse threw him — an episode Thomas says shows "the distinction between magic and religion was an impossibly fine one." He notes an agnus dei nonetheless "failed to save St Albans Abbey from being struck by lightning."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas develops this material as the core evidence for his argument that theological caveats about faith-dependence did little to prevent popular use of these objects as automatically effective, mechanically operating charms.
