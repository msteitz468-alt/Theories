## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that magic, alchemy, astrology, and even orthodox religious practices such as petitionary prayer possessed a "self-confirming character": once their premises were accepted, any failure could be explained away from within the system (a mistaken calculation, a missed ritual precaution, insufficient faith), so that the reaction against magic could never arise from disappointed clients pooling their experience — it had to come "from outside the system altogether."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes a contemporary on failed cures: "the wizards blame them that they came not in time, or they applied not the means aright, or that they wanted faith to believe... and therefore they advised them to go to a more cunning man." He gives the alchemist's perpetual optimism despite repeated failure ("ful ofte it happeth so, / The pot tobreketh, and farewel, al is go!... Another tyme it may be well ynow"), the pilgrims to St Winifred's Well who believed unanswered prayers meant God had chosen "to grant them extra spiritual rewards instead," and millenarians who, when the Second Coming failed to occur on schedule, simply recalculated: "there had been a mistake in the calculation." He concludes: "There was no way in which these systems of thought could be undermined from inside."

## Contexts Within the Source
This opens Thomas's final chapter and frames his central puzzle: since magical systems cannot be self-defeating, the historian must explain what external forces caused early modern English people to abandon them — a question he says social anthropologists "have as yet thrown little light" upon.
