## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that ghost-belief and witchcraft-belief were often intertwined: a person troubled by a poltergeist or apparition might attribute the haunting to the malevolence of a living neighbour, treating the "ghost" as evidence of witchcraft rather than as an independent spirit.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas gives the 1613 Southwark case in which a woman called another "a hag," asserting "that her ghost doth continually haunt her and her husband that they cannot thrive." He cites a 1600 case brought to the astrologer Simon Forman involving a woman haunted at night by a ghost she attributed to the witchcraft of her son-in-law, and notes that the case-books of the physician Richard Napier "contain dozens of examples of patients 'haunted with spirits.'" He also records the folk remedy that "a spayed bitch... was said to be able to protect a house against being haunted."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas treats this overlap as evidence for his broader thesis (developed fully in ch. 21) that magic, witchcraft, and other supernatural explanatory systems in this period were not neatly separated but blurred into one another in practice, sharing practitioners, remedies, and underlying assumptions about the moral origins of misfortune.
