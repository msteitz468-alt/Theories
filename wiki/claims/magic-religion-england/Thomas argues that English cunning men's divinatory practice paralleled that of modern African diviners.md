## Claim Statement
Thomas explicitly draws on social anthropology to argue that the practice of English cunning men in thief-detection cases closely resembles that of modern African diviners as documented by anthropologists: both operate with divinatory methods "open to manipulation," rely on leading questions and audience cues during a séance-like consultation, and generally end up confirming a suspicion the client already privately held rather than producing genuinely new information.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas cites the anthropological finding that a diviner "'smells out' the answer to the problem, with the aid of leading questions and a clear series of indications from his audience as to whether or not he is on the right track," and that "almost invariably the client has a definite suspect in mind, but one for whose guilt conventional evidence is lacking." He treats this cross-cultural parallel as validating a functionalist reading of English cases such as Thomas Nottingham's fifteenth-century identification of Agnes Watts as a thief, corrected only after his client whispered a hint.

## Contexts Within the Source
This comparative anthropological method — explicitly borrowed from studies of contemporary African diviners and also invoked elsewhere in the book regarding sectarian healing and cargo cults — is one of Thomas's signature interpretive tools throughout *Religion and the Decline of Magic*, used to make sense of English practices without treating them merely as irrational superstition to be dismissed.
