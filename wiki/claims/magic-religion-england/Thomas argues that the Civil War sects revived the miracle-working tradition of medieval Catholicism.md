## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the explosion of sectarian activity after the collapse of the ecclesiastical courts in 1640 saw Puritan and radical sects such as the Baptists and Quakers effectively resurrect the miracle-working, faith-healing dimension of medieval Catholicism which the Protestant Reformation had officially disavowed — "without its Roman and hierarchical features."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas records Hanserd Knollys and William Kiffin attempting to restore sight to the blind by prayer and holy oil, Matthew Coker announcing in 1654 that God had given him "the gift of healing by touch," and over 150 cures attributed to the Quaker leader George Fox, who left a "Book of Miracles" for publication. Thomas quotes a "modern authority": "Many of Fox's cures must be treated as the normal control of strong personality over physical or mental illness," while noting Fox himself believed in their supernatural, not merely psychological, character.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas draws an explicit anthropological parallel to modern Pentecostal healing movements among the Bantu and other African separatist churches, arguing that the promise of supernatural relief was a major factor in the sects' recruiting success among the poor, for whom the helplessness before disease that medieval Church magic had once addressed remained unresolved after a century of Protestantism.
