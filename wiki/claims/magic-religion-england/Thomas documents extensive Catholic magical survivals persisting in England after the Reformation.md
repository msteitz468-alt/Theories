## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that despite the vehemence and speed of official Protestant rejection of ecclesiastical magic, older Catholic protective practices survived widely at the popular level well into the seventeenth century, especially — but not only — in regions with weak preaching ministries, and were kept alive further by continuing Continental Catholic practice and recusant propaganda.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas cites a 1604 report that Lancashire people still crossed themselves "in all their actions, even when they gape," continued pilgrimage to St Winifred's Well at Holywell throughout the century, and the 1589 Caernarvonshire custom of dedicating cattle to St Beuno to raise their market price. He quotes Sir Benjamin Rudyerd's 1628 remark to the Commons about "the utmost skirts of the North, where the prayers of the common people are more like spells and charms than devotions," and a 1584 Puritan estimate that "three parts at least of the people" remained "wedded to their old superstition still."

## Contexts Within the Source
This qualifying evidence closes Chapter 3 before Thomas poses his transitional question: since the Reformation dismantled the Church's magical remedies without providing technical replacements, how did people cope with the persisting hazards of daily life — a question Chapter 4 (Providence) and later chapters on cunning folk and astrology take up.
