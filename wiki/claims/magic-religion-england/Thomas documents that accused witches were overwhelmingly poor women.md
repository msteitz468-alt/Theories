## Claim Statement
Thomas documents statistically that judicial records reveal two consistent facts about accused witches: they were poor and they were overwhelmingly women. Of 109 persons executed on the Home Circuit as tabulated by Ewen, only seven were men; of nearly 600 persons accused there, all but four were tradesmen, husbandmen, labourers, or their womenfolk. Thomas rejects a purely psychoanalytic or sexual explanation for the female preponderance, arguing instead that women were simply the most economically dependent and socially vulnerable members of the community, and therefore the most exposed to accusation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"James I estimated the ratio of female witches to male at twenty to one. Alexander Roberts put it as high as a hundred to one. In fact, of the one hundred and nine persons whom Ewen showed to have been executed on the Home Circuit only seven were men." Richard Bernard: witches were "'very miserable poor, the basest sort of people.'"

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas explicitly argues (later, in ch. 17) against seeing the gender pattern as reflecting a "war between the sexes," since witnesses and victims were as often female as male; the deeper explanatory variable is economic dependency, not gender per se.
