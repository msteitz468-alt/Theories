## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that when disaster (plague, fire, flood, storm) struck a specific community, clergy commonly identified the precise local sin responsible, producing a form of moral scapegoating in which named individuals or houses within the community were blamed for bringing God's wrath on their neighbors.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes the vicar of St Dunstan's, Cranbrook, Kent, recording in the parish register that the 1597–8 plague which killed 190 was a judgement for "that vice of drunkeness which did abound here," beginning "in the house of one Brightlinge" and ending in that of "Henry Grymocke, who was a pot companion." He notes the minister at Hitchin blamed the 1665 plague on the local prostitute, and cites the incumbent of Newport, Shropshire recording the 1665 fire (162 families homeless, £30,000 damage) with the register entry: "Newport, sin no more, lest a worse punishment befall thee."

## Contexts Within the Source
Appears in section 1 of Chapter 4 as evidence that the doctrine of providence was not merely abstract theology but was actively applied by parish clergy to interpret real local catastrophes, feeding directly into Puritan campaigns for moral reformation.
