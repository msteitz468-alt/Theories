## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that at the start of the sixteenth century astrology was not a marginal or esoteric belief system but "part of the educated man's picture of the universe and its workings," inseparable from accepted physics, medicine, botany and metallurgy.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"There was nothing esoteric about these general assumptions. At the beginning of the sixteenth century astrological doctrines were part of the educated man's picture of the universe and its workings." Thomas explains that the four elements of the sublunary region were believed kept in ceaseless permutation by the movements of the heavens, so that "astrology was thus less a separate discipline than an aspect of a generally accepted world picture."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas opens chapter 10 by tracing astrology's descent from Babylonian, Greek, Roman and Arab learning through to Ptolemy, stressing that English astrology remained "recognizably the same subject" as ancient astrology despite Renaissance popularization. He notes that total scepticism about basic astrological principles was "highly exceptional" before the seventeenth century.
