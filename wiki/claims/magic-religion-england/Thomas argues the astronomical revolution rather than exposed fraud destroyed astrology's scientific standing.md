## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that astrology's intellectual collapse by the end of the seventeenth century resulted specifically from the undermining of the Aristotelian distinction between sublunary and celestial bodies — not from Copernican heliocentrism as such, and not primarily from any decisive contemporary refutation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"What really destroyed the possibility of scientific astrology was the undermining of the Aristotelian distinction between terrestrial and celestial bodies, what Bacon called 'the imaginary divorce between superlunary and sublunary things.'" Thomas notes that heliocentrism itself "was consistent with astrology" and quotes Sir Christopher Heydon: "'Whether (as Copernicus saith) the sun be the centre of the world,' the astrologer careth not." He observes that "scarcely anyone attempted a serious refutation of astrology in the light of the new principles" — the subject was largely "left to die a natural death."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas traces the gradual erosion through Tycho Brahe's demonstration that comets lay above the moon, Galileo's telescopic discoveries, and finally Newton's mechanistic universe, and documents lingering scientific ambivalence (Bacon, Wren, Thomas Browne) before astrology became, by Swift's time, simply "a joke."
