## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the sharp analytic distinction between religion (non-coercive prayer, dependent on God's free will) and magic (coercive, automatically effective ritual) — a distinction "popular with nineteenth-century anthropologists" — was in fact "first hammered out... by sixteenth-century Protestant theologians," starting with the Lollards' 1395 Twelve Conclusions, which condemned ecclesiastical exorcisms and hallowings as "the very practice of necromancy."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes the Puritan Richard Greenham distinguishing a minister's counsel from "a magician (who, by an incantation of words, makes silly souls look for health)." He quotes Tyndale attacking Catholics who trust "in the multitude of words... as a conjurer doth in his circles, characters, and superstitious words of his conjuration." He notes the Lollards' specific test case: if holy water's exorcism really worked material effects, it would be the best medicine for any sickness, which it plainly was not.

## Contexts Within the Source
Opens Chapter 3. Thomas presents the Lollard critique as the direct intellectual ancestor of the mainstream Tudor Reformation's attack on Catholic ritual, and frames the whole chapter around this newly sharpened conceptual boundary.
