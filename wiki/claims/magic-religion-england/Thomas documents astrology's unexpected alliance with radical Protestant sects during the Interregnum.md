## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that although mainstream Puritan and Presbyterian clergy were astrology's fiercest enemies, the Independents and radical sects of the Civil War period — Levellers, Ranters, Fifth Monarchists, and especially the Behmenist/Familist mystics — furnished astrology with many active enthusiasts, closely bound up with William Lilly's political alliances.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas lists Leveller and Army radical clients of Lilly and Booker, including Richard Overton, Major Rainsborough, and Cornet Joyce, and notes that leading astrologers themselves had radical or sectarian histories: Simon Forman ("very radical"), John Pool ("a strong republican"), Nicholas Culpepper, and John Gadbury (an early Ranter). He documents the Behmenist fascination with an "astrology within Man" (quoting Thomas Tryon) among figures like John Pordage and Charles Hotham, and notes John Webster's attack on the universities for neglecting "so noble and beneficial a science" as astrology.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas frames this as evidence against a simple correlation between "rationalism" and either science or sectarianism: astrology could serve conservative, radical, mystical or Erastian purposes simultaneously, showing "the striking eagerness of some Independents" to make political use of a genuinely believed-in art rather than mere cynical propaganda.
