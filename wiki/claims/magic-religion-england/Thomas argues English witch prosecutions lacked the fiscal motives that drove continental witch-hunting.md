## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that, unlike on the Continent where a convicted witch's confiscated property enriched the lord, the Inquisition, or trial officials — making "witch-hunting self-sustaining" and "a major trade" — the English statutes of 1563 and 1604 safeguarded the heir's inheritance and the widow's dower, and the accused were typically desperately poor, so that prosecutors gained little or nothing financially. Trials could even be a net expense to the parish, which sometimes had to pay gaolers' fees for indigent accused.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"On the Continent, moreover, there had been fiscal motives for witch-hunting, since the witch's goods might go to the lord, or the Inquisition, or the officials responsible for the trial. But in England no such incentive existed... Most of the victims were extremely poor. It is hard to see what their accusers could have gained by the prosecution." Cites the Warboys witches (1593), whose forty pounds of goods went to Sir Henry Cromwell as manorial lord, as an unusually clear exception.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas deploys this contrast with continental practice as one of his key arguments against explanations of English persecution driven by self-interested authorities, reinforcing his broader thesis that prosecution welled up from below.
