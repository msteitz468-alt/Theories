## Claim Statement
Thomas documents an extensive body of everyday omens and prohibitions — governing numbers, sides, animals, objects, and chance occurrences — believed to bring good or bad fortune for unstated reasons, going well beyond the modern remnants of spilled salt or walking under ladders. He notes contemporaries themselves described the sheer volume of such beliefs: "We have an infinite number... used amongst us" (1612).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas catalogues: it was unlucky to disinherit an eldest son or lose a family "luck"; unlucky to choose a pregnant woman as godmother; thirteen was unlucky at table (echoing the Last Supper); it was unlucky to make an early will; unlucky to be accompanied by a clergyman while hunting; unlucky to whistle after dark in Cleveland, wear a diamond wedding-ring, or kill swallows ("a more fearful sacrilege than to steal a chalice out of a church"). Protective virtues were attributed to bay trees (against thunderstorms), south-running water, vervain, fern seed, milk (against lightning fires), and a caul. Birds and animals carried prognostic meaning: chattering magpies meant guests coming, ravens foretold plague, a hare crossing one's path meant bad luck. He records the 1601 House of Commons debate in which a fainting secretary provoked disputes over whether it was "malum omen" or "bonum omen."
