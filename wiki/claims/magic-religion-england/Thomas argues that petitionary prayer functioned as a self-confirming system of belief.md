## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that post-Reformation belief in the efficacy of petitionary prayer was structured so that it could never be disconfirmed: successful prayers were recorded as "signal returns to prayer," while unsuccessful ones were explained away as due to the petitioner's insufficient contrition, an unsuitable request, or God's inscrutable decision to withhold a blessing for the petitioner's own ultimate good.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "The belief in petitionary prayer was thus a self-confirming system. Once the petitioner had accepted the doctrine, his faith need never slacken, however unsuccessful his own requests for material aid." He cites the Homily on Prayer's command to ask God daily for material and spiritual goods, Bishop Latimer's boast that "faithful prayer faileth never," and the Puritan practice of keeping journals recording "signal returns to prayer," such as *The Life of Dr Samuel Winter* (1603–66), which enumerated eleven instances of efficacious prayer.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates this within his broader argument that religion, even after the Reformation stripped away much of the medieval Church's institutional magic, continued to offer practical, quasi-magical solace for misfortune. He notes theologians always had ready explanations for failure: "God doth know better what is expedient for us than we ourselves."
