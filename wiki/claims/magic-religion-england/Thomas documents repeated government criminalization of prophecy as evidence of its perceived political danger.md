## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the seriousness with which Tudor and later governments treated the suppression of ancient prophecies — through statutes, proclamations, and prosecutions — is itself the best evidence of the genre's perceived power to incite rebellion and instability.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas cites the 1541–2 Act making it felony to predict the future of persons via heraldic devices, its 1549–50 successor "against fond and fantastical prophecies," and its renewal in 1563. He notes a friar was hanged in 1402 for a seditious gloss on the Bridlington prophecy, and that under Elizabeth I "the government remained apprehensive about ancient prophecies capable of contemporary application," investigating figures such as John Birtles found with "a certain old book of prophecy, wherein is great pictures, some with beards" in connection with a 1584 plot against the Queen.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates this alongside his broader argument (echoed for astrology) that repression without intellectual refutation "did more to foster belief than to end it." He quotes Sir Edward Coke on "how credulous and inclinable our countrymen in former times" had been to such fabricated prophecies.
