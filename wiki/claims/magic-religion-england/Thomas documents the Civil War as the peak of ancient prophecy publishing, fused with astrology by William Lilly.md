## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the Civil War and Interregnum produced an unprecedented boom in printed ancient prophecy, and that William Lilly was the pivotal figure who deliberately merged the genre of ancient (Galfridian) prophecy with astrological prognostication, treating both as mutually reinforcing evidence for his political forecasts.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas traces the explosive growth of prophetic anthologies: "Two strange prophesies had become Four several strange prophesies before the end of 1642, Seven several strange prophesies in 1643, Nine notable prophesies in 1644, and no fewer than Fourteene strange prophesies by 1649." Lilly named his almanac Merlinus Anglicus and published A Collection of Ancient and Moderne Prophecies (1645) and Monarchy or no Monarchy (1651), the latter concluding that "Charles I was the last King of England, and there was no political future for his son." Thomas notes Lilly's Prophecy of the White King reportedly "sold 1800 copies in three days," and printer's records confirm the 1645 Collection ran to three impressions totalling 4,500 copies.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas observes that readers rarely distinguished astrological forecasts from ancient prophecy or religious revelation, and that Lilly accepted a divine origin for ancient prophecies, "though he believed they had been communicated to their authors via the crystal ball" — illustrating the porous boundary between the different divinatory genres Thomas treats across chapters 10–13.
