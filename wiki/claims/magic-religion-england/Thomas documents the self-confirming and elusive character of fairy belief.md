## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that fairy-belief was structurally immune to disproof: fairies were held to appear only to the deserving and never to those who sought them out or who revealed their dealings with them to others, so that failure to see a fairy, or the drying-up of "fairy gold," could always be explained without undermining the underlying belief. This elusiveness made fairy-belief an ideal vehicle for confidence tricksters.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes that believers "knew that he could never count on actually seeing the fairies himself, for the little people were notoriously jealous of their privacy and would never appear to those who were so curious as to go looking for them." He notes Joan Tyrry (1555) said she would never see the fairies again after being forced to confess her dealings with them before an ecclesiastical court, and that "everyone knew that a regular supply of fairy gold would dry up immediately its recipient bragged of it to anyone else." He gives the case of Alice West, who told a victim that revealing the secret would bring "the danger of the fairies," and when the victim later fell lame, blamed his own indiscretion. He closes with Falstaff's line: "they are fairies; he that speaks to them shall die."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas draws an explicit parallel to the self-confirming character of astrology and magic generally (developed further in ch. 22), noting fairy-belief "protected them from easy exposure" in the same way as other magical systems.
