## Claim Statement
Thomas argues against the view that witch-prosecution in England was a primarily Puritan phenomenon. He notes that the Catholic Sir Thomas More favored capital punishment for invoking spirits, that Marian bishops showed as much zeal against magic as their Protestant successors, and that the Civil War and Interregnum were not followed by any Puritan-led crusade against witches — the Council of State in 1650 explicitly declined to issue special instructions on witch prosecution. Conversely, prominent sceptics (Harsnet, Bancroft) belonged to the Arminian camp usually opposed to Puritanism, showing no simple correlation between religious affiliation and zeal for prosecution.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"a simple equation of strong Protestantism with a strong desire for witch-prosecution will not work." "After the King's execution there was no orgy of witch-prosecution... At home the trials of the period stemmed from local circumstances and were unrelated to the triumph of Puritanism at the centre."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas uses Matthew Hopkins as a test case: Hopkins himself had no marked religious predilections, though his colleague Stearne was a Puritan, and concludes that witch-prosecution needed only "the stimulus" of the law of the land, not religious zeal, to proceed.
