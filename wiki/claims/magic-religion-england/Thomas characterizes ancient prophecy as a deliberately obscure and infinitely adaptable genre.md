## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the "ancient prophecy" formed a distinct genre from astrology or religious revelation: an elusively vague piece of verse or prose, resting on no defined magical or religious foundation, usually attributed to a venerable figure (Merlin, Bede, Becket, Mother Shipton) and claimed to have been discovered by chance in an old manuscript or ruined building — a form whose very obscurity made it endlessly reapplicable to new events.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"It was not a straightforward prediction, but an elusively vague or ambiguous piece of prose or verse, resting on no clearly defined foundation, either magical or religious." Thomas catalogues typical origin-claims: prophecies "found of late in the Abbey of St Benet in Norfolk," discovered "in an old cell at Syon," or "in a plate of brass in Folkestone in Kent." He identifies the animal-symbolism type (from Geoffrey of Monmouth's Merlin — the White Dragon, the Mouldwarp) as the most common, alongside "painted prophecies" and letter/number ciphers like "When Hempe is spun, England's done."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas explains that most such prophecies fell into two categories: genuine older utterances reinterpreted to fit new events "in flagrant disregard of their original circumstances of composition," or outright contemporary inventions given a fraudulent pedigree — some of which "convict themselves out of their own mouths," such as an alleged prophecy of "a Jesuit in Henry VII's time," decades before the Jesuit order existed.
