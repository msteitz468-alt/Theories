## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that it was "excessively rare" for accusers to identify a witch without already having a suspect in mind, and that accused witches always lived in the same neighbourhood as their supposed victim and were always believed to bear a specific, and specifically *justifiable*, grudge against them. This means that the identification of a witch was never random: it depended on a pre-existing, deteriorated personal relationship in which the accuser was typically aware — at some level — that he or she had wronged the accused, most commonly by refusing charity.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The first feature which emerges from a scrutiny of witchcraft accusations is... that it was excessively rare for men to decide that they had been victims of witchcraft without also having a particular suspect in mind." "The important point is that, paradoxically, it tended to be the witch who was morally in the right and the victim who was in the wrong."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas draws explicit comparison to social-anthropological studies of witchcraft accusation in modern Africa, and treats the cunning man/diviner not as the originator of suspicion but as a confirmer of suspicions the client already held — "merely the mouthpiece of corporate hostility towards the suspected witch."
