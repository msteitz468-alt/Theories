## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that medieval England, despite believing in the possibility of maleficium for centuries, saw very little prosecution because popular faith in ecclesiastical protective magic kept the fear of witches under control. It was the Reformation's removal of that protective apparatus — not the passage of the 1542, 1563, or 1604 statutes, which mostly just gave "added publicity" to an existing anxiety — that "for the first time threatened to get dangerously out of hand," explaining why English witch-prosecution and the Reformation arrived together and why prosecution emanated from below rather than being imposed by church or state.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"It was because of the popular faith in such remedies that so few instances of positive maleficium had been alleged in the Middle Ages, even though the belief in witchcraft was already in existence... after the Reformation the barrier was withdrawn. Ecclesiastical magic crumbled, and society was forced to take legal action against a peril which for the first time threatened to get dangerously out of hand."

## Contexts Within the Source
This claim answers the question posed at the end of ch. 14 (why persecution was concentrated in the century and a half after Elizabeth's accession) and explains why, unlike on the Continent, English witch-prosecution "bore an essentially grass-roots character" rather than starting from above.
