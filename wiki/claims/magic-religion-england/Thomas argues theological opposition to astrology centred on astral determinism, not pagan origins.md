## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the deep theological hostility to judicial astrology stemmed neither from its intellectual weaknesses nor from its heathen origins, but from the conviction that its predictive claims implied an astral determinism incompatible with Christian doctrines of free will and moral autonomy.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The real origin of the theological attack on astrology was the conviction that the astrologers taught an astral determinism which was incompatible with Christian doctrines of free will and moral autonomy." Thomas traces this objection to St Augustine and Aquinas, and notes theologians "saw greater danger in an exact social science than they did in a natural one" — the natural, agricultural and medical applications of astrology were readily conceded by the medieval Church, but "the judicial side of the art" was condemned as heretical presumption to certain foreknowledge.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas contrasts this with the relative ease with which Christian apologetics accommodated natural science generally, citing Genesis i.14 and the Star of Bethlehem as scriptural warrant for astronomy, against the hostile reference in Isaiah xlvii.13. He notes that in strict theory astrologers themselves professed compatibility with free will ("the stars inclined, but did not compel"), but concedes clergy were right to worry that ordinary clients failed to observe this nuance.
