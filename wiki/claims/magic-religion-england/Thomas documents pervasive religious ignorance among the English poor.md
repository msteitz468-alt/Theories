## Claim Statement
Thomas documents extensive and persistent ignorance of even elementary Christian doctrine among large sections of the English population, particularly the rural poor and inhabitants of forest and heath areas, from the sixteenth century through the eighteenth.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Examples include a 1551 survey of the Gloucester diocesan clergy finding that of 311 clergy, 171 could not repeat the Ten Commandments and ten could not repeat the Lord's Prayer; an Essex minister's 1598 report that "the poor people do not understand as much as the Lord's Prayer"; and William Pemble's story of a sixty-year-old regular churchgoer who, on his deathbed, described the soul as "a great bone in his body." John Norden (1607) described heath-dwellers "as ignorant of God... as the very savages amongst the infidels." Bishop Jewel lamented, "Many are so ignorant... they know not that there are any Scriptures."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas stresses this was not a new post-Reformation problem — citing the medieval preacher John Bromyard's story of the shepherd who knew "the father and the son" (as sheep) but not "that third fellow" — nor confined to remote areas or later industrial towns; it was, he argues, "always... there," a permanent feature of the gap between clerical learning and popular capacity, exemplified by John Locke's remark that talking theology to a day-labourer was like "talk[ing] Arabic."
