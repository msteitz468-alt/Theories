## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that two novel intellectual assumptions, consolidated by the new mechanical philosophy of Hobbes, Descartes's followers, and eventually Newton, eroded belief in witchcraft: first, the assumption of an orderly, regular universe unlikely to be disrupted by capricious divine or diabolical intervention; second, growing confidence that natural causes would eventually be found for still-mysterious phenomena. Materialist philosophers effectively "jettisoned demons from the natural world" by denying the reality of incorporeal substances capable of assuming bodily form.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Most demons, he [Hobbes] declared, were 'but idols or phantasms of the brain.'" "it is… simply impossible for either the Devil or witches to change or alter the course that God hath set in nature" (John Webster). "The absurdity of witchcraft could henceforth be justified by reference to the achievements of the Royal Society and the new philosophy." Notes that John Conduitt, one of three MPs who initiated the 1736 repeal, was Newton's nephew-in-law and admirer.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas also notes the ironic role of surviving Neoplatonic credulity (belief in sympathetic healing, action at a distance) in enabling early sceptics like Scot and Webster to dispense with witchcraft specifically while remaining credulous about other occult phenomena.
