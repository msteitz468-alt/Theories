## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that religion and magic both helped people cope with misfortune, but that magic was inherently narrower in scope than religion: magic offered a "collection of miscellaneous recipes" addressing specific, concrete problems (witchcraft, illness, theft, unhappy relationships), whereas Christianity offered "a guiding principle, relevant to every aspect of life," a comprehensive worldview, an explanation of human existence, and the promise of a future life.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "Religion, astrology and magic all purported to help men with their daily problems by teaching them how to avoid misfortune and how to account for it when it struck," but "to stress this point is not to trivialize religion or to reduce it to a mere system of magic." He quotes an unnamed authority: "Religion refers to the fundamental issues of human existence while magic always turns round specific, concrete and detailed problems." He concludes: "Popular magic in England discharged only a limited number of functions... It was a collection of miscellaneous recipes, not a comprehensive body of doctrine... at the popular level magic's role was much more limited than that of religion."

## Contexts Within the Source
This claim sets up Thomas's broader argument in the section that the clergy nonetheless treated cunning folk and astrologers as rivals, because both competed to explain misfortune and to offer supernatural remedies, even though their functions were not truly co-extensive.
