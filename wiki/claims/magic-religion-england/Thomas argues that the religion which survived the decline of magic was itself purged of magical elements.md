## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the decline of magic was not simply a matter of religion driving out its rivals, because the religion that ultimately survived was not the religion of Tudor England: as the Devil was banished to Hell and God confined to working through natural causes, "special providence" and private revelation gave way to a Providence operating through natural law, and Christian theology became compatible with the mechanical philosophy — meaning religion itself had to shed its "primitive magical elements" to endure.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "this process was not simply a matter of religion driving out its rivals, for the religion which survived the decline of magic was not the religion of Tudor England. When the Devil was banished to Hell, God himself was confined to working through natural causes." He quotes a Scottish Enlightenment writer: superstition "has yielded only to the light of true religion, or to the study of nature, by which we are led to substitute a wise providence operating by physical causes in the place of phantoms that terrify or amuse the ignorant," and John Wesley (1781): "The doctrine of a particular providence is absolutely out of fashion in England." He concludes: "the official religion of industrial England was one from which the primitive 'magical' elements had been very largely shorn."

## Contexts Within the Source
This closes Thomas's chapter-21 synthesis and directly anticipates chapter 22's fuller treatment of the intellectual revolution (mechanical philosophy) that dismantled the theological and magical worldview alike, framing the "triumph" of religion as itself a casualty and beneficiary of the same intellectual transformation that killed magic.
