## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that Thomas Hobbes's Leviathan (1651) gave the fullest and most systematic philosophical statement of a century of Protestant polemic against sacramental magic, arguing that true consecration only changes the "use" of an object (from common to holy), not its physical "nature," and that Catholic claims of a real change of nature — in the Mass above all, but also in baptism and other rites — amounted to "conjuration or incantation."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Hobbes at length: to consecrate is "to sanctify or make it God's... and thereby to change not the thing consecrated, but only the use of it... But when, by such words, the nature or quality of the thing itself, is pretended to be changed, it is not consecration, but... a vain and impious conjuration." Hobbes extended the charge to baptism, where "the abuse of God's name in each several person... maketh up the charm," and to marriage, extreme unction, and church consecration alike.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas presents Hobbes as the culmination of the tradition running from the Lollards through Reginald Scot's Discoverie of Witchcraft (1584), which had already argued Catholic exorcism formulae differed from "back-street conjurers... in no circumstances, but that the Papists do it without shame openly."
