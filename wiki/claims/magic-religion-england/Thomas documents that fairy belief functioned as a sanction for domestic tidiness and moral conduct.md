## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that by the seventeenth century fairies were believed to reward good housekeeping (a clean, tidy house, food and water left out for them) and to punish neglect — pinching slovenly servants, spoiling milk and butter, and generally tormenting the negligent. Fairy-belief thus reinforced practical standards of cleanliness, sexual propriety, neighbourliness (prompt return of borrowed household goods), and vigilant infant care, drawing an explicit parallel with modern anthropological findings on Irish peasant fairy-belief.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Herrick's verse instructing housemaids: "Sweep your house; who doth not so, / Mab will pinch her by the toe," and Ben Jonson's lines on the Fairy Queen who "pinches country wenches / If they rub not clean their benches." He writes: "It would be an exaggeration to say that seventeenth-century serving-maids only did their work conscientiously because they were afraid of being tormented by the fairies, but the direction in which fairy-beliefs influenced those who held them is obvious enough." Fairies also "had a great hatred of lust and lechery," insisted on the prompt return of borrowed spits and pewter, and were believed to snatch unbaptized or unattended infants and substitute a changeling, making "a moment's neglect" of a newborn dangerous. Thomas cites the modern anthropological claim that "the fairy faith... enforces definite behaviour on the countryman."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes fairy-belief could also be exploited as an excuse — a parent could disown a retarded child as a "changeling," a quack doctor could blame fairies for failures, and confidence tricksters like Judith Philips, Alice and John West, and the Anthony Ashley case defrauded credulous clients by promising access to the Queen of the Fairies.
