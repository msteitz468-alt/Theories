## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that from Henry VII through William III, English monarchs, courtiers, and intellectuals routinely consulted or patronized astrologers, despite the absence of a substantial vernacular astrological literature before the mid-seventeenth century.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas catalogues numerous cases: Henry VII and his opponents both used the Italian astrologer William Parron; Henry VIII patronized Nicholas Kratzer and received advice from John Robins; Cardinal Wolsey timed a French embassy by astrology; Jerome Cardan cast Edward VI's nativity; Elizabeth I's courtiers, including Leicester, Burghley, Essex and Hatton, employed or studied under astrologers, and John Dee chose her coronation date. Charles II "took astrological advice upon occasions," and Sir John Trenchard, Secretary of State to William III, "confessed on his death-bed that everything the astrologer had predicted for him had come true." Intellectual sympathizers named include Raleigh, Robert Burton, Sir Thomas Browne, Isaac Newton (as a young man), and John Dryden.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas uses this catalogue to argue that "the availability of English treatises on astrology is thus a poor barometer for the actual prestige of the subject" — belief and patronage at the highest social levels long preceded the popular vernacular literature of the Interregnum.
