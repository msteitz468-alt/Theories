## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the three English witchcraft statutes (1542, repealed 1547; 1563, repealed 1604; 1604, repealed 1736) treated the crime primarily as an act of anti-social harm rather than heresy, since severity of punishment tracked the degree of actual damage — death, injury, or none — rather than proof of a covenant with Satan. Only the 1604 Act made entertaining an evil spirit a felony in itself, and even then retained lighter penalties for lesser maleficium, showing the legislators never fully adopted the continental view that any act of magic implied a diabolical compact.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The gravity of the offence depended upon the degree of the injury suffered by the witch's victims, not on any postulated covenant... it would have been pointless for them to draw distinctions between degrees of magic, since all would have been equally reprehensible" under the fully continental doctrine.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas uses close statutory analysis to support his larger claim that English witch-prosecution was driven by fear of maleficium rather than by imported theological zeal against heresy.
