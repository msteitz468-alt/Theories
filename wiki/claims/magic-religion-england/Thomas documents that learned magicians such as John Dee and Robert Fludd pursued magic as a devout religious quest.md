## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that for its most sophisticated practitioners, ceremonial magic and alchemy were experienced and justified as a devout religious discipline rather than a rival to religion: theurgy required prayer, fasting and a "rigorous course of... devotional preparation," and adepts believed themselves engaged in ascending toward God, not defying him.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas records that at Prague, John Dee told Emperor Rudolph II that after forty years of seeking knowledge in vain, he resolved "with the aid of a special stone and holy angels" to intercede with God directly, always taking care that the spirits he contacted were good, and that "at no point in his occult wanderings did he consider himself to have passed the bounds of Christianity." Robert Fludd took a vow of chastity, "regard[ing] the flesh as the root of all evil." Thomas quotes Sir Walter Raleigh: "The art of magic is the art of worshipping God." He also notes many medieval and early modern alchemists were monks or clergy (John Thornborough, Bishop of Worcester; Meric Casaubon), and that the transmutation of metals was secondary for most to "the spiritual transformation of the adept."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas presents this "gnostic" tradition of seeking "knowledge by revelation, through prayer, fasting and communion with God" as evidence that, at the intellectual level no less than the popular level, magic and religion functioned as "travelling companions along the path to one identical and comprehensive truth" rather than as opposed systems, even though most orthodox Calvinist clergy condemned alchemy as diabolical pride.
