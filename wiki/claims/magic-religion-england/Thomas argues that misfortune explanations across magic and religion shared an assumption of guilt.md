## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that despite their rivalry, religious and non-religious explanations of misfortune (witchcraft, fairies, ghosts) shared a common underlying ethical premise: that suffering was probably deserved, with the sufferer himself the most likely culprit. This shared assumption made both magic and religion powerful instruments of social control, since misfortune prompted moral self-examination and reinforced existing social norms.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "What is most notable about the non-religious explanations of misfortune is that they usually shared with theologians the same ethical assumption that suffering was probably due to someone's moral fault, with the most likely culprit the sufferer himself." He notes "fairies and ghosts were similarly more inclined to torment those who had failed in some aspect of their social duty," and that even witch-beliefs, "though superficially an attempt to shuffle off the blame on to a third party, were... unlikely to be invoked unless the victim himself was conscious of some moral fault." He concludes: "This implied link between misfortune and guilt was a fundamental feature of the mental environment of this period... Both magic and religion thus became an important means of social control."

## Contexts Within the Source
This claim directly synthesizes Thomas's earlier arguments about providence (moralized misfortune under Protestant theology) and the "charity-refused" model of witchcraft accusation, presenting them as instances of one common pattern spanning religious and magical belief alike.
