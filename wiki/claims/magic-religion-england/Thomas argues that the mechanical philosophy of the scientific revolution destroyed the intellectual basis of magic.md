## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the seventeenth-century scientific and philosophical revolution — the rejection of scholastic Aristotelianism and Neoplatonic microcosm-macrocosm theory in favour of the mechanical philosophy — destroyed the intellectual foundation of astrology, chiromancy, alchemy, physiognomy, and astral magic, while an accompanying epistemological demand for empirical demonstration undermined witchcraft prosecution, astrology, and claims of direct religious inspiration alike. He notes, however, that magic and early science had initially developed together rather than in opposition.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "With the collapse of the microcosm theory went the destruction of the whole intellectual basis of astrology, chiromancy, alchemy, physiognomy, astral magic and their associates... The Cartesian concept of matter relegated spirits, whether good or bad, to the purely mental world; conjuration ceased to be a meaningful ambition." He cites Casaubon's 1614 redating of the hermetic corpus, Mersenne and Gassendi's refutation of Robert Fludd, Robert Boyle's chemistry, the Royal Society's disproof of spontaneous generation, and John Ray's rejection of the doctrine of signatures. He notes William Harvey dissected a supposed witch's-familiar toad, and Henry Briggs "abandoned his interest in astrology, once he discovered that its principles were incapable of demonstration." He quotes Samuel Butler: "There is no certain knowledge without demonstration."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas is careful to note the union of magic and science was genuine and productive before it dissolved — Neoplatonic and hermetic thinking "had stimulated such crucial discoveries... as heliocentrism, the infinity of worlds, and the circulation of the blood" — and that the change reached the educated elite well before it percolated to the "people at large," as shown by Ephraim Chambers's cautious 1728 Cyclopaedia still crediting amulets and occult influences.
