## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that astrology appealed as a means of explaining personal misfortune — illness, sterility, bankruptcy, political failure — in a way that removed both individual guilt and the appearance of pure random chance, offering "intellectual long-stop" against the unattractive alternatives of blind chance or a capricious deity.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"No doubt it was more comforting to learn that one had been crossed at birth than to be told that one had no one to blame for one's misfortunes but oneself." Thomas cites John Aubrey consoling himself for his "sundry worldly failures" with the opinion that he had been "labouring under a crowd of ill directions," and quotes Edmund's speech in *King Lear* mocking those who "make guilty of our disasters the sun, the moon, and the stars." He also quotes the astrologer John Butler: "There really was no such thing as chance in nature."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas presents this as the second, more practical, layer of astrology's appeal (after its intellectual comprehensiveness): by establishing a client's nativity the astrologer offered a form of self-knowledge Thomas compares explicitly to modern psychoanalysis — "the reward would be a penetrating analysis of the individual's innermost attributes."
