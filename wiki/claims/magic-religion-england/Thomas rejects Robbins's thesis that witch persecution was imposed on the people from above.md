## Claim Statement
Thomas explicitly rejects Rossell Hope Robbins's argument (in his *Encyclopedia of Witchcraft and Demonology*, 1959) that the theological concept of witch-as-heretic was "never of the people" but was cynically manufactured and imposed by self-interested clerics and inquisitors. Thomas counters that the bulk of English accusations concerned maleficium rather than heresy, that England had no fiscal incentive for witch-hunting (unlike the Continent, where a witch's confiscated goods enriched lords or inquisitors), and that even Matthew Hopkins's campaign — the closest English analogue to a top-down persecution — succeeded only because it exploited pre-existing popular suspicions rather than manufacturing them.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Professor Robbins's interpretation is a valuable reminder that the witch-beliefs of our period contained an element for which no earlier precedent is to be found... But as an explanation of the increased witch-prosecution in sixteenth- and seventeenth-century England, his argument will hardly do." "There is no reason, therefore, to think that the persecution of witches in England originated in a campaign led by clerics and lawyers against the instincts of ordinary people."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas builds this rebuttal on the absence of financial motive in English cases (unlike the Continent) and on the judiciary's demonstrated reluctance, rather than eagerness, to convict from the later seventeenth century onward.
