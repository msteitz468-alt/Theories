## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that, despite compulsory Sunday church attendance being a criminal offence, actual church-going in Tudor and Stuart England fell well short of the legal ideal, especially among the poor, and that congregations who did attend often behaved with open irreverence.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes that the near-universal Easter communion attendance achieved in Counter-Reformation France was "almost never attained in England," citing that in 1540–42 not half the communicants of St Giles, Colchester, attended church, and that in 1633 Great Yarmouth had twelve hundred Easter absentees. He records disruptive congregational behaviour: jostling, spitting, knitting, joking, sleeping, guns fired in church, and a Wisbech tailor's 1601 blasphemous parody of a sermon on "Thou art Peter." Archbishop Grindal's 1571 injunctions explicitly targeted "especially householders," implicitly excusing servants and the poor from strict enforcement.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas connects low and irreverent attendance to Puritan complaints (Richard Baxter's 1691 lament about "tinkers and sow-gelders") that the "far greater part of the people" resented "practical godliness," and situates this as further evidence that the Church's formal social dominance (established in §1 of the chapter) coexisted with substantial popular indifference in practice.
