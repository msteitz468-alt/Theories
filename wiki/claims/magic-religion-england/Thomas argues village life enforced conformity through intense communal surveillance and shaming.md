## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that Tudor-Stuart village society had almost no concept of privacy: neighbours felt entitled to monitor and comment on each other's marriages, sleeping arrangements, and personal affairs, and used institutions such as the cucking-stool, the church courts, "rough music," and public penance to police scolds, cursers, and other nonconformists. The witch, in his framing, was the extreme case of the "malignant or non-conforming person against whom the local community had always taken punitive action in the interests of social harmony," and Reginald Scot's remark that witches' "chief fault... is that they are scolds" reflects a real overlap between the two categories of offender.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"If the records of Tudor and Stuart village life leave any single impression, it is that of the tyranny of local opinion and the lack of tolerance displayed towards nonconformity or social deviation." A Duke of Stettin visitor was told in 1602 "that in England every citizen is bound by oath to keep a sharp eye at his neighbour's house, as to whether the married people live in harmony." Scot: witches' "chief fault... is that they are scolds."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas connects this village intolerance directly to witch prosecution and to the parallel outlet of arson as a weapon of the socially outcast, arguing both were forms of retaliation available to those excluded from legitimate legal or physical redress.
