## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the popular rejection of Catholic ecclesiastical magic was genuine and widespread, not merely elite-imposed, as shown by ordinary people's own crude, deliberate acts of profaning formerly holy objects — evidence, he argues, that "the decline of old Catholic beliefs was not the result of persecution; it reflected a change in the popular conception of religion."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas records that a Downhead, Somerset man claimed "his mare will make as good holy water as any priest can"; a Lincoln shoemaker's wife said her urine was as good holy water "as [that] the priest now makes"; Dean Whittingham of Durham's wife burned St Cuthbert's banner and used holy-water stoups for salting fish; and "four drunken fellows" in Derbyshire led a newly calved cow around the font and read the churching service over it. Common people fed holy bread to dogs and gave images to children as dolls.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas presents this coarse, sometimes deliberately obscene popular iconoclasm as evidence of a genuinely bottom-up as well as top-down Reformation, distinguishing it from the more decorous theological polemic of bishops and reformers discussed earlier in the chapter.
