## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that popular magic could only be genuinely displaced once religion offered people a psychologically satisfying alternative to magical shortcuts — not by the medieval strategy of providing a rival ecclesiastical magic, but by the distinctively Protestant teaching that practical difficulties should be solved through a combination of self-help, hard labour, and prayer, making magical remedies seem impious specifically because they were "too easy."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Francis Bacon's objection to magic which sought "those noble effects which God hath set forth unto man to be bought at the price of labour, to be attained by a few easy and slothful observances," and the physician John Cotta's near-identical formulation that "God hath given nothing unto man but for his travail and pain." He concludes that mere ecclesiastical court repression, however sustained, was insufficient on its own: "if they had had only the church courts to fear, the wizards need never have disappeared," since courts "only worked well so long as they were enforcing standards to which the local communities themselves subscribed."

## Contexts Within the Source
This is Thomas's synthesizing thesis for the whole magic-versus-religion comparison across chs. 7–9: the medieval Church tried and largely failed to compete with lay magic by supplying its own rival magic; the Reformation instead tried to discredit the entire category of magical solution, an approach only partially successful (since, as ch. 9 shows throughout, magic kept creeping back into religious practice) but which nonetheless represented, in Thomas's account, "advance in a fundamentally new direction" toward valuing "a technological solution to... problems rather than a magical one."
