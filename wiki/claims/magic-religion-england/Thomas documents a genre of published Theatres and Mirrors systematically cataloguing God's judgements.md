## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that recording God's providences and judgements became "a religious duty for everyone," producing both a private diary-keeping culture and a substantial published genre of compilations — including Thomas Beard's The Theatre of Gods Iudgments (1597) and Samuel Clarke's Mirror or Looking-Glasse (1646) — and even organized, quasi-scientific data-collection projects modeled on natural philosophy.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes Beard's book (Oliver Cromwell's schoolmaster) was "several times reissued and augmented" and abridged as The Thunderbolt of Gods wrath (1618). He describes Matthew Poole's Cromwellian-era "Design for registring of Illustrious Providences," with county secretaries forwarding material to Syon College, explicitly paralleling "the methods used by the scientists of the Royal Society for collecting and classifying natural phenomena," and its transatlantic successor, Increase Mather's Essay for the Recording of Illustrious Providences (Boston, 1684).

## Contexts Within the Source
Closes section 2 of Chapter 4. Thomas also discusses the partisan Mirabilis Annus (1661–2), a fabricated anti-Royalist prodigy compilation that Richard Baxter thought did the Dissenting cause "more harm than good," showing how the genre could be weaponized politically.
