## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that political astrological predictions during the Civil War were taken so seriously partly because they were self-fulfilling — belief in a predicted victory could itself raise morale and affect outcomes — and this made astrologers valuable enough to both sides that governments regularly censored, imprisoned, or courted them.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"When Cromwell's army was in Scotland, Lilly records, a soldier stood with his almanac in his hand, crying out as the troops passed by, 'Lo, hear what Lilly saith; you are in this month promised victory; fight it out, brave boys!'" During the siege of Colchester (1648) Lilly and Booker were sent to encourage the besieging soldiers "assuring them the town would very shortly be surrendered, as indeed it was." Thomas also notes: "It was said that if the King could have bought over Lilly he would have been worth more than half a dozen regiments."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates this within a wider discussion of astrology's self-fulfilling character across domains, including economic forecasts of famine ("Pamphlets which prognosticated famine have been causes of the same," per the Earl of Northampton) and stolen-goods detection functioning as a deterrent, "like the thief-magic of the cunning men."
