## Claim Statement
Thomas documents the royal touch — a sanctioned religious ceremony, initiated by Edward the Confessor and formalized under Henry VII, in which the monarch personally touched sufferers from scrofula ("the King's Evil") and gave each a gold touch-piece — as the one form of magical healing to which official Church and state indulgence was fully extended, reaching its peak popularity under Charles II.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas records that Charles II touched over 90,000 people between 1660–83, with a peak of 8,577 in a single year (1682–83); "near half the nation," by one contemporary's estimate. Included in the Book of Common Prayer from 1634, the ceremony survived until Queen Anne (whose patient Samuel Johnson was touched as an infant); William III refused to perform it. Thomas cites Reginald Scot's contemporary observation that opinion varied on the cure's source: "Some refer [it] to the property of their persons, some to the peculiar gift of God, some to the efficacy of words." He notes the ceremony's overtly political use — Charles I's Royalist propagandists cited his cures at Holmby House as proof against the injustice of his execution, and the Duke of Monmouth performed the ceremony to bolster his own throne claim in 1680 and 1685.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas argues the ceremony's religious trappings (Anglican prayers, consecration with holy oil) were, in popular understanding, "merely a protective framework for a more primitive piece of magic," since most people regarded the healing power as an "innate, mystical quality attaching to his office" rather than dependent on prayer, and the ability to heal became a touchstone of legitimate kingship, later exploited by Jacobite propaganda for the exiled Stuarts.
