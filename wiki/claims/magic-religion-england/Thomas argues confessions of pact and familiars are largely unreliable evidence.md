## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that surviving confessions of diabolical compact, though the object of much historical controversy, cannot generally be taken at face value. Although formal torture was theoretically forbidden in England, techniques such as sleep deprivation (Matthew Hopkins's regular practice), leading interrogation, and psychological pressure produced confessions that conformed to interrogators' preconceived expectations rather than to any independent reality; a "free confession... happeneth very rare and seldom," according to a 1624 commentator.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"witches, long-tortured with watching and fasting, and pinched when but ready to nod, are contented causelessly to accuse themselves, to be eased of present pain." Hopkins's interrogation used "questions so leading as to be clearly in the 'have-you-stopped-beating-your-wife?' category." John Palmer's 1649 confession to turning into a toad shows "men could confess to actions which were obviously impossible."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas nonetheless resists total dismissal: some confessions were unsolicited or contained unconventional details unlikely to have been invented by an interrogator, and he argues a minority of accused witches genuinely believed themselves to have made a symbolic alliance with the Devil out of poverty and despair.
