## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that Protestant theology, having removed the possibility that fairies (like ghosts) were the returning dead, forced fairies into a binary of good or evil spirits — and that most Protestant theologians concluded fairies were in practice devilish. Protestant polemicists further claimed, inaccurately, that fairy-belief was itself a Catholic priestly invention used to defraud the ignorant.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes that Reformation theology "took away the remaining possibility that fairies might be ghosts of the dead. Fairies could only be good or evil spirits, and of the two possibilities the latter was much more likely." He cites Thomas Jackson: "It was pointless trying to distinguish good fairies from bad ones... the Devil was behind them all," noting this "was the official doctrine of most Protestant teachers." He documents the polemical claim that fairies were "conceits... whereby the Papists kept the ignorant in awe" (echoed in Bishop Corbett's poem The Faeryes Farewell), which Thomas judges "grossly unfair, not only because fairy-beliefs were older than Roman Catholicism, but because the medieval Church had itself been hostile to fairy mythology."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes that even sympathizers with fairy-belief, such as Robert Herrick ("Theirs is a mixt religion, part pagan, part papistical") and Goodwin Wharton's fairy-informant Mrs Parish, described the fairy kingdom in explicitly (mock-)Roman Catholic terms, reflecting the Protestant association of fairies with Popery.
