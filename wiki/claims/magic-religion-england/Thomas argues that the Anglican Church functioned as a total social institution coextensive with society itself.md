## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the post-Reformation Church of England was "nothing less than society itself in one of its most important manifestations": mandatory from birth (baptism, compulsory Sunday attendance), reflecting and reinforcing social hierarchy in its seating and communion practices, administering morality through the ecclesiastical courts (marriage, defamation, probate, sexual conduct), financed by compulsory tithes, and doubling as the lowest unit of secular local administration.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes that Anglican seating "reflected the social gradations among the parishioners," that "the humbler members of the congregation stood up and bowed" for gentry, and that God himself was popularly conceived as "the great landlord." A Kentish parson in 1543 taught there were three separate Heavens for different social ranks. The ecclesiastical courts could impose "some humiliating form of public penance or even... total social and economic ostracism" through excommunication. Thomas notes Dissenting congregations recreated the same all-embracing framework of communal supervision, citing agenda items from a Northamptonshire Independent congregation policing members' "conjugal affection."

## Contexts Within the Source
This sets up the chapter's central question: given this seemingly "impregnable position," why did people still resort to magic, astrology, and rival systems of belief? Thomas answers that despite its formidable institutional reach, orthodox religion "did not enjoy a monopoly of popular loyalty" and remained "highly vulnerable to competition from outside."
