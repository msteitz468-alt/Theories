## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that all post-Reformation English theologians taught that nothing could happen without God's permission, and that the Reformation period saw a new, harder insistence on divine sovereignty that went beyond the medieval position (exemplified by Aquinas) which had allowed room for genuine chance or luck alongside providence.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Bishop Thomas Cooper: "That which we call fortune is nothing but the hand of God... Chance or fortune are gods devised by man and made by our ignorance." He quotes John Knox rejecting "Fortune and adventure" as "the words of Paynims." He contrasts Aquinas, who "stressed that the notion of Divine Providence did not exclude the operation of chance or luck," with Bishop Pilkington's flat sixteenth-century assertion "that there was no such thing as chance," and notes Cromwell told Parliament that "fate" was "too paganish a word."

## Contexts Within the Source
Opens section 1 ("The divine origin of misfortune") of Chapter 4, establishing the theological premise from which Thomas derives the "self-confirming" psychology of providence discussed later in the chapter.
