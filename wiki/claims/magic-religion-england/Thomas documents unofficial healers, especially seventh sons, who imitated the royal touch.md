## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that alongside the royal touch, a class of unofficial "strokers" claimed the power to heal by touch — most notably "seventh sons" (or seventh sons of seventh sons), a belief attested in England from the early seventeenth century — and that the government of Charles I repeatedly prosecuted such rivals as a threat to the crown's monopoly on miraculous healing.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Cases include the Frenchman Boisgaudre (1632), who claimed to have cured 200 people and attributed his powers to being the youngest of seven sons, dealing with up to 140 cases a day; James Leverett (1637), an alleged seventh son who declared "God bless; I touch; God heals" in obvious imitation of royal ceremony, and was whipped and sent to Bridewell by the Privy Council; and Richard Gilbert, a Somerset seventh son who began "stroking" at one day old and was holding weekly healing sessions by age five. Thomas also records "the boy of Godlyman" and "the wise boy at Wisall" as similar itinerant child-healers.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas classifies these unofficial healers alongside the royal healer as deriving power from "the accident of birth" rather than personal holiness, contrasting them with figures like Valentine Greatrakes who claimed a directly bestowed, individual divine gift; he notes the belief in seventh-son healing persisted in rural England into the nineteenth century.
