## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the pre-Reformation English Church, like "nearly every primitive religion," offered its adherents not only salvation but a supernatural means of practical control over the earthly environment, and that this magical dimension was reinforced (not merely tolerated) by official liturgy, which contained blessings for houses, cattle, crops, ships, tools, armour, wells, kilns, journeys, duels, and battles.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas cites William Perkins's epigraph: "a great part of it is mere magic," and writes that "the ecclesiastical authorities had developed a comprehensive range of formulae designed to draw down God's practical blessing upon secular activities," basic to which was "the idea of exorcism, the formal conjuring of the devil out of some material object." He notes the consecration formula for holy bread asked God to bless it "so that all who consume it shall receive health of body as well as of soul."

## Contexts Within the Source
Opens Chapter 2. Thomas draws an explicit anthropological parallel to conversions among "the missionaries of more recent times," where converts likewise expect a "new and more powerful magic," framing the medieval English case as one instance of a general pattern rather than an aberration.
