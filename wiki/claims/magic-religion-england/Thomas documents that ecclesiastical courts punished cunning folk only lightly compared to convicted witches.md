## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that, despite fierce rhetorical hostility from theologians, the ecclesiastical courts in practice treated cunning folk ("white witches") with relative leniency — typically penance or excommunication rather than death — and that proposals to impose capital punishment on them repeatedly failed to become law, in marked contrast to the harsher secular statutes against maleficent witchcraft.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes that a reformed canon law commission under Edward VI recommended "the heaviest penalties" for cunning men and their clients but the scheme never took effect, as did Bishop William Alley's 1563 proposal to Convocation for "penal, sharp, yea, capital pains." Instead, church courts treated popular magic "as neither more or less serious than such other routine offences as sabbath-breaking, defamation and fornication." In the diocese of York in the later sixteenth century, about half of sorcery cases were dismissed, often after the accused produced compurgators — as in the case of Agnes More (1583), released because she was "a poor woman and not of ability to bring in any of her neighbours to purge her." Convicted wizards were usually sentenced only to public penance in a white sheet.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas uses this leniency to argue that clerical anti-magic rhetoric far outstripped actual institutional capacity or will to suppress it, and that a cunning man's ultimate fate depended heavily on local popularity and ability to muster supporters (compurgators) rather than on the theological severity of the offence.
