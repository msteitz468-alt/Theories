## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that claims to divine revelation and prophecy during the Interregnum functioned above all as a "validating myth": private individuals, especially the poor and uneducated who lacked access to formal Biblical exegesis or political philosophy, could claim divine sanction for radical political or social programmes that could not otherwise be defended in a battle of learned texts, since "prophecy claimed to supersede the mere written law of God."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas cites the Digger prophet William Everard, who in 1649 pleaded a divine vision as authority for his communist ideas at St George's Hill; the vision attributed to the Winchester minister John Brayne (1649) predicting that "the meanest people that are now despised shall have first the revelation of truth"; and the rise of the Fifth Monarchy Men, an "association of preachers, soldiers and urban lower classes" who sought the rule of the Saints marked by "the abolition of tithes, the reform of the law, the raising of the humble, and the pulling down of the great." He notes the Fifth Monarchists' failed risings under Thomas Venner in 1657 and 1661.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas explicitly compares this millenarian sentiment to the "cargo cults" of modern Melanesia as anthropologically parallel phenomena of expectant deliverance among the deprived, though he argues the Fifth Monarchy Men's hope sprang less from long-endured deprivation than from the unprecedented events of the regicide itself.
