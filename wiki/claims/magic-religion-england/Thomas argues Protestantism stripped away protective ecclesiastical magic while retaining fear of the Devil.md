## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the medieval Catholic Church had offered ordinary people a substantial arsenal of protective magic against witchcraft — holy water, the sign of the cross, consecrated herbs, blessed bells and candles — which Protestant reformers dismissed as empty, superstitious "toys" incapable of frightening the Devil. Yet Protestant theology simultaneously insisted on the Devil's continuing reality and his freedom, by God's inscrutable permission, to torment even the godly. The result was that believers were left with no certain remedy against maleficium beyond prayer, fasting, and passive endurance — counter-magic itself being theologically prohibited — which left legal prosecution of the witch as the only sanctioned "cure."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"All the old mechanical protections were dismissed as empty symbols, lacking any efficacy in themselves." "The new religion made the situation even bleaker by playing down the importance of guardian angels, and denying the intercessionary power of saints, while at the same time placing an unprecedented stress upon the reality of the Devil." "Religion offered no certain immunity; counter-magic was prohibited. The ultimate onus of checking the damage done by witchcraft thus fell on the courts."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas cites the survival of old Catholic protective formulae among ordinary Protestants (crossing oneself, cutting a cross into bread dough, cunning folk using holy oil) as evidence of the emotional gap this left, and quotes Lecky's observation that "if men had been a little less superstitious, the effects of their superstition would have been much more terrible."
