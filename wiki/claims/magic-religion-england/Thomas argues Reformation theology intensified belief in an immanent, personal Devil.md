## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the Reformation, far from weakening belief in Satan, strengthened it. Protestant preachers filled sermons and daily conversation with tales of the Devil's direct intervention in ordinary life; the Elizabethan Communion Service warned communicants that the Devil could literally enter them "as he entered unto Judas"; and children were raised with vivid, terrifying images of demons. The belief that Satan might appear personally to tempt, torment, or seize the guilty was, Thomas argues, a "literal reality for most devout Englishmen," and was even elevated into an argument for the existence of God, since denying the Devil implied denying God.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The Reformation did nothing to weaken this concept; indeed it almost certainly strengthened it... For Englishmen of the Reformation period the Devil was a greater reality than ever – the 'prince and God of this world'." "'If there be a God, as we most steadfastly must believe,' wrote Roger Hutchinson, 'verily there is a Devil also; and if there be a Devil, there is no surer argument... that there is a God.'"

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas draws on Puritan diaries, doctors' case-books (Napier, Lilly), and cautionary pamphlets to show the psychological reality of the Devil for ordinary believers, laying groundwork for his subsequent argument that diabolical compact allegations against witches drew plausibility from this general religious climate.
