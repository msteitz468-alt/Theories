## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that early modern ghosts were never depicted as motiveless or random; they invariably reappeared for a specific purpose, most commonly to denounce undetected crimes (especially murder), enforce unpaid debts and wills, protect the property of the dead, or warn the living. Ghost-belief thus functioned as an extra sanction against wrongdoing, operating precisely where ordinary law-enforcement or detection had failed.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes that ghosts "always had some particular reason for his reappearance... they were no more motiveless than witches; they had an important social role to play." He gives examples: the ghost of Fletcher who haunted his wife's murderous lover until he confessed; John Baldock who gave himself up after being haunted by the ghost of a man he had killed; the pamphlet title Strange and Wonderful news from Lincolnshire describing a ghost that revealed a "Bloody Murther." He notes ghosts "did not compete with ordinary methods of detection or law-enforcement, but reserved his intervention for those cases where an offence would have been undiscoverable by normal means." Other ghosts returned over unpaid debts, misappropriated bequests, or broken promises, such as the woman in Beccles haunted by her husband for failing to carry out his charitable bequests.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates this within his broader argument (echoing his treatment of witchcraft and providence) that early modern supernatural belief systems functioned as informal mechanisms of social control, reinforcing charity, honesty, and respect for the dead's wishes in a society lacking effective policing or record-keeping.
