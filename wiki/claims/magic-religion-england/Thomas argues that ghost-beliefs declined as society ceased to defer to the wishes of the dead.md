## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the essential function of ghost-belief was to enforce reverence for the dead and compliance with their dying wishes, a function only intelligible in a "relatively traditional society" where the living are expected to be governed by ancestors' presumed wishes. The decline of ghost-belief tracked the breakdown of this traditional relationship to the past, accelerated by the Reformation's abolition of Purgatory and the associated destruction of chantries and prayers for the dead, and completed by a more general social readiness to accept innovation unconstrained by ancestral precedent.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas writes: "Ghost-beliefs are thus more likely to be important in a relatively traditional society... where the links with the dead are deliberately preserved." He notes "we live in a society which has cut off its roots in the past" and that "men grew prepared to accept innovation, unmoved by the prospect of their ancestors turning in their graves." He documents the Reformation's destruction of the "2,374" perpetual chantries and the end of masses/obits for the dead, quoting the Catholic William Allen's 1565 lament: "Now there is no blessing of man's memory at all." He also cites a modern French historian: "Life ceased to look to death for its perspective." He links this decline explicitly to his earlier discussion of ancient prophecies losing their legitimating function.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes the change was gradual and incomplete — landed families, strict settlements, and testamentary law preserved some deference to the dead well into the eighteenth century — but concludes that by the eighteenth century "men's actions were less explicitly governed by concern for the wishes of their ancestors or their spiritual welfare," so that when men stopped seeing ghosts "it was because such apparitions were losing their social relevance, not just because they were regarded as intellectually impossible."
