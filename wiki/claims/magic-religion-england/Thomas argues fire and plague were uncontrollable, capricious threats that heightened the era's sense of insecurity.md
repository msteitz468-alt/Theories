## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that fire, alongside plague, was among the greatest and most psychologically disturbing threats of the period precisely because it struck suddenly and unpredictably — "no telling whose turn it might be next" — while contemporaries possessed almost no effective means of prevention, firefighting, or insurance.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes fire-fighting technique was "virtually unchanged... between the Norman Conquest and the death of Elizabeth I," with equipment limited to leather buckets, ladders, and hooks; there was no organized fire insurance until the last two decades of the seventeenth century, and victims relied on Church briefs. He gives figures of 89 fires causing over £1,000 damage each in the last 50 years of the century (total £913,416), excluding the Great Fire of 1666 (£10 million damage, over 13,000 houses, ~100,000 homeless). He quotes a contemporary: "He which at one o'clock was worth five thousand pounds... had not by two o'clock so much as a wooden dish left."

## Contexts Within the Source
Part of the environmental groundwork of Chapter 1; Thomas uses fire, like plague, as an emblem of "the instability of human fortunes" that made explanatory and consolatory belief systems (religious, magical, providential) psychologically necessary.
