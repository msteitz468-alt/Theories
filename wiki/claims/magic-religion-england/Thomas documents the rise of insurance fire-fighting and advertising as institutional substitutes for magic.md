## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that improved communications (newspapers, advertisements for lost goods and missing persons, a penny post from 1680), and the development of marine, fire, and eventually life insurance in the later seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, provided practical alternatives to the traditional services of village cunning folk — reducing recourse to magic for finding stolen goods and cushioning against disaster, even though these institutions developed too late to have caused magic's initial intellectual decline.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas describes the 1657 Office of Public Advice and its weekly bulletins of lost goods and runaway servants, noting: "the coffee-house or newspaper office had become the obvious place to refer problems about lost goods. The need for the cunning man was accordingly reduced." He traces fire insurance from Nicholas Barbon's 1680 Fire Office through the Friendly Society (1684) and Hand-in-Hand (1696), and life insurance from John Graunt's 1662 mortality tables and Edmond Halley's 1693 life table to the Amicable Society (1706) and the Society for Equitable Assurances (1762). He quotes Daniel Defoe (1697): mutual insurance allowed "all the contingencies of life [to] be fenced against… as thieves, floods by land, storms by sea, losses of all sorts, and death itself." He also documents the development of the manual fire-engine (patented 1625), leather hosepipe, and fire-escape.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas presents these developments as evidence of a "new spirit of self-help" running alongside, but not causally prior to, the abandonment of magic — since, as he argues elsewhere in the chapter, magic's decline preceded rather than followed these institutional substitutes.
