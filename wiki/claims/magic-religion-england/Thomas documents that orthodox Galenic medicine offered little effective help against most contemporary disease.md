## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that university-trained physicians, working from humoral theory (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile), were largely unable to diagnose or cure most contemporary diseases, that their services were financially out of reach for most of the population, and that even leading physicians such as Thomas Sydenham privately doubted the value of their own profession.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Robert Burton: "Many diseases they cannot cure at all... a common ague sometimes stumbles them all." He notes the Royal College of Physicians kept its membership deliberately small (a dozen members for a London of 60,000 at its 1518 founding, still only forty Fellows by 1663), that a physician cost about a pound a day, and cites Sydenham's view that "it would have been better for many patients if the art of physic had never been invented," and Hobbes's preference for "an experienced old woman" over "the learnedst but unexperienced physician."

## Contexts Within the Source
Presented as part of Thomas's environmental survey to explain why unorthodox healers — cunning folk, wise women, empirics — retained such prestige, and to establish the practical vacuum that would later be filled by both church magic and its post-Reformation successors.
