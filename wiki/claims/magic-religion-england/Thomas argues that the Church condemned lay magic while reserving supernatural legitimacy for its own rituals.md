## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the medieval and, in modified form, the Protestant Church operated a coherent but self-serving theological logic: any supernatural effect had to derive from God (through sanctioned Church ritual) or an admitted miracle, or else it was diabolical — a framework that let the Church license its own magical remedies (holy water, consecrated bells against storms) while condemning formally identical lay practices performed outside its authority.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas cites Bishop Ralph Baldock of London's 1311 mandate against sorcery for recovering lost goods, foretelling the future, and conjuring spirits with stones or mirrors, and Bishop Edmund Bonner's 1554 assertion that "witches, conjurers, enchanters, and all such like, do work by the operation and aid of the Devil" and commit "so high offence and treason to God, that there can be no greater." He notes Dante's Inferno punished diviners with heads twisted backward. He shows the boundary shifted with theological fashion: for Neoplatonist Robert Fludd the weapon-salve was a legitimate natural remedy, but for his Aristotelian opponent William Foster it was diabolical, since "Aristotle shown that nothing could work at a distance."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas stresses that what counted as "magic" versus legitimate religion or nature depended on the Church's prior, contestable assumptions about what was "natural," meaning the category of the diabolical expanded or contracted according to intellectual fashion (Aristotelian vs. Neoplatonist) as much as according to fixed doctrine.
