## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that the printed astrological almanac, beginning with Andrew Boorde's 1545 prognostication, became one of the most commercially successful genres of early modern English print, with sales that rivalled or exceeded the Bible.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"By 1600 there had been probably over 600 different almanacs published in England... The number of separate almanacs issued in the seventeenth century has been estimated at more than 2,000... William Lilly's annual almanac and prognostication, Merlinus Anglicus, printed 13,500 copies in 1646, 17,000 in 1647, and 18,500 in 1648. By 1649 it was said to be selling nearly 30,000 a year... Not even the Bible sold at this rate." Thomas estimates total seventeenth-century almanac production at 3–4 million copies, possibly an underestimate.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas explains the almanac's appeal: cheapness (typically twopence), regional customization (editions for Aylesbury or Saffron Walden), and practical content (legal terms, land measurement, medical recipes) alongside astrological forecasts and the "Anatomical Man" diagram. He notes almanacs and Bibles alone were exempted from the Stationers' Company's standard print-run limits, reflecting anticipated demand.
