## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the most sustained English opposition to astrology came from Puritans and Presbyterians, and that this hostility was rooted not in superior scientific rationalism but precisely because Calvinist predestination and astrological fatalism were structurally so similar that astrology appeared as a blasphemous secular rival to the doctrine of election.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes John Gadbury's taunt that the fiercest opponent of astrology was "the angry presbyter, who of all men most emulates astrology, and yet hugs an opinion an hundred times more ridiculous than any one principle thereof… viz., that of predestination in the most terrifying sense." He notes the astrologers' textbooks even claimed to indicate "a client's prospects of getting to Heaven," prompting Bishop Carleton's complaint that "we must repair to the astrologer to know who are regenerate in the Church and who are not."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas rejects the "tempting" hypothesis linking Puritan rationalism to opposition to astrology, noting Puritans often conceded astrologers' predictions came true but attributed this to diabolical assistance ("Do astrologers foretell right sometimes?" sneered John Geree. "So do witches"). He documents the Presbyterian-dominated 1652 petition to Parliament to suppress judicial astrology, contrasted with greater Laudian/Arminian tolerance (Laud, Sanderson, Heylyn).
