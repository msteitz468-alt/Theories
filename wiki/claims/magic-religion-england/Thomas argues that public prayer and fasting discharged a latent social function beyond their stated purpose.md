## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that collective prayers, Fast Days, and Days of Humiliation should not be judged only by whether they materially "worked," since they also served sociological "latent functions" — demonstrating communal solidarity in the face of crisis, reaffirming shared ethical standards, and, in a political context, rallying party members and signalling factional positions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes an anthropologist on the religious ceremonies of the Dinka of the Sudan: "instrumental efficacy is not the only kind of efficacy to be derived from their symbolic action. The other kind is achieved in the action itself." He notes that Elizabethan Puritans "zealously attended an organized fast in order to meet fellow members of the godly party," that Long Parliament leaders used Fast Day sermons to signal changes in the party line, and that a Royalist contemporary observed an Army fast usually heralded "some new act of mischief" from Cromwell's soldiers. He also cites Lord Palmerston's 1853 dismissal of a national fast petition against cholera as evidence of the later decline of this social function amid growing class-consciousness.

## Contexts Within the Source
This forms Thomas's concluding methodological point for the chapter: religious ritual is not to be dismissed as either simply "true" magic or simple fraud, but understood through its social functions, a framework he draws explicitly from sociology (citing Durkheim implicitly through the language of collective solidarity) and social anthropology.
