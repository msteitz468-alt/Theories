## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that judicial astrology comprised four main branches: general predictions (forecasting weather, crops, epidemics, politics and war from eclipses and conjunctions), nativities (horoscopes cast at birth, followed up by "annual revolutions"), elections (choosing the right moment for an action), and horary questions (resolving any question by reading the heavens at the exact moment it was posed).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"During the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries there were four main branches to the practice of judicial astrology... First, there were the general predictions... Secondly, there were nativities... The details of the client's nativity were also needed before he could avail himself of the astrologer's third main service, that of making elections... Finally, there were horary questions, the most controversial part of the astrologer's art."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes horary questions were an Arab-era addition developed after Ptolemy, based on the principle that "as the nativity is the time of the birth of the body, the horary question is the time of the birth of the mind." He stresses that a competent practitioner was expected to master all four branches, and that astrological medicine (diagnosing illness from a "decumbiture" figure and a urine sample) was a related fifth specialism.
