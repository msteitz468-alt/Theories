## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the widespread early modern habit of reading comets, monstrous births, earthquakes, and apparitions in the sky as divine "prodigies" or "portents" was not a symptom of scientific ignorance but sprang from "a coherent view of the world as a moral order," and remained consistent with growing knowledge of natural causation — comets did not stop being read as divine warnings once their natural, predictable causes became understood.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes the mathematician William Oughtred carefully recorded an army seen in the sky at Blackheath in 1643, and that Archbishop Laud was "badly shaken" when his portrait fell in 1640. He quotes the antiquarian Ralph Thoresby's 1682 diary: "I am not ignorant that such meteors proceed from natural causes, yet [they] are frequently also the presages of imminent calamities." He states such prodigy-belief "is not universal in primitive societies" and that "a certain amount of scientific awareness may be required before any irregularities can attract attention."

## Contexts Within the Source
Opens section 2 ("Cautionary tales") of Chapter 4; Thomas uses this argument to push back against the assumption that Stuart England's scientific pioneers were embarrassed by or exempt from prodigy-belief.
