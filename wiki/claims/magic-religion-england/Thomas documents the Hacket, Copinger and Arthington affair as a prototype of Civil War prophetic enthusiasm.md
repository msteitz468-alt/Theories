## Claim Statement
Thomas presents the 1591 episode of William Hacket — an illiterate ex-serving-man who claimed to be the Messiah and gathered two Puritan gentlemen, Edmund Copinger and Henry Arthington, as his "prophets of mercy and judgement" — as an early, isolated foretaste of the mass prophetic and messianic enthusiasm that would sweep the sects during the Civil War and Interregnum.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Hacket announced himself King of Europe and forerunner of the Last Judgement on a cart in Cheapside on 16 July 1591, declaring that the Privy Council should be reconstituted and the Queen had forfeited her crown. He was executed; Copinger starved himself to death in gaol; Arthington recanted. Thomas notes an old man later told Richard Baxter that Arthington and Copinger "had been possessed with the spirit of the Grindletonians," the Yorkshire Antinomian sect preceding the Seekers and Quakers, and that Hacket's ceremonial cursing of sinners anticipated Lodowick Muggleton's practice in the 1650s.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates Hacket alongside a "small army of pseudo-Messiahs" under Elizabeth (John Moore, Elizeus Hall, Ralph Durden, Miles Fry) and argues that only the "firm maintenance of social order during the previous century" prevented such figures from triggering a wider millenarian explosion before the breakdown of censorship and social controls during the Interregnum made mass enthusiasm possible.
