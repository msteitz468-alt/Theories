## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the development of statistical and probabilistic reasoning — through the mortality-table work of Graunt, Petty, and Halley and the continental mathematics of Cardan, Fermat, Huygens, Pascal, the Bernoullis, and de Moivre — gave educated Englishmen a new, non-mystical way of understanding chance, so that random misfortune came to be seen as statistically patterned rather than as requiring a supernatural cause.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas notes "it was also in the later seventeenth century that the word 'coincidence,' in the sense of the juxtaposition of causally unrelated events, first appeared." He cites John Arbuthnot's 1692 translation of Huygens on gaming odds, quoting Arbuthnot's claim that a chance event "was merely one whose causes were not known" but that its probability could nonetheless be calculated, even in human affairs such as politics — "a kind of analysis of the quantity of probability in casual events." Thomas concludes: "it is the awareness that they are indeed random which distinguishes us from our ancestors."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas treats this alongside the rise of embryonic economics and sociology as one of the "new technical aids" (broadly conceived) that displaced astrology's explanatory ambitions — the social sciences "took over the explanatory aspirations of astrological inquiry," rejecting the notion that social phenomena were purely random.
