## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that Margaret Murray's influential thesis (from her *Deutsche Mythologie*-inspired reading, elaborated across several books) that accused witches belonged to an organized surviving pagan "old religion" worshipping a "Horned God" in covens of thirteen is "almost totally groundless" for England. Murray relied heavily on continental and Scottish confessions extracted under torture and on a highly selective reading of English pamphlets (notably the confession of John Palmer of St Albans, 1649), while ignoring the systematic trial records assembled by C. L. Ewen. English witchcraft, Thomas concludes, was neither an organized religion nor a cult: malevolent magic was practised by isolated individuals, and there is no reliable evidence of a real "coven" or ritual sabbath.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Her conclusions, at least so far as they relate to England, were almost totally groundless... she never succeeded in showing that the word 'coven' ever meant anything in England other than 'association'." "English witchcraft, therefore, was neither a religion nor an organization."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates Murray's work as historically important — the leading anthropologically-minded alternative to purely "rationalist" dismissal of witch-belief in her day — but insists close scrutiny of English trial records refutes it decisively.
