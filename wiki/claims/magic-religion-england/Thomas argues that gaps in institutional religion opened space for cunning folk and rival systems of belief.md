## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the cumulative gaps documented across chapter 6 — the loss of the confessional, uneven clerical quality and non-residence, low attendance, widespread ignorance, and latent scepticism — together explain why "the hold of organized religion upon the people was never so complete as to leave no room for rival systems of belief," setting up the book's subsequent chapters on magic, astrology, and cunning folk.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas synthesizes the chapter by noting that magic could offer, "less hesitant[ly]," the "supernatural gifts or special access to some occult source of wisdom" that an overstretched, ignorant, or absent clergy could not consistently supply, and that religious indifference, from the churchwarden who "would give money to pull the parish church down" to the money-lender who declared "What pass I for my soul?", reflects a substantial population for whom organized religion carried little practical weight.

## Contexts Within the Source
This functions as the pivot between Thomas's account of orthodox religion's theoretical dominance (chs. 1–6) and his turn, in the following chapters, to the concrete magical practices — healing, thief-detection, divination — that filled the resulting vacuum. Thomas is careful to note the extent of medieval popular religious apathy is itself poorly documented, cautioning against assuming a golden age of universal medieval piety.
