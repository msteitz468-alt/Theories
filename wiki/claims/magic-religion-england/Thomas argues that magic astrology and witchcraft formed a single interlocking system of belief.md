## Claim Statement
Thomas argues, as a synthesizing conclusion, that the various magical beliefs he has examined — astrology, geomancy, palmistry, physiognomy, alchemy, witchcraft-diagnosis, and cunning-folk practice — were not discrete traditions but a single interconnected system, linked both intellectually (astrology providing the theoretical justification for related divinatory arts) and practically (the same practitioners moved fluidly between them).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Thomas quotes Cornelius Agrippa: "All these skills of divination are rooted and grounded upon astrology." He notes geomancy "employed the twelve astrological houses" and was, per its leading textbook, "none other thing but astrology"; alchemy was "mere 'kitchen magic or chimney astrology.'" He documents practitioners of striking versatility: Simon Forman "practised astrology, geomancy, medicine, divination by facial moles, alchemy and conjuring"; William Lilly "also practised conventional medicine, spirit-raising, treasure hunting and the conjuration of angels and fairies." He notes astrologers like Richard Saunders and Joseph Blagrave claimed "astrological diagnosis was the only sure way by which witchcraft could be discovered," dismissing white witches and cunning men as frauds, while other astrologers like John Dee and Christopher Hall actively confirmed witchcraft suspicions. He also traces the overlapping mythology of "planets" and "fairies" as both classified into black, green, and white/blue types by cunning folk such as Anne Baker and John Walsh.

## Contexts Within the Source
This is the opening synthesis of Thomas's concluding chapter, drawing together evidence scattered across the earlier chapters on astrology, witchcraft, cunning folk, and ghosts/fairies to argue for the underlying unity of "magical" belief in the period, while noting astrology alone retained enough theoretical apparatus to claim scientific respectability among some educated practitioners such as John Gadbury.
