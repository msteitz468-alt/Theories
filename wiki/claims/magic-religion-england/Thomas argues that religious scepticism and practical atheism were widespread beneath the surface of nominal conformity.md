## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that, alongside the well-documented aristocratic "atheism" of figures like Christopher Marlowe and Sir Walter Raleigh's circle, there existed a continuous and probably underestimated undercurrent of popular scepticism about core Christian doctrines — the immortality of the soul, the reality of Heaven and Hell, and even the existence of God — stretching from pre-Reformation heresy trials through the Interregnum sects.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Examples span centuries: a 1493 London woman convicted of magic who said she had "a heaven in this earth" and did not care about the next; a 1596 Elizabethan Surrey J.P., William Gardiner, accused of saying "God hath nothing to do with the world since he created it"; the 1635 Durham man Brian Walker who said "I do not believe there is either God or Devil"; and Ranters like Richard Coppin ("There was no Hell, save in man's imagination"). Between 1548 and 1612 at least eight people were burned for anti-Trinitarian beliefs, including the ploughwright Matthew Hamont (Norwich, 1578), who called the New Testament "a mere fable."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas argues the church courts likely captured only the tip of an iceberg of genuine private disbelief, since heresy carried the risk of execution until 1677 and social ostracism thereafter; he cites Lord North's later observation that belief in an afterlife was "very small, especially among the vulgar," and cautiously critiques Durkheimian narratives that romanticize medieval society as uniformly and organically religious.
