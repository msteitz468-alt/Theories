## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that Reginald Scot's *Discoverie of Witchcraft* (1584) established a sceptical framework that changed little for over a century: Scot classified all supposed witches into four types — the innocent falsely accused, the deluded who believed themselves in league with Satan but were harmless, genuine poisoners who did real harm by natural means, and fraudulent cunning folk/impostors who deserved punishment for deceit but not death for supernatural crimes. Scot denied that any accused witch could have made a genuine corporal pact with the Devil or harmed anyone by supernatural means, while still supporting prosecution of frauds.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"All witches, said Scot, came in one of four categories... he was certain that none of them could have made a corporal pact with the Devil or succeeded in harming her neighbours by supernatural means. He was all for the prosecution of the cunning men and wise women. But he was implacably opposed to putting old women to death for being guilty of an impossibility."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes Scot's arguments were "essentially continuing the traditional Protestant onslaught upon the relics of paganism" found in Catholic teaching, drew on the Cleves physician Johan Weyer's medical scepticism, and remained a minority position — regarded by many contemporaries as tantamount to atheism — until reinforced a century later by mechanical philosophy.
