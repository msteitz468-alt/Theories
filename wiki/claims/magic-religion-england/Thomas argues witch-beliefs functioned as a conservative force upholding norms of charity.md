## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that, paradoxically, fear of witchcraft acted as a real deterrent against uncharitable behaviour and thus helped uphold the traditional obligations of neighbourliness at a time other economic forces were eroding them. A reputation for witchcraft could function as a poor woman's "last line of defence," since neighbours who feared her supposed powers gave her food, fuel, or favorable prices they would otherwise have withheld — an effect Thomas compares directly to Evans-Pritchard's observation that Azande witch-belief is "a valuable corrective to uncharitable impulses."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"Witches, it was rightly said, could not harm folk who were liberal to the poor, and the most Christian preservative against witchcraft was to be charitable." Cases include Jennet Device's family in the 1612 Lancashire trial, whom neighbours dared not offend, and Elizabeth Fletcher of Jacobean Yorkshire, whose wealthiest neighbours "unbesought... provided her with fire and meat from their own tables."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas balances this "conservative" function against its opposite: once a reputation for witchcraft was fixed, a suspect could equally be ostracized and denied "the common necessaries of life," so the belief could both enforce and eventually justify the breakdown of charitable norms depending on how firmly suspicion had taken hold.
