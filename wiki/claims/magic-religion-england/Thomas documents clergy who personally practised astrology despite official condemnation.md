## Claim Statement
Thomas documents a persistent tradition of clergymen who themselves practised or defended judicial astrology, most notably Richard Napier, rector of Great Linford — evidence that the official theological condemnation of astrology coexisted with widespread clerical involvement in it.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
Richard Napier, "one of the most fashionable practitioners of astrology and physic of the seventeenth century," said prayers before setting every astrological figure; his knees, John Aubrey reported, were "horny with praying," yet "his voluminous papers show how closely his religion was intertwined with his medicine and astrology and, for that matter, magic and conjuring." Thomas also names William Bredon, vicar of Thornton, "an able astrologer... reluctant to set figures on a Sunday," and quotes John Gadbury's 1656 remark that he could have named "many reverend divines in most counties in England" active in astrology but withheld their names to protect them.

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas notes this clerical involvement extended into the following century (Joshua Childrey, John Butler, Charles Atkinson) and observes that astrologers' case-books record numerous clerical clients seeking advice mainly on their own preferment prospects, undercutting any clean line between orthodox religion and astrological practice.
