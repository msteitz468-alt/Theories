## Claim Statement
Thomas documents that, after the Reformation formally abolished the medieval Catholic ritual of exorcism as empty and unnecessary ("miracles have ceased"), Puritan clergy developed an alternative remedy of fasting and prayer for supposed demonic possession, which by the 1590s had become entangled with factional Church politics. The Puritan exorcist John Darrell's spectacular cures were denounced as fraud by the Arminian faction (Samuel Harsnet, Richard Bancroft), leading to Canon 72 of 1604, which banned unlicensed clerical exorcism and effectively ended the practice within the conforming Church of England.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"The culmination of the controversy was the ruling in Canon 72 of the new Church Canons of 1604 that henceforth no minister... was to attempt... by fasting and prayer, to cast out any devil or devils, under pain of the imputation of imposture." George More, Darrell's ally, argued: "if the Church of England have this power to cast out devils, then the Church of Rome is a false church."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas situates the affair within the broader factional politics of the Elizabethan Church and notes that nearly every one of Darrell's dispossession cases resulted in a witch being blamed for the victim's affliction, linking possession-belief directly to witchcraft accusation.
