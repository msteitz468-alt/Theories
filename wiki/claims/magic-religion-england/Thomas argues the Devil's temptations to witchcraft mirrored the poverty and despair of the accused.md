## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the recorded temptations attributed to the Devil in witch confessions are strikingly modest and pathetic, matching the real desperation of the accused: promises of food, a few shillings, having debts paid, or an end to want, rather than great riches. He interprets witchcraft as, psychologically, "a method of bettering one's condition when all else had failed" — a substitute for impotence — but one distinguished from other forms of magic by its central aim of avenging oneself on hostile neighbors rather than merely gaining material benefit.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"What is so pathetic about most of these temptations is that the bait was so small." Elizabeth Southern was promised two and sixpence which the Devil then failed to hand over, "complain[ing] of the hardness of the times." Nathanael Homes: "'Lowness of condition,' thought... was the first ingredient in the making of a witch... at such times as these, the Devil... approacheth near to them, offering them aid… upon his conditions."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas links material poverty to religious despair as a dual precondition, noting that some of Hopkins's victims described the Devil promising relief from damnation as well as from hunger, and treats the confession psychology as having "subjective reality" even where objectively fantastic.
