## Claim Statement
Thomas argues that the judges and juries who brought English witch-trials to an end were not, for the most part, motivated by outright denial that witchcraft was possible, but by "a heightened sense of the logical difficulty of proving it to be at work in any particular case." The "proofs" relied on — the insensible witch's mark, the animal familiar, the confession — all turned out to be fatally ambiguous (a natural excrescence, a harmless pet, a coerced or delusional statement), and this evidentiary skepticism, rather than a coherent philosophical rejection of the supernatural, was what actually stopped convictions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence
"What influenced them was not a denial of the possibility of witchcraft as such, but a heightened sense of the logical difficulty of proving it to be at work in any particular case." Cites the 1697 remark of a former Scottish Secretary that French courts "never try them now, because... it is impossible to distinguish possession from nature in disorder; and they choose rather to let the guilty escape than to punish the innocent."

## Contexts Within the Source
Thomas contrasts this practical, evidentiary skepticism with the more thoroughgoing philosophical position of writers like Reginald Scot, arguing the latter remained "a self-conscious minority" throughout most of the period and was not what actually ended prosecutions.
