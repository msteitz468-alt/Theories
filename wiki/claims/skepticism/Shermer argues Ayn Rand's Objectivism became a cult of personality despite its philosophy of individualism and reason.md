## Claim Statement

Shermer argues that Ayn Rand's Objectivist movement — a philosophy explicitly built on reason,
individualism, and the rejection of all authority — nonetheless became a textbook cult of personality
through its internal logic that the philosopher's reasoning is final Truth and dissent therefore
proves the dissenter's irrationality. The Objectivist case demonstrates that a cult need not be
religious; any group organized around absolute Truth claims and a charismatic leader can exhibit all
nine cult characteristics Shermer enumerates.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Rand's core philosophy:** Objectivism in four principles (from *Atlas Shrugged*):
1. Metaphysics: Objective Reality
2. Epistemology: Reason
3. Ethics: Self-interest
4. Politics: Capitalism

Shermer accepts much of this — "commitment to reason is admirable" — but identifies one fatal flaw:
"the belief that absolute knowledge and final Truth are attainable through reason."

**The paradox of individuality producing groupthink:** "The last thing a cult leader wants is for
followers to think for themselves." Yet because Objectivism held that anyone who disagreed with Rand
had, by definition, reasoned incorrectly, the philosophy contained the mechanism of its own
cultification. Nathaniel Branden's list of the Collective's implicit premises (from *Judgment Day*):
- "Ayn Rand is the greatest human being who has ever lived."
- "Atlas Shrugged is the greatest human achievement in the history of the world."
- "Ayn Rand … is the supreme arbiter in any issue pertaining to what is rational, moral, or
  appropriate to man's life on earth."
- "No one can be a fully consistent individualist who disagrees with Ayn Rand on any fundamental
  issue."
- "But it is best not to say most of these things explicitly… One must always maintain that one
  arrives at one's beliefs solely by reason."

**Nine characteristics of a cult (Shermer's taxonomy):**
1. Veneration of the leader — to the point of virtual sainthood
2. Inerrancy of the leader — the leader cannot be wrong
3. Omniscience of the leader — pronouncements accepted on all subjects
4. Persuasive techniques — recruitment and belief reinforcement
5. Hidden agendas — true beliefs not disclosed to recruits
6. Deceit — leaders' flaws covered up
7. Financial and/or sexual exploitation — assets invested; sexual relations with followers
8. Absolute truth — leader/group has discovered final knowledge
9. Absolute morality — right and wrong thought and action binding on members and outsiders

**The Rand–Branden affair as illustration:** Rand and Nathaniel Branden (25 years her junior)
conducted a secret affair for years, rationalized by Rand as the logical consequence of the two
greatest intellects on the planet falling in love. When Branden later began an affair with a third
woman, Rand excommunicated him, threatened to destroy his career, and reportedly cursed him with
twenty years of impotence. Her six-page open letter to followers omitted all mention of the actual
offense; the NBI lecture staff condemned Branden "in complete ignorance of what really happened."
A former student raised the question of whether assassination would be "morally appropriate."

**The key flaw is absolute morality, not reason itself:** "The cultic flaw in Rand's philosophy is
not its use of reason … The fallacy is its belief that absolute knowledge and final Truth are
attainable through reason." Morals "do not exist in nature and thus cannot be discovered. In nature
there are only actions… their actions become moral or immoral only when someone else judges them as
such." Absolute morality is the mechanism through which a group becomes dangerous to individual
freedom.

**Two caveats Shermer insists upon:** (1) Criticizing the founder or followers does not negate the
philosophy itself — "components of a philosophy must stand or fall on their own internal consistency
or empirical support." (2) Criticism of part of a philosophy does not gainsay the whole; one may
accept Rand's metaphysics of objective reality and her epistemology of reason while rejecting her
absolute morality.

**What separates science from Objectivism:** "What separates science from all other human activities
is its commitment to the tentative nature of all its conclusions. There are no final answers in
science, only varying degrees of probability." Objectivism's absolute certainty placed it outside
science precisely at the point where the method of inquiry was replaced by the defense of fixed
conclusions.

## What this counters in the wiki

This claim is largely standalone as a case study in cult mechanics, but:

- It implicitly counters any source in the wiki that presents intellectual or philosophical movements
  with absolute truth claims (Objectivism-adjacent or other) as immune to the cult dynamics Shermer
  identifies.
- The nine-characteristic taxonomy applies directly to the anatomy of belief-groups analyzed in
  [[Why people believe weird things (Shermer's thesis)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer says he has read all of Rand's work and accepts much of it. He describes the Library of
Congress survey finding that *Atlas Shrugged* ranked second only to the Bible in readers' lifetime
significance — indicating the genuine philosophical impact of the work. William F. Buckley Jr. is
noted as a prominent critic who admitted he never actually read the book, illustrating that many
attacks on Rand are themselves poorly grounded. Shermer's criticism is surgical: absolute morality
as the fatal flaw, not the entirety of Objectivism.
