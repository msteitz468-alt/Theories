## Claim Statement

Shermer argues that once a fringe claim — specifically Holocaust denial — has entered wide public consciousness, the appropriate response is informed public rebuttal, not silence or suppression. Suppression backfires in at least three ways:

1. **It lets deniers control the narrative on the facts they are right about.** Deniers know that the human-soap story is a myth; deniers know that memory is fallible. If historians keep these acknowledged limitations "off the record," the public learns about them from deniers rather than from historians — which looks like a cover-up and strengthens the denial movement's credibility.

2. **It creates martyrs and validates the conspiracy frame.** The Marco Polo (Japanese magazine) incident: when Jewish organizations successfully pressured advertisers to force the magazine to fold after it published a Holocaust-denial article, deniers used this as proof of Jewish control of the media — exactly the conspiracy narrative they were promoting. Burning Ernst Zündel's Toronto office similarly generated fund-raising appeals and martyr narratives.

3. **It chills legitimate scholarship.** Elizabeth Loftus's work on the fallibility of eyewitness memory is scientifically correct and important; her testimony in the Demjanjuk trial was defensible. But because she was afraid to be used by deniers, she ultimately did not testify. Deniers then found her book anyway and used it.

Shermer's proposed principle: "The government should never, under any conditions, limit the speech of anyone anytime; private organizations should also have the freedom to restrict the speech of anyone anytime within their own institution." But: once a claim is in the public consciousness, the principle of Skeptic magazine applies — "when a fringe group or extraordinary claim has gained widespread public exposure, a proper rebuttal deserves equal public exposure."

The Clarence Darrow lesson from the Scopes trial (quoted by Shermer): once a mechanism of censorship exists, it can be turned against you. "If today you can take a thing like evolution and make it a crime to teach it in the public schools, tomorrow you can make it a crime to teach it in the private schools…"

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From Chapter 12: "Like the Loftus-Demjanjuk story, I heard about these events through the deniers themselves, who take such incidents and use them to prove their point about what 'the Jews' are capable of doing."

On the Donahue experience: Shermer describes how Donahue's approach — repeatedly calling deniers antisemitic rather than engaging their specific claims — allowed David Cole to repeatedly look more credible by staying focused on physical evidence while Donahue made ad hominem arguments.

On why historians keep things "off the record": "Off the record, I do not place much validity in survivors' testimony because their memories are faulty… Off the record, the deniers have identified some things that need further research. In my opinion, trying to keep these things off the record is going to backfire on historians."

Shermer's four reasons not to suppress: they might be completely right; they might be partially right; being completely wrong, their errors help clarify what is true; in science we can never know absolute truth. And: "Being tolerant when you are in the majority means you have a greater chance of being tolerated when you are in the minority."

## What this counters in the wiki

This claim bears directly on how the wiki itself should function. It supports the wiki's protocol of neutral attribution (recording "Smith claims X" rather than either endorsing or suppressing X), because suppression of claims does not make them go away — it removes the rebuttal from public view.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer acknowledges the risk of "dignifying" fringe claims by debating them. His appearance on Donahue was criticized by at least one Holocaust scholar who said it would not have happened without him. He responds that the show would have happened anyway and that his presence, despite the chaos, was preferable to leaving the deniers unchallenged.
