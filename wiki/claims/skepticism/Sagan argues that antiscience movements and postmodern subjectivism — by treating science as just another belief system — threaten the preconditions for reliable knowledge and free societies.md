## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that a cultural tendency to treat science as merely one "belief system" among many —
alongside New Age doctrines, shamanism, postmodern relativism, and politicized pseudoscience (e.g.,
Soviet Lysenkoism, Nazi "Aryan science") — is not a benign philosophical stance but a form of
social danger. When a society loses confidence in the distinction between well-tested and poorly-
tested claims, it becomes vulnerable to charlatans, authoritarian ideologues, and catastrophic
policy errors.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Ch. 14 ("Antiscience")** is Sagan's most systematic treatment. He identifies two main strands:

1. **Political antiscience** — the suppression or distortion of science by state power:
   - *Lysenkoism*: Stalin's endorsement of Trofim Lysenko's rejection of Mendelian genetics, which
     condemned Soviet biology for two generations and contributed to agricultural failure. Lysenko's
     claim that his "dialectically correct" genetics would produce a new crop of winter wheat was
     testable and falsified — but the political machinery prevented the falsification from having
     effect. Hermann Muller was driven out of Moscow for defending classical genetics.
   - *Nazi "Aryan science"*: Johannes Stark's distinction between "Jewish science" (relativity,
     quantum mechanics) and "Aryan science." Hitler: "A new era of the magical explanation of the
     world is rising, an explanation based on will rather than knowledge. There is no truth, in either
     the moral or the scientific sense."
   - *McCarthyism*: Edward Condon's loyalty hearings, in which being "at the forefront of quantum
     mechanics" was treated as evidence of revolutionary political tendencies.

2. **Cultural/postmodern antiscience** — the relativist claim that science is simply a cultural
   narrative with no special claim to truth:
   - Sagan quotes the epigraph to Ch. 14: a New Age summary that "there's no such thing as objective
     truth . . . science itself is irrational or mystical . . . just another faith or belief system or
     myth, with no more justification than any other."
   - He distinguishes questions genuinely susceptible to cultural bias (what is "great" art?) from
     questions where cultural bias in *methodology* does not affect the *results*: quantum mechanics
     predicts spectral lines, semiconductor behavior, and laser properties to high precision in every
     culture. "Mathematics might be prized or ignored, but it is equally true everywhere."
   - The postmodern critique of scientists' biases ("Darwin was motivated by class interest; Kepler
     by religious medievalism") is acknowledged as partly valid but ultimately irrelevant to
     *whether the theories are correct*, since theories must survive scrutiny by investigators with
     opposing biases.

**Ch. 13 ("Obsessed with Reality")** extends the argument to everyday pseudoscience:

- A long catalog of pseudoscientific claims (astrology, Bermuda Triangle, psychic surgery, dowsing,
  ESP, faith healing, channelling) is analyzed with the Kit. The Carlos hoax is the centrepiece: an
  Australian *Sixty Minutes* production proved that a completely fabricated channeller — invented by
  James Randi and performed by a naive actor — attracted media coverage and a near-filled Sydney
  Opera House with no fact-checking whatsoever.
- Sagan's concern is not merely that people are fooled, but that the *practice of critical thinking
  becomes socially devalued* when public discourse is saturated with unchallenged pseudoscience:
  "The Carlos Foundation offered for sale an 'Atlantis crystal'. No parody is too implausible."

He closes with a warning that transcends the New Age context: "Most of these figures are only after
your money. That's the good news. But what worries me is that a Carlos will come along with bigger
fish to fry — attractive, commanding, patriotic, exuding leadership . . . Perhaps he'll be selling
you a war, or a scapegoat . . . accompanied by warnings about the dangers of scepticism."

## What this counters in the wiki

- **[[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]]**: Icke's framework
  explicitly treats mainstream science as a control mechanism and positions his own synthesis as a
  higher form of knowledge. Sagan's argument is that this rhetorical move — treating the demand for
  evidence as evidence of co-option — is structurally identical to what Lysenko's defenders and
  the Nazi "Aryan science" proponents did.
- **[[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]** and related conspiracy frameworks: These treat the
  "established framework" of official knowledge as so thoroughly corrupted that alternative,
  unverifiable sources (insider leaks, classified documents) become the primary evidence. Sagan's
  antiscience chapter is a direct argument that abandoning falsifiability in favor of insider
  narrative is how catastrophic errors — Lysenkoism, Nazi racial biology — become institutionally
  entrenched.
- **[[Chariots of the Gods (von Däniken)]]** and **[[Fingerprints of the Gods (Hancock)]]**:
  Both treat mainstream archaeology and geology as institutionally blind to anomalies. Sagan's
  response is that science is an open institution that "invites every one to come, study its
  methods, and suggest improvement" (Morris Cohen's formulation) — unlike non-rational
  authoritarianism, which "regards the demand for the redemption of its paper as a disloyal lack of
  faith."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan is not a defender of the scientific establishment as such. He acknowledges:
- Scientists have aided racist and eugenicist programs; they built nuclear and chemical weapons.
- Individual scientists exhibit observational selection and wishful thinking.
- Scientific institutions can suppress dissent and protect influential figures.

His argument is about the *method*, not the *practitioners*: the error-correcting machinery of
science — controlled experiments, peer review, independent replication, quantitative prediction —
works despite the human weaknesses of the people who use it. "Science is a collective enterprise
with the error-correction machinery often running smoothly."

He also acknowledges the genuine value of folk knowledge and pre-scientific empirical traditions
(quinine from Amazonian bark, local weather prediction) as long as they are held with appropriate
scope: "Ethnomedicine, yes; astrophysics, no."
