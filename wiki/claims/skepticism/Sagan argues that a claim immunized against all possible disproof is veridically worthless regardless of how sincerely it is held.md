## Claim Statement

Sagan argues, via the "Dragon in My Garage" parable, that any claim defended by auxiliary escape
clauses that make it permanently immune to testing carries zero verifiable content. Sincerity,
emotional intensity, and the inability of a skeptic to *disprove* the claim are all irrelevant:
"Your inability to invalidate my hypothesis is not at all the same thing as proving it true."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The parable (Ch. 10) is Sagan's central logical argument and the conceptual linchpin of the book's
skeptical project. The dragon that floats, breathes heatless fire, and cannot be spray-painted
because she is incorporeal is functionally identical to no dragon at all. Each auxiliary explanation
preserves the claim against a proposed test — but collectively, they make the claim say nothing
about the world.

Sagan extends this to the alien abduction context explicitly:

> "If any powers whatever may be ascribed to the aliens — because their technology is so advanced —
> then we can account for any discrepancy, inconsistency or implausibility. For instance, one
> academic UFOlogist suggests that both the aliens and the abductees are rendered invisible during
> the abduction (although not to each other); that's why more of the neighbours haven't noticed.
> Such 'explanations' can explain anything, and therefore in fact nothing."

He also applies it to claims about higher dimensions (John Mack's appeal to beings from "another
dimension"), the invisibility of entities to witnesses, and the convenient failure of all implants
to have detectable alien-origin signatures.

The parable is explicitly paired with [[The Baloney Detection Kit (Sagan)|the Baloney Detection
Kit's]] falsifiability criterion and the SETI analogy: radio astronomers who find an anomalous
signal do not announce alien contact precisely because they insist on tests that could falsify the
hypothesis before they assert it.

## What this counters in the wiki

- **[[Passport to Magonia: From Folklore to Flying Saucers (Vallée, 1969)]]**: Vallée's
  "control system" and "Esoteric Intervention" hypotheses are, in Sagan's framework, structured to
  be permanently unverifiable: the controlling intelligence is posited to be able to adjust its
  behavior to prevent any definitive detection. Sagan's argument implies this makes such hypotheses
  veridically empty regardless of how sophisticated they are.
- **[[Communion (Strieber)]]**: Strieber's argument that the visitors may come from "another
  dimension" or exist outside normal space-time is the same move Sagan critiques in Mack —
  invoking physics language to reach a conclusion that conveniently evades all physical testing.
- **[[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]]**: Icke's framework of
  shapeshifting reptilians existing at a different frequency similarly builds in non-detectability as
  a design feature. Sagan's criterion applies: a claim that cannot be wrong tells us nothing about
  the world.
- **[[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]**: Cooper's government-alien treaty framework explains
  the absence of public evidence by positing a perfect, decades-long cover-up. This is the dragon's
  invisible fire: the very absence of evidence is reframed as evidence of the cover-up's perfection.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan distinguishes unfalsifiable claims from hypotheses that are merely hard to test with current
technology. He accepts that some ideas in science are temporarily untestable. The criterion applies
to claims that are *in principle* immune: where every conceivable observation has been declared
compatible with the hypothesis in advance.
