## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that scientists cannot disclaim responsibility for how their discoveries are used. When the technological application of scientific work is foreseeable, scientists who accept funding to pursue it become morally implicated in the outcome — for good or ill. He argues further that scientists have a special prophetic duty to alert the public to dangers arising from science, and that the ethical responsibility of scientists must be "extraordinarily high, unprecedentedly high" given the power of modern technology to harm at planetary scale.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 16, Sagan uses the career of Edward Teller as a sustained case study: a physicist who devoted decades to justifying the hydrogen bomb he helped create, who exaggerated the promise of Star Wars to President Reagan, and who consistently prioritized weapons development over arms control — with consequences Sagan argues may have "imperilled life on this planet" more than the work of any other individual. Sagan also praises counter-examples: Oppenheimer's conscience, the ten thousand scientists who publicly pledged not to work on Star Wars, Linus Pauling's campaign for the Limited Test Ban Treaty.

Sagan quotes the Australian philosopher John Passmore: a scientist who accepts government funds knowing his work will be useful to them "is subject, properly subject, to praise or blame in relation to any innovations which flow from his work."

He concludes: "The price of moral ambiguity is now too high. For this reason... the ethical responsibility of scientists must also be high, extraordinarily high, unprecedentedly high."

In Chapter 24, he extends this to a broader argument: that scientists who remain silent about misapplications of science are analogous to the Spanish Inquisition handing heretics to the secular arm while denying responsibility for what follows.

## What this counters in the wiki

- [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]: Cooper's narrative of government secrecy and unaccountable power largely ignores the internal ethical dissent within science (Oppenheimer, Szilard, Pauling, the anti-SDI scientists). Sagan's account provides an alternative to conspiracy framing: documented, named scientists who publicly resisted governmental misuse of their work through legitimate channels.
- [[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]]: Icke's account of elite technocratic control treats scientists as passive instruments of power. Sagan documents both the complicity Icke alleges and the substantial tradition of scientific resistance to it — a nuance absent from Icke's framework.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan acknowledges that moral ambiguity pervades all human endeavour, not just science, and that "perhaps it is not so much scientists as people who are morally ambiguous." He notes that even Enrico Fermi — who believed policy was "not his business" — co-authored a document calling the hydrogen bomb "evil." He does not claim scientists are uniquely culpable, only that their power makes the stakes uniquely high.
