## Claim Statement

Sagan argues, using the case of James Clerk Maxwell, that the most consequential discoveries in science have consistently come from curiosity-driven, undirected basic research rather than from government-directed applied programmes. Maxwell was not trying to invent radio or television — he was following an aesthetic intuition about the symmetry of his equations in a vacuum. The radio, television, radar, and telecommunications infrastructure of the modern world traces to that single "nerdish" aesthetic judgement. Sagan argues that cutting basic research funding is therefore "like eating the seed corn" — mortgaging the future for short-term savings.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Chapter 23 tells the Maxwell story from childhood curiosity through his discovery that electricity and magnetism unify into light. Sagan uses the "Westminster Project" thought experiment: even if Queen Victoria in 1860 had assigned Britain's leading scientists and engineers a million pounds to invent radio and television, they would almost certainly have failed — because the underlying physics (Maxwell's equations) had not yet been worked out, and no one could have predicted the path to them. The parallel with SETI (cancelled by Congress in 1993 after one year) is explicit: the programme cost "about one attack helicopter per year."

He documents the decline of US basic research funding, the loss of industrial research, and the pressure on the National Science Foundation to move toward applied technology — and argues these trends, if continued, are self-defeating.

## What this counters in the wiki

This claim does not directly counter any specific alternative source in the wiki, but it provides Sagan's argument for why conspiratorial framings of scientific institutions as primarily serving elite control are incomplete: science's most transformative outputs (radio, penicillin, DNA structure) have come from precisely the "useless" basic research that would be most easily cut by any short-term-thinking authority, whether corporate or governmental.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan concedes that scientists have a corresponding obligation to explain their work clearly to non-expert audiences, and criticizes the SSC proposal specifically for failing to make its case to Congress. He also acknowledges that industrial and applied research can produce genuine innovation — he simply argues that it depends on a foundation of basic research that market forces alone will not sustain.
