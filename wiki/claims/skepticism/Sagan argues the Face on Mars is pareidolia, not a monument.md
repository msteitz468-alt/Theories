## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that the kilometre-wide hill in Mars's Cydonia region, nicknamed "The Face," is a
natural geological formation misidentified as a humanoid monument due to the brain's innate tendency
to recognise faces in random patterns (pareidolia). The same cognitive bias, Sagan argues, underlies
the Martian canal myth, tabloid claims about faces on other planets, and ancient-astronaut
interpretations of geological symmetry.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sagan establishes the neurological mechanism first: face-recognition is hard-wired from infancy; its
extreme efficiency produces false positives — faces in moon craters, geological formations, cloud
patterns, tortillas. With 100,000 close-up photographs of Mars available, finding one patch that
resembles a face is statistically unsurprising.

On the Cydonia Face specifically:

- A strategically placed "nostril" is a black dot caused by missing radio-transmission data, not a
  surface feature.
- The best images show one side lit, the other in shadow; enhanced shadow analysis yields "something
  rather unfacelike." The Face is "at best half a face."
- The surrounding terrain contains similar blocky mesas that no one reads as sculpture — a fact
  the monument hypothesis does not explain.
- Speculations about a nearby "city," astronomical alignments 500,000 years ago, and a NASA cover-up
  are unsupported extrapolations from a single ambiguous Viking image.
- The Martian pyramid analogy: miniature pyramid-forms (dreikanters) are produced by wind erosion in
  Antarctica and in Mars's intense duststorms; no builders required.

Sagan's precedent-setting case is the Martian canals (1877 onward): hundreds of professionally
mapped geometrically regular canals that were shown by Mariner 9 to be entirely non-existent. "Even
a succession of professional scientists can make serious, even profound errors in pattern
recognition… especially where the implications of what we think we are seeing seem to be profound."

He explicitly recommends high-resolution orbital photography: the hypothesis is *falsifiable* — one
thing that distinguishes it from most pseudoscience.

## What this counters in the wiki

This claim directly rebuts any use of the Cydonia Face as evidence for ancient astronauts or a
lost Martian civilisation. It counters the interpretive framework of:

- [[Chariots of the Gods (von Däniken)]] — von Däniken-adjacent reasoning that anomalous landforms
  prove ancient extraterrestrial construction
- [[Fingerprints of the Gods (Hancock)]] — lost-civilisation claims that rely on geometric
  "impossibilities" as proof

See also concept page [[Pareidolia and the Face on Mars (Sagan)]].

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan does not dismiss the Face categorically without better data. He explicitly states: "I might be
wrong. It's hard to be sure about a world we've seen so little of in extreme close-up." He calls for
more missions and higher-resolution imaging. His position is that the hypothesis is improbable but
scientifically testable — unlike most UFO/abduction claims.
