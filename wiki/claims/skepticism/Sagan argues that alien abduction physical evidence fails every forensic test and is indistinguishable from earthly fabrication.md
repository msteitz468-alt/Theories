## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that despite decades of claims, not one piece of physical evidence attributed to alien
abduction has withstood independent scientific scrutiny. Every proposed artifact — body scars, soil
disturbance, implants, alleged hybrid pregnancies, autopsy footage — is either (a) consistent with
mundane human causes, (b) untested by peer-reviewed science, or (c) demonstrated to be a hoax.
The pattern, he argues, is exactly what the "invisible dragon" parable predicts: the claim is
permanently insulated from evidential falsification.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sagan's case in Ch. 10 is a systematic inventory of the failure of physical evidence:

- **Scars and "scoop marks"**: The most common evidence. Sagan notes well-documented psychiatric
  conditions in which people scar themselves without conscious awareness, and that the scars occur
  only in body locations the claimant cannot easily examine (and thus cannot easily show to
  independent physicians).
- **Implants**: The case of Richard Price, cited as "the best case" by abduction proponents. After
  26 years, an alleged alien implant fell out. MIT and Massachusetts General Hospital scientists
  identified it as "collagen formed by the body at sites of inflammation plus cotton fibres from
  Price's underpants."
- **Soil disturbance**: "4,400 physical trace cases from 65 countries" have been claimed. Sagan
  points out that none has been published in a peer-reviewed physics, chemistry, metallurgy, or
  soil-science journal demonstrating that the traces could not have been generated by humans.
- **Alien autopsy footage (1995)**: The Murdoch-television "autopsy" is shown to have an unfalsified
  film leader (the actual film magazine was not submitted for dating — only a cut strip that could
  have been taken from a 1947 newsreel), making the dating claim worthless.
- **Missing fetuses / hybrid pregnancies**: No anomalous sonogram, amniocentesis result, or
  miscarriage has ever been medically documented, despite the large number of claims.
- **No technology retrieved**: "There is no retrieval of cunning machinery far beyond current
  technology. No abductee has filched a page from the captain's logbook . . . or come back with
  detailed and verifiable scientific information not hitherto available on Earth."

Sagan compares the evidentiary standard to the Marian apparition tradition — "a pathetic few 'signs'
of evidence — a stone or a footprint and never anything unfakeable." He draws on the forensic
standard used in criminal investigation: evidence that would "convince a jury that hasn't already
made up its mind."

The SETI comparison is instructive: professional radio astronomers who detect an anomalous signal
do not announce alien contact — they demand independent confirmation from multiple observatories,
systematic elimination of all terrestrial explanations, and repeatable detection. Abduction
advocates, Sagan argues, apply none of these standards.

## What this counters in the wiki

- **[[The UFO Experience (Hynek)]]**: Hynek's classification system treats physical trace cases as
  among the most compelling evidence for an unidentified phenomenon. Sagan argues the traces are
  systematically unanalyzed and therefore carry no weight.
- **[[Missing Time (Hopkins)]]**: Hopkins grounds his case partly in the convergence of testimony
  and the presence of scars. Sagan's analysis applies directly: scar evidence is non-diagnostic and
  testimony produced under hypnosis is unreliable.
- **[[Passport to Magonia: From Folklore to Flying Saucers (Vallée, 1969)]]**: Vallée's catalogue
  of physical trace cases and "landing reports" is referenced by Sagan as the kind of data that
  "4,400 physical trace cases" statistics come from. Without peer-reviewed analysis, Sagan argues,
  the catalogue proves only that many people have made claims, not that anything anomalous occurred.
- **[[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]**: Cooper asserts crashed alien hardware in government
  hands. Sagan's standard for what such evidence would actually require — peer-reviewed materials
  science showing unknown alloys or isotopic compositions — applies directly: none has appeared.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan explicitly does not rule out the possibility that a future, well-attested, physically
documented alien contact event could arise. "Perhaps some day there will be a UFO or alien abduction
case that is well attested, accompanied by compelling physical evidence." He also acknowledges the
genuine intellectual excitement that such a discovery would generate — "discoveries like this are
what scientists live for" — which is why the absence of such evidence from the scientific literature
is, for him, significant rather than trivial.
