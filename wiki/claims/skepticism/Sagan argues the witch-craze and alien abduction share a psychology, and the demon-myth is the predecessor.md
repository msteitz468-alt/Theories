## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that the medieval witch-craze and the modern alien abduction phenomenon are expressions
of the same underlying psychological process: vivid, culturally shaped hallucinations of sexual
assault and abduction by aerial non-human beings, interpreted through the dominant cosmology of each
era. The demon tradition, with its incubi, succubi, wall-penetrating beings from the upper air, and
multi-generational reproductive interference, contains virtually every element of the modern alien
abduction template — without the spaceship.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sagan traces the demon tradition from Greek philosophy (Plato, Aristotle) through Augustine's
demonisation of all pagan spirits and the medieval consensus: demons inhabit the upper air, pass
through walls, sexually assault sleepers, collect and redistribute human semen, and produce hybrid
offspring. Aquinas and Bonaventura are cited on demonic artificial insemination.

The nightmare connection is explicit: *mare* (Old English) meant the incubus that sat on the
sleeper's chest — exactly the sleep-paralysis pressure sensation also reported by abductees.

The Cydonian parallel the chapter develops:

- 1645 Cornwall: Anne Jefferies found crumpled on the floor, recalls "half a dozen little men,"
  paralysis, transport to "a castle in the air," sexual assault, return. The beings were called
  fairies. Had she grown up in a UFO culture, her account would be indistinguishable from a modern
  abduction report.
- Victorian spiritualism produced "spirits" performing the same psychological role.
- The content of encounters tracks the Zeitgeist: contactees in the 1950s warned of nuclear war;
  1990s abductees warn of AIDS and environmental destruction. Sagan asks: "Can it be that aliens know
  only as much as those who report their presence?"

Whitley Strieber is quoted within Ch. 7 describing his own visitors: "Whatever was there seemed so
monstrously ugly, so filthy and dark and sinister. Of course they were demons. They had to be…" Sagan
notes that Strieber later became open to the possibility of dreams or hallucinations.

Sagan's synthesis: "Is it possible that people in all times and places occasionally experience vivid,
realistic hallucinations, often with sexual content, about abduction by strange, telepathic, aerial
creatures who ooze through walls, with the details filled in by the prevailing cultural idioms?"

## What this counters in the wiki

This is Sagan's most direct engagement with the demonological tradition as an explanatory category.
It counters:

- [[Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures (Bane)]] — which documents the demon
  tradition as a genuine cross-cultural phenomenon; Sagan's argument is that the phenomenon is
  genuine (as a neurological and psychological event) but the supernatural interpretation is not
- [[Communion (Strieber)]] — Strieber's own demon ambivalence is cited as evidence that the

- [[Whitley Strieber]] — his account is placed in a long lineage of culturally shaped hallucination

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan grants that alien abduction enthusiasts have a ready counter-argument: aliens have *always*
been visiting us, and what we called demons or fairies in earlier eras were the same aliens. He takes
this seriously enough to rebut it: (a) no flying saucer reports exist before 1947; (b) no world
religion uses saucer iconography; (c) a genetic programme allegedly begun millennia ago by vastly
superior beings should be complete by now. He finds the cultural-hallucination explanation more
parsimonious.

He also acknowledges a residue of intellectual humility: Gibbon's observation that "the most
curious, or the most credulous, among the pagans" were persuaded of supernatural realities suggests
that gullibility is a human constant, not confined to primitive ages.
