## Claim Statement

Shermer argues that Holocaust denial and evolution denial are structurally isomorphic pseudoscholarship movements. Both are ideologically motivated, both use the same five rhetorical moves, and both follow the same developmental trajectory from a fringe of true believers toward a "respectable" mainstream face.

The three-part parallel (from Chapter 9, used to introduce both Part 3 and Part 4):

1. Both movements find errors in opponents' scholarship and imply those conclusions are entirely wrong — as if historians and scientists never make ordinary mistakes.
2. Both movements quote prominent figures (Gould, Mayr; Bauer, Hilberg) out of context to make it appear those figures support the denier's position.
3. Both movements treat genuine internal scholarly debate as evidence that the field is "coming apart at the seams" — when in fact internal debate is how healthy fields advance.

The irony Shermer notes: Holocaust deniers can at least be partially right on minor factual points (the human soap story is a myth; the figure for Auschwitz deaths has been revised downward from 4 million to ~1 million). Evolution deniers cannot even be partially right — one supernatural intervention voids all natural law.

The developmental parallel (Chapter 13, comparing creationism and Holocaust denial as social movements):
1. Early: wide diversity of thought; extreme fringe; little mainstream success.
2. Middle: attempt to shed radical fringe connections and claim "scientific/scholarly" credentials (creation-science in the 1970s; founding of IHR in 1978).
3. Later: emphasis shifts from anti-establishment rhetoric to positive claims and equal-time arguments.
4. Finally: First Amendment / academic-freedom strategy to demand access to public platforms (state equal-time laws; Bradley Smith's college newspaper ads).

And the signature move across both: "Just one proof!" — creationists demand just one transitional fossil; deniers demand just one proof that Jews were killed in gas chambers. Shermer's response in both cases: no single piece of evidence proves anything; it is the convergence that matters.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From Chapter 9 (end): "The similarities between the two in their methods of reasoning are startling." Shermer then lists the three parallel moves verbatim.

From Chapter 14 (opening): "Their fallacies of reasoning are eerily similar to those of other fringe groups, such as creationists." Followed by the five-method checklist.

On the fringe-to-mainstream trajectory (Chapter 13): Shermer traces IHR's deliberate shift from Carto-era "ranting, racialist-populist pamphleteering" to "serious, nonpartisan revisionist scholarship" — echoing creation-science's shift from biblical literalism to "abrupt-appearance theory."

From Chapter 14 on post-hoc rationalization: "He is relying on what might be called post hoc rationalization — after-the-fact reasoning to justify contrary evidence — and then on demanding that the Holocaust historian disprove each of his rationalizations. But the convergence of positive evidence supporting the Holocaust means that the historian has already met the burden of proof."

## What this counters in the wiki

- This claim is the methodological bridge between Parts 3 and 4 of the book. It is Shermer's argument that the same pattern of bad reasoning recurs across different subject-matter domains.
- The "just one proof" move and the post hoc rationalization pattern are also visible in conspiracy-theory literature such as [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] and [[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]].
- See [[Science and skeptical inquiry as a candle in the dark (Sagan)]] for the Sagan-side treatment of how pseudoscience insulates itself from refutation.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer does not argue that all claims rejected by the mainstream are pseudoscience. He explicitly states that "outsiders can make worthwhile contributions" (Gell-Mann) and that historical knowledge is revisable. The distinction he draws is between genuine revision based on new evidence and post hoc rationalization driven by prior ideological commitment.
