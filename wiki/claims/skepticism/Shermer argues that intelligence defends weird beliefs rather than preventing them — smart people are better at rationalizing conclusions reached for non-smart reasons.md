## Claim Statement

Shermer argues that the standard skeptical assumption — that higher intelligence and education
protect against pseudoscience — is empirically weak and theoretically backwards. Intelligence is
orthogonal to belief: it is statistically unrelated to *what* people believe, but it powerfully
predicts *how well* they defend what they already believe. Smart people arrived at their weird
beliefs through the same non-rational channels as everyone else (emotion, culture, upbringing,
hope), but their analytical training gives them better tools to rationalise and insulate those
beliefs against challenge. "Smart people believe weird things because they are skilled at
defending beliefs they arrived at for non-smart reasons."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The Easy Answer**: Shermer states his thesis twice in Chapter 18 for emphasis. The mechanism:
most people "come to our beliefs for a variety of reasons having little to do with empirical
evidence and logical reasoning… [including] genetic predispositions, parental predilections,
sibling influences, peer pressures, educational experiences, and life impressions." Smart people
are *better* at sorting incoming data through those prior filters and at producing sophisticated
rationalizations of the sorted result.

**Intelligence is orthogonal**: Research finds modest and inconsistent correlations. The New Age
movement has increased paranormal belief specifically among "those with higher intelligence, higher
socioeconomic status, and higher educational levels" (Vyse, 1997). Science education shows
essentially no correlation with scepticism of pseudoscience (Walker, Hoekstra & Vogl, 2001).
Magicians report that scientists and Mensa members are *easier* to fool than average audiences,
because their confidence in their own analytical ability makes them more susceptible to misdirection.

**Intellectual Attribution Bias**: Shermer and Sulloway's survey found people are nearly 9:1 more
likely to attribute their own belief in God to *rational* reasons (design argument, personal
experience) while attributing *other people's* belief to *emotional* reasons (comfort, upbringing).
Smart people, having more elaborate intellectual justifications available, exploit this bias most
effectively. "Smart people, because they are more intelligent and better educated, are better able
to give intellectual reasons justifying their beliefs that they arrived at for nonintellectual
reasons."

**Confirmation Bias**: Shermer cites Nickerson (1998): the confirmation bias may account for "a
significant fraction of the disputes, altercations, and misunderstandings that occur among
individuals, groups, and nations." David Perkins (1981) found a *positive* correlation between
intelligence and ability to justify beliefs, and a *negative* correlation between intelligence
and openness to alternative positions. Smart people generate more sophisticated reasons to dismiss
hostile evidence.

**Case studies from the academy**:
- Frank Tipler (Tulane cosmologist, peer of Hawking): used 122 pages of mathematics to "prove"
  God and bodily resurrection from physics — Kip Thorne found each step sound, the leaps unsound.
- John Mack (Harvard psychiatry, Pulitzer Prize): concluded alien abductions represent genuine
  alien intelligence after "suspending" his empirical framework; Harvard investigated but could not
  revoke tenure.
- David Jacobs (Temple University history professor): admits his alien abduction evidence is
  "anecdotal and thus nonfalsifiable" but believes anyway.
- Courtney Brown (Emory University political science): grounds alien belief in "Scientific Remote
  Viewing," requires Emory affiliation to be suppressed from his public alien discussions.

The marketing pattern: PhD credentials are prominently displayed on book covers precisely because
the claim is weird. The "smart man endorses weird belief" endorsement is the deliberate strategy.

## What this counters in the wiki

- **[[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]** and **[[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]]**: The "smart people" thesis directly explains the social spread of conspiracy literature among educated audiences. The internal consistency and comprehensive explanatory power of grand conspiracy frameworks mimic the markers of rigorous intelligence — which activates the intellectual attribution bias ("I believe this for rational reasons") and confirmation bias ("everything I read confirms it") in exactly the readers most capable of elaborating and defending the framework further.
- **[[Communion (Strieber)]]**: Strieber is an articulate, literary writer, not a credulous fool. Shermer's framework predicts that intelligence does not prevent adoption of the alien-contact narrative — it enables a more elaborate, more convincing construction of it (as Strieber's subsequent books amply demonstrate).
- **[[The Demon-Haunted World (Sagan)]]**: Companion diagnosis and remedy. Sagan's Baloney Detection Kit (see [[The Baloney Detection Kit (Sagan)]]) is the corrective practice that Shermer's thesis identifies as necessary: the formalised habits (independent replication, falsifiability, Occam's Razor, logical fallacy recognition) that counteract the intellectual attribution and confirmation biases that intelligence alone cannot override.
- **[[What techniques of mass social control do the sources allege?]]**: The mechanism by which intelligent operators construct persuasive intellectual frameworks to justify pre-formed conclusions is the same mechanism Shermer uses to explain how ideological systems (political, religious, conspiratorial) achieve and maintain hold over educated adherents.
- **[[Alien abduction as misperception, hallucination, and cultural narrative (Sagan)]]**: Sagan and Shermer converge: the academic endorsement of abduction narratives by Mack and Jacobs is explained by Shermer's smart-people thesis as intelligence in service of emotionally compelling pre-formed belief, not in service of evidence-following.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer acknowledges some genuine correlations: higher education is associated with *lower* belief
in possession by the devil, astrology, and haunted houses (Gallup, 2001). He also notes that
occasionally fringe beliefs turn out to be correct — being smart does make one better at
generating *good* defences of correct fringe positions as well as wrong ones. His thesis is
strictly about the *process*, not the outcome: intelligence is deployed after the belief is
formed, not before.
