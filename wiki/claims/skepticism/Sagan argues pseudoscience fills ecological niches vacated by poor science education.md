## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that pseudoscience is not simply error or deception but a structural consequence of
scientific illiteracy: where genuine science fails to reach people, pseudoscience fills the gap,
exploiting the same appetite for wonder and cosmic significance that science could satisfy. The
dynamic obeys a Gresham's Law — bad science drives out good — because pseudoscience is cheaper to
produce, easier to present, and more emotionally satisfying.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The "Mr Buckley" framing (Ch. 1): A curious, intelligent cab driver knows detailed lore about
Atlantis, channelling, Nostradamus, and crystals but has never heard of plate tectonics, molecular
biology, or quantum indeterminacy. Sagan's diagnosis: the culture failed to deliver genuine science
and pseudoscience filled the vacancy. "Sparse and poor popularizations of science abandon ecological
niches that pseudoscience promptly fills."

The mechanism:

- Pseudoscience is easier to contrive than science because "distracting confrontations with reality —
  where we cannot control the outcome of the comparison — are more readily avoided."
- Standards of evidence are relaxed; emotional payoffs are high.
- Pseudoscience "speaks to powerful emotional needs that science often leaves unfulfilled": it offers
  magical powers, spiritual hunger, cures, cosmic centrality, survival after death, and the
  reassurance that "someone older, smarter and wiser is looking out for us."
- A survey Sagan cites suggests 95% of Americans are scientifically illiterate — the same fraction
  as African Americans who were illiterate before the Civil War. "Anything like 95% illiteracy is
  extremely serious."

The global dimension: Sagan cites pseudoscience surges in post-Communist Russia and China as
supporting evidence. When critical thinking is suppressed and then released, "a host of confident or
charismatic claims — especially those that told us what we wanted to hear — gained a vast following."
Russia is "awash in UFOs, poltergeists, faith healers, quack medicines."

The democratic stakes: Sagan argues (Ch. 2) that a civilisation whose key decisions depend on
science and technology but whose citizens don't understand either is "a prescription for disaster" —
"a combustible mixture of ignorance and power is going to blow up in our faces."

## What this counters in the wiki

This is the foundational sociological argument underlying Sagan's specific rebuttals. It implicitly
frames the whole wiki corpus of alternative sources as products of the ecological niche that
scientific illiteracy creates. Most directly relevant to:

- [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] — a product of precisely the "emotionally satisfying
  claims that tell us what we want to hear" dynamic Sagan describes
- [[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]] — similarly

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan is careful to distinguish pseudoscience from honest error in science, and to avoid simple
condescension toward believers. He attributes the problem primarily to institutional and cultural
failure, not to individual stupidity. He also acknowledges that even smart, educated people are drawn
to pseudoscience: Transcendental Meditation "has attracted a large number of accomplished people,
some with advanced degrees in physics or engineering. These are not doctrines for nitwits. Something
else is going on."
