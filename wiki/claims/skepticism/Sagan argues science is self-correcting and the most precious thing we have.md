## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that science — meaning the method of provisional, evidence-tested, self-correcting
inquiry, not merely its products — is the single most precious thing humanity has, more valuable than
any particular body of knowledge. He contrasts this with pseudoscience, which mimics science's
surface features while being immune to correction.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Ch. 1 Sagan opens with Einstein's epigraph: "All our science, measured against reality, is
primitive and childlike — and yet it is the most precious thing we have." He then frames the book
around a chance encounter with a cab driver (called "Mr Buckley") who is fascinated by Atlantis,
channelling, and crystals but knows nothing of plate tectonics, DNA, or the age of the universe. The
driver is not stupid; the culture has failed him by preferring "pretence and confusion" to genuine
science.

In Ch. 2, Sagan enumerates science's self-correcting mechanisms:

- Every scientific paper carries an error bar — "a quiet but insistent reminder that no knowledge is
  complete or perfect."
- Science has "built-in, error-correcting machinery at its very heart": hypotheses are falsifiable,
  results are reproducible, criticism is the coin of the realm.
- Science repeatedly awards its highest honours to those who *disprove* established beliefs (example:
  Einstein's Special Relativity overturning Newtonian absolute rest; Taylor-Hulse pulsars testing and
  confirming General Relativity).
- Unlike religion or pseudoscience, science institutionalises "Mistrust arguments from authority."
- Ann Druyan is quoted: "Science is forever whispering in our ears, 'Remember, you're very new at
  this. You might be mistaken. You've been wrong before.'"

Sagan draws a direct parallel between the values of science and democracy: both require free exchange
of ideas, diversity of opinion, vigorous debate, and accountability of claims to evidence.

He identifies pseudoscience as science's opposite: hypotheses are framed to be *invulnerable* to
disproof; when they fail, conspiracies are deduced; practitioners "are defensive and wary."

## What this counters in the wiki

This claim functions as the foundational warrant for Sagan's skeptical positions throughout. It
implicitly counters claims made from authority or revelation rather than evidence across the entire
wiki corpus, but most directly the epistemological stance of:

- [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] — where suppressed documents and insider testimony replace
  peer review
- [[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]] — where intuition and
  synthesis of heterodox sources substitute for falsifiable claims

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan does not present science as morally pure or technically infallible. He acknowledges that
scientists built 60,000 nuclear weapons, exposed citizens to radiation, participated in Tuskegee, and
have produced thalidomide, nerve gas, and ecological destruction. "The sword of science is
double-edged." His argument is comparative: science remains preferable to alternatives because it has
error-correcting machinery that alternatives lack.

He also notes that the scientific community can be slow, tribal, and resistant to heterodox findings
— just not permanently so.
