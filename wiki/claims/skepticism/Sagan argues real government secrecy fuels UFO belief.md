## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that genuine, legitimate government secrecy around military technology — spy balloons,
reconnaissance aircraft, missile re-entry tests, air defence probing operations — provides a
non-conspiratorial explanation for both the phenomenon of unexplained aerial sightings and for the
pattern of official denial that UFO researchers interpret as a cover-up of alien contact.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sagan frames the argument in Ch. 5 ("Spoofing and Secrecy"):

**Balloon programmes:** In the 1950s the Air Force conducted extensive high-altitude balloon
missions (Skyhook, Mogul, Moby Dick, Genetrix, Grandson) carrying reconnaissance cameras and
intelligence sensors. Their payloads were classified even when the balloons themselves were known.
High-altitude balloons can appear saucer-shaped from the ground; their erratic movement when caught
by wind gusts mimics the "impossible manoeuvres" attributed to UFOs. A Navy official (Urner Lidell)
told Sagan he believed all UFO reports were due to military balloons.

**Roswell:** Sagan discusses the July 1947 Roswell incident. A subsequently declassified Air Force
investigation (1994) identifies the debris as from Project Mogul, a classified long-range acoustic
detection system aimed at Soviet nuclear tests. The "alien hieroglyphics" recalled by witnesses were
decorative icons on radar reflectors made by New York toy and novelty companies. The Air Force's own
files show no heightened communications traffic that would have accompanied a genuine alien crash.

**Spoofing operations:** The Air Force routinely used high-performance aircraft to probe adversary
air defences. When a spoofing run generated visual and radar sightings inside US territory, the Air
Force could truthfully deny knowledge of the incident while having bureaucratic reasons to stay
silent. This silence looks like a cover-up without being one about aliens.

**NSA and FOIA:** FOIA requests to the NSA about UFOs return pages heavily redacted — not because
of aliens, but because releasing context around "a UFO was spotted" would identify which nation's
military communications the NSA was intercepting and with what methods. Sagan draws a parallel to an
FOIA request about Elvis Presley yielding similarly blacked-out NSA material.

**MJ-12 documents:** Sagan reviews the supposed 1947 Truman executive order establishing a committee
to investigate crashed saucers (the "Majestic 12" documents, delivered mysteriously to a UFO-oriented
film producer in 1984). He applies the "provenance" test used for forged artwork: documents of great
import with a five-century hole in provenance should be treated with maximum suspicion. The Air Force
judged MJ-12 bogus; UFO analyst Philip Klass found lexicographic and typographic inconsistencies.
Sagan compares it to the medieval Donation of Constantine — a politically convenient forgery.

**Project Bluebook critique:** Sagan served on the Air Force Scientific Advisory Board committee
that reviewed Project Bluebook and found it "lackadaisical and dismissive" — sloppy bureaucratic
management, not a rigorous investigation and not a cover-up of alien evidence.

His conclusion: the government has probably classified some UFO-related files, but the classified
material concerns conventional military hardware, not extraterrestrial technology. The Cold War is
over; he calls for declassification.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan explicitly does not dismiss the possibility that some residual, genuinely anomalous cases
remain unexplained. He says he is "perfectly prepared to believe that at least some UFO reports and
analyses, and perhaps voluminous files, have been made inaccessible to the public." His argument is
not that there is no secrecy, but that the *content* of what is secret is classified military
hardware, not alien spacecraft. He also acknowledges that a more serious, scientifically rigorous UFO
study than Bluebook "is even likely" — for military intelligence reasons.
