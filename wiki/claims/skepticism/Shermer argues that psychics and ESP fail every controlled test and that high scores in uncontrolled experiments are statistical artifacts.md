## Claim Statement

Shermer argues that when psychic and ESP claims are subjected to controlled experimental conditions,
they consistently fail to perform above chance. Apparent successes in uncontrolled settings are fully
explained by the normal distribution of outcomes: in any large group given a chance-level test, some
will score high by pure probability. Believers misidentify these statistical outliers as evidence of
genuine psi ability, committing the representativeness and coincidence fallacies.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The Edgar Cayce / A.R.E. demonstration (Ch. 4):** Shermer visits the Association for Research
and Enlightenment (A.R.E.) in Virginia Beach, founded to preserve Edgar Cayce's psychic readings.
The organization runs an ESP experiment using standard Zener cards (five symbols; chance = 5/25).

Key observations from the experiment:

- Thirty-five participants ran two trials of 25 cards.
- In trial 1: three people scored 2 (below chance), three scored 8 (above chance), twenty-nine
  scored between 3 and 7.
- In trial 2: different people scored the outliers (1, 2, 9 correct) — not the same individuals
  who scored high or low in trial 1.
- The instructor defined scores of 3–7 as "chance," above 7 as "evidence of ESP," and below 3 as
  "negative ESP" — a post-hoc classification with no predictive value.

Shermer's statistical rebuttal: the results exactly match the expected bell curve for a random
process. "Statistics tells us that given a large enough group, there should be someone who will score
fairly high. Believers in psychic power tend to focus on the results of the most deviant subjects
and tout them as proof of the power." On a television show reaching millions of viewers, statistical
deviants at 3 or 4 standard deviations above the mean will always appear — entirely by chance.

**Cayce's track record:** Martin Gardner and James Randi independently document Cayce's failures
(patients who died before his readings arrived; diagnoses in osteopathic language borrowed from the
osteopath present during his trances; cures that were "prescriptions from a medieval herbalist").
Randi notes Cayce's reliance on qualifying language: "I feel that…" and "perhaps" — hedges against
accountability.

**Cytotoxic blood-testing analogy (Ch. 1):** Shermer notes that during Race Across America
preparation, he and his teammates submitted one man's blood under several names for food-allergy
testing; each sample returned with different allergies. This is an accessible demonstration that
even commercially marketed tests can produce random noise.

**The telephone "synchronicity" follow-up:** A woman at the A.R.E. cites going to call her friend
just as her friend calls her as proof of psychic communication. Shermer asks how many times she
called and the friend did not call; the woman realizes she only remembers the hits. "I only remember
the times these events happen, and I forget all those others." This is the representativeness
fallacy (#14) in action. Her conclusion, however, is that "psychic power works sometimes but not
others" — a classic unfalsifiable rationalization (fallacy #11).

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer concedes that the A.R.E. experiment he witnessed was informal and not a rigorous scientific
study. His argument is statistical and methodological, not based on an exhaustive literature review
of parapsychology. He acknowledges (Ch. 5) that the mind has genuine altered states capable of
unusual experiences, but argues this is a neurological and psychological fact, not a psi fact.
