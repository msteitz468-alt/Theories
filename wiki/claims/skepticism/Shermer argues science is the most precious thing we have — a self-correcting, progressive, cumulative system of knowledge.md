## Claim Statement

Shermer argues that science is uniquely progressive among human traditions — not morally superior,
but demonstrably cumulative in the precise sense that useful knowledge is retained and nonuseful
knowledge is discarded through external testing. This makes it categorically different from
pseudoscience, religion, and art, which do not operate by this mechanism. The claim closes with
Einstein's aphorism that science is "primitive and childlike" measured against reality — "and yet it
is the most precious thing we have." Shermer adopts this phrase as his own chapter title, making a
deliberate echo of Carl Sagan's similar formulations.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Defining progressive:** Shermer takes care to strip the word of moral loading. "Scientific
progress is the cumulative growth of a system of knowledge over time, in which useful features are
retained and nonuseful features are abandoned, based on the rejection or confirmation of testable
knowledge." By this definition, science and technology are the *only* cultural traditions that are
progressive in an actual, definable manner. "Pseudoscientists do not correct the errors of their
predecessors; they perpetuate them."

**Paradigm shifts retain the good:** Einstein on replacing Newton — "Creating a new theory is not
like destroying an old barn and erecting a skyscraper… it is rather like climbing a mountain, gaining
new and wider views." Darwin replaced special creation but retained Linnaean classification,
comparative anatomy, descriptive geology. Change is cumulative, not destructive.

**Pirsig's Paradox resolved:** The chapter addresses the internalist-externalist debate in
philosophy of science (are scientific laws "in nature" or "in the mind"?). Shermer resolves it: a
scientific law describes "a regularly repeating action open to rejection or confirmation" — the
description is in the mind, but the repeating action is in nature, confirmed by external testing.
Ghosts lack any such external confirmation. The difference between ghosts and scientific laws is
real and significant.

**Pseudoscience vs. science:** "Pseudoscience [claims] are presented so that they appear scientific
even though they lack supporting evidence and plausibility." Alien abduction claims are pseudoscience
not merely because evidence is lacking but because they are highly implausible (thousands of people
beamed aboard spacecraft undetected). SETI is not pseudoscience because it is plausible even without
current evidence.

**Pseudohistory vs. history:** The Afrocentric claim that Aristotle stole his philosophy from the
Library of Alexandria is pseudohistorical because the Library was built after Aristotle's death —
a checkable historical fact. Distinguishing history from pseudohistory uses the same logic as
distinguishing science from pseudoscience: does the claim survive external validation?

**The Einstein close:** "One thing I have learned in a long life: that all our science, measured
against reality, is primitive and childlike — and yet it is the most precious thing we have." Shermer
adopts this as the chapter title, linking to Sagan's near-identical framing in *The Demon-Haunted
World*.

## What this counters in the wiki

- [[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]] and [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] — both assume that official science is a conspiracy and that alternative,
  untestable frameworks are equally or more valid. Shermer's criterion of cumulative external
  validation disqualifies such frameworks as pseudoscience regardless of their internal coherence.
- [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]] — the anthropic/designer argument, treated there
  as evidence, is addressed by Shermer's category of the "construct" (a non-testable statement to
  account for observations) as distinct from a theory.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer explicitly concedes that science is "double-edged" — it produced the atomic bomb alongside
medical advances, and may yet cause the extinction it helps to understand. He does not claim science
is morally superior or that scientifically advanced societies are happier. "Whether or not a culture
pursues science and technology does not make one culture better than another or one way of life more
moral than another." His claim is epistemic, not civilizational.
