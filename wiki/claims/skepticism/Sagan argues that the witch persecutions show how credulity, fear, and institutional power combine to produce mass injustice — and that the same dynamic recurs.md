## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that the European witch mania of the fifteenth to seventeenth centuries is not a historical aberration but a case study in how any society can systematically persecute the innocent when it combines: (1) popular credulity and superstition; (2) institutional structures that reward accusation and punish skepticism; (3) the absence of procedural protections against coerced confession; and (4) the suppression of dissent. He argues that many of the specific protections in the US Bill of Rights — trial by jury, no self-incrimination, due process, freedom of speech and press, separation of church and state — are legible as direct responses to the abuses documented in witch trials. He also argues that the same pattern has recurred in Stalinist show trials, McCarthyism, and other modern episodes, and will recur again unless citizens understand how it works.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Chapter 24 devotes extensive space to the Jesuit priest Friedrich von Spee's 1631 whistle-blowing account *Cautio Criminalis*, from which Sagan quotes at length. Von Spee's 38-point analysis shows how the logic of witch prosecution was structurally self-sealing: if the accused showed fear, that proved guilt; if she showed no fear, that also proved guilt (witches are bold). Confession under torture was called "voluntary." Denials were disregarded. Accusers naming further names were believed; those who protested the system were themselves suspected.

Sagan writes: "If we're absolutely sure that our beliefs are right, and those of others wrong; that we are motivated by good, and others by evil; that the King of the Universe speaks to us... then the witch mania will recur in its infinite variations down to the time of the last man."

He documents the spread of the Soviet practice of rewriting history and manufacturing confessions as a modern analogue, and notes that "with critical thinking in decline, restructuring societal memories even without much attention from the secret police seems possible" in the age of television.

## What this counters in the wiki

- [[Encyclopedia of Demons in World Religions and Cultures (Bane)]]: Bane's encyclopedic treatment of demonic entities across cultures treats demon belief primarily as religious/folkloric data. Sagan's chapter places the same European demonology in a political and judicial context: the demons people believed in resulted in the torture and execution of tens of thousands of innocent people. The belief system itself is documented as the mechanism of mass murder.
- [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] and [[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]]: Both sources use a witch-hunt logic of their own — identifying a hidden malevolent power and assigning guilt by association with conspiracy symbols. Sagan's witch-craze analysis is the structural counterpoint: the same logic that identified witches (any behavior can be reinterpreted as confirming guilt; denials prove obstinacy; accusers are rewarded) is legible in conspiratorial frameworks.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan credits the courageous dissenters who opposed the witch mania — von Spee, Reginald Scot, Alonzo Salazar de Frias — as "heroes of our species." He attributes the eventual end of witch trials to their protests, the spread of Enlightenment ideas, the danger the mania posed to the emerging merchant class, and the extension of accusations to privileged classes. He does not argue that all accusations of wrongdoing are witch-hunts; he argues the specific epistemic structure (unfalsifiable guilt, rewarded accusation, punished skepticism) is the diagnostic feature.
