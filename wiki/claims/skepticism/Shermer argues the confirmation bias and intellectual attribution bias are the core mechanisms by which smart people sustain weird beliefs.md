## Claim Statement

Shermer argues that two cognitive biases — the **confirmation bias** and the **intellectual
attribution bias** — are the primary engines by which smart people sustain and elaborate weird
beliefs. Both biases are *amplified* by intelligence: higher analytical ability produces more
sophisticated dismissal of disconfirming evidence and more elaborate intellectual justifications
of pre-formed conclusions. Francis Bacon identified the confirmation bias in 1620; Shermer
applies it specifically to explain why debunking smart believers is harder than debunking
credulous ones.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Confirmation Bias** (Chapter 18, "Smart Biases in Defending Weird Beliefs"):

Shermer opens with Bacon (1620): "The human understanding when it has once adopted an opinion…
draws all things else to support and agree with it. And though there be a greater number and
weight of instances to be found on the other side, yet these it either neglects and despises, or
else by some distinction sets aside and rejects."

Citing Nickerson (1998), Shermer argues the confirmation bias "would have to be among the
candidates" for the single most problematic aspect of human reasoning, potentially accounting
for "a significant fraction of the disputes, altercations, and misunderstandings that occur
among individuals, groups, and nations."

Key experimental evidence assembled by Shermer:
- Sherman & Kunda (1989): students shown evidence contradicting a deeply held belief tended to
  "attenuate the validity" of that evidence and "accentuate" supporting evidence.
- Kuhn (1989, 1994): children and adults exposed to inconsistent evidence for preferred theories
  "either failed to acknowledge discrepant evidence or attended to it in a selective, distorting
  manner." In a later murder trial study, subjects composed a preferred narrative first and then
  sorted evidence to fit it — the more committed to a single story, the more *confident* in
  their conclusion.
- Darley & Gross (1983): the same child's test results were rated above grade level by subjects
  told the child was from a high socioeconomic background, and below grade level by subjects told
  the child was from a low background — identical data, confirmation-filtered by expectation.
- Even Eddington's famous 1919 confirmation of Einstein's general relativity was, historians
  found, a case of "Eddington could only claim to have confirmed Einstein because he used
  Einstein's derivations in deciding what his observations really were" — observation and
  prediction circularly confirming each other.

Smart people are specifically better at the *sophisticated dismissal* of hostile evidence:
generating principled-sounding methodological objections, finding edge cases, citing
counter-authorities, reframing the question — all of which close the belief off to correction.
"The more intelligent the better" at this. Numerology illustrates the mechanism: "you can start
off with the number you seek and try to find some relationship that ends in that number, or… you
crunch through the numbers and see what pops out." Gardner: "how easy it is to work over an
undigested mass of data and emerge with a pattern" — and the more intelligent the analyst, the
more plausible-looking the pattern.

**Intellectual Attribution Bias** (Chapter 18):

Shermer and Sulloway's survey found that people attribute their own belief in God to rational
reasons (design argument, personal experience of God) at nearly 9:1 odds over attributing *other
people's* belief to rational reasons — for others, emotional reasons (comfort, upbringing) are
assigned. Chi-square significance: p < .0001. Smart people, with more elaborate rational
justifications ready to hand, exploit this bias most effectively:

"Smart people, because they are more intelligent and better educated, are better able to give
intellectual reasons justifying their beliefs that they arrived at for nonintellectual reasons.
Yet smart people, like everyone else, recognize that emotional needs and being raised to believe
something are how most of us most of the time come to our beliefs."

The two biases interact: the intellectual attribution bias supplies the *motivation* (I must
have rational grounds for my belief — I always do, unlike others), while the confirmation bias
supplies the *mechanism* (I will find and curate those rational grounds while dismissing the rest).

## What this counters in the wiki

- **[[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]]** and **[[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]]**: Both works depend on readers applying confirmation bias at scale — noticing confirming connections across vast bodies of heterogeneous material while treating contradictions as disinformation, cover-up, or agent misdirection. The intellectual attribution bias then allows readers to experience this selection as a rational, evidence-following process ("I have done the research") rather than an emotional one. Shermer's two-bias framework is the psychological mechanism behind the subjective experience of "waking up" to a conspiracy worldview.
- **[[Alien abduction as misperception, hallucination, and cultural narrative (Sagan)]]**: Sagan and Shermer converge on the same point from different angles: the confirmation bias explains why abduction researchers like John Mack find the experiences "compelling" — they have adopted the believer's narrative framework, within which all ambiguous evidence is sorted toward confirmation and all disconfirmation is reframed as limitation of current methodology.
- **[[What techniques of mass social control do the sources allege?]]**: The confirmation and intellectual attribution biases are also the mechanisms that ideological systems — religious, political, conspiratorial — exploit to self-reinforce. Whether or not intentional "mass social control" exists as alleged, Shermer's framework explains how belief systems achieve closure against disconfirmation in individual minds.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer notes that science contains a partial corrective to the confirmation bias: independent
replication by others who may *not* share the original investigator's prior commitment. This is
what "sets science apart from all other ways of knowing." But even within science, as the
Eddington case shows, the confirmation bias operates — it is merely *more likely* to be caught
and corrected in a community with adversarial peer review. Outside that community, in
pseudoscience or conspiracy research, no such correction mechanism exists.
