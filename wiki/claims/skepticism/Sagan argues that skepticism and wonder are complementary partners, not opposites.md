## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that science depends on holding two seemingly contradictory attitudes in productive tension: absolute openness to new ideas (wonder), and the most ruthlessly skeptical scrutiny of all ideas (criticism). These are not opposites but partners. A scientist who possesses only one without the other is either a dogmatic cynic who misses genuine discoveries, or a credulous enthusiast who cannot distinguish truth from nonsense.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In Chapter 17, Sagan writes: "At the heart of science is an essential balance between two seemingly contradictory attitudes — an openness to new ideas, no matter how bizarre or counterintuitive, and the most ruthlessly sceptical scrutiny of all ideas, old and new. This is how deep truths are winnowed from deep nonsense."

He uses quantum mechanics and special relativity as examples: claims that "time slows as I walk" or that "matter and antimatter are created from nothing" sound absurd and would be dismissed by a pure skeptic, yet they are verified physics. Conversely, accepting every counterintuitive idea without scrutiny leads to believing astrology and levitation.

Sagan explicitly criticizes the CSICOP skeptical movement for its polarizing "Us v. Them" attitude, noting that compassionate engagement with pseudoscience believers would be more effective than contemptuous debunking.

Both skepticism and wonder are called **skills that must be cultivated**: "Their harmonious marriage within the mind of every schoolchild ought to be a principal goal of public education."

## What this counters in the wiki

This claim directly counters the epistemological frameworks in sources that treat the non-rational, experiential, or supernatural as straightforwardly valid alternatives to scientific analysis:

- [[Communion (Strieber)]] and [[Missing Time (Hopkins)]]: Strieber's and Hopkins's accounts treat anomalous experiences as self-evidencing. Sagan's framework argues that wonder at strange experiences is legitimate, but must be paired with the demand for evidence before concluding that the experience corresponds to an external reality.
- [[Passport to Magonia: From Folklore to Flying Saucers (Vallée, 1969)]]: Vallée demonstrates wonder without fully applying Sagan's standard of skeptical scrutiny to his own hypothesis of a control system.
- [[Behold a Pale Horse (Cooper, 1991)]] and [[Everything You Need to Know But Have Never Been Told (Icke, 2018)]]: Both treat conspiratorial intuition as a substitute for evidence. Sagan's claim is the direct methodological counterpoint: wonder at anomalies is healthy; concluding that the anomalies confirm a pre-existing theory without rigorous falsifiability tests is the failure mode.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan acknowledges that skeptics can themselves fail: he admits to having heard "that unpleasant tone in my own voice." He also grants that roughly one per cent of ideas that "smell like pseudoscience" may turn out to be right, and he names three ESP claims he thinks deserve serious study. He explicitly refuses to sign a CSICOP manifesto against astrology because he found its tone "authoritarian" rather than scientific.
