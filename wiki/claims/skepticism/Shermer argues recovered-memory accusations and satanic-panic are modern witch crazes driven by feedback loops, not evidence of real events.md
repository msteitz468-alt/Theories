## Claim Statement

Shermer argues that the Satanic panic of the 1980s and the recovered-memory movement of the 1990s
are structurally identical to medieval witch crazes: self-organizing social feedback loops in which
initial accusations generate more accusations through rumor, media amplification, and therapeutic
suggestion, reaching a critical peak before reversing as the accused begin to fight back. Neither
movement produced evidence for the underlying claims (widespread Satanic cult abuse; millions of
repressed abuse memories). Both are "epidemics of accusation," not epidemics of actual events.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The witch-craze template:** Shermer identifies eight structural features shared by medieval and
modern witch crazes:
1. Victims tend to be women, the poor, the marginalized.
2. Sex or sexual abuse is typically involved.
3. Accusation = guilt.
4. Denial = further proof of guilt.
5. Once claims become known, similar claims proliferate.
6. A peak of accusation in which almost no one is above suspicion.
7. The pendulum reverses as the innocent fight back legally.
8. The movement fades; proponents shift to the margins.

**Satanic panic as moral panic:** Citing Richardson, Best, and Bromley's *The Satanism Scare*,
Shermer argues the evidence for widespread Satanic cults is "virtually nonexistent." The movement
served "talk-show hosts, book publishers, anti-cult groups, fundamentalists, and certain religious
groups" who all profited from the claims. Police departments spent tax dollars on training workshops
on "combating the satanist menace." The driving force, per La Fontaine's 1994 UK government study:
evangelical Christians and "children pressured or coached by their mothers."

**Recovered-memory movement:** Bass and Davis's *The Courage to Heal* (1988, 750,000 copies) stated
the foundational error: "If you think you were abused and your life shows the symptoms, then you
were." This converts symptom into proof, eliminating falsification. The implied scale is impossible:
if one-third to one-half of women were sexually abused, "about 85–100 million Americans [are]
involved in sexual abuse — impossible."

**Therapeutic generation of false memories:** The mechanism: therapist assigns survivor books and
support-group videos → "recovered memories" of abuse emerge after weeks of suggestive questioning,
hypnosis, visualization, or sodium amytal → father / grandfather / neighbor named → confrontation
and family rupture. Memories absent at the outset of therapy appear after sustained suggestion.

**Feedback loop reversal:** By the mid-1990s the loop had reversed:
- Gary Ramona won $500,000 against two therapists who helped his daughter fabricate memories
  (oral sex with family dog, etc.), with loss of his $400,000-a-year career.
- Vynnette Hamanne won $2.7 million against psychiatrist Diane Bay Humenansky, who "discovered"
  100 personalities and Satanic ritual abuse featuring "dead babies served buffet-style."
- George Franklin's 1991 first-degree murder conviction — based solely on a 20-year recovered
  memory — was overturned in 1995 after the defense showed the "memory" could have been
  constructed from newspaper accounts.
- East Wenatchee, Washington (1995): Detective Robert Perez drove a local witch craze of sexual
  abuse accusations against poor families; one woman was charged with 3,200 acts of abuse; one
  man charged with 12 sexual acts in a day "which he admitted was impossible even when he was
  a teenager." When accused families hired private attorneys, all charges were dropped.

**The Phantom Gasser of Mattoon (1944)** — a perfect compressed two-week witch craze — opens the
chapter as the controlling analogy: mass hysteria sparked by one newspaper story, expanded by
further stories, ending in police dismissal of "wild imaginations."

**Chaos/complexity framing:** Shermer models the witch craze as a self-organizing system driven by
feedback loops: accusation → denial (denial = proof) → more accusation → critical mass → collapse.
Medieval data from Macfarlane (1970) on England 1560–1620 support the model. Trevor-Roper's
*European Witch-Craze* documents how "the more evidence was pressed, the worse the conclusions it
yielded" — sabbat frequency rising from once a week to almost every day as the loop accelerated.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer explicitly acknowledges that childhood sexual abuse is "real and tragically more frequent
than any of us like to think." His argument is not that abuse does not occur but that the recovered-
memory mechanism is generating false accusations at a rate that cannot reflect actual events. He
warns that the legal backlash may cause genuine sex offenders to go free "in the inevitable backlash
against the panic." The existence of real abuse makes the witch-craze diagnosis more, not less,
important to get right.
