## Claim Statement

Shermer argues that the demarcation between science and non-science (pseudoscience, religion, ideology) is not a philosophical nicety but a practical necessity with legal, educational, and civic stakes. The 1987 Edwards v. Aguillard Supreme Court case forced the American scientific community to articulate this demarcation concisely for the first time in a document with public force: the amicus curiae brief signed by 72 Nobel laureates, 17 state academies of science, and 7 scientific organizations.

From that brief, Shermer extracts a working definition of science: "Science is devoted to formulating and testing naturalistic explanations for natural phenomena. It is a process for systematically collecting and recording data about the physical world, then categorizing and studying the collected data in an effort to infer the principles of nature that best explain the observed phenomena."

The five essential characteristics of science (from McLean v. Arkansas, 1982): (1) guided by natural law; (2) explanatory by reference to natural law; (3) testable against the empirical world; (4) conclusions are tentative; (5) falsifiable.

Creation-science fails all five because it requires the inerrancy of scripture prior to any evidence: "No geological difficulties, real or imagined, can be allowed to take precedence over the clear statements and necessary inferences of Scripture." This is the inverse of science's openness to disproof.

Shermer emphasizes that this definition was politically negotiated — "made politically, not philosophically" — but was sufficient to swing the decisive vote in a 7-to-2 decision. The definition is not the final word in philosophy of science; it is a pragmatic threshold.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From the Nobel laureates' amicus brief (Chapter 11): "An explanatory principle that by its nature cannot be tested is outside the realm of science. Science is not equipped to evaluate supernatural explanations for our observations; without passing judgment on the truth or falsity of supernatural explanations, science leaves their consideration to the domain of religious faith."

Murray Gell-Mann on creation-science: "They've just scratched out 'religion' and in its place put 'science.' [Like the Monty Python fish license sketch — the petitioner just crossed out 'dog' and wrote in 'cat.']"

Gell-Mann on why the community unified: "It's not so much that we were being attacked from the outside, since outsiders can make worthwhile contributions. It's that these people were talking utter nonsense."

On theories vs. facts (from the brief): "Facts are the properties of natural phenomena… theories are explanatory ideas about those facts. An explanatory principle is not to be confused with the data it seeks to explain."

Also from the brief: "Even the most robust and reliable theory… is tentative. A scientific theory is forever subject to reexamination and — as in the case of Ptolemaic astronomy — may ultimately be rejected after centuries of viability."

Shermer's own taxonomy of three science-religion models (Chapter 10): same-worlds, separate-worlds (Darwin, Gould), conflicting-worlds (atheists and creationists). He implicitly favors the separate-worlds model.

## What this counters in the wiki

This demarcation argument is the methodological backbone against which any alternative or fringe claim in the wiki can be evaluated. It applies to:
- Fringe historical claims that rely on "just one proof" strategies (see [[Holocaust denial as pseudohistory (Shermer)]]).
- Anthropic / intelligent-design arguments presented as scientific (see [[The Forbidden Universe (Picknett & Prince)]]).
- The broader argument that the scientific establishment is a gatekeeping religion — directly answered by Shermer: to call evolutionary biology a religion is "to so broaden the definition of religion as to make it totally meaningless."

See also [[Science and skeptical inquiry as a candle in the dark (Sagan)]] for the companion argument that science's self-correcting method is its defining strength.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Shermer acknowledges that "in spite of centuries of attention by scientists and philosophers of science, no concise definition of science has ever been accepted by the community of scientists and scholars" — the brief offered a temporary, politically motivated consensus. The definition "was made politically, not philosophically" and is "left there to be recalled should similar circumstances again arise."
