## Claim Statement

Sagan argues that the historical record of Marian apparitions in medieval and Renaissance Spain
provides a structural template that matches modern alien abduction accounts in almost every
important detail. Both categories of experience — he contends — are most plausibly explained by
dream-states, hypnagogic or hypnopompic episodes, and the powerful role of cultural expectation,
rather than by literal contact with supernatural or extraterrestrial beings.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Sagan draws heavily on William A. Christian Jr's *Apparitions in Late Medieval and Renaissance
Spain* (1981), which catalogs dozens of authenticated Marian apparitions. He lists the structural
parallels:

- The beings are **diminutive** (2½–4 feet in both traditions).
- They **come from the sky**.
- The content of the message is prosaic and mundane, despite its claimed celestial origin.
- There is a **clear connection with sleep**: many witnesses were just waking; accounts often have
  the discontinuities and impossible-to-describe qualities of dream material.
- **Witnesses hesitate to speak**, especially after male authority figures ridicule them — yet they
  persist, insisting on the reality of the experience.
- The **"signs" or evidence offered are invariably fakeable** by humans: a stone, a footprint,
  welts on the skin. Nothing unfakeable is ever produced.
- **Others present at the same spot perceive nothing.**
- Legends **circulated widely through preaching, pilgrimage, and itinerant storytellers**, which
  coordinated the imagery different witnesses would "independently" report.

The chapter title echoes a c. 1400 treatise by Jean Gerson, *On the Distinction between True and
False Visions*, which Sagan notes established criteria for "credible" witnesses that amounted to
political compliance with church authority — a circular validation system.

Sagan also notes that motivation matters: shrines generated pilgrimage economies; witnesses gained
social status; clergy had strong vested interests in the reality of visions. He does not claim
deliberate fraud in most cases — "I'm not suggesting that most witnesses made the whole business up.
Something else was going on." That something else, he argues, is primarily psychology.

## What this counters in the wiki

- **[[Missing Time (Hopkins)]]** and the Hopkins-era abduction tradition treat the parallelism
  between historical "little people" encounters and modern abduction accounts as evidence of a
  recurring real phenomenon. Sagan inverts this: the parallel is evidence of a recurring
  *psychological and cultural* phenomenon, not an external one.
- **[[Passport to Magonia: From Folklore to Flying Saucers (Vallée, 1969)]]**: Vallée uses the
  folklore parallel to argue for a deeper trans-historical phenomenon. Sagan uses the same evidence
  base to argue for a purely terrestrial psychological explanation — the parallels are a feature of
  human minds and social systems, not of a recurring non-human presence.
- **[[Communion (Strieber)]]**: Strieber's first-person account of overwhelming presence and
  paralysis in bed at night fits Sagan's sleep-transition hypothesis closely.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Sagan acknowledges the genuine agony in many witness accounts and refrains from dismissing all
witnesses as frauds or mentally ill. He recognizes that in some fraction of cases, there may have
been deliberate manufacture of signs for economic reasons, but that in most cases the witnesses
"really and truly" believed what they reported. His skeptical argument is structural and
probabilistic — not a claim that every witness lied, but that the entire class of experiences has
a more parsimonious explanation.
