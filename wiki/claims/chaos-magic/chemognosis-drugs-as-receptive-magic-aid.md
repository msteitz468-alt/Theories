## Claim Statement

Carroll holds that chemical and botanical substances have historically been integral to animism,
shamanism, and magic, and can make "various occult powers more accessible," but that none of them
"confers magical abilities by themselves." Their utility is strictly bounded: they assist
*receptive* magic (astral travel, divination, invocation) and are largely useless or dangerous
in *active* forms (sigil casting, enchantment, magical combat). Training remains the decisive
variable — "the adept should be able to obtain these experiences without chemical assistance"
after sufficient practice.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From the "Chemognosis" chapter of [[Liber Null & Psychonaut (Carroll)]]:

> "Chemical agents of natural and manufactured origin have always played a significant role in
> animism, shamanism, and magic. These substances can make various occult powers more accessible,
> but none of them confers magical abilities by themselves."

On the training variable:

> "The training and abilities of the users of the drugs account for many of the differences of
> effect noticed at lower dosages. Quantities which may evoke only mild euphoria or nausea in
> untrained subjects may be sufficient to allow the adept to enter trance or ecstatic states."

On the limits of chemognosis:

> "In general, chemical agents are only useful in receptive magic—such as astral travel, divination,
> and invocation—and after a while, the adept should be able to obtain these experiences without
> chemical assistance. Chemical agents find very little application in more active forms of magic
> such as sigil casting and enchantment. In magical combat, their use may prove disastrous."

Carroll gives explicit safety warnings both at the opening and closing of the chapter, stating
that "all drugs are poisons" and that distinguishing a fatal from a psychoactive dose "is
impossible to assay by amateur methods."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter is framed as a historical survey, not an instruction manual, with the publisher's
disclaimer prominent. It sits within the *Psychonaut* section alongside the "Animism and Shamanism"
chapter (which treats chemognosis in its pre-modern context) and the sections on [[The Rites of Chaos]]
(which mention "chemical or botanical elixirs" in the preparation phase of the Mass of Chaos).

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Carroll's cautions are extensive. On synthetic LSD specifically he notes that it has "acquired
an aura of paranoia and madness" and that "it is notoriously difficult to direct perception of
particular visions within it." Solanaceae-based flying ointments "carry a severe risk of fatal
poisoning." The chapter ends with a second formal warning: "These techniques are mentioned only
for the sake of historical completeness."
