## Claim Statement

Carroll presents sigilization as the systematic solution to the "lust of result" problem. Because
desire that remains in conscious awareness attracts the ego and the counter-will, Carroll proposes
a three-stage procedure: (1) the desire is encoded into an abstract glyph (sigil) designed not to
immediately suggest its content; (2) both the glyph and the desire are deliberately expelled from
waking consciousness; (3) the glyph is then concentrated on during a state of [[Gnosis (chaos magic)]]
so that the will acts on it subconsciously. The sigil functions as a carrier that slips the
desire past the mind's censoring and interfering activity, delivering it to the deeper will.
Carroll extends this principle beyond simple spells to evocation (constructing entity-sigils from
attribute symbols), divination (sigilizing the object of inquiry), enchantment, and the Augoeides
operation.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Sigils work because they stimulate the will to work subconsciously, bypassing the mind."
(Liber MMM, "Sigils")

"In constructing a sigil, the aim is to produce a glyph of desire, stylized so as not to
immediately suggest the desire… To successfully lose the sigil, both the sigil form and the
associated desire must be banished from normal waking consciousness." (Liber MMM, "Sigils")

"The sigil is charged at moments when the mind has achieved quiescence through magical trance, or
when high emotionality paralyzes its normal functioning." (Liber MMM, "Sigils")

"Image concentration… is the basis of casting sigils and creating independent thought forms."
(Liber MMM, "Image Concentration")

"Magical will may exert its effects directly on the universe, or it may use symbols or sigils as
intermediaries." (Liber LUX, "Enchantment")

## Contexts Within the Source

The technique appears as a practical section within the Neophyte syllabus (Liber MMM) alongside
ritual and dreaming. Carroll presents it as building directly on image concentration from the
trance training. In Liber LUX it reappears as the primary practical tool in enchantment, as the
structural element of evocation (elemental sigils), and as part of the daily Augoeides invocation.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Carroll warns that "a record should be kept of all work with sigils but not in such a way as to
cause conscious deliberation over the sigilized desire" — excessive record-keeping can reintroduce
the conscious mind and undercut the working. He also notes that sigils can "become conscious" and
advises the banishing ritual as a protection against problematic sigil obsession. The enchantment
section observes that direct magical will (without sigil intermediary) represents a higher and
more difficult form of the art.
