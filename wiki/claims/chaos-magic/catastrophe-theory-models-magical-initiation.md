## Claim Statement

Carroll holds that René Thom's catastrophe theory provides a qualitative but non-quantitative
model of how sudden discontinuous changes of state occur in magical and mystical contexts —
initiation, illumination, conversion, and belief change. The theory is applied via the cusp
catastrophe surface to four domains: occult vs. materialist worldviews, change of belief, mystical
initiation, and magical initiation. In each domain it predicts both which paths are safe and which
are dangerous, and explains why mystics and magicians traditionally denigrate each other's
single-axis approach to training.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From the "Catastrophe Theory and Magic" chapter of [[Liber Null & Psychonaut (Carroll)]]:

> "Catastrophe theory is a mathematical tool which allows sudden discontinuous changes to be
> represented by a topological model… The theory provides a qualitative but not a quantitative
> description of how a sudden change is likely to occur. It tells us that something unusual is to
> be expected under certain conditions, but it does not tell us exactly when to expect it. It does,
> however, tell us how to provoke catastrophes."

On magical vs. mystical training:

> "Thus, we see why mystic masters denigrate power for its own sake, and magical masters denigrate
> knowledge for its own sake. Each removes the candidate further from his objective and necessitates
> a catastrophic change to achieve the desired effect."

Carroll describes the optimal initiation path as the D → A route: a balanced, gradual increase in
both knowledge and power/experience. The double catastrophe surface shows "how perilously thin the
path D → A becomes at one point," presented "without further comment."

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter includes four figures (catastrophe surfaces and their applications). It is the most
explicitly mathematical passage in the book. Carroll acknowledges that most mathematicians consider
the theory "black magic," finding this apt. The chapter directly follows "Anecdotes" (Carroll's
autobiographical casebook) and precedes "Chaos Theory and Magic," which extends the same
mathematical-scientific framing.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Carroll notes that catastrophe theory is qualitative only: it tells you *when to expect* a
catastrophic transition but not exactly *when* it will occur. The model is therefore predictive
in structure but not in timing. He also notes that "transitions away from A [the tough-minded
occultist position] are unlikely, as they would involve eliminating experiences from memory" —
implying that genuine magical experience, once had and critically processed, is effectively
irreversible.
