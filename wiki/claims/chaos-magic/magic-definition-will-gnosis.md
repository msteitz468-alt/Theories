## Claim Statement

Carroll holds that magic is "the science and art of causing change to occur in conformity with
will," and that the will can function magically only when the mind has been silenced. The ordinary
mind, left to itself, perpetually interferes with the will — through lust of result, egotistical
identification, fear of failure, and the contrary wish not to achieve one's desire. The antidote
is a specific altered state — gnosis — in which these interferences are suspended and the will
operates undivided. Gnosis is therefore not merely preparatory but structurally necessary:
without it, magical acts misfire regardless of intent.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

"Magic is the science and art of causing change to occur in conformity with will. The will can
become magically effective only when the mind is focused and not interfering with the will."
(Liber MMM, "The Magical Trances")

"Altered states of consciousness are the key to magical powers. The particular state of mind
required has a name in every tradition: No-mind. Stopping the internal dialogue… In this book,
it will be known as gnosis." (Liber LUX, "Gnosis")

"It is during these moments of single-pointed concentration, or gnosis, that beliefs can be
implanted for magic, and the life force induced to manifest." (Liber LUX, "Gnosis")

## Contexts Within the Source

The claim appears first in Liber MMM as the rationale for the entire trance-training syllabus
(motionlessness → breathing → not-thinking → magical trances). It recurs in Liber LUX as the
theoretical anchor for the full range of inhibitory and excitatory techniques. Carroll presents
it as the single thread connecting all historical magical traditions.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Carroll notes that not every desired gnostic method is suitable for every magical operation:
"Certain forms of gnosis lend themselves more readily to some forms of magic than others."
Inhibitory and excitatory techniques cannot be used simultaneously in the same operation, only
sequentially. There is also no "magic drug which will, by itself, have the required effect" —
substances can augment but not replace the gnostic state.
