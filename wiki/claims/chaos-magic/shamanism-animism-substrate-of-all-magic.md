## Claim Statement

Carroll holds that animism — "our oldest magical and mystical tradition" — is the fountainhead of
all religious arts and magical sciences, and that shamanism (its northern trance-centred form) is
the ultimate source of the full range of occult themes: divination, healing, exorcism, magical
attack, atavism, spirit congress, and gnosis. All later traditions (Gnosticism, witchcraft,
Rosicrucianism, and contemporary chaos magic) are presented as attempts to recover this
primordial knowledge. Carroll therefore frames chaos magic as a renaissance of shamanic
technology, not an invention.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From the "Animism and Shamanism" chapter of [[Liber Null & Psychonaut (Carroll)]]:

> "Animism is our oldest magical and mystical tradition. It is from animism that all religious arts
> and magical sciences originate."

> "The basic principles of magic, like the basic principles of science, do not change, but they can
> become lost. Animism and shamanism present a very full magical technology which resumes all occult
> themes. Mankind now stands in greater need of these abilities than at any time since the first
> aeon if it is to understand rather than destroy itself and the environment."

> "The extraordinary traditions of shamanism are the fountainhead of all occult systems, and it is
> to animism and shamanism that we must look if we wish to pick up the pieces of magic, man's
> oldest science, and use them again."

From the "Gnosticism" chapter, reinforcing the derivation:

> "The timeless themes of magic appear in their completeness in Gnosticism because it was able to
> draw its techniques from Egyptian learning, from the Greek Mystery Schools, and from systems
> further east, each of which had preserved traditions from that ultimate wellspring of magic —
> animism and shamanism."

Carroll also notes that despite "enormous geographical separation between animistic cultures, they
share almost identical methods" — a convergence he takes as evidence for a common underlying
magical reality.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is made in the "Magical Perspectives" and "Animism and Shamanism" sections of
*Psychonaut*, which frame the animistic panpsychic view against scientific materialism and
religious anthropomorphism. Carroll argues that animists extend consciousness to all things, which
he considers "a far more respectful and generous attitude" than the materialist denial of
consciousness or the religious denial of animal souls.

Shamanic initiation is described specifically: it involves a death-rebirth vision in which the
candidate's body is dismembered and reassembled with an additional element (an extra bone, magical
quartz stones, or an animal spirit), symbolizing the acquisition of the aetheric force field.
The shaman's soul then journeys through an otherworld while in ecstatic or drug-induced trance.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Carroll acknowledges that shamanic power "cannot be progressively accumulated like other
technology": shamans "will be lucky if their apprentices make any advance beyond their own
achievements," and traditions "require a continual influx of talent just to prevent themselves
from degenerating." This sets a principled limit on the recoverability of shamanic knowledge.
