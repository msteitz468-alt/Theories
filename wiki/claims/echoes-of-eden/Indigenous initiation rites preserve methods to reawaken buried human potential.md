## Claim Statement

Wallis argues that indigenous **initiation ceremonies and shamanic traditions** around the world preserve ancient "methods for releasing" the human potential that was switched off — and that modern industrial societies, lacking such "rites of passage," leave people unable to access or integrate these capacities.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

His framing case is "When Troy was Taken": a thirteen-year-old boy in Arizona (2006) abruptly taken by two stern men for a wilderness initiation — left in unfamiliar mountains to find "water, food and shelter," his every response "observed by unseen witnesses and helpers." Wallis reports that "guardians of indigenous narratives" across southern Africa, Native America, and Australia share ceremonies that interweave "paleocontact, human potential and ancient methods for releasing it," echoing the strange-encounter testimonies of modern experiencers. He laments that without "intentional ceremonies and rites… it is the accidents of life" that trigger awakening, and notes the "curation of shamanic abilities."
