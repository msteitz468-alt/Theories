## Claim Statement

Wallis argues that knowledge of paleocontact and buried human potential follows a recurring pattern: "human history — written and oral — is a treasure house of disclosure and suppression, secrets, and revelations," in which "those who strike the most gold are often quick to rebury it in their own vaults and fortresses." He further contends that modern military, intelligence, and government agencies are quietly interested in "archaeology, indigenous rituals, and initiation practices" for this very reason.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He frames the whole book as travelling "from Senate briefings in Washington… to secret ceremonies in southern Africa and Native America, from strange phenomena in Australia and Iraq to mysterious encounters in modern Brazil and ancient Greece," to show "why military, intelligence and other government agencies in the twenty-first century are so interested in archaeology, indigenous rituals, and initiation practices — and why you and I should be too." He links the suppression of indigenous knowledge to the conquests of history (e.g. the targeting of those with "clear sight, telepathic and precognitive skills").
