## Claim Statement

Huxley proposes that the perennial philosophy's central metaphysical assertion is the existence of a divine Ground of all being — a spiritual Absolute that underlies and pervades everything that exists. This Ground is not knowable by discursive reason, conceptual argument, or sense experience alone; it can only be apprehended by a direct, unitive intuition that becomes available when the ordinary ego-self is sufficiently stilled and purified. Huxley draws on Hindu, Buddhist, Taoist, Christian, and Sufi sources to argue that this claim is universal, and that the conditions for achieving such direct knowledge — moral purification, self-emptying, and contemplative practice — are similarly universal across traditions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Huxley opens Chapter II with a definitive statement: "The divine Ground of all existence is a spiritual Absolute, ineffable in terms of discursive thought, but (in certain circumstances) susceptible of being directly experienced and realized by the human being." He specifies further that "the last end of man, the ultimate reason for human existence, is unitive knowledge of the divine Ground—the knowledge that can come only to those who are prepared to 'Die to self' and so make room, as it were, for God."

In the Introduction, Huxley explains why ordinary inquiry cannot reach the Ground: "The Perennial Philosophy is primarily concerned with the one, divine Reality substantial to the manifold world of things and lives and minds. But the nature of this one Reality is such that it cannot be directly and immediately apprehended except by those who have chosen to fulfil certain conditions, making themselves loving, pure in heart, and poor in spirit." He uses a scientific analogy to sharpen the point: just as "nothing in our everyday experience gives us any reason for supposing that water is made up of hydrogen and oxygen; and yet when we subject water to certain rather drastic treatments, the nature of its constituent elements becomes manifest," so too "when that mind is subjected to certain rather drastic treatments, the divine element, of which it is in part at least composed, becomes manifest."

The ineffability of the Ground is addressed directly in the anthology selections Huxley assembles. Shankara, from the *Viveka-Chudamani*, is quoted: "The nature of the one Reality must be known by one's own clear spiritual perception; it cannot be known through a pandit (learned man)." And: "Disease is not cured by pronouncing the name of medicine, but by taking medicine. Deliverance is not achieved by repeating the word 'Brahman,' but by directly experiencing Brahman." From Dionysius the Areopagite, Huxley quotes the teaching that "this darkness, though of deepest obscurity, is yet radiantly clear" and that the only way to know the divine is "through the abandonment of all things, to praise Him who is beyond and above all things." Huxley also cites the *Brhadaranyaka Upanishad*: "The significance of Brahman is expressed by neti neti (not so, not so); for beyond this, that you say it is not so, there is nothing further. Its name, however, is 'the Reality of reality.'"

The key epistemological point — that knowledge of the Ground requires a transformed mode of being in the knower — is stated in the Introduction: "Knowledge is a function of being. When there is a change in the being of the knower, there is a corresponding change in the nature and amount of knowing." Only by undergoing moral and contemplative transformation can a human being achieve "the self-validating certainty of direct awareness."

## Contexts Within the Source

Chapter II is the book's primary metaphysical chapter, providing the ontological foundation for everything Huxley discusses elsewhere. The divine Ground is at once immanent and transcendent, personal (as God or Isvara) and supra-personal (as the modeless Godhead), and these distinctions run through Huxley's quotations from Eckhart, Ruysbroeck, Shankara, and others. Huxley notes that different religions have emphasized different aspects of the Ground — exclusively personal, exclusively transcendent, or supra-personal — and argues that exclusive emphasis on any single aspect produces spiritual distortion. The full teaching requires acknowledging the Ground as simultaneously "immanent as well as transcendent, supra-personal as well as personal." This metaphysical claim underpins the psychological claim elaborated in [[Huxley: "That art thou" — the self's deepest identity is with the divine Ground]] and both together constitute the doctrinal heart of [[The perennial philosophy]] as Huxley presents it. See also the framing thesis in [[Huxley: a single Perennial Philosophy underlies all the great religious traditions]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Huxley acknowledges the severe semantic difficulty of speaking about the Ground at all: "So far, then, as a fully adequate expression of the Perennial Philosophy is concerned, there exists a problem in semantics that is finally insoluble." He concedes that discursive knowledge about the Ground is not merely second-hand but is structurally "paradoxical knowledge," since "direct knowledge of the Ground cannot be had except by union, and union can be achieved only by the annihilation of the self-regarding ego." He also notes that this puts the teaching beyond the reach of "empirical theology" as normally practised, because such theology "confined their attention more or less exclusively to the experience of those… called 'the unregenerate.'" These are epistemological caveats from within the tradition rather than counter-arguments against it; the source presents no sustained argument that the divine Ground does not exist or cannot be directly known.
