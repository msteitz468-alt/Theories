## Claim Statement

Huxley argues that unitive knowledge of the divine Ground — the highest form of human experience, in which the individual soul directly apprehends its identity with the Absolute — is conditionally attainable only through a thoroughgoing "self-naughting": the systematic mortification of the ego, its desires, its self-will, and even its most exalted emotions. Humility, non-attachment, charity, and passive contemplation are not supplementary virtues but necessary preconditions; without the death of separate selfhood, the divine Ground remains inaccessible because the ego's craving for separateness is the very obstacle that blocks it. Traditions as varied as Christian mysticism, Buddhist Vedanta, and Sufism are assembled by Huxley to demonstrate that this requirement is universal and unavoidable.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The thesis is stated with maximal clarity in Huxley's discussion of faith and prayer. He writes that "a mind busily engaged in the voluntary and intellectual activity, which is 'religious faith' cannot possibly be in the state of selflessness and alert passivity which is the necessary condition of the unitive knowledge of the Ground." He further observes that "faith in the existence and power of any supernatural entity which is less than ultimate spiritual Reality, and in any form of worship that falls short of self-naughting, will certainly… result in improvement of character, and probably in posthumous survival of the improved personality under 'heavenly' conditions. But this personal survival within what is still the temporal order is not the eternal life of timeless union with the Spirit."

The word "self-naughting" functions in Huxley's argument as a technical term covering several related disciplines. Drawing on the Buddhist text *The Awakening of Faith* by Ashvaghosha, Huxley describes the "expedient means" required for unitive knowledge of "Thusness": "The list of these indispensable means includes charity and compassion towards all sentient beings, sub-human as well as human, self-naughting or mortification, personal devotion to the incarnations of the Absolute Buddha-nature, and spiritual exercises designed to free the mind from its infatuating desires for separateness and independent selfhood." The Buddhist "Way of Wisdom" described in the same passage requires dismantling "the individual's instinctive and fundamental… urges — the will to live and to enjoy, the will to cherish one's personality, the will to propagate, which give rise to greed and lust, fear and anger, infatuation, pride and egotism."

Huxley connects this Buddhist account directly to the Christian mystical tradition. In the chapter on contemplation he quotes Eckhart: "I tell you that no one can experience this birth (of God realized in the soul) without a mighty effort. No one can attain this birth unless he can withdraw his mind entirely from things." And he paraphrases the consistent Christian testimony that "Adoration is an activity of the loving, but still separate, individuality. Contemplation is the state of union with the divine Ground of all being. The highest prayer is the most passive. Inevitably; for the less there is of self, the more there is of God."

The social and historical dimension of the requirement is equally plain. Huxley explains that Protestant reformers such as Luther and Calvin achieved widespread popularity in part because their doctrines "could be saved without going through that distasteful process of self-naughting, which is the necessary pre-condition of deliverance into the knowledge of eternal Reality." Emotionalism in religion is treated in the same vein: the "experience" induced by revivalist techniques is "always excitement of that individualized self, which must be died to by anyone who aspires to live to divine Reality." True mystical experience, by contrast, is "the direct apprehension of the divine Ground in an act of intuition possible, in its fulness, only to the selflessly pure in heart."

The Sufi testimony further corroborates the thesis. A Sufi master describes twelve years of inner smithing — "I put it in the furnace of austerity and burned it in the fire of combat, I laid it on the anvil of reproach and smote it with the hammer of blame" — before finally attaining union: "without intrusion of creatures, through God's help alone, I attained unto God." The Buddhist *Way of Tranquillity* similarly insists that "all kinds of ideation are to be discarded as fast as they arise; even the notions of controlling and discarding are to be got rid of. One's mind should become like a mirror, reflecting things, but not judging them or retaining them."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the book's central practical counterpart to the metaphysical thesis in [[Huxley: there is a divine Ground of all being, knowable by direct intuition]]. If the Ground is real and directly knowable, the spiritual life is the process of removing the ego-obstacle that prevents such knowledge. Huxley's survey of chapters on suffering, faith, prayer, idolatry, emotionalism, ritual, and contemplation all converge on this point: separateness from the Ground is suffering; the intensification of selfhood is the root of evil; the way back is self-annihilation or "dying to the self." The claim also extends the framing thesis of [[Huxley: a single Perennial Philosophy underlies all the great religious traditions]] by showing that the practical disciplines required for unitive knowledge are as cross-traditional as the metaphysics. ## Opposing Information Within the Source

Huxley acknowledges that the practical demands of self-naughting are severe enough that the vast majority of religious practitioners — including sincere ones — do not undertake them. "All are called to contemplation," he writes, quoting St. John of the Cross: "God does not reserve such a lofty vocation to certain souls only; on the contrary, He is willing that all should embrace it. But He finds few who permit Him to work such sublime things for them. There are many who, when He sends them trials, shrink from the labour and refuse to bear with the dryness and mortification." The source also recognizes that emotional religion and sacramental practice offer partial goods — improvement of character and "heavenly" survival — short of total unitive knowledge, and Huxley does not dismiss these partial goods; he only insists they are not the highest.
