## Claim Statement

Huxley argues that beneath the diversity of the world's great religious traditions lies a single, universal core — the *philosophia perennis* — comprising a shared metaphysics, psychology, and ethic. This perennial philosophy holds that a divine Reality underlies the world of things and minds, that the human soul is in some measure identical with or akin to that Reality, and that the final end of human existence is unitive knowledge of that divine Ground. Huxley maintains that this teaching is not the invention of any one age or culture but has been independently rediscovered by mystics and contemplatives across every tradition and era, from ancient India and China through Sufi Islam and medieval Christian mysticism to the Quakers of the seventeenth century.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the opening paragraph of his Introduction, Huxley defines his subject with precision: "PHILOSOPHIA PERENNIS—the phrase was coined by Leibniz; but the thing—the metaphysic that recognizes a divine Reality substantial to the world of things and lives and minds; the psychology that finds in the soul something similar to, or even identical with, divine Reality; the ethic that places man's final end in the knowledge of the immanent and transcendent Ground of all being—the thing is immemorial and universal." He goes on to assert that "Rudiments of the Perennial Philosophy may be found among the traditionary lore of primitive peoples in every region of the world, and in its fully developed forms it has a place in every one of the higher religions."

Huxley insists that the testimony he draws upon is first-hand rather than speculative: "in every age there have been some men and women who chose to fulfil the conditions upon which alone, as a matter of brute empirical fact, such immediate knowledge can be had; and of these a few have left accounts of the Reality they were thus enabled to apprehend." These first-hand witnesses — called "saint," "prophet," "sage," or "enlightened one" by those who knew them — are the primary sources from which Huxley assembles his anthology. He presents parallel passages from the Upanishads and Shankara, from the Taoist *Book of Chuang Tzu*, from Mahayana sutras, from Sufi poetry, and from Christian mystics such as Eckhart, William Law, and the Quakers, arguing that all of them articulate the same essential doctrine.

The universality of the teaching is further underlined by Huxley's note that "the inexhaustible theme has been treated again and again, from the standpoint of every religious tradition and in all the principal languages of Asia and Europe." He characterizes it as "the Highest Common Factor in all preceding and subsequent theologies."

## Contexts Within the Source

This thesis functions as the book's entire organizing principle. Every subsequent chapter — on the divine Ground, on self-knowledge, on grace, on contemplation and action — is presented as an exposition of one facet of the perennial philosophy. Huxley explicitly introduces it as a three-part structure (metaphysics + psychology + ethics) that he then unpacks through the anthology of selected passages. The claim is therefore not one argument among many but the framework within which all other arguments are embedded. It links directly to the companion claims [[Huxley: there is a divine Ground of all being, knowable by direct intuition]] and [[Huxley: "That art thou" — the self's deepest identity is with the divine Ground]], each of which elaborates one of the three structural pillars. See also [[The perennial philosophy]] for the concept hub.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Huxley himself notes significant obstacles to accepting his synthesis. He acknowledges that direct apprehension of the divine Reality "cannot be directly and immediately apprehended except by those who have chosen to fulfil certain conditions, making themselves loving, pure in heart, and poor in spirit," which means that the testimony on which the perennial philosophy rests is, by definition, restricted to a minority. He also concedes that "familiarity with traditionally hallowed writings tends to breed… a kind of reverential insensibility," and that empirical theologians who have tried to base theology on ordinary unregenerate experience have produced results that "carry so little conviction." Beyond these methodological caveats, the source presents no internal counter-argument that directly challenges the universality of the perennial philosophy itself.
