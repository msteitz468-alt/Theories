## Claim Statement

Ronson argues the army's New Age / psychic experiments did not simply vanish but mutated, resurfacing in the War on Terror as psychological operations and abusive interrogation — most strikingly the use of **music as torture** (Barney the Purple Dinosaur's "I Love You," Metallica) blasted at detainees. He frames this as the dark repurposing of Jim Channon's pacifist idea of deploying sound and "frequencies" on the battlefield.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Ronson describes a torture undertaken not at Abu Ghraib but inside a shipping container behind a disused railway station in **al-Qa'im, Iraq (May 2003)**, involving Barney — "really just as horrific, in its own way," but un-photographed and so met with amusement rather than revulsion. He links the contemporary "frequencies" work and non-lethal acoustic weapons to Channon's manual and to MKULTRA-era research. The book's chapter "The 1953 House" recounts the case of [[Frank Olson]]: Ronson notes the public reacted to Olson's CIA-LSD death with the same "amused and fascinated" response as to Barney-torture, "triggered by the disconcertingly surreal combination of dark intelligence secrets and familiar pop culture."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Ronson presents the lineage as a journalist's connective argument rather than a proven chain of command, and is careful to distinguish Channon's benign intent from the uses others made of his ideas.
