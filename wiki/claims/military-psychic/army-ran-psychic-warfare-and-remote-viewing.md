## Claim Statement

Ronson documents that the U.S. Army and intelligence community seriously pursued psychic capabilities during the Cold War — most prominently a **remote-viewing / "psychic spy"** program — championed at the top by Major General [[Albert Stubblebine III]], the Army's chief of intelligence, who believed psychic powers (walking through walls, telepathy, psychic healing) should become standard intelligence tools.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Stubblebine repeatedly tried to walk through his office wall ("the wall is an illusion… all I have to do is merge the spaces") and demonstrated spoon-bending to Special Forces. Ronson interviews former military psychic spies including Sergeant **Lyn Buchanan** (author of *The Seventh Sense: The Secrets of Remote Viewing*), and examines **Uri Geller**'s claim to have been a "reactivated" psychic spy in the War on Terror — laying out "four possible scenarios" rather than asserting it as fact. The program is the historical Star Gate / Grill Flame lineage of army-funded remote viewing.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Ronson stresses the claims are hard to verify and partly self-promotional ("the damn psychic spies should be keeping their damn mouths shut… instead of chit-chatting all over town"), and he never confirms Geller's account.

## Contexts Within the Source

These programs grew partly out of [[Jim Channon's First Earth Battalion seeded New Age ideas into the U.S. military|Jim Channon's First Earth Battalion]] and connect, in Ronson's telling, back to the CIA's [[Operation MK-ULTRA]].
