## Claim Statement

Ronson presents the 1993 FBI siege of David Koresh's Branch Davidians at Waco as an early, chaotic field test of the same psychological-warfare and "frequency" ideas later used in the War on Terror — most concretely the round-the-clock acoustic bombardment of the church, and a seriously-considered plan to beam the "voice of God" subliminally into Koresh's head.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

After a BATF raid on 28 February 1993 turned into a 51-day siege, the FBI (from mid-March) blasted the compound — holding seventy-nine people including twenty-five children — with Tibetan Buddhist chants, screeching bagpipes, crying seagulls, helicopter-rotor noise, dentist drills, sirens, dying-rabbit sounds, a train, and Nancy Sinatra's "These Boots Are Made for Walking," songs distorted by slowing or speeding them up. Survivor **Clive Doyle** told Ronson the monks were "pretty ominous" and asked whether they had been hit with "subliminal sounds"; Ronson notes the FBI kept playing the Buddhist chants even after a Dalai Lama complaint because the agent in charge "didn't have anything else to do at night." At a press conference FBI spokesman **Bob Ricks** professed not to know "what psychological warfare is."

Ronson ties the episode to the silent-sound thread: in March 1993 the FBI flew Russian "psycho-correction" scientist **Dr Igor Smirnov** from Moscow to Arlington, Virginia, where (with CIA, DIA and ARPA representatives) they proposed using the phone lines so that "underneath" the negotiators' voices a subliminal "voice of God" would tell Koresh what the FBI wanted. Smirnov said it was technically possible but refused to sign a waiver accepting responsibility if it triggered mass suicide, so the plan collapsed — though an agent told him they had already lined up **Charlton Heston** to "play the voice of God." Ronson reports the music bombardment was likely inspired by the 1989 PsyOps blasting of Noriega at the Vatican Embassy with Guns N' Roses, itself traced to Jim Channon's First Earth Battalion ethos.

## Contexts Within the Source

Ronson frames Waco as a "long-awaited golden opportunity" for out-of-the-box thinkers who, after Fukuyama's "end of history," lacked suitable adversaries to experiment on — drawing an explicit parallel to Abu Ghraib, where "the US government behaved like a grotesque caricature of itself."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Ronson stresses the operation was incoherent rather than masterful: most noises came from an agent's wife who "scooped them up" from a local museum; survivor Doyle suspected "the FBI were just like idiots, and it was just chaos out there." A lurid related rumour (an invisible "Mr B" Special Forces burglar sprung from prison to fix bugs in the compound) Ronson explicitly flags as unverified, "a rumour" — by contrast with the Smirnov meeting, which "can safely be considered true."
