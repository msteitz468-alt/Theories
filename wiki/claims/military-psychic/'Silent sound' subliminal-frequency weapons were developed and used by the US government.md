## Claim Statement

Ronson traces a real patent-and-research trail suggesting the U.S. government developed — and may operationally use — "silent sound" technology: subliminal audio and frequency effects designed to implant emotions, voices, or fears into people's heads without their awareness, an apparently mechanistic counterpart to the book's psychic-warfare ideas.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The chief documentary anchor is **Dr Oliver Lowery** of Georgia, who on 27 October 1992 received **US Patent #5,159,703** for a "**Silent Subliminal Presentation System**" — non-aural carriers modulated "for inducement into the brain," able (per Lowery's publicity) to "silently induce and change the emotional state in a human being" across a list of 12 positive and 26 negative emotions. In 1996 his company **Silent Sounds Inc.** posted that "all schematics have [now] been classified by the US Government," that they made tapes for foreign governments "with the permission of the US State Department," and that "the system was used throughout Operation Desert Storm (Iraq)." In a phone call (in which he refused to be named, claiming to speak from "a fourteen-storey building behind three layers of barbed-wire defences" via call divert), Lowery likened the "massive," 25-year silent-sound research to the **Manhattan Project**, said "we only use the bad stuff on the bad guys," and claimed the Americans "warped [Iraqi soldiers'] brains for a hundred days" in the first Gulf War.

Lowery name-checked Russian scientist **Dr Igor Smirnov** of Moscow's "Institute for Psycho-Correction," who Ronson says had blasted subliminal messages at addicts and (a decade earlier, for the Soviet government) at Red Army troops bound for Afghanistan. Ronson also frames the related "**Bucha Effect**" (Ch. 8): non-lethal-weapons expert Commander **Sid Heal** recounts that 1950s helicopters fell from the sky because the rotor blades strobed sunlight at "the approximation of human brainwave frequency," interfering with the brain — and speculates that the Barney/strobe-light technique used on detainees is a deliberate bid to exploit this effect, "going for the amygdala," "the ultimate non-lethal." In Ch. 13, Colonel John Alexander, after first denying knowledge of silent sounds and "psycho-correction" devices, is shown to have co-authored a leaked report listing a "Psycho-Correction Device."

## Contexts Within the Source

Ronson treats this as the moment a "least plausible conspiracy theory" appeared partly corroborated, while staying pointedly skeptical: ITN denied ever running the silent-sound Gulf War story Lowery cited, and Ronson notes Lowery "seemed to have verified" the theory yet could equally be "a fantasist." He links the thread back to the same lineage as goat-staring and wall-walking — a mechanistic version of what ambient composer Steven Halpern had suggested to Jim Channon in the late 1970s.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Ronson cannot verify Lowery's identity or his Gulf War claims, finds no ITN archive supporting them, and concedes detainee Jamal felt nothing unusual after his Guantánamo music exposure — allowing that "they blasted Jamal with silent sounds and it just didn't work," or that none of it is real.
