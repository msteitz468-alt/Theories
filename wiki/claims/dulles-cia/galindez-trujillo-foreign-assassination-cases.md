## Claim Statement

Talbot presents the March 1956 disappearance of Columbia University lecturer Jesús de Galíndez — abducted in New York after writing a damning dissertation on the Dominican dictator Rafael Trujillo — and the U.S. government's long, lucrative coddling of Trujillo's "operatic reign of terror" as a case study in the "dangerous ideas" of the Dulles era: the elimination of inconvenient figures abroad and the embrace of murderous strongmen as Cold War clients.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Chapter 13 recounts that "shortly after 9:00 p.m. on March 12, 1956, Jesús de Galíndez, a lecturer in Spanish and government at Columbia University," vanished after a seminar and "was never seen again by friends or colleagues." A Basque who had fought Franco, Galíndez had fled to the Dominican Republic, found Trujillo's "fire" worse than "Franco's frying pan," and later written a 750-page dissertation, "The Era of Trujillo," exposing the regime; Trujillo's agents tried to buy the manuscript "for as much as $25,000," and when Galíndez refused, "the dictator decided that left him with only one course of action."

Talbot details Trujillo's "operatic reign of terror," including "the horrific 1937 mass slaughter of thousands of Haitian immigrant workers," torture at the Nigua camp and "La Cuarenta dungeon," and bodies "displayed in macabre festivals." He documents U.S. complicity: by 1955 "John Foster Dulles's State Department was celebrating the strongman as 'one of the hemisphere's foremost spokesmen against the Communist movement'"; Nixon "made a public display of embracing Trujillo"; Trujillo "reaped $25 million a year in foreign aid from Washington," and "the CIA further enriched the dictator with secret payments, delivering suitcases stuffed with cash to his hotel suite whenever he visited New York." Galíndez himself, Talbot notes, was an FBI informant whom the bureau valued even as he denounced that "since John Foster Dulles entered into the picture, the United States has started to write the blackest pages of its international relations" — and an FBI memo five days before he vanished warned his dissertation "may involve informant in personal difficulties."

Talbot lists Trujillo among the foreign leaders the post-Watergate Church and Pike Committees forced the CIA to account for targeting — alongside Patrice Lumumba, Ngo Dinh Diem, and Fidel Castro — though CIA witnesses claimed "its assassins had proved inept or were beaten to the punch," a modesty Talbot rejects.

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter's "dangerous ideas" frame casts these cases as showing the CIA functioning as "the black-gloved fist" of the power elite under Dulles — willing to tolerate, finance, and shield brutal anti-Communist dictators, and to see troublesome exiles eliminated, in the name of Cold War interests.
