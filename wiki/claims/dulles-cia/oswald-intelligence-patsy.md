## Claim Statement

Talbot argues Lee Harvey Oswald was a likely-innocent "patsy" who behaved like a trained intelligence operative, was supposed to be killed leaving the Texas School Book Depository, and was silenced by Jack Ruby before he could stand trial. Oswald publicly insisted, "I'm just a patsy!"

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot cites HSCA chief counsel G. Robert Blakey that Oswald was supposed to be killed at the scene, and that a "credible" case for his innocence could be made. He recounts: ballistics that didn't match the weapon; the "magic bullet" found nearly pristine; no fingerprints on the Mannlicher-Carcano and no gunpowder on Oswald's cheek; witness Buell Wesley Frazier insisting Oswald's package was too small for a rifle; the Zapruder film and ~21 officers indicating shots from the grassy knoll (front). Oswald's attempted phone call to a Raleigh, NC, Army-intelligence contact is read by CIA veteran Victor Marchetti as reaching for an intelligence "cut-out" — which, Marchetti says, "was his death warrant… it was executive action." The CIA's own photo-analysis unit reportedly concluded conspiracy, but its report was suppressed.

## Contexts Within the Source

This supports the broader thesis [[The Devil's Chessboard argues a deep state removed President Kennedy]] and the [[E. Howard Hunt's deathbed account named a CIA plot to kill Kennedy]]. Talbot quotes Charles de Gaulle telling Alain Peyrefitte that Oswald was "a front" and that "security forces were in cahoots with the extremists."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Talbot notes Blakey continued to believe in Oswald's guilt, and that the 1979 HSCA report found a conspiracy "involving Oswald and other unknown parties" — i.e., the official record did not exonerate Oswald.
