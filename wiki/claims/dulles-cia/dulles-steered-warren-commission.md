## Claim Statement

Talbot argues that Dulles — fired by Kennedy two years earlier and, in Talbot's thesis, complicit in the murder — got himself appointed to the Warren Commission and worked behind the scenes with former CIA colleagues to steer the inquiry toward "lone gunman" Oswald and away from the agency. One observer said it should have been called "the Dulles Commission." See [[Event: The Warren Commission (1963–1964)]].

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "The man who should have been in the witness chair wound up instead in control of the inquiry." (Ch. 20)

Talbot disputes LBJ's 1971 claim that he named Dulles and John McCloy because "Bobby Kennedy asked me to put [them] on it," calling it "ludicrous." He quotes McCloy that the Commission's purpose was to "lay the dust… not only in the United States, but all over the world." Talbot separately documents Dulles's campaign to make Truman retract his December 22, 1963 *Washington Post* op-ed warning the CIA had become "a symbol of sinister and mysterious foreign intrigue" — including a falsified memo claiming a "senile" Truman disowned his own article.

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter frames the Commission as "an establishment cover-up," consistent with de Gaulle's prediction that the American security forces would "close ranks… throw Noah's cloak over these shameful deeds."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Talbot presents the Dulles-as-mastermind thesis as an argument built on circumstantial pattern rather than a confession. See [[Controversy: Was Allen Dulles complicit in the Kennedy assassination?]].
