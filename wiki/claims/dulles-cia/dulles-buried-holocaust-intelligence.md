## Claim Statement

Dulles received credible, early intelligence about the Final Solution from sources including German industrialist Eduard Schulte and diplomat Fritz Kolbe, yet did "nothing to publicize" it and kept it from forcing Allied action. Talbot says few of Dulles's 300-plus Bern communiqués mentioned the killing of Jews, and "none carried a sense of urgency about the Final Solution."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Schulte carried word of Auschwitz gassings to Zurich in July 1942; Kolbe smuggled cables on the deportation of Hungary's Jews. Talbot says Dulles redirected the president's attention to the threat of German Communist organizing instead, and used the Nazis' own euphemisms ("conscription," "labor service") in his cables. When shown the Vrba-Wetzler Auschwitz report by reporter Walter Garrett on June 22, 1944, Dulles read it and said, "One has to do something immediately" — which Talbot calls a "pantomime of concern," since Dulles had been receiving such reports for over two years.

> WWII historian Neal H. Petersen: Dulles's "reticence on this subject is among the most controversial and least understandable aspects of his performance in Bern."

## Contexts Within the Source

Talbot situates this within the broader State Department's obstruction (the Breckinridge Long memos, the rejection of the *St. Louis* refugees) and Dulles's own early credulity toward *The Protocols of the Elders of Zion* during his postwar-I posting in Turkey.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Talbot records that academic researchers "remain reluctant to pass judgment" on Dulles and that Petersen could not explain the silence — i.e., the source presents the omission as an unresolved puzzle, not a proven intent.
