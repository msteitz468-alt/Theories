## Claim Statement

Days after publicly denouncing Soviet "brain warfare" in an April 10, 1953 Princeton-alumni speech, Dulles authorized MKULTRA — a CIA mind-control program (launched with a $300,000 budget, growing to multimillion-dollar scale over a quarter century) that enlisted dozens of universities and hospitals and used drugs, especially LSD, on often-unwitting subjects treated as "expendables." It was preceded by Operation Artichoke (1951), named after Dulles's favorite vegetable.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot says [[Richard Helms (CIA director)]] oversaw MKULTRA and warned the work must be kept secret; drug expert Sidney Gottlieb dosed subjects and developed undetectable poisons. Dulles "kept grilling Gottlieb" on whether LSD could program "selected individuals [to] commit acts of substantial sabotage or acts of violence, including murder." At Camp King and Haus Waldhof in Germany, CIA scientists worked alongside ex-Nazi doctors; one Artichoke protocol stipulated "disposal of the body is not a problem." Harvard physician Henry Knowles Beecher drew on Dachau research in his LSD work.

## Contexts Within the Source

The chapter links MKULTRA to the suspicious death of CIA biochemist [[Frank Olson]] (1953). Talbot notes the program surfaced in the 1975 Rockefeller Commission inquiry, where President Ford apologized to the Olson family.
