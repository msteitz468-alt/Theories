## Claim Statement

Talbot adopts sociologist C. Wright Mills's concept of "the power elite" — the interlocking corporate, governmental, and military "command posts" — to characterize the America that the Dulles brothers served and ran. He argues that by the 1950s the United States had become "a democracy in form only," dominated by a self-perpetuating "fraternity of the successful" centered on Wall Street and the Council on Foreign Relations, with Wall Street lawyers and bankers like the Dulleses forming its "invisible elite."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Chapter 9 opens with the 1952 Eisenhower-Nixon victory as "the triumph of 'the power elite,' in the phrase coined by sociologist C. Wright Mills." Talbot summarizes Mills's 1956 book *The Power Elite*: America "was ruled by those who control the 'strategic command posts' of society — the big corporations, the machinery of the state, and the military establishment," cliques "drawn together by their deep mutual stake in the 'permanent war economy.'" Mills called their "professional and social synchronicity" "the fraternity of the successful." Talbot quotes Mills directly that "the United States had largely become a democracy in form only" and that the ordinary ebb-and-flow theory of competing interests was "a fairy tale . . . not adequate even as an approximate model of how the American system of power works."

Talbot identifies the Dulleses as Mills's inner core: "It was men like John Foster Dulles and Allen Dulles whom Mills had in mind when he wrote of the power elite's inner core" — the Wall Street lawyers and bankers who were the "in-between types" shuttling between "Manhattan corporate suites and Washington command posts," what Mills called America's "invisible elite." He adds that Mills "was among the first to take note of how 'national security' could be invoked by the power elite to more deeply disguise its operations," and that "the rise of Dulles's spy complex in the 1950s would further undermine a U.S. democracy that, as Mills observed, was already seriously compromised by growing corporate power."

## Contexts Within the Source

Talbot uses Mills's framework to situate Dulles not as an aberration but as the executor of a structural oligarchy, with the Council on Foreign Relations as its recruiting ground (Eisenhower filled his cabinet from "the Dewey-Dulles–Rockefeller-Luce–dominated New York nexus"). He notes the CIA later identified Mills "as one of the leading intellectual threats to the established order."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Talbot records that contemporaries pushed back on Mills: corporate lawyer Adolf Berle found "an uncomfortable degree of truth" in the book but dismissed it as "an angry cartoon, not a serious picture," and Arthur Schlesinger Jr. charged that Mills seemed "more intent on stirring the masses than on stimulating serious academic debate."
