## Claim Statement

Talbot argues that under Dulles, with Eisenhower's "license to kill," the CIA "became an effective killing machine," targeting foreign leaders deemed threats to U.S. interests — including the Congo's Patrice Lumumba, South Vietnam's Ngo Dinh Diem, the Dominican Republic's Rafael Trujillo, and Fidel Castro — and that Dulles was "an assassination enthusiast throughout his espionage career."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot says Dulles proposed assassinating Stalin at a 1952 Paris summit (rejected by Walter Bedell Smith). At a 1957 dinner, asked if he could find an assassin for Egypt's Nasser, Dulles answered in "dead seriousness," describing the ideal fanatic. In *The Craft of Intelligence* Dulles attributed an "executive action" murder section to the KGB — which Talbot says "is precisely what Dulles himself had done." The Doolittle Report (1954), commissioned by Eisenhower, concluded "there are no rules in such a game. Hitherto acceptable norms of human conduct do not apply" — language Talbot says gave Dulles further justification.

## Contexts Within the Source

Dulles is described operating with virtually no congressional oversight, relying on Senate allies like Prescott Bush. Talbot notes the later Church and Pike Committees forced the CIA to account for these plots, though the agency downplayed its effectiveness.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Talbot notes CIA witnesses told Congress their assassins "had proved inept or were beaten to the punch" — a claim he says was "too modest."
