## Claim Statement

Talbot argues that although Harry Truman signed the CIA into existence under the National Security Act of 1947 intending it to be primarily a coordinator of intelligence for the White House, Allen Dulles — initially as a private citizen and CIA insider, then as deputy director — transformed it into "a Cold War colossus far more powerful and lethal than anything Truman had imagined": an aggressive, largely unaccountable covert-action apparatus that ran sabotage, subversion, and assassination operations and recruited ex-Nazis and other "useful people" without scruple.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The Prologue states that Dulles "stunned Harry Truman, who signed the CIA into existence in 1947, by turning the agency into a Cold War colossus far more powerful and lethal than anything Truman had imagined." Truman, Talbot writes, "took a dim view of a powerful spy agency, fearing that it might turn into a rogue outfit, and he insisted that the CIA serve primarily as a coordinator of intelligence reports for the White House" (Ch. 6).

Chapter 6 describes Dulles, as a private Wall Street lawyer, gathering OSS veterans — Richard Helms, Frank Wisner, Tracy Barnes, Kermit "Kim" Roosevelt — at his office and pushing the administration "to take full advantage of the broad powers granted the newborn CIA." In 1949 Dulles helped create the Office of Policy Coordination (OPC), "buried . . . in the State Department bureaucracy under a purposefully dull name," and maneuvered Wisner into running it; by 1952 the OPC ran "forty-seven overseas stations" with "nearly three thousand employees." Talbot writes that "Dulles and Wisner were essentially operating their own private spy agency," run "with little government oversight and few moral restrictions," many of whose "recruits were ex-Nazis." The chapter's title, "Useful People," refers to the ex-fascists and compromised assets the new establishment enlisted.

## Contexts Within the Source

Talbot frames the CIA's consolidation as the institutional core of the "secret government" thesis: an apparatus Dulles built to wage his own "no-holds-barred war with the Soviet bloc" outside presidential control, the same pattern of insubordination he had shown toward FDR in wartime Switzerland. He notes the OPC's tactics were nominally sanctioned by the NSC 10/2 memo, but says "it is uncertain how fully informed" Truman was about its exploits.
