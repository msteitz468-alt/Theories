## Claim Statement

In return for [[Karl Wolff]]'s cooperation on Operation Sunrise, Dulles offered the SS general immunity and protected him from prosecution at Nuremberg, where as Himmler's former chief of staff he "almost certainly would have been convicted of capital crimes." Wolff appeared at Nuremberg only as a witness; through Dulles's interventions he ultimately received a four-year denazification sentence reduced to time served, walking free in June 1949.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot states Dulles buried an incriminating OSS report blaming Wolff for the "wholesale slaughter of populations," substituting his own portrait of Wolff as a "moderate" and "gentleman." Two Swiss intermediaries later confirmed an immunity arrangement was made. Wolff's leverage is documented: in February 1947 he wrote President Truman that "I received from Mr. Dulles and his secretary, Mr. Gaevernitz, an explicit promise" of freedom for the Sunrise SS officers. A July 1942 Wolff letter surfaced at Nuremberg thanking a transport official that "a train has been leaving daily for Treblinka with 5,000 members of the chosen people."

## Contexts Within the Source

Talbot ties this to the wider pattern of [[The CIA recruited Nazis through the ratlines and the Gehlen Organization]]: Dulles's deputy Gero von Schulze-Gaevernitz and counterintelligence officer [[James Jesus Angleton]] are described protecting other Sunrise figures (Eugen Dollmann, Walter Rauff).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Dulles repeatedly insisted Wolff "never asked for such protection and he had never offered it," writing in his Sunrise memoir that "the conclusions [about Wolff] must be left to history." Talbot records but rejects this denial.
