## Claim Statement

The book's overarching argument is that by late 1963 a consensus had formed within America's "deep state" — the "subterranean network of financial, intelligence, and military interests" — that Kennedy was a national-security threat who "must be removed," and that Allen Dulles "was the only man with the stature, connections, and decisive will" to bring his overseas killing machine "home to Dallas."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "All that his establishment colleagues had to do was to look the other way—as they always did when Dulles took executive action." (Ch. 20)

Talbot catalogues elite hostility: the Rockefeller brothers' clashes with JFK over taxes, the Alliance for Progress, and Latin American oil; the *Wall Street Journal* and Luce press calling Kennedy a weak "dream world" leader; national-security figures like Angleton and LeMay viewing him as "a degenerate, and very likely a traitor." He notes Treasury Secretary C. Douglas Dillon — who oversaw the Secret Service — was conveniently abroad on November 22. He borrows "deep politics" from scholar Peter Dale Scott and cites Donald Gibson's framing of an "establishment crime."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Talbot explicitly concedes "there is no evidence that reigning corporate figures like David Rockefeller were part of the plot… or had foreknowledge," framing their role only as hostility that "emboldened" Dulles. See [[Controversy: Was Allen Dulles complicit in the Kennedy assassination?]].
