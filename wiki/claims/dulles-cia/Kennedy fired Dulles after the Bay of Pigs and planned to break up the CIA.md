## Claim Statement

After the failed [[Event: Bay of Pigs invasion (April 1961)]], President Kennedy forced Dulles out of the CIA in November 1961 — becoming "the first and only president who dared to strip Dulles of his formidable authority" — and intended to replace him with Richard Bissell and to break up the agency. Talbot quotes JFK's reported vow to "splinter the CIA in a thousand pieces and scatter it to the winds."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot recounts that Dulles, in unfinished drafts of an article "My Answer to the Bay of Pigs," inadvertently revealed how the CIA "contrived to lure the young president" into the invasion, confident JFK would be forced to commit U.S. military force when "the chips were down" — but Kennedy refused. Dulles called the Bay of Pigs "the blackest day of my life." Talbot says that rather than retiring quietly, Dulles "continued to operate as if he were still America's intelligence chief," turning his Georgetown home into "the center of an anti-Kennedy government in exile."

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the hinge of the book: Talbot argues the Kennedy–Dulles "duel" defined Washington's "deep politics" and set up the assassination thesis. Dulles had stacked the new administration with loyalists (McGeorge and William Bundy, Lemnitzer, Dean Rusk).
