## Claim Statement

As OSS station chief in Bern, Allen Dulles secretly negotiated with Nazi leaders for a separate peace that would remove Hitler but preserve the Reich's anti-Soviet apparatus — directly violating President Roosevelt's declared policy of unconditional surrender. Talbot calls this "Allen Dulles's reign of treason." Early talks (1943) ran through SS agent Prince Max Egon von Hohenlohe; the culminating effort was Operation Sunrise (1945) with SS general [[Karl Wolff]]. See [[Event: Operation Sunrise (1945)]].

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Talbot states Dulles "was more in step with many Nazi leaders than he was with President Roosevelt." After the January 1943 Casablanca Conference declared unconditional surrender, Dulles assured Hohenlohe the declaration was "merely a piece of paper to be scrapped without further ado if Germany would sue for peace." Dulles and Hohenlohe agreed the Soviet Union was the enemy, that Germany should be a bulwark against "the Bolshevik and Slavic menace," and "that there was probably no room for the Jewish people in postwar Europe."

> "Thus began Allen Dulles's reign of treason as America's top spy in Nazi-occupied Europe." (Ch. 1)

Dulles gave Hohenlohe the OSS code number 515. Talbot notes Supreme Court Justice Arthur Goldberg, who served with Dulles in the OSS, "later declared that both Dulleses were guilty of treason."

## Contexts Within the Source

Talbot frames this as continuous with Dulles's prewar business ties: through [[Sullivan & Cromwell]] he and brother [[John Foster Dulles]] had represented German cartels (IG Farben, Krupp). Dulles is said to have collaborated with [[Bank for International Settlements]] president Thomas McKittrick against Roosevelt's Project Safehaven, which sought to confiscate hidden Nazi assets.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Talbot notes the 1943 Hohenlohe initiative "went nowhere" while FDR lived and held firm; "there was nothing Dulles could do but bide his time."
