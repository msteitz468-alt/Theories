## Claim Statement

Huxley reports that under mescaline ordinary objects ceased to be utilitarian things and blazed with intrinsic significance — he perceived their **"Istigkeit" (is-ness)**, "the miracle, moment by moment, of naked existence." Place, distance, and time fell away; the mind perceived "in terms of intensity of existence, profundity of significance, relationships within a pattern." The title (from Blake) frames this as perception **cleansed** of the habitual reducing filter.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Gazing at a small vase of flowers: "I was not looking now at an unusual flower arrangement. I was seeing what Adam had seen on the morning of his creation — the miracle, moment by moment, of naked existence." Asked if it was agreeable, he answered: "it just is." He reaches for Eckhart's word: "*Istigkeit* — wasn't that the word Meister Eckhart liked to use? 'Is-ness'… a transience that was yet eternal life … pure Being … the divine source of all existence" — identifying it with "Sat Chit Ananda, Being-Awareness-Bliss."

Spatial and temporal categories dissolved: "Place and distance cease to be of much interest… The mind does its perceiving in terms of intensity of existence, profundity of significance." Of the chair legs: "how miraculous their tubularity, how supernatural their polished smoothness!" The essay's title comes from Blake's line that Huxley invokes — "If the doors of perception were cleansed every thing would appear to man as it is, Infinite" — and he repeats, "This is how one ought to see, how things really are."

## Contexts Within the Source

The first-person datum that the theory of [[Huxley: the brain is a "reducing valve" that filters Mind at Large]] is meant to explain, and the perception Huxley says the artist has "congenitally" — feeding [[Huxley: the visionary experience is the wellspring of art and religion]]. The "is-ness" of naked existence is his experiential touch-point with the [[The perennial philosophy|perennial philosophy]]'s divine Ground.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Huxley is candid that the cleansed vision came with a "profound change for the worse" in the will and an "enormous… irrelevance" of human relations — a one-sided contemplation "incompatible with action." He also notes the experience has a terrifying edge ("the brink of panic," the "Mysterium tremendum") and that for the unprepared or unwell it can become hellish rather than beatific.
