## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that the non-human intelligences encountered under DMT — the "machine elves," clownlike entities, and insectoid/reptilian teachers first named by Terence McKenna and documented at length in the earlier chapters of the book — are in fact the "ancient teachers of mankind" referenced in the book's subtitle. In Part V he synthesizes his case that these beings, consistently encountered across cultures and millennia in altered states of consciousness, have actively shaped humanity's symbolic, religious, and civilizational development. He further proposes that these intelligences may be encoded as interactive virtual teachers within human DNA, placed there by "clever entities" at the origin of life, and that accessing them through hallucinogens like DMT constitutes the "next stage in our evolution."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hancock's Part V concludes his synthesis of the "ancient teachers" hypothesis. He identifies the DMT beings with the non-human intelligences that recur across all shamanic and visionary traditions: "non-human, apparently non-physical intelligences, whose first interactions with us are documented in rock paintings of therianthropes dating back 35,000 years, and who inspired all the world's religious traditions, are still present amongst us today, following their own agenda according to their own timetable and purposes" (lines 5191–5192).

The title of Chapter 12 — "Amongst the Machine Elves" — adopts McKenna's coinage directly, and Part V explicitly recasts those same beings as teachers with an evolutionary agenda. Hancock quotes Rick Strassman's bafflement at "the surprising and remarkable consistencies among volunteers' reports of contact with nonmaterial beings . . . The highly intelligent beings of this 'other' world are interested in the subject, seemingly ready for his or her arrival and wasting no time in 'getting to work' . . . They . . . communicated with the volunteers, attempting to convey information by gestures, telepathy, or visual images. The purpose of contact was uncertain, but several subjects felt a benevolent attempt on the beings' part to improve us individually or as a race" (lines 5108–5109).

Strassman's volunteer Sara, quoted in Part V, reports: "They want us to come back and teach us and play with us. I want to go back and learn" (line 5092). Another volunteer, Jeremiah, announced mid-session: "I felt evolution occurring. These intelligences are looking over us" (lines 5170–5172) — which Hancock calls coming "as close to the truth as anyone."

Hancock proposes a speculative synthesis: "the blueprints were always present in some vital area of DNA right from the moment that life first began replicating on this planet, and that they were put there by 'clever entities' in the expectation that the processes of evolution would one day result in a creature that could understand and make use of them. If there is any truth to this scenario, then it could be that the next stage in our evolution depends on our willingness to give full attention to the ancient interactive teachers in our DNA" (lines 5168–5170).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the culminating synthesis of [[Supernatural (Hancock)]]. The "machine elves" encounter is described in detail in Chapter 12 and the DMT research chapters; Part V brings all strands together — shamanic spirit beings, fairy encounters, UFO abductions, and DMT entities — into a single framework. Hancock argues throughout (see [[Shamanism and altered states of consciousness (Hancock)]]) that all these phenomena represent contact with the same non-human intelligences, encountered through the same neurological doorway that hallucinogens open.

The claim connects to the book's framing question (introduced just before Part V at line 4941): "who are these ancient teachers, who some call 'gods,' and some call 'aliens' or 'spirits,' who come to us in altered states of consciousness with strange messages that have shaped the course of human history?" Part V is Hancock's answer.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock is careful to present this as speculation, not established fact. He acknowledges that Rick Strassman — one of the leading researchers whose DMT data he relies on — started from the assumption that the consistency of DMT reports "must arise from some biological cause," attributing them to hard-wired brain centers. Strassman himself is quoted asking skeptical questions: "How could anyone believe there were chunks of brain tissue that, when activated, flashed encounters with beings, experimentation and reprogramming?" (line 5114) — before admitting the biological explanation is insufficient.

Benny Shanon, also cited in Part V, resists the supernatural interpretation entirely, preferring to explain universal ayahuasca visions through "human creativity" and neuroanatomical structures (lines 5122–5138). Hancock acknowledges that the "inward generation" hypothesis — that these beings are somehow generated within the brain or genome — cannot be ruled out, even if he finds it less convincing than the parallel-dimension hypothesis.
