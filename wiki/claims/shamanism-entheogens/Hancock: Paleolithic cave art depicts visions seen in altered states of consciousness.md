## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that the great painted caves of the Upper Paleolithic — Chauvet, Lascaux, Pech Merle, Altamira, and roughly 300 other European sites — are not hunting-magic records but visual documents of trance visions experienced by shamans in altered states of consciousness. He grounds this thesis in David Lewis-Williams's neuropsychological model, which identifies "entoptic phenomena" (geometric phosphene patterns generated by the nervous system), therianthropic human-animal hybrid figures, and the deep, difficult-to-access locations of the paintings as convergent evidence that shamans entered the caves to memorialize hallucinations — and perhaps to contact supernatural beings believed to dwell within the rock itself. Hancock reports that this interpretation has been "the most widely accepted theory of cave art since the mid-1990s," supported by Lewis-Williams, Jean Clottes, and David Whitley, among other leading scholars.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hancock introduces the argument in Chapter Two while touring Pech Merle, noting the puzzle that scholars cannot explain "why the people of that period penetrated the deep limestone caves of France and Spain to make images in total darkness" (line 519). The cave, he observes, required prehistoric visitors to "negotiate perilous deadfalls, and crawl on their bellies through narrow and constricting slits and gullies" — evidence that visits were rare, deliberate, and ceremonially charged rather than casual.

He frames Lewis-Williams's theory explicitly in Chapter Three:

> "According to this theory prehistoric rock and cave art around the world expresses mankind's first and oldest notions of the supernatural, of the 'soul', and of realms of existence beyond death — notions that took shape in 'altered states of consciousness' most likely brought on by the consumption of psychoactive plants." (line 543)

Lewis-Williams's neuropsychological model, Hancock reports, rests on controlled experiments in which volunteers given hallucinogenic substances saw "various kinds of abstract geometrical patterns known as 'entoptic phenomena.'" Since "scientists believe that neither the average size of our brains nor their basic wiring has changed at all in the last 50,000 years," Lewis-Williams reasoned "that our prehistoric ancestors would have seen much the same geometric patterns had they entered hallucinatory states" (lines 1011–1013). Hancock then describes how the comparison of entoptic patterns with San rock art, Coso engravings, and Upper Paleolithic cave paintings — rendered as a side-by-side visual table in the text — demonstrates "strikingly identical" geometrical elements across all three traditions (line 1090).

The therianthropic figures — half-human, half-animal beings — supply a second strand of evidence. Hancock notes that the Sorcerer of Trois Frères cave has "the ears of a wolf, the eyes of an owl, the antlers of a stag, the tail of a horse, the claws of a lion, and the feet, legs and body of a human being" (line 979), matching the kinds of hybrid entities he himself encountered under ayahuasca. He writes that Lewis-Williams's colleagues concluded that "as for the monstrous half-man, half-beast therianthropes, the shamans believed that to enter the otherworld they had to adopt various animal forms and the paintings depicted them at various stages of their transformation" (line 1092).

The "wounded man" figures — human forms pierced by spear-like lines, found at Pech Merle, Cougnac, Gabillou, and southern African rock-shelter sites — provide a third pillar. Lewis-Williams interprets these, Hancock reports, as the graphical expression of somatic hallucinations: "hallucinations 'of an alarming stretching of the scalp; sometimes it seems as if a fish-hook is dragging the scalp as much as 30 cm above the head'" and "stabbing and agonizing pains" reported by shamans worldwide (lines 1168, 1158). Lewis-Williams concludes, as Hancock quotes him:

> "The neuropsychological and ethnographic evidence that I have adduced strongly suggests that, in these subterranean images, we have an ancient and unusually explicit expression of a complex shamanic experience that is informed by altered states of consciousness. That experience comprised isolation and sensory deprivation by entrance into an underground realm, 'death' by a painful ordeal of hallucinatory multiple piercing, and emergence from those dark regions of an inspired, reborn shaman." (line 1276)

Hancock ties the specific choice of deep caves to the phenomenology of trance: "Many laboratory subjects report experiencing a vortex or rotating tunnel that seems to surround them . . . The sides of the vortex are marked by a lattice of squares like television screens" (line 1304). Upper Paleolithic people "looking for locations in the everyday world where they could best reproduce their impressions of the vortex as they entered the spirit world" would have found "the walls and ceilings of the deep dark caves" a natural choice (line 1308). He concludes that the caves were perceived as "portals to a supernatural nether realm" and that "shamans in suitably altered states of consciousness inside the caves would have seen utterly convincing visions of the denizens of the spirit world 'floating' through the thin membrane of the rock walls and beginning to materialize before their eyes surrounded by scintillating geometrical patterns" (line 1312).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the central structural pillar of Part I and of [[Supernatural (Hancock)]] as a whole. It supports — and is supported by — [[Shamanism and altered states of consciousness (Hancock)]], which supplies the broader cross-cultural shamanic framework. The neuropsychological model also feeds directly into [[Hancock: psychoactive plants sparked the symbolic "creative explosion" in human evolution]], where Hancock asks whether access to altered states was the catalyst for the Upper Paleolithic behavioral revolution. The San bushmen ethnography that Lewis-Williams used to decode rock-art symbolism is treated by Hancock as a bridge between the deep prehistoric record and documented, living shamanic traditions.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock acknowledges that Lewis-Williams's theory, while now mainstream among cave-art specialists, is "not to the liking of some scholars" (line 226). He also notes a significant methodological weakness: Lewis-Williams himself has never consumed any psychoactive plants, entered trance by dancing, or subjected himself to any altered state of consciousness, making him, in Hancock's view, an unsuitable judge of whether such experiences could have convinced our ancestors of the reality of a spirit world. Lewis-Williams told Hancock flatly that the spirit world encountered in trance is "just a silly illusion" (line 559) — a position Hancock contests, though he is careful to separate Lewis-Williams's evidential case (which he endorses) from Lewis-Williams's metaphysical conclusion (which he rejects). Hancock also notes that we "cannot prove" caves were used shamanically in exactly the ways he describes, and that some scholars reject any overarching theory of cave art, preferring simply "to spend their time accumulating and sorting data" (line 427).
