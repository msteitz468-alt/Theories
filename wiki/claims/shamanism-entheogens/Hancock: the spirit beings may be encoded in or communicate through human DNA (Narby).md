## Claim Statement

Hancock develops the "cosmic serpent" thesis of anthropologist Jeremy Narby, arguing that the twin-serpent / double-helix imagery recurrent in shamanic ayahuasca and DMT visions may not be coincidental but may reflect a real connection between altered-state consciousness and DNA. Narby's central claim — which Hancock endorses as a serious working hypothesis — is that DNA is in some way "minded" and may encode intelligent messages from "clever entities" who originally designed it; hallucinogens like ayahuasca temporarily retune the brain to access that information. Hancock reinforces this with Rick Strassman's DMT research, multiple volunteers' visions of DNA spirals, his own DMT experience of "the dance of DNA," the linguistic structure of non-coding ("junk") DNA, and Francis Crick's reported use of LSD at the moment of discovering the double helix. The beings shamans encounter in trance may, on this view, be the very "clever entities" whose messages are encoded in our DNA — or they may be the experiential form in which that information presents itself to human consciousness.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Hancock's own visions.** During his second DMT session, Hancock reports seeing "pairs of colorful writhing snakes wrapped around one another, drawn down to a minute, submicroscopic scale, as though I were being allowed to peer deep into the nucleus of a cell and to witness the dance of DNA — the ultimate 'Master of Transformations'" (line 3216). He had earlier seen an "ivory-colored, organic-looking tube" covered in scrolling "scripts, signs, and numbers" (line 3214). Checking his ayahuasca notebooks from the Amazon, he found an almost identical vision: "snakes arrange themselves into patterns of interlaced wheels and spirals. Then they merge into one large mass and finally break apart into pairs of individuals that wind around each other like the DNA double helix" (lines 3614–3616).

**Strassman's volunteers and DNA imagery.** Several of Rick Strassman's DMT subjects independently reported similar imagery: Karl saw "spirals of what looked like DNA, red and green"; Cleo saw "a spiral DNA-type thing made out of incredibly bright cubes"; Sara said "I felt the DMT release my soul's energy and push it through the DNA"; Rex believed the insect-beings he encountered had "manipulated my DNA and changed its structure" (lines 3622–3460). Hancock notes: "many of them had also spoken of DNA" (line 3622).

**Narby's thesis.** The anthropologist Jeremy Narby, drawing on years of fieldwork in the Amazon and his own ayahuasca experiences, argued in *The Cosmic Serpent* (1995) that the double helix — "an extremely sophisticated technology" whose "cell-based life DNA informs made the air we breathe, the landscape we see, and the mind-boggling diversity of living beings" — is also the source of shamanic visions. Narby told Hancock directly, at a meeting in Switzerland on May 27, 2004:

> "Correct me if I'm wrong, but when I read *Cosmic Serpent*, if I were to take your idea as far as it would go, it's almost that there's a message, encoded in our DNA, which we can read, or see, or interact with through ayahuasca. Is that right?"
> "Or a bunch of messages. I think that we haven't even found all that goes with it."
> "Who put the message there?" I asked.
> "I don't have the faintest," Narby replied, "but I think it was some entity, or several entities, that were rather clever." (lines 3696–3702)

Hancock reports that Narby's hypothesis is that ayahuasca "opens a door to nonmaterial levels of reality where that mind can be contacted" and that shamans have accessed hidden messages in DNA for millennia (lines 3710–3718).

**Junk DNA and the language argument.** Hancock cites research by Eugene Stanley (Boston University / Harvard Medical School) who, applying Zipf's Law to DNA base sequences, found that non-coding ("junk") DNA — constituting 90–97 per cent of the human genome — obeys the same mathematical law as all human languages, while coding regions do not. Stanley concluded that the non-coding regions contain "'a structured language fundamentally unlike the coding in genes'" and stated: "'the junk DNA may carry some kind of message'" (lines 4099, 4036). When Hancock contacted Stanley in 2005, he confirmed: "'You bet I stand by them! Nothing has been refuted'" (line 4117). Hancock comments: "It is difficult to see how the accidental processes of chemistry alone could have produced the intense language-like organization embedded in the so-called junk sequences" (line 4133).

**Crick's LSD experience.** Hancock notes that Francis Crick, co-discoverer of the double helix, reportedly used LSD as a "thinking tool" at the Cavendish Laboratory in the early 1950s and, according to a report in *The Mail on Sunday* (August 8, 2004), "had privately admitted to colleagues that he was under the influence of LSD in 1953 at the moment when he 'perceived the double helix shape' and unraveled the structure of DNA" (lines 3846–3850). Crick later published the hypothesis that DNA had been seeded on Earth by an alien civilization — "directed panspermia" — which Hancock notes is structurally similar to Narby's suggestion that DNA was designed by "clever entities" and to indigenous Amazonian cosmologies (lines 3876–3890, 3892–3894).

**The convergence.** Hancock concludes:

> "Perhaps our ancestors' discovery of trance techniques and widespread use of hallucinogens not only shattered five million years of mental rigidity with extraordinary and life-changing experiences, but also gave them access to specific information, recorded billions of years previously in their DNA, deposited there by Narby's 'clever entities,' to await the evolution of creatures that could make use of it." (lines 3732–3734)

And, at the close of Chapter Fourteen:

> "It may be the case that hallucinations of the sort that convey veridical knowledge about DNA or about plants, or about how to cure a certain sickness, or about the nature of reality, are as effective a technology as bio-engineering and genetic manipulation for exploring the true potential of the legacy stored inside all our cells. It may be, in other words, that the ancient teachers of mankind have been inside us all along but that we must enter altered states of consciousness in order to hear what they have to say." (lines 4165–4165)

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the climactic thesis of Part III and is developed across Chapters Eleven through Fourteen. It builds on [[Shamanism and altered states of consciousness (Hancock)]], which establishes the cross-cultural consistency of visions (including serpent imagery), and on [[Hancock: DMT and other hallucinogens grant access to seemingly autonomous intelligent beings]], which supplies Strassman's data on reliably encountered entities. Hancock positions the DNA hypothesis as a complement — not a replacement — to his parallel proposal that spirit worlds may exist as genuinely freestanding other dimensions. Both models, he argues, could explain why shamans across cultures access the same beings, the same imagery, and the same practical knowledge (plant medicines, cosmological maps).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock is careful to present this as a hypothesis rather than a proven fact. He acknowledges that mainstream evolutionary science holds that "blind Darwinian forces of natural selection alone are quite sufficient to explain the structure and workings of DNA and its involvement with life" (line 3712), and would not accept the idea of DNA as either intentionally designed or "minded." He notes that Narby "nowhere commits as to the exact status or provenance of these entities" (line 3718). The Crick/LSD story rests on a tabloid news report, not a peer-reviewed source, and Hancock flags that he is citing it as suggestive rather than conclusive (lines 3832–3850). He also acknowledges that Francis Crick himself, a committed atheist, "did not believe in the supernatural" and developed his panspermia theory partly as a rational alternative to any spiritual explanation (lines 4017–4019). Stanley's linguistic study of junk DNA is real peer-reviewed science (1994, *Science*), but Hancock notes the researchers themselves described it as a possibility — "may carry some kind of message" — not a certainty.
