## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that the Upper Paleolithic "symbolic revolution" — the sudden, dramatic appearance of sophisticated art, religion, and fully modern human behavior in the archaeological record approximately 40,000–30,000 years ago — was catalyzed, at least in part, by humanity's discovery of and engagement with psychoactive plants that induced altered states of consciousness. He contends that anatomically modern humans had existed for roughly 160,000 years before this explosion with brains identical to ours yet behaved as if they were "almost like another species," and that something — most plausibly the encounter with trance states and the visions they produced — provided the missing "x-factor" that triggered the leap. Hancock reports this not as established fact but as a hypothesis he finds highly plausible and underexplored by mainstream science.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hancock establishes the evolutionary puzzle at the opening of his research narrative, explaining that his ibogaine experiment was undertaken "as part of a wider, longer-term investigation into a mysterious 'before and after moment' that took place in human prehistory, perhaps as recently as 40,000 years ago. Before it, other than a very few widely scattered and isolated examples, there is nothing in the archaeological record left by our ancestors that we would instantly recognize as modern human behavior. After it, the signs that creatures exactly like us have arrived are everywhere, most notably in the first definite evidence for beliefs in supernatural realms and beings — evidence, in other words, for the birth of religion" (line 221).

He elaborates the puzzle in Chapter Two, pointing out that anatomically modern humans had achieved "full anatomical modernity" by 196,000 years ago with "the same large, complex brains as we do," yet symbolic behavior did not appear until much later and did not reach "critical mass" until 40,000–30,000 years ago (lines 433–493). As Stanford Professor Richard Klein is quoted as observing: after this point, "ever more closely packed cultural revolutions have taken humanity from the status of a relatively large mammal to something more like a geologic force" (line 493). There was, crucially, "no increase in human brain size between 100,000 and 40,000 years ago" that might explain the leap (line 495). Hancock frames this as "the greatest riddle of archaeology" in the words of David Lewis-Williams, and "the question of questions in paleoanthropology" in the words of Ian Tattershall of the American Museum of Natural History (line 513).

His proposed answer builds through Chapters Three and Four. Lewis-Williams's neuropsychological model leads Hancock to conclude that "somewhere after 50,000 years ago, but before 30,000 years ago, neurologically modern humans in Europe . . . discovered and began to exploit a previously untapped capacity of their brains — the ability to enter altered states of consciousness and to experience extremely convincing hallucinations" (line 1284). He considers multiple routes into trance — dancing, starvation, sensory deprivation in caves — but gives particular weight to psychoactive plants:

> "Equally likely . . . is that anatomically modern humans present in Europe around 40,000 years ago encountered a potent natural hallucinogen — perhaps the mushroom Psilocybe semilanceata, or perhaps another native Old World mushroom containing psilocybin . . . A number of other European plants, notably members of the Solanaceae (potato) family, such as datura, mandrake, henbane, and belladonna, are also powerfully psychotropic and would undoubtedly have produced the sorts of visions and experiences of a parallel spirit world that the cave paintings seem to reflect." (lines 1289–1294)

Hancock argues that "most likely, plant hallucinogens were discovered by hunter-gatherers who initially tried them out as a food; but, whatever the reason, those who ate these substances entered trance and experienced visions of a seamlessly convincing and terrifyingly real supernatural otherworld" — and that this encounter gave birth to religion, to art, and to the complex symbolic culture of the Upper Paleolithic (line 1296).

The boldest statement of the thesis appears near the end of Chapter Four, where Hancock steps beyond Lewis-Williams's deliberately non-supernatural framing to propose that altered-state exploration

> "played the catalytic role in extracting our ancestors from the five-million-year torpor of the hominid line, galvanized their intuition and creativity, helped them to build a stable and nurturing, non-violent society that lasted for 25,000 years — in itself a staggering accomplishment — and set them on a dramatically new course of evolution." (lines 1314–1318)

He also raises the possibility that supernatural beings contacted in these trance states were themselves real, active agents in human evolution — "that our ancestors experience their great evolutionary leap forward of the last 50,000 years not just because of the beneficial social and organizational by-products of shamanism but because they were literally helped, taught, prompted, and inspired by supernatural agents" (line 1364). He flags this as speculative and acknowledges it "sounds preposterous at first to anyone educated in the Western logical positivist tradition" (line 1366).

His personal ayahuasca and ibogaine experiments in the Amazon and in Bath run through Part I as first-person evidence that plant-induced visions are powerful enough to have convinced our ancestors of a spirit world. After his ayahuasca sessions, he asks: "If I, with all the knowledge and rationalism of the twenty-first century at my disposal, could be so persuaded by the apparently supernatural realms and spirit beings that ayahuasca introduced me to, then what would our ancestors have thought if they had chanced upon similar hallucinogenic plants 30,000 years ago and seen similar wonders?" (line 789).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the overarching explanatory goal of [[Supernatural (Hancock)]]; the cave-art argument in [[Hancock: Paleolithic cave art depicts visions seen in altered states of consciousness]] is the primary evidence Hancock marshals in its support. [[Shamanism and altered states of consciousness (Hancock)]] supplies the cross-cultural framework — shamanic initiation, spirit journeys, and trance-induced visions as universals of human neurology — that makes the hypothesis plausible across time and geography. The claim also connects forward to later sections of the book (beyond the assigned range) dealing with DMT, fairies, and alien contact as possible modern manifestations of the same altered-state phenomena.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock acknowledges several serious objections. First, the timing problem: anatomically modern humans existed with identical brains for over 100,000 years before the creative explosion, and psychoactive plants would presumably have been available throughout that period — so the mere availability of plants cannot alone explain why the explosion happened when it did. Hancock does not resolve this fully. Second, Lewis-Williams himself — the chief academic proponent of the altered-states theory of cave art — is "of absolutely one mind with the mass of scientists" in denying any real supernatural dimension to the visions, insisting that "the entire inspiration for 25,000 years of Upper Paleolithic cave paintings reduces to nothing more than the fevered illusions of disturbed brain chemistry" (line 1354–1356). Hancock accepts Lewis-Williams's evidential case for the role of trance but rejects his metaphysical conclusion that the visions are merely hallucination. Third, the hypothesis that supernatural "teachers" actively participated in human evolution is Hancock's own extrapolation beyond the archaeological record and is presented by him as speculative inquiry, not established argument. He concedes that "even to whisper such questions amounts to scientific sacrilege of the worst order" (line 1324).
