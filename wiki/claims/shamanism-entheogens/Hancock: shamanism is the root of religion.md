## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that all the world's major religions, without exception, ultimately descend from shamanic altered-state experiences and encounters with supernatural beings — and that organized religion is, at its core, the bureaucratization of shamanism. The original founders of every religion were, in his view, "shamans of the highest order" who experienced genuine visionary states (most likely induced or facilitated by psychoactive plants or other trance techniques) and whose reports of those encounters crystallized into doctrine. The priests, ministers, rabbis, and mullahs who inherited these traditions have, Hancock argues, lost access to the primary experiences and function merely as administrators of systems they can no longer refresh from the inside. This pattern is documented through Christianity, Judaism, Islam, ancient Greek mystery religion, Vedic Hinduism, and ancient Maya and Egyptian religion.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The core argument.** Hancock opens Chapter Fifteen by noting that "if we probe deeply enough into the foundations of all the world's great religions, is that they rest upon a bedrock of supernatural encounters and experiences involving powerful and charismatic individuals." He continues:

> "Although such beliefs quickly crystallize into dogmas passed on from generation to generation, it is clear, even from the few examples given above, that they were not originally conjured out of thin air, or arrived at through scholarly study, or deliberately devised to assuage supposed human needs, but that they arose in every case solely out of attempts to describe, depict, and explain the supernatural experiences of their founders — who were, by any standards, shamans of the highest order." (line 4189)

He cites anthropologist Weston La Barre: "'All our knowledge of the supernatural derives de facto from the statements made by religious visionaries and ecstatics, i.e. prophets and shamans . . . Priests only administrate the ecclesia established on this supernatural basis'" (line 4191).

**The bureaucratization of shamanism.** Hancock argues that the "salaried priests, ministers, rabbis, mullahs, and bishops" who now run the world's religions "are not shamans. On the contrary, they are no more likely than most members of their congregations to encounter spirits in their daily experience. They teach, and adhere, often rigidly, to what they themselves have been taught about the supernatural, but they have no special abilities and have practiced none of the special skills needed to penetrate supernatural realms and return with hard-won knowledge of real value to their communities" (lines 4195–4196). He adds: "Shamanic ecstasy lies at the root of all religions and, as Weston La Barre admits, 'the nature of the shamanic ecstasy may be illuminated by attention to ancient hallucinogens'" (line 4201).

**Christianity.** Hancock argues Christ was "so obviously and so profoundly a shaman": his "ordeal of crucifixion and piercing, followed by death and subsequent resurrection as a spiritualized being equipped with the power to save souls, is essentially the story of the wounded man — the story that is told by all shamans everywhere of their own initiatory agonies, death, and resurrection" (line 4211). Early Gnostic Christians, he reports, "believed that salvation was to be attained through a special sort of 'knowledge of the true nature of things' that could not be taught but only revealed directly to the initiate" — a directly shamanic epistemology (line 4213). Catholic suppression of Gnostic and direct-visionary Christianity from the fourth century AD onwards is presented as the decisive moment at which the shamanic original was replaced by institutional religion (lines 4227–4229). Shamanic vestiges survive, Hancock argues, in the cults of saints (St. Sebastian, pierced by arrows, mirrors the universal "wounded man" figure), in Santa Claus (traced to Siberian shamanic fly-agaric mushroom ceremonies), and in the visions of Jeanne d'Arc and Bernadette Soubirous, both of whom displayed classic shamanic trance states and encounters with supernatural entities (lines 4231–4377).

**Muhammad and Islam; Moses and Judaism.** Hancock notes that Muhammad's encounters with "a majestic supernatural being . . . whose apparitions were preceded by 'a peculiar sound like the tinkling of bells'" match the structure of shamanic initiations; that the Koran is said to be "'a perfect transcription of an eternal tablet preserved in heaven'" — paralleling the shamanic pattern of a book shown by spirits in the otherworld (line 4179). He similarly connects Moses's "burning bush" vision and the "wheels of Ezekiel" to ayahuasca-like altered states (lines 4447–4451, 4433–4445). The Hebrew scholar Benny Shanon, Professor of Psychology at the Hebrew University in Jerusalem, is cited for the argument that "the origins of the Hebrew religion are rooted in experiences of this sort . . . made possible — as they still are today in the Amazon — by the consumption of psychoactive plants," specifically acacia and Peganum harmala (Syrian rue), which together produce "a biochemical configuration identical to that produced by the Amazonian plants of which ayahuasca is made" (lines 4447–4451).

**Eleusis and ancient Greece.** Hancock argues that the Eleusinian Mysteries — the most important religious institution of classical Greece, which initiated Plato, Aristotle, and Sophocles among tens of thousands of others over perhaps 2,000 years — were "communal shamanic ceremonies involving the ingestion of drugs." The sacred potion *kykeon*, drunk by every initiate, contained hallucinogenic alkaloids from ergot-contaminated barley, as identified by mycologist R. Gordon Wasson, LSD discoverer Albert Hoffman, and classicist Carl Ruck (lines 4474–4531).

**Vedic Hinduism.** Similarly, Hancock reports that the soma of the Rigveda — a drink described as both a god and a consciousness-altering plant, responsible for visions of flight, light, and divine contact — has been identified by Wasson as the hallucinogenic fly agaric mushroom Amanita muscaria, whose unique ability to retain psychoactivity through the urinary tract matches obscure Vedic descriptions of priests urinating "on-flowing soma" (lines 4533–4587).

**Ancient Maya and Egypt.** In Chapter Sixteen, Hancock extends the argument to pre-Columbian Central America and ancient Egypt: "it is certain that at some stage, and for a very long while, shamanism, the techniques of shamanism, the experiences of shamanism, and the discoveries of shamanism played a central role in the evolution of both of them" (line 4601). Mayan therianthropic art, vision-serpent imagery, Books of the Dead, and the hero twins' ordeal in Xibalba are all interpreted as religious crystallizations of shamanic trance states and encounters with otherworldly beings.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the culminating argument of [[Supernatural (Hancock)]] and runs throughout all of Part IV. It depends on [[Shamanism and altered states of consciousness (Hancock)]] for the cross-cultural framework of shamanic experience, and on [[Hancock: DMT and other hallucinogens grant access to seemingly autonomous intelligent beings]] for the mechanism by which visionary encounters — including encounters with beings construed as gods, angels, or spirits — are generated. The claim also links back to the Part I thesis that religion itself began in the painted caves: "Since religious beliefs are so important, it should not be controversial to state clearly what the evidence shows about their ultimate source and inspiration" (line 4183).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock notes that the argument faces "stiff resistance from a faction of archaeologists who appear to object in principle to the notion that the first religious experiences of our ancestors were induced by hallucinogens" (line 4591). He acknowledges that mainstream scholars, including many theologians and historians of religion, reject the shamanic-origins model. The claim that Christ was a shaman and that early Christianity was overtly shamanistic is a strongly revisionist reading; Hancock does not pretend it is the established view. He also concedes uncertainty about the specific trance techniques used by the Gnostics and does not claim to know whether Bernadette Soubirous used any psychoactive plant (lines 4305–4323). The link between Moses and ayahuasca-like plants is described by Shanon as a "tentative hypothesis" (line 4447), not proven history.
