## Claim Statement

Eliade argues that shamanic traditions across the world share a common cosmological structure in which the universe is divided into three superimposed regions — sky, earth, and underworld — connected by a central vertical axis. This axis is variously embodied as the World Tree, the Cosmic Pillar, or the Cosmic Mountain, and it passes through a "hole" or opening that allows communication and passage between the zones. The shaman is the specialist who exploits this cosmological architecture: he ascends through the axis to the sky to meet the gods, or descends through it to the underworld to combat demons and recover lost souls.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The three-zone cosmological scheme and the shaman's vertical journeys through it are the structural backbone of the book. In the Epilogue Eliade summarizes the shaman's core capacity as the ability to "go up into the sky and meet the gods, to descend to the underworld and fight the demons, sickness, and death" (line 10544). He describes how "the shaman's adventures in the other world, the ordeals that he undergoes in his ecstatic descents below and ascents to the sky" provide the source material for an entire mythology of the beyond (line 10548). Eliade likewise notes that "in all probability many features of 'funerary geography,' as well as some themes of the mythology of death, are the result of the ecstatic experiences of shamans" who travel through the three zones and return to describe them (line 10546).

In an extended endnote in the book's final apparatus (line 10531) Eliade distinguishes true shamanic ecstasy from mere "possession" precisely by reference to this cosmological movement: "while the shaman's soul (or 'principal soul') was traveling in the upper or lower worlds, 'spirits' could take possession of his body," but possession forecloses the defining shamanic act — "his ascent to the sky or descent to the underworld." The contrast makes clear that vertical movement across the three zones is what defines the shaman's vocation.

The book's table of contents identifies discrete chapters on "The Three Cosmic Zones and the World Pillar," "The Cosmic Mountain," and "The World Tree," and the index entry for "Axis, Cosmic/axis mundi/Axis of World" (line 15479) references the concept across more than a dozen sections of the main text. Eliade's discussion of the Altai séance — in which the shaman ritually climbs a birch tree whose notches represent the successive heavens — exemplifies the physical enactment of passage along this axis, a motif he traces from Siberia and Central Asia to the Americas, Oceania, and South Asia.

## Contexts Within the Source

This cosmological claim is the structural precondition for virtually every other thesis in the book. The shaman's ability to heal, retrieve lost souls, escort the dead, and receive divinatory knowledge all depend on his command of the vertical axis connecting the three worlds. The concept of the "Center of the World" — the point at which the axis pierces each zone, and where passage between them is possible — is Eliade's term for the sacred geography that makes shamanic technique possible. This claim connects directly to [[Shamanism as archaic technique of ecstasy (Eliade)]], which characterizes shamanism as the mastery of ecstasy rather than mere possession or trance.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Eliade acknowledges that the specific cosmological schema — particularly the number of heavens (seven or nine) and the precise identity of the axis (birch tree, mountain, pillar) — varies across cultures and has been historically diffused and modified by contact with Near Eastern and Buddhist cosmologies. He notes that Oriental religious ideas "penetrated far into Central and North Asia and contributed considerably to giving Central Asian and Siberian shamanism their present features." However, he argues this historical diffusion only modified the "ritual formula and cosmological implications of the celestial ascent"; the underlying three-zone structure and the axis appear cross-culturally in regions where such diffusion is excluded, suggesting a "primordial" structure rather than a purely historical one. Scholars who would restrict shamanism to Siberia would contest the universalizing claim.
