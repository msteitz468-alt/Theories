## Claim Statement

Eliade argues that the most precise and least hazardous definition of shamanism is "technique of ecstasy." He insists that the shaman must be distinguished from all other magicians, sorcerers, medicine men, and spirit-mediums by one specific criterion: the shaman specializes in a trance during which his soul is believed to leave the body and ascend to the sky or descend to the underworld. Crucially, the shaman is not a passive vessel for spirit-possession but an active master who controls spirits without becoming their instrument. This makes the shaman, above all else, the great specialist in the human soul and the community's mediator between living and dead, terrestrial and celestial.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Eliade stakes his definitional claim at the close of the book's opening section. Having surveyed the confusion caused by using "shaman," "sorcerer," and "medicine man" interchangeably, he writes: "Throughout the immense area comprising Central and North Asia, the magico-religious life of society centers on the shaman... the shaman, and he alone, is the great master of ecstasy. A first definition of this complex phenomenon, and perhaps the least hazardous, will be: shamanism = technique of ecstasy."

He sharpens the distinction between the shaman and other religious specialists: "the shaman specializes in a trance during which his soul is believed to leave his body and ascend to the sky or descend to the underworld." This soul-flight is what separates the shaman from possession-mediums — "the shaman controls his 'spirits,' in the sense that he, a human being, is able to communicate with the dead, 'demons,' and 'nature spirits,' without thereby becoming their instrument." Eliade acknowledges shamans are "sometimes found to be 'possessed,' but these are exceptional cases for which there is a particular explanation."

The shaman's social role follows directly from this ecstatic specialization: "The shaman is the great specialist in the human soul; he alone 'sees' it, for he knows its 'form' and its destiny." Eliade positions shamanism among mysticism rather than religion broadly construed: "it would be more correct to class shamanism among the mysticisms than with what is commonly called a religion... shamanism always remains an ecstatic technique at the disposal of a particular elite and represents, as it were, the mysticism of the particular religion." The shaman "cures... accompanies their dead to the 'Realm of Shades,' and serves as mediators between them and their gods, celestial or infernal, greater or lesser."

In the Foreword Eliade makes the term's scope explicit: "shamanism is precisely one of the archaic techniques of ecstasy—at once mysticism, magic, and 'religion' in the broadest sense of the term."

## Contexts Within the Source

This thesis is the organizing spine of the entire book. Every subsequent chapter — on initiatory sickness, shamanic costume, celestial ascents, descents to the underworld, and global parallels — is an elaboration of what it means to be a "master of ecstasy." Eliade's insistence on soul-flight as the defining feature (rather than possession, trance in general, or magical power) shapes all his comparisons across Siberian, Australian, North and South American, and Oceanic shamanism. See also [[Shamanism as archaic technique of ecstasy (Eliade)]] for the concept-hub treatment of this thesis.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Eliade himself notes that the definition, while "least hazardous," is necessarily a simplification: "shamanism coexists with other forms of magic and religion" and is "not necessarily the religion" of Central and North Asia. He acknowledges that some shamans are found in states of possession rather than deliberate soul-flight, treating these as exceptional. The 2004 foreword by Wendy Doniger flags a broader scholarly objection: Eliade's cross-cultural universalism rests on assumptions about "primitive" peoples that are no longer accepted, and critics such as Guilford Dudley accused him of being "the antihistorian of religions" for subordinating historical specificity to synchronic pattern. Eliade's own caveat is that his archetypes are not Jungian universals but structural recurrences that can nonetheless be modified by history.
