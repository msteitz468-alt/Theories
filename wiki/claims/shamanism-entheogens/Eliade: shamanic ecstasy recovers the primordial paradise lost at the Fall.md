## Claim Statement

Eliade argues that the shaman's ecstatic ascent to the sky is not merely a technical feat but carries a deep mythological meaning: it reactualizes the primordial condition of humanity before a mythical rupture — figured as a "fall" or "ritual fault" — severed easy communication between heaven and earth. The shaman alone, through ecstasy, is temporarily able to return to that paradisal state. This interpretive framework is Eliade's master key: shamanism, wherever it appears worldwide, is understood as a collective "nostalgia for paradise," a nostalgic attempt to recover the beatific illud tempus ("that time") when gods, spirits, and mortals could move freely between the three cosmic zones.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The thesis is stated with particular clarity in the chapter on symbols of passage and ascent, where Eliade writes: "what the shaman can do today in ecstasy could, at the dawn of time, be done by all human beings in concreto; they went up to heaven and came down again without recourse to trance. Temporarily and for a limited number of persons — the shamans — ecstasy re-establishes the primordial condition of all mankind. In this respect, the mystical experience of the 'primitives' is a return to origins, a reversion to the mystical age of the lost paradise." The same passage makes the myth of rupture explicit: "the symbolism of the 'Center of the World' is also indissolubly connected with the myth of a primordial time when communications between heaven and earth, gods and mortals, were not merely possible but easy and within reach of all mankind. The myths we have just reviewed generally refer to this primordial illud tempus, but some of them tell of a celestial ascent performed by a hero or sovereign or sorcerer after communication was broken off; in other words, they imply the possibility, for certain privileged or elect persons, of returning to the origin of time, of recovering the mythical and paradisal moment before the 'fall,' that is, before the break in communications between heaven and earth."

In the Conclusions, Eliade expands this into a structural argument. A "reduction or even the total loss in religious currency of uranian Supreme Beings is sometimes indicated in myths concerning a primordial and paradisal time when communications between heaven and earth were easy and accessible to everyone; as the result of some happening (especially of a ritual fault), these communications were broken off and the Supreme Beings withdrew to the highest sky." The shaman's ecstasy is therefore "a reactualization of the mythical illud tempus when men could communicate in concreto with the sky." Eliade is unequivocal: "It is indubitable that the celestial ascent of the shaman (or the medicine man, the magician, etc.) is a survival, profoundly modified and sometimes degenerated, of this archaic religious ideology centered on faith in a celestial Supreme Being and belief in concrete communications between heaven and earth."

The Epilogue crystallizes the affective dimension of this interpretation: "More than once we have discerned in the shamanic experience a 'nostalgia for paradise' that suggests one of the oldest types of Christian mystical experience." This single phrase — "nostalgia for paradise" — anchors Eliade's entire comparative interpretation of shamanism as a universal longing to undo the original cosmic rupture.

An earlier passage underlines that this myth of celestial ascent is introduced already in the context of shamanic vocation: "the shamanic experience is equivalent to a restoration of that primordial mythical time and the shaman figures as a privileged being who individually returns to the fortunate condition of humanity at the dawn of time. Many myths, some of which will be recounted or referred to in the following chapters, illustrate this paradisal state of a beatific illud tempus, to which shamans intermittently return during their ecstasies."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is Eliade's central interpretive conclusion for the book as a whole, synthesizing the ethnographic surveys of every region into a single hermeneutic. It connects to [[Shamanism as archaic technique of ecstasy (Eliade)]] — the defining concept that ecstasy (specifically the soul-journey or ascent) is the differentiating mark of shamanism — and to [[Eliade: shamanism is a universal archaic phenomenon found worldwide]], since the universality of shamanism is precisely what allows the "nostalgia for paradise" interpretation to function cross-culturally. The Tibetan evidence (sovereigns ascending to heaven by the primordial rope named dmu-t'ag, with communications never wholly broken for kings) is cited as a surviving trace of the myth. The Ghost Dance movement is read similarly: it "reactualized the mythical illud tempus when communications with the sky, the Great God, and the dead were accessible to every human being."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Eliade acknowledges that the myth of primordial paradise and its rupture is not uniform: not all shamanic cultures articulate the ecstasy explicitly as a recovery of a lost state, and the mythological "fall" narrative is itself subject to historical layering and regional variation. He also notes that many modern shamanic forms have been so heavily transformed — by the ancestor cult, by spirit possession, by Buddhist and Lamaist influences — that the original ascensional ideology has been obscured or "degenerated." His caveat that the celestial ascent of the shaman is "a survival, profoundly modified and sometimes degenerated" implicitly acknowledges that the clean myth-of-paradise framework is a scholarly reconstruction rather than a universally self-conscious native claim.
