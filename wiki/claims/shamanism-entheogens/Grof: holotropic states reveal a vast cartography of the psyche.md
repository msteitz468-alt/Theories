## Claim Statement

Stanislav Grof argues that systematic work with LSD and other holotropic states of consciousness reveals that the psyche is far larger than mainstream psychiatry acknowledges. His research compelled him to expand the conventional map of the mind — limited to postnatal biography and the Freudian individual unconscious — by adding two vast transbiographical domains: the perinatal (organized around the biological experience of birth) and the transpersonal (ancestral, collective, archetypal, and cosmic). Grof presents this expanded cartography not as speculation but as an empirical finding forced upon him by thousands of clinical sessions.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the core interview, Grof describes the stages through which his understanding evolved, stating that "responding to the observations of LSD experiences, it was necessary to make some radical revisions and additions to the model used in psychiatry and psychology. The cartography of the psyche had to be vastly expanded and deepened with the inclusion of two large additional domains, the perinatal and the transpersonal; the latter realm overlaps significantly with Jung's idea of the collective unconscious."

The inadequacy of the older model had become clear early in his research: "Many found that the current model, limiting the psyche to postnatal biography and the Freudian individual unconscious, was superficial and inadequate. The new map of the psyche that emerged out of this research added two large transbiographical domains — the perinatal level, closely related to the memory of biological birth, and the transpersonal level, harboring the historical and archetypal domains of the collective unconscious."

Grof emphasises the heuristic power of LSD as the instrument that exposed this hidden architecture: "In one of my early books, I suggested that the potential significance of LSD and other psychedelics for psychiatry and psychology was comparable to the value the microscope has for biology and medicine or the telescope has for astronomy." He further notes that "these substances function as unspecific amplifiers that increase the cathexis (energetic charge) associated with the deep unconscious contents of the psyche and make them available for conscious processing."

The expanded cartography is described in terms of five major phases of understanding: pharmacological laboratory research; psycholytic psychotherapy and COEX systems (postnatal biographical layer); the perinatal matrices (BPMs I–IV); transpersonal experiences and world mythology; and finally archetypes and archetypal cosmology.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the master frame of the entire interview section "My Explorations of LSD: From Pharmacology to Archetypes." Every subsequent discovery — COEX systems, perinatal matrices, transpersonal experiences — represents a layer of this expanded cartography. It connects directly to [[Grof: the perinatal matrices shape the psyche through the experience of birth]], [[Grof: the psyche extends into transpersonal realms beyond the personal]], and [[Grof: LSD and holotropic breathwork are tools for healing and self-exploration]]. The concept hub [[Holotropic states of consciousness (Grof)]] is the umbrella category under which the whole cartography is explored.

The foreword Grof wrote to Albert Hofmann's *LSD: My Problem Child* (also reproduced in this volume) reinforces this point, noting that LSD research "not only provided deep insights into the world of psychiatric patients, but also revolutionized the understanding of the nature and dimensions of the human psyche."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Grof himself acknowledges that mainstream psychiatry resisted this cartography. He notes that "the current model, limiting the psyche to postnatal biography and the Freudian individual unconscious," was the institutional consensus, and that phenomena from the perinatal and transpersonal domains "were simply too fantastic to be believable for an average professional." He does not disavow these objections but argues that direct clinical experience overcomes them. The source presents no internal counter-argument that undermines his position.
