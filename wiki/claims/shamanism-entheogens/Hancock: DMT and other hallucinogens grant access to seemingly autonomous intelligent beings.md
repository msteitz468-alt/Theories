## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that DMT (dimethyltryptamine) — the principal active molecule in ayahuasca and an endogenous human neurotransmitter — reliably produces encounters with beings that experiencers perceive as independently real, purposive, and possessed of their own intelligence, rather than as self-generated projections of the unconscious. Drawing on psychiatrist Rick Strassman's controlled clinical trials at the University of New Mexico in the 1990s, on pre-1960s DMT research by Stephen Szara and others, and on his own smoked-DMT experiences, Hancock contends that multiple subjects in completely separate settings, at different times and from different cultures, reported encounters with near-identical entities — elves, insectoids, machine-beings, clowns, fairy-like figures — that behaved as though they expected the visitor and had their own agenda. This consistency, Hancock argues, cannot be explained within orthodox models of hallucination (which hold that trance visions are "culturally controlled" and "derived from memory") and is the strongest evidence that the beings encountered in trance may have some form of objective, independent existence.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**Strassman's core finding.** Hancock reports that when Rick Strassman began human DMT trials in the 1990s,

> "Many of the subjects experienced intense encounters with supernatural, non-physical beings during their DMT trips, encounters that they often received — like the shamans of old — as profound and disturbing revelations. Several different volunteers gave accounts of virtually identical experiences, and even seemed to be entering the same realms and communicating with the same beings." (line 3120)

Strassman himself is quoted looking back on the project in 2001: "'When reviewing my bedside notes I continually feel surprise in seeing how many of our volunteers "made contact" with "them" or other beings. At least half did in one form or another. Research subjects used expressions like "entities," "beings," "aliens," "guides," and "helpers" to describe them. The "life-forms" looked like clowns, reptiles, mantises, bees, spiders, cacti, and stick figures.'" (lines 3326–3330)

Strassman is further quoted: "'I was unprepared for the overwhelmingly frequent reports of contact with beings. They challenged my view of the brain and reality'" (line 3322).

**The autonomy problem.** Crucially, Hancock emphasizes that the bodies of Strassman's volunteers remained on hospital beds throughout, yet they underwent experiences phenomenologically identical to UFO abductions — including surgical procedures, implants, and examinations by beings with clear agendas. The subject Ben, after 36 silent minutes, reported: "'There were four or five of them. They were on me fast . . . They weren't benevolent but they weren't non-benevolent. They probed, they really probed. They seemed to know time was limited'" (lines 3408–3410). Dimitri announced: "'WHAM! I felt like I was in an alien laboratory . . . There were beings . . . They had a space ready for me. They weren't as surprised as I was'" (line 3420).

**Hancock's own encounters.** In his first well-dosed DMT session, Hancock reports entering "some strange, pristine geometrical space on the other side of the wall" and becoming aware of "the presence of other intelligences with me in this weird geometrical room with transparent planes and impossible angles. What I could see were hints and suggestions of very rapid, purposive activity around the edges of the room, as though beings were there but moving so fast that they were a blur" (lines 3190–3196). He concludes: "I felt a strong sense of communication with them. It dawned on me that this place I was in had something to do with teaching. I was here to learn, and these high-speed little machine-like beings were my instruments of instruction" (line 3196). After returning to normal consciousness, he felt that "enormous amounts of data had been transferred to me" (line 3238).

**The pre-abduction precedent.** Hancock notes that the identical entities — insectoids, dwarfs, and probing beings — appeared in DMT research conducted in the 1950s, a decade before UFO abductions became publicly known. Stephen Szara's patient KV (April 30, 1956) reported: "'I saw strange creatures, dwarfs or something, they were black and moved about'" (line 3510). An American subject in 1957 described being "'In a big place, and they were hurting me. They were not human. They were horrible'" (lines 3512–3514). Timothy Leary in 1966 described "two magnificent insects . . . skin burnished, glowing metallic, with hammered jewels inlaid" and "elf-like insects merrily working away" (lines 3516–3518). Hancock concludes: "That 'alien-like' and 'insectoid alien-type' entities should have popped up in scientific papers in the 1950s and 1960s, doing exactly the same kinds of things to hallucinating DMT subjects as aliens would much later become famous for doing to abductees, is extremely significant. It certainly rules out any simplistic attempt to derive the alien imagery in the reports of Strassman's volunteers from the mass culture of the 1990s" (line 3520).

**The cross-subject consistency problem.** Hancock reports that he, Strassman's volunteer Rex, and Strassman's volunteer Aaron — three people who have never met, from different cultural backgrounds, who received DMT a decade apart in utterly contrasting settings (jungle vs. clinical hospital) — all encountered near-identical "alien-type insectoid" beings that were intrusive, probing, and possessed of their own agenda (lines 3480–3492). He argues: "if what David Lewis-Williams calls Stage 3 hallucinations are 'culturally controlled' and 'derived from memory,' as orthodox scientists teach, then there is simply no way that Rex, Aaron, and I, with so little in common, could have experienced such similar encounters with unpleasant and intrusive 'alien-type insectoids'" (line 3490).

**Strassman's receiver model.** Strassman is quoted as remaining "open to the possibility that the beings encountered on these trips . . . are absolutely real but normally opaque to us until our 'receiver wavelength' is reset by DMT or other similar hallucinogenic molecules" (line 3532). He is also cited speculating about dark matter: "'It may be that DMT alters the characteristics of our brains' so that we are able to perceive what the physicists call 'dark matter' — the 95 per cent of the universe's mass that is known to exist but that at present remains invisible to all our senses and instruments" (line 3600).

**The brain-as-receiver model.** Hancock frames the broader implication: "I do not regard it as an a priori impossibility that spirit worlds and beings may in some significant sense be real . . . the possibility that everyone who has ever experienced abduction by aliens or fairies, or initiation with painful piercings and tortures by spirits, automatically takes for granted — namely, that the otherworlds we explore and the supernatural entities we encounter in trance states do have an objective reality all of their own and do have an independent existence outside our brains" (lines 3110, 3670).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim supplies the central empirical argument of Part III. It connects directly to [[Shamanism and altered states of consciousness (Hancock)]], which shows that cross-cultural shamanic traditions have always described encounters with beings who teach, heal, and instruct. It also grounds [[Hancock: the spirit beings may be encoded in or communicate through human DNA (Narby)]], since the consistent identity of the beings across cultures and settings is one of the key anomalies that drives Narby's DNA hypothesis. The claim also bridges the shamanic/spirit encounter tradition with the alien and fairy encounter phenomena treated extensively in Part II.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock acknowledges the standard skeptical rebuttal at length: by the 1990s, UFO abduction imagery was culturally ubiquitous, and Strassman's volunteers may simply have replayed "internalized tabloid clichés" during their hallucinations (line 3500). He concedes this cannot be definitively ruled out for any individual contemporary account. The pre-1960s data (Szara, Turner, Merlis, Leary) is his strongest counter-argument but not conclusive proof of independent reality. Strassman himself is presented as professing "embarrassment and consternation at volunteers' numerous reports of contact with what they took to be other 'freestanding realities'" (line 3530) and ultimately stopped his research partly because the mainstream scientific framework gave him no way to interpret or report such findings responsibly. Hancock also notes that he himself was never able to sustain a clear view of the entities — "I have only the haziest recall of their appearance" — owing to inexperience and poor delivery method (line 3240). The shamanic explanation, that the beings are real inhabitants of genuine non-physical dimensions, "would face catastrophic implosion" for Western science if ever proven, which is presented as a sociological rather than evidential obstacle (line 3314).
