## Claim Statement

Hancock argues in Part V that the most parsimonious explanation for the universal, cross-cultural consistency of spirit-world encounters — experienced in shamanic trance, DMT sessions, fairy encounters, and UFO abductions — is not that the brain generates these visions internally, but that they represent genuine perceptions of an objectively real parallel dimension or plane of existence. He presents this as a deliberate open question rather than a proven conclusion, but marshals substantial evidence and the admissions of mainstream researchers to argue that the "extra-human realm" hypothesis cannot be dismissed. Hallucinogens like DMT, on this view, function as receivers that retune human consciousness to frequencies normally imperceptible, allowing contact with a freestanding reality.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hancock frames the central tension of Part V explicitly: "The alternative to experiences like these being inwardly generated is that they genuinely do 'reflect patterns exhibited on another, extra-human realm.' Much of the evidence we have explored in these pages is at least suggestive of this possibility" (lines 5176–5177).

He quotes Rick Strassman directly on the receiver hypothesis: "By conceiving of the brain as a receiver of information . . . one can accommodate the biological model of changing brain function with a chemical. At the same time, it allows for the possibility that what is being received, while not usually perceptible, is consistently and veri[fiably] existent for a large number of individuals. It may, indeed, reflect stable, free-standing, and parallel planes of reality" (lines 5132–5134).

Hancock finds the physical and behaviorally consistent evidence for these encounters compelling enough to reject the purely inward explanation: "Such evidence can only add weight to the scary but intriguing possibility that the reason why human experiences of the 'non-real' demonstrate so many astonishing commonalities is that they do in fact 'reflect patterns exhibited on another, extra-human realm.' The bottom line would be that non-human, apparently non-physical intelligences, whose first interactions with us are documented in rock paintings of therianthropes dating back 35,000 years, and who inspired all the world's religious traditions, are still present amongst us today, following their own agenda according to their own timetable and purposes" (lines 5190–5192).

He singles out the evolutionary paradox as the most damaging objection to the purely neurological explanation: "if the materialist view of the universe is correct, then it is hard to see how attending to anything 'non-real' could have been at all adaptive or could have contributed usefully to our ancestors' chances of survival . . . On the other hand, if the shamanic view of the universe is correct, then attending closely to spirit realms and beings makes complete sense, as does their universality, even if Western scientists persist in their delusion that such realms and beings are not real" (lines 5128–5130).

Hancock closes his main text with an image of the megalithic stones at Avebury "standing stark, in silhouette, like doors leading to another world" (line 5258) — a phrase that gives the chapter its title and crystallizes his argument that the spirit world is a genuine destination accessible through the right portal.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the deliberate culmination of [[Supernatural (Hancock)]]. The entire book builds toward this unresolved but earnest open question. Hancock's argument unfolds in two stages in Part V: first, demolishing the "inward generation" hypothesis by showing that natural selection cannot plausibly explain why the genome would encode an elaborate, useless database of "non-real" visions; second, making the positive case for the parallel-dimension hypothesis on grounds of consistency, physical traces, and the admissions of scientists like Strassman and Shanon.

The argument is grounded in Hancock's broader thesis about [[Shamanism and altered states of consciousness (Hancock)]]: that the painted caves of Upper Paleolithic Europe are the earliest surviving evidence of humans accessing this same spirit world, and that the tradition has never broken — it runs continuously from the shamans of 35,000 years ago through fairy lore, through the Eleusinian Mysteries, through to modern DMT sessions and UFO abductions.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock is explicit that this remains an open question and does not claim proof. He presents both main alternatives fairly: (1) the "inward generation" hypothesis, that universal hallucinations are somehow hard-wired into human (and animal) neurology or DNA; and (2) the "extra-human realm" hypothesis, that they are veridical perceptions of a parallel dimension. He acknowledges that "coincidence" aside, neither Strassman nor Shanon — the leading scientists in this field — can offer a definitive explanation in either direction (lines 5140–5142).

Benny Shanon is quoted as resisting the extra-human explanation: he "rejects the notion that visionary experiences might in some way be 'transmitted' from other dimensions" and prefers to explain commonalities through human creativity and neurological structure (lines 5135–5138). Shanon's fallback position — "if the commonalities in the ayahuasca experience cannot be accounted for in ordinary psychological terms, then perhaps we have no choice but to shift from the internal domain to the external one" (line 5138) — is presented by Hancock as a reluctant concession toward his own position.

Hancock also acknowledges that his own DMT experience — feeling less like a spirit encounter and more like "a personal session with an interactive computer program" (lines 5018–5019) — complicated simple acceptance of the parallel-dimension hypothesis and pointed toward the alternative virtual-teacher/DNA hypothesis he also entertains.
