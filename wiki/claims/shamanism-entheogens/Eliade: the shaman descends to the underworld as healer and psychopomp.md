## Claim Statement

Eliade argues that the descent to the land of the dead is the second great shamanic feat and the one most directly tied to the shaman's social role. The shaman descends to the underworld for two complementary purposes: to retrieve the fugitive or stolen soul of a living patient (the healing function), and to escort the newly dead to their realm so they do not haunt the living (the psychopomp function). In both cases the journey is possible only because the shaman has mastered the techniques of ecstasy — his soul can "penetrate the underworld" and return at will. Eliade describes the shaman as "the psychopomp par excellence," and states that "all through Asia and North America, and elsewhere as well (e.g., Indonesia), the shaman performs the function of doctor and healer; he announces the diagnosis, goes in search of the patient's fugitive soul, captures it, and makes it return to animate the body that it has left."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

**The infernal descent séance.** Eliade reconstructs the Altaic "black" shaman's descent from accounts collected by Anokhin and Potanin. The shaman traverses seven successive underground "obstacles" (pudak), accompanied by ancestors and helping spirits, until he reaches Erlik Khan's palace "built of stone and black clay." After presenting gifts and getting Erlik drunk — miming every stage of the god's intoxication — he is blessed and "joyously returns to earth, riding not a horse but a goose." At the séance's end, "someone takes the drum from his hands and strikes it three times. The shaman rubs his eyes as if waking. Asked: 'How was your journey? What success did you have?' he answers: 'The journey was successful. I was well received!'" Eliade stresses that these descents "are undertaken especially to find and bring back a sick person's soul."

**The healing function.** Chapter VII opens with a direct statement: "The principal function of the shaman in Central and North Asia is magical healing. Several conceptions of the cause of illness are found in the area, but that of the 'rape of the soul' is by far the most widespread. Disease is attributed to the soul's having strayed away or been stolen, and treatment is in principle reduced to finding it, capturing it, and obliging it to resume its place in the patient's body." Eliade records the Buryat procedure in which the shaman determines whether the patient's soul "has strayed away or if it has been stolen from him and is a captive in Erlik's prison," then searches "the forests, the steppes, and even the bottom of the sea." Among the Tremyugan, "abandoning his body, his soul enters the underworld and goes in search of the patient's soul. He persuades the dead to let him bring it back to earth by promising them the gift of a shirt or other things... When he wakes from his ecstasy the shaman has the patient's soul in his closed right hand and replaces it in the body through the right ear."

**The psychopomp function.** Eliade's account of Goldi funerary practice is especially detailed. During the kazatauri ceremony the shaman dons his costume, searches for the deceased's soul, makes it enter a cushion (fanya), and finally at sunset sets off on a sled for the land of the dead: "At once the dead gather and ask the shaman to tell them his name and that of the newcomer. The shaman is careful not to give his real name; he searches through the crowd of spirits for the close relatives of the soul he is conducting, so that he may entrust it to them. Having done so, he hastens to return to earth." For the Altaians, "some Altaic shamans even escort the soul to the underworld, and, in order not to be recognized by the inhabitants of the nether regions, they daub their faces with soot." Eliade explains why the shaman is uniquely qualified: "because he has traveled it many times himself, he is thoroughly familiar with the road to the underworld; then too, only he can capture the intangible soul and carry it to its new dwelling place."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is the complement of [[Eliade: magical flight and ascent to the sky are central shamanic feats]]. Eliade presents both journeys as expressions of the same ecstatic mastery described in [[Shamanism as archaic technique of ecstasy (Eliade)]]: the shaman is "healer and psychopomp... because he commands the techniques of ecstasy." The underworld shares the same Cosmic Axis cosmology as the celestial ascent — indeed, the same World Tree that the shaman climbs to the sky also has roots reaching the nether regions. Eliade also notes a degree of blending between the two directional techniques: in the Altaic descent, the shaman returns on a goose (a celestial symbol), and among the Yakut the healing séance typically culminates in a celestial ascent even when the immediate cause of illness is demonic possession or soul-theft from below.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Eliade notes considerable variety in shamanic practice and cautions that "real shamanic trances are comparatively rare, and the majority of séances involve only an ecstatic journey undertaken by the spirits or the fabulous account of adventures whose mythological prototypes are already known." He also observes that descents to the underworld, "being more dangerous and more secret, were less easily accessible to European observers," meaning the ethnographic record may underrepresent this role. Additionally, the psychopomp function is not universal: among the Tungus the shaman is summoned to escort the dead only if they linger unusually long, suggesting the role may originally have been more universal and was later narrowed in some cultures.
