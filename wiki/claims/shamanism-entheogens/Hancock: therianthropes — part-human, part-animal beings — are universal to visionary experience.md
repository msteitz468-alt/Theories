## Claim Statement

Hancock argues that therianthropic figures — beings that combine human and animal characteristics — are the single most consistent feature shared by Upper Paleolithic cave art, surviving shamanic traditions worldwide, and modern altered-state visions induced by hallucinogens. He contends that their ubiquitous recurrence across tens of thousands of years and across entirely unconnected cultures is too systematic to be coincidental, and that it points toward a shared visionary domain accessible via trance that generates these hybrid forms reliably. Hancock treats the therianthrope not as a culturally invented symbol but as a percept — something actually encountered by the visionary mind when the brain's "receiver wavelength" is shifted by hallucinogens or other trance-induction techniques.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hancock opens Part II by identifying therianthropes as the mystery that first drew him to his inquiry: "My first landmark, and the mystery that originally drew me to this inquiry, was the realization that encounters with supernatural beings are documented in the oldest representational art that has so far been found anywhere in the world — art depicting therianthropes and dating back more than 30,000 years. The discovery that shamans in surviving hunter-gatherer cultures routinely experience encounters with virtually identical beings when they enter trance, and that I could and did encounter such beings myself under the influence of the same hallucinogens that the shamans use, convinced me that there was a real mystery to explore here" (line 1378).

The therianthrope motif reappears explicitly when Hancock explores the alien-abduction literature and finds the same shape-shifting dynamic at work: "aliens very frequently first present themselves in the form of animals, or with hybrid animal and alien characteristics, before appearing in their more familiar tabloid identity as small humanoid beings with heart-shaped heads, large eyes, etc., etc." (lines 1897–1900). He cites John Mack's clinical summary of abductee reports — "The aliens appear to be consummate shape-shifters, often appearing initially to the abductees as animals — owls, eagles, raccoons and deer are among the creatures the abductees have seen initially" (line 1918) — and notes that shamans across cultures acquire spirit helpers that "almost always appear in the form of animals or therianthropes" (lines 1950–1952).

The key archaeological anchor is the Sorcerer of Trois Frères, whose relevance Hancock makes explicit: "the famous therianthrope known as the Sorcerer is also found. Thought to be more than 15,000 years old, it has the legs and upper body of a human being, its face is dominated by large owl-like eyes and a beak, and antlers jut from the top of its head" (line 1994). He connects this prehistoric image directly to a contemporary UFO abductee, Carol, who during her encounter saw "what looked to be a typical shaman, with a heavy fur robe and an antlered headpiece," construed as the Celtic deity Cernunnos — "half animal and half person" (line 1936) — before the owl in the sky transformed into an alien being. Hancock frames the cave art's owloid humanoids at Trois Frères similarly, noting that "archaeologists judge [them] to have distinctly humanoid characteristics" (line 1994).

Hancock explicitly names the shared visionary logic: "Emerging from this, an important universal theme has been identified: shamans acquire supernatural helpers or guides — spirits who teach them how to become great healers. These spirit guides almost always appear in the form of animals or therianthropes" (lines 1950–1952). He traces this pattern from Eskimo shamans whose helpers are animals appearing in human form, to Australian Aborigine initiates whose calling involves beings "transformed into huge birds" (line 1981), to Amazonian shamans whose doctorcitos arrive "sometimes in the form of animals and sometimes in the form of 'small people'" (line 2026).

The convergence of these three streams — prehistoric cave art, living shamanism, and hallucinogen-induced vision — is precisely what Hancock says he had "no idea" was connected at the outset: "Before I began this research I had no idea that therianthropes were the subject matter of the most ancient religious art of mankind. Nor had it ever occurred to me . . . that many of the animals depicted in painted caves and rock shelters all around the world might be best understood as representations of transformed shamans or spirits in animal form seen during hallucinatory trances" (line 1892).

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim is foundational to Hancock's larger argument in [[Supernatural (Hancock)]]. Part I of the book (outside this subagent's range) establishes, following David Lewis-Williams' neuropsychological model, that trance-induced entoptic and iconic hallucinations provide the best explanation for Upper Paleolithic cave art. Part II extends that argument by showing that therianthropes — the most distinctive figures in that art — remain live percepts in shamanic and modern visionary experience. The continuity of the therianthrope motif anchors Hancock's claim, developed further in Chapters 6–10, that shamanic spirits, fairy beings, and alien entities are historically continuous manifestations of the same category of non-human intelligences encountered in altered states. See also [[Shamanism and altered states of consciousness (Hancock)]] for the broader context of how shamanic trance techniques are argued to unlock this shared visionary domain.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hancock acknowledges the mainstream scientific position throughout: conventional psychology holds that "full-blown iconic and representative hallucinations . . . are not only not real in any sense, but also that their contents are 'derived from memory' and life experiences, and 'are all culture specific: at least in some measure, people hallucinate what they expect to hallucinate'" (lines 2055–2056). He also notes Lewis-Williams' own culturally conditioned interpretation — "a San shaman may see an eland antelope; an Inuit will see a polar bear or a seal" (lines 2068–2069) — as a genuine complication for his cross-cultural universality thesis, though he argues these are surface differences over a shared underlying perception. Hancock concedes he cannot prove that Upper Paleolithic cave artists experienced the full range of trance perceptions described by modern shamans and abductees: "we have no ethnography from the Upper Paleolithic" (line 2318). He presents the therianthrope universality as compelling evidence rather than proven fact.
