## Claim Statement

Eliade argues that the shaman's essential social function depends on his mastery of a company of tutelary and helping spirits — often appearing in animal form — who transport him on ecstatic journeys, reveal the causes of illness, and fight on his behalf against hostile powers. Alongside this command of spirits, the shaman employs a special "secret language" or "spirit language" to communicate with them, a linguistic register that is unintelligible to ordinary people and that forms a consistent feature across vastly different shamanic traditions from the Arctic to Southeast Asia. The ritual costume and drum serve as material instruments — embodiments of the shaman's spirit-helpers and cosmological knowledge — that authenticate and facilitate this mastery.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

In the opening of Chapter 9 (line 6221), Eliade summarizes the Eskimo shamanic complex as exhibiting, among other universal elements, "acquisition of one or more familiar spirits" and "secret language" as constitutive features. The séance itself demonstrates this double structure: "The shaman sings for a long time in 'secret language' to evoke the spirits. Falling into trance, he speaks in a high, strange voice that seems not to be his" (line 6227). The helping spirits are indispensable instruments of travel: "Securely tied, and sometimes separated from the rest of those present by a curtain, they begin by invoking their familiar spirits; with their help they leave the earth and reach the moon or enter the depths of the earth or the sea" (line 6247). The Eskimo case — where a shaman was "carried to the moon by his helping spirit (in this case, a bear)" — illustrates the animal-form that such spirits commonly take. Outside the séance, the shaman maintains "his long dialogues with his helping spirits, and his need for quiet" (line 6239).

In Chapter 10, the same pattern appears among the Semang and Sakai of the Malay Peninsula. The Semang hala calls on the cenoi — luminous spirit-helpers described as "nephews of God" — and communicates with them in an unknown tongue: "the hala and his assistant sing and speak in an unknown language, which, when they leave the cabin, they profess to have forgotten" (line 7089). The cenoi "have sung through their mouths" and it is they who diagnose illness and prescribe treatment. Among the Sakai and Jakun, the helping spirits are inherited and obtained through dreams, and "the helping spirits are sometimes invoked in Malay" (line 7101), showing how the institution adapts to local linguistic context while preserving its structural role.

Chapter 12 offers a cross-cultural synthesis. In Tibetan tantrism Eliade identifies what he calls the "tongue of the ḍākinī" as a parallel phenomenon: "Tibetan tantrism has a secret language, called the 'tongue of the ḍākinī'... All this in some measure approaches the 'spirit language' or 'secret language' of the shamans, both of North Asia and of Malaya and Indonesia" (line 9017). He offers a structural explanation for this linguistic feature: "we know that the shamanic 'spirit language' not only attempts to imitate animal cries but contains a certain proportion of spontaneous creations presumably explained by preecstatic euphoria and ecstasy itself" (line 9017). The reference to imitating animal cries links the secret language directly to the animal-form helping spirits whose idiom the shaman learns to approximate.

The ritual costume, noted by Eliade as a complex topic deserving its own exhaustive ethnographic study (acknowledged in Chapter 1), persists as a reference point throughout the chapters in this range. In Chapter 12, discussing Padmasambhava's mythical biography, Eliade observes that dancing in "seven ornaments of bone" recalls "the Siberian shaman's costume" (line 9001), and he notes that Tibetan skeleton-dance costumes "recall the costumes of Central and North Asian shamans" in which Lamaist influences "reinforced very ancient autochthonous conceptions" already embedded in shamanic dress (line 9002). The drum, likewise, functions not as a simple percussion instrument but as a vehicle: Bon shamans are "believed to use their drums as vehicles to convey them through the air" (line 8976 area), and the drum's form and symbolism carry traces of the shaman's cosmological knowledge.

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim elaborates on the decisive distinction Eliade maintains throughout the book: the shaman is not passively possessed by spirits but actively commands them. The helping spirits are instrumental to the soul-flight that defines shamanism in the strict sense (see [[Shamanism as archaic technique of ecstasy (Eliade)]]). The secret language is the shaman's privileged access-code to the spirit world — a mode of communication that confirms his elected, non-ordinary status. Eliade documents these features from the Arctic Eskimo to the Malay Pygmies and the Tibetan Bon tradition, arguing for a structural universality beneath cultural variation.

The costume and drum are treated more fully in Chapter 5 of the book (outside this range), but they resurface in Chapters 12 and 13 as Eliade triangulates between Siberian, Tibetan, and Chinese variants, each time showing how material objects encode the shaman's cosmological mastery and spirit-relationships.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Eliade notes that the boundary between commanding spirits and being possessed by them is porous in practice, and that "hybridized" shamanic traditions increasingly blur this line under Lamaist or other external influences — particularly among the Tungus, where the "incarnation of spirits" has overshadowed the original celestial ascent. The spirit-language in Southeast Asian contexts also shows contamination: among the Sakai and Jakun, helping spirits are "sometimes invoked in Malay," suggesting the secret language can be partially replaced by an ordinary tongue under acculturation. Eliade treats these as signs of "decadence" of the classic form rather than refutations of the underlying structural claim.
