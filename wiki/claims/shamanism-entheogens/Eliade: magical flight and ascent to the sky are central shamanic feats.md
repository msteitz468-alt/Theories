## Claim Statement

Eliade argues that ecstatic "magical flight" — the shaman's soul abandoning his body to ascend through multiple heavens — is one of the two defining and most spectacular shamanic feats, the counterpart to the descent to the underworld. The ascent is enacted through a cluster of interlocking symbols: climbing the notched birch that represents the World Tree (Cosmic Axis), riding the soul of a sacrificed horse, straddling a goose-effigy and imitating bird cries, and invoking the celestial Birds of Heaven (Märküt) to perch on the shaman's shoulder. The shaman can accomplish this because, as Eliade writes, his soul can "safely abandon his body and roam at vast distances, can penetrate the underworld and rise to the sky."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Eliade grounds the celestial ascent in his extended account of the Altaic horse-sacrifice séance recorded by W. Radlov. During the second evening of the ceremony the shaman (kam) climbs the notched birch step by step — each notch (tapty) representing a heaven — crying "I have climbed a step, / Aihai, aihai! / I have reached a plane." Simultaneously he mounts a horsehide that represents the soul of the sacrificed horse and orders his human companion (baš-tut-kan-kiši) to hurry, since that man's soul must accompany the horse-soul throughout the celestial journey.

Bird symbolism pervades the ritual. Before the ascent proper the kam straddles a goose-scarecrow, "rapidly waving his hands as if to fly," and sings: "Under the white sky, / Over the white cloud; / Under the blue sky, / Over the blue cloud: / Rise up to the sky, bird!" He then imitates the goose's cackle. Later he invokes the Märküt, the five heavenly eagle-spirits: "Birds of Heaven, five Märküt, / Ye with mighty copper talons... / Perch on my right shoulder!" — and droops his shoulder as if under the weight of a giant bird. A footnote records that poles with wooden birds at their tops were erected near the sacrificial table, "for the bird... symbolizes the shaman's magical power of flight."

Ascending heaven by heaven — in the third he switches back to the goose, "riding" it when the sacrificed horse's soul tires — the shaman passes through up to nine or twelve celestial planes to appear before the supreme sky-deity Bai Ülgän. The birch inside the yurt is explicitly glossed: "This birch symbolizes the World Tree, which stands at the Center of the Universe, the Cosmic Axis that connects sky, earth, and underworld; the seven, nine, or twelve notches (tapty) represent the 'heavens,' the celestial planes."

Eliade emphasizes that the ecstatic technique — not the sacrifice itself — is what distinguishes the shaman from ordinary sacrificial priests: "His technique of ecstasy enabled him to abandon his body and undertake the journey to the sky. Hence it was easy for him to repeat the celestial journey, taking with him the soul of the sacrificed animal to present it directly and concretely to Bai Ülgän." The shaman returns to earth only after receiving Bai Ülgän's oracular pronouncements, then collapses "exhausted" and "remains motionless and dumb," waking "as if after a long absence."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim belongs to Chapter VI, the book's first extended séance description. Eliade places the ascent as the positive pole of the shamanic cosmos, paired with the descent to the underworld (see [[Eliade: the shaman descends to the underworld as healer and psychopomp]]). Both feats rest on the same foundational premise articulated at the chapter's opening: "Healer and psychopomp, the shaman is these because he commands the techniques of ecstasy — that is, because his soul can safely abandon his body and roam at vast distances." The World Tree / Cosmic Axis cosmology that validates the ascent is the same structure that the shaman navigates downward in healing séances, linking this claim directly to [[Shamanism as archaic technique of ecstasy (Eliade)]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Eliade himself notes a caveat: in the Altaic horse-sacrifice séance as Radlov recorded it, "the 'ecstasy' is obviously an imitation. The shaman laboriously mimes an ascent (after the traditional canon: bird flight, riding, etc.) and the interest of the rite is dramatic rather than ecstatic." He suggests the real trance occurs at other séances and that the shaman's role in this particular ceremony may be a relatively late usurpation of an earlier sacrificial priest. This does not undermine the symbolic centrality of the ascent, but it complicates claims of uniformly intense ecstatic experience.
