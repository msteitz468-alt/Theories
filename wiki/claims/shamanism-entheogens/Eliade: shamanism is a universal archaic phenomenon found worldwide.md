## Claim Statement

Eliade argues that although Siberia and Central Asia represent the heartland where shamanism is most fully and dramatically documented, the core ideology and characteristic techniques of shamanism appear across the Americas, Southeast Asia, Oceania, Australia, and Indo-European antiquity. For Eliade, this worldwide distribution is not the result of a single diffusion from a single center but reflects an ecstatic experience that is "fundamental in the human condition, and hence known to the whole of archaic humanity." Shamanism is therefore, in his framing, an archaic and near-universal phenomenon — structurally consistent even where regional variations abound.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The claim is articulated most explicitly in the Conclusions, where Eliade writes that the ideology and characteristic techniques of shamanism "are attested in archaic cultures, where it would be difficult to admit the presence of paleo-Oriental influences. It is enough to remember, on the one hand, that Central Asian shamanism is part and parcel of the prehistoric culture of the Siberian hunters, and, on the other, that shamanic ideologies and techniques are documented among the primitive peoples of Australia, the Malay Archipelago, South America, North America, and other regions." He grounds this universality not in historical diffusion but in the nature of ecstasy itself, calling the ecstatic experience "a 'primary phenomenon'" because "we see no reason whatever for regarding it as the result of a particular historical moment, that is, as produced by a certain form of civilization. Rather, we would consider it fundamental in the human condition, and hence known to the whole of archaic humanity; what changed and was modified with the different forms of culture and religion was the interpretation and evaluation of the ecstatic experience."

This universalist position is reinforced by the book's cross-cultural evidence: the Conclusions survey the entire span of prior chapters — the Americas (North American healing séances, South American shamanism), Southeast Asia and Oceania (Malay Archipelago, Australian medicine men), and Indo-European antiquity — and note that "the symbolism of ascent is documented everywhere and in all historico-religious contexts." Eliade also marshals archaeological evidence to push the claim back into deep prehistory: "What appears to be certain is the antiquity of 'shamanic' rituals and symbols," citing interpretations of the Lascaux paintings as depicting shamanic trance and arguing that Europe offers evidence for "the earliest forms of shamanism (Lascaux) with the plastic representations of the bird, the tutelary spirit, and ecstasy" as early as approximately 25,000 years ago.

In the Epilogue Eliade further describes shamanism as a persistent type of religious experience across the full history of mysticism: "There is no solution of continuity in the history of mysticism. More than once we have discerned in the shamanic experience a 'nostalgia for paradise' that suggests one of the oldest types of Christian mystical experience."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim functions as the book's overarching comparative thesis. Each preceding chapter — on Siberian and Central Asian shamanism, the Americas, Southeast Asia, Oceania, and the Indo-Europeans — supplies regional evidence that Eliade synthesizes in the Conclusions into the argument for universality. It is inseparable from [[Shamanism as archaic technique of ecstasy (Eliade)]], which defines ecstasy (not spirit possession or trance in general) as the specific differentiating feature that unifies these worldwide expressions. The universality claim also undergirds [[Eliade: shamanic ecstasy recovers the primordial paradise lost at the Fall]], since the structural consistency of shamanism worldwide is what makes the "nostalgia for paradise" interpretive framework applicable across cultures.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Eliade himself acknowledges the complexity: he documents regional variation and the considerable influence of Buddhism (Lamaism), Iranian religions, and Mesopotamian civilization on Siberian and Central Asian shamanism, and concedes that "Tungus shamanism as it exists today cannot be considered a 'classic' form of shamanism" due to the predominant role of spirit possession over sky ascent. He also acknowledges that scholars such as Shirokogoroff have argued for the relatively recent and historically conditioned character of shamanism in specific regions. His response is to distinguish between accretions from external influences and the underlying ecstatic core, which he treats as archaic and pan-human — a methodological move that he is aware requires qualification.
