## Claim Statement

The system holds — and Crowley's 1909 record presents — that the 10th Aethyr (ZAX) is the location of the Abyss: the threshold between the lower planes (accessible to ordinary magicians) and the higher planes (accessible only to Masters of the Temple). Choronzon, described as "the mighty devil that inhabiteth the outermost Abyss" and whose name numerates to 333, is the entity of that Abyss. Crowley's record describes the encounter as one of the central events of his visionary sequence — a confrontation with the principle of Dispersion and meaninglessness that must be survived before the higher Aethyrs can be entered. The text frames this descriptively as a report of what occurred and what the system teaches; it does not present Choronzon as an independently verified metaphysical entity.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The 11th Aethyr (IKH) prepares the approach: "Yet must he that understandeth go forth unto the outermost Abyss, and there must he speak with him that is set above the four-fold terror, the Princes of Evil, even with Choronzon, the mighty devil that inhabiteth the outermost Abyss." The same Aethyr warns: "Whoso passeth into the outermost Abyss, except he be of them that understand, holdeth out his hands, and boweth his neck, unto the chains of Choronzon. And as a devil he walketh about the earth, immortal, and he blasteth the flowers of the earth, and he corrupteth the fresh air ..."

The record of ZAX itself opens with elaborate protective precautions: Crowley placed himself within a triangle inscribed with the name Choronzon and protective names; Neuburg (the Scribe) remained in a circle fortified with divine names and the Magick Dagger. Crowley notes: "This Aethyr being accursed, and the seer forewarned, he taketh these precautions."

Choronzon's self-description: "I am the Master of Form, and from me all forms proceed. I am I." The text describes Choronzon as shifting rapidly between forms to seduce or deceive, taking the appearance of a beautiful woman (to tempt the Scribe), the voice of Crowley himself (to confuse the Scribe), and a suffering figure asking for pity.

The demon's theological self-characterization: "Choronzon hath no form, because he is the maker of all form ... And, because he is himself, therefore he is no self." And: "Images, images, images, all without control, all without reason. The malice of Choronzon is not the malice of a being; it is the quality of malice, because he that boasteth himself 'I am I,' hath in truth no self ... there is no centre, nay, nothing but Dispersion."

The Scribe's note confirms: "Choronzon is dispersion; and such is his fear of concentration that he will obey rather than be subjected to it, or even behold it in another." The encounter ends when Crowley writes the name BABALON, described as "victory over Choronzon," and Choronzon "was no more manifest." After the ceremony "a great fire was kindled to purify the place, and the Circle and Triangle were destroyed."

The 12th Aethyr (LOE) connects Choronzon to the "Dark Brothers": "yet of their own poison do they perish, and in their lonely fortresses shall they be eaten up by Time that hath cheated them to serve him, and by the mighty devil Choronzon, their master, whose name is the Second Death."

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The text is presented as a visionary record, not a factual report. The scribe's account makes clear that events were subjectively experienced and partially confusing even in the moment. Crowley himself observes that Choronzon's strategy was disruption of attention — the demon "goeth from subject to subject," making sustained command difficult. The Scribe's note records that he "was overshadowed" and spoke "as it were in spite of himself, remembering afterwards scarcely a word." The episode in which Choronzon physically breaches the circle by trickling sand into it — and Neuburg must repel him with a dagger — is presented as a literal occurrence within the text, but the wider context makes clear the entire account is framed as a visionary working.

The material includes violent and antinomian imagery characteristic of Crowley's style (Choronzon's threats against the Scribe's body; references to plague, lust, corruption). This is recorded descriptively as part of the text's content; it is not endorsed.
