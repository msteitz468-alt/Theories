## Claim Statement

Liber AL vel Legis declares that the whole of the law governing human action is encapsulated in the formula **"Do what thou wilt shall be the whole of the Law"** (Chapter I, v.40), complemented by **"Love is the law, love under will"** (I:57). The text names this system **Thelema** (I:39) and states there is "no law beyond Do what thou wilt" (III:60). This is presented not as permission for arbitrary desire but as an injunction to discover and enact one's deepest, authentic purpose — the True Will — with "pure will, unassuaged of purpose, delivered from the lust of result" described as "every way perfect" (I:44).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From [[The Book of the Law (Crowley)]]:

> "The word of the Law is THELEMA." (I:39)

> "Do what thou wilt shall be the whole of the Law." (I:40)

> "thou hast no right but to do thy will." (I:42)

> "Do that, and no other shall say nay." (I:43)

> "for pure will, unassuaged of purpose, delivered from the lust of result, is every way perfect." (I:44)

> "Love is the law, love under will." (I:57)

> "the word of Sin is Restriction." (I:41)

> "There is no law beyond Do what thou wilt." (III:60)

Chapter II reinforces the rejection of reason as a check on will: "the pit called Because" (II:27) is presented as the fatal error, and "Because be accursed for ever!" (II:29). The text insists: "If Will stops and cries Why, invoking Because, then Will stops & does nought" (II:30).

## Cross-source connections

Liber AL's formula of True Will is foundational to the chaos magic tradition documented in [[Liber Null & Psychonaut (Carroll)]]: Carroll's concept of the magical "True Will" builds directly on Crowley's Thelemic framework while stripping it of its religious framing.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The text does not define "True Will" with precision, leaving open whether it means a metaphysical essence or a practical psychological orientation. The formula "Love is the law, love under will" qualifies the antinomian reading: will operates within love, not in spite of it. The text also explicitly distinguishes two kinds of love: "the dove, and…the serpent" (I:57), warning against confusing them.
