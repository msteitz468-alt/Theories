## Claim Statement

Liber AL presents itself as a scripture dictated over three days in Cairo in 1904 by an entity called **Aiwass**, described as "the minister of Hoor-paar-kraat" (I:7). The text frames this event as the proclamation of the **Aeon of Horus** — the Crowned and Conquering Child — succeeding the earlier Aeons of Isis and Osiris. Ra-Hoor-Khuit is declared to have "taken his seat in the East at the Equinox of the Gods" (I:49), displacing the dying-and-rising Osirian dispensation. The three-day writing is commemorated as a ritual feast within the Thelemic calendar (II:38).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From [[The Book of the Law (Crowley)]]:

> "Behold! it is revealed by Aiwass the minister of Hoor-paar-kraat." (I:7)

> "My scribe Ankh-af-na-khonsu, the priest of the princes, shall not in one letter change this book." (I:36)

> "Change not as much as the style of a letter; for behold! thou, o prophet, shalt not behold all these mysteries hidden therein." (I:54)

> "Ra-Hoor-Khuit hath taken his seat in the East at the Equinox of the Gods; and let Asar be with Isa, who also are one. But they are not of me." (I:49)

> "A feast for the three days of the writing of the Book of the Law." (II:38)

> "when Hrumachis shall arise and the double-wanded one assume my throne and place. Another prophet shall arise, and bring fresh fever from the skies…" (III:34)

The text also commands universal distribution: "to each man and woman that thou meetest, were it but to dine or to drink at them, it is the Law to give" (III:39); and affirms "the Law is for all" (I:34).

## Cross-source connections

The figure of Aiwass as a praeternatural intelligence dictating scripture parallels the angel-dictation model underlying [[Enochian magic originated in the angelic revelations to John Dee and Edward Kelley (1582–1584)]]. Where Dee received the Enochian calls from angelic intermediaries, Crowley claims to have received Liber AL from Aiwass — both posit an authoritative non-human source legitimising a new magical dispensation.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

The text acknowledges that even the prophet does not fully comprehend its contents ("thou, o prophet, shalt not behold all these mysteries hidden therein," I:54), and that a future figure will come who understands more ("There cometh one to follow thee: he shall expound it," II:76). The aeon itself is bounded: III:34 anticipates its eventual supersession by another aeon under "Hrumachis."
