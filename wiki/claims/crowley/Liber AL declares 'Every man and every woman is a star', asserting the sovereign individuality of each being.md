## Claim Statement

Liber AL opens with the proclamation "Every man and every woman is a star" (I:3), a declaration that each individual human being possesses a unique, sovereign nature analogous to a star moving in its own orbit. The text elaborates this via the cosmology of Nuit — infinite space containing all stars — and through the doctrine that each person's True Will, like a star's path, is distinct and inviolable. The "word of Sin is Restriction" (I:41): to interfere with another's True Will is the fundamental transgression.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From [[The Book of the Law (Crowley)]]:

> "Every man and every woman is a star." (I:3)

> "Every number is infinite; there is no difference." (I:4)

> "I am Infinite Space, and the Infinite Stars thereof, do ye also thus. Bind nothing! Let there be no difference made among you between any one thing & any other thing; for thereby there cometh hurt." (I:22)

> "The word of Sin is Restriction." (I:41)

> "yea! deem not of change: ye shall be as ye are, & not other. Therefore the kings of the earth shall be Kings for ever: the slaves shall serve. There is none that shall be cast down or lifted up: all is ever as it was. Yet there are masked ones my servants: it may be that yonder beggar is a King." (II:58)

The star-metaphor situates each person within Nuit, who is the infinite space in which all stars move. Each star has its unique orbit (its True Will), and Nuit's nature as "None and Two" (I:28) means that individual multiplicity and cosmic unity are simultaneously true.

## Cross-source connections

The Thelemic doctrine of individual sovereignty as cosmic necessity is taken up in the chaos magic tradition in [[Liber Null & Psychonaut (Carroll)]], where Carroll reframes True Will as the individual's "True Will" that chaos magic seeks to identify and enact — the Crowleyan framework stripped of its theistic framing.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Chapter II contains passages (vv.21, 58–60) that interpret the star-doctrine in a strongly hierarchical and antinomian direction — "the law of the strong" — presenting inequality as a natural consequence of distinct True Wills rather than a moral endorsement. These are the text's own doctrinal positions, presented descriptively here without endorsement.
