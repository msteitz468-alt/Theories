## Claim Statement

Crowley teaches that Magick (spelled with a K to distinguish it from prestidigitation) is formally defined as "the Science and Art of causing Change to occur in conformity with Will," and that this definition extends to every intentional human act — making Magick coextensive with purposive life itself.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From the Introduction to *Magick in Theory and Practice*:

> "I. DEFINITION. MAGICK is the Science and Art of causing Change to occur in conformity with Will."

> "(Illustration: It is my Will to inform the World of certain facts within my knowledge. I therefore take 'magical weapons', pen, ink, and paper; I write 'incantations' — these sentences — in the 'magical language'… The composition and distribution of this book is thus an act of MAGICK by which I cause Changes to take place in conformity with my Will.)"

> "II. POSTULATE. ANY required Change may be effected by the application of the proper kind and degree of Force in the proper manner through the proper medium to the proper object."

From the Twenty-Eight Theorems:

> "(1) Every intentional act is a Magical Act."

> "(8) A Man whose conscious will is at odds with his True Will is wasting his strength. He cannot hope to influence his environment efficiently."

> "(9) A man who is doing his True Will has the inertia of the Universe to assist him."

> "(23) Magick is the Science of understanding oneself and one's conditions. It is the Art of applying that understanding in action."

> "(25) Every man must do Magick each time that he acts or even thinks, since a thought is an internal act whose influence ultimately affects action."

> "(27) Every man should make Magick the keynote of his life. He should learn its laws and live by them."

Crowley also explains his choice of the word itself: "I chose therefore the name 'MAGICK' as essentially the most sublime, and actually the most discredited, of all the available terms. I swore to rehabilitate MAGICK, to identify it with my own career; and to compel mankind to respect, love, and trust that which they scorned, hated and feared."

## Cross-source connections

Peter Carroll's *[[Liber Null & Psychonaut (Carroll)]]* derives its working definition of Magick — causing changes in consciousness or the external world through non-ordinary means by an act of will — directly from this Crowleyan formulation, though Carroll drops the Thelemic framework and the universal application, focusing instead on the non-ordinary or "impossible" operations.

## Opposing or Limiting Notes Within the Source

Crowley acknowledges that "in the present state of our knowledge and power some changes are not possible in practice." Theorem 20 notes that "Man can only attract and employ the forces for which he is really fitted." Theorem 21 qualifies that "his power to utilize that force is limited by his mental power and capacity, and by the circumstances of his human environment." The definition is universal in scope but the practical application is always limited by the individual's own nature and development.
