## Claim Statement

Vallée argues that the **absurdity** of UFO encounters and contactee messages is not a
defect but a *function*: it simultaneously (a) repels rational scientific investigation and
(b) lends the phenomenon the irrational, faith-based character of a religion. "The new
belief is completely lacking in logic. That is the key to its power. It serves to keep
scientists away. The more absurd the statement, the stronger its effect." He attributes the
insight to [[Major Murphy (Vallée's intelligence source)]]: the confusion "may have been put
there deliberately to achieve certain results," one being "to keep scientists away," the
other "to create the conditions for a new form of social control."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "When the Establishment is rational, absurdity is dynamite. Through the contactees, the
> Manipulators are undermining both religion and science." (Ch. 3)

> "I deliberately went out of my way to find out, armed with the knowledge that they
> understood this rule: the way to a man's belief is through confusion and absurdity. The
> absurdity of many UFO stories and of many religious visions is not a superficial logical
> mistake. It may be the key to their function." (Ch. 5)

Vallée illustrates this with the deliberately meaningless "mystical jargon" of Dr.
[[Grace Hooper Pettipher]] and the Melchizedek pamphlets, which he says "lulls the mind of
the hearer into a semihypnotic state until it is ready to receive the real message." He
also notes the new UFO faith makes "our religions seem obsolete" while preachers (Billy
Graham, Carl McIntire) reabsorb UFOs as angels.

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the *Messengers of Deception* development of a theme Vallée had already advanced in
his earlier books — see [[Vallée concludes a superior intelligence would necessarily appear absurd]]
(from *Passport to Magonia*). Here the absurdity is tied explicitly to deliberate human
manipulation rather than only to a folkloric "control system."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Vallée concedes the manipulation thesis is unproven (he calls the "Manipulators" a
*hypothesis* and admits "I do not have any proof that a single group of men is
responsible"), and that some absurdity may be intrinsic to the phenomenon rather than
engineered — consistent with his Magonia argument that the same absurd traits recur across
centuries of folklore.
