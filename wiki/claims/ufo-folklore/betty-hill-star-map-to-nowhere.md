## Claim Statement

Vallée argues that the celebrated "star map" Betty Hill recalled under hypnosis — interpreted
by schoolteacher Marjorie Fish as pointing to **Zeta Reticuli** — proves nothing about UFO
origins. He calls it "a map to nowhere": a staged display "placed there for Betty Hill to
see, not for the pilot to use," and "another deliberate attempt to lead us astray."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Vallée grants both that the Hills genuinely encountered something and that Marjorie Fish's
model-building was ingenious and accurate. His statistical objection ("Zeta Ridiculous and the
Computer Freaks"): Fish chose *one* viewing angle for *one* set of 16 of 46 candidate stars,
out of roughly 2×10²⁵ possible configurations, never asking how many other viewpoints would
match equally well. He adds that the map "is not drawn to scale" — the Zeta Reticuli
separation is exaggerated "to the point where the map would be useless for navigation."

He situates the Hill map among **at least nine** mutually contradictory "revelations" of
saucer origins (the 1897 airship's "anywhere," Uranus, Clarion, Orion, Lanulos, UMMO/Wolf 424,
Hoova, Titan), arguing the contradictions show the "origins" are symbolic plants. He compares
it to the WWII deception of changing road signs for a captured German general to mislead the
High Command.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Vallée states the Hills "really saw a UFO," that "something unknown stopped their car and took
control of their minds," and that Betty's recollection of a map "is a fact, and not simply
something she invented" — his target is the *interpretation*, not the witnesses' sincerity.
