## Claim Statement

Vallée summarizes his investigation in **six social effects** that belief in UFOs is likely to
produce "whether such physical objects exist or not." A condensed four-point version was
delivered as his statement to the United Nations Special Political Committee (Nov. 27, 1978).

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

The six effects (Ch. 11):

1. **Widens the gap between the public and scientific institutions** — rejected witnesses turn
   away from science toward "high-demand philosophies and pseudosciences."
2. **Undermines the image of humans as masters of their own destiny** — "Chariots of the Gods"
   / ancient-astronaut themes credit civilization to celestial intervention.
3. **Promotes the political unification of the planet** — "the most commonly recurring theme,"
   a yearning for global peace focused away from Earth.
4. **May become the basis of a new "high-demand" religion** — UFO creeds emphasizing sexual
   repression, racial segregation, and strict discipline (he cites "the Two" and the
   Melchizedek groups).
5. **Spreads irrational, faith-based motivation** — a growing belief that phenomena are
   "unknowable" by rational process, eroding support for rational science.
6. **Includes belief in higher races and totalitarian systems that would eliminate
   democracy** — "celestial blood," a revived "chosen people," and instructions (Vorilhon,
   Bernard) that democracy is obsolete.

> "If the Manipulators do exist, I certainly salute their tenacity, but I am curious about
> their goals… One would like to know more about the image of humanity such Manipulators
> harbor in their own minds — and in their hearts. Assuming, of course, that they do have
> hearts." (Ch. 11)

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Vallée notes effect 3 (global peace) and others may cut "positive or negative" depending on how
the channeled emotion is handled, and frames the six as effects to be reckoned with rather than
proof of intentional design.
