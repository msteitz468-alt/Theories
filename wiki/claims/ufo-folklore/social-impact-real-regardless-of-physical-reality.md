## Claim Statement

Vallée argues that, for the purpose of understanding the phenomenon's effect on society, "it
doesn't matter any more whether flying saucers are real or not." Because enough people
believe in UFOs and in the possibility of contact, the **social, historical, and political
consequences** of that belief are already real and operative — independent of the underlying
physics.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "It can be expressed very simply: it doesn't matter any more whether flying saucers are real
> or not… I am speaking of their social impact." (Ch. 2)

He draws the analogy to belief in Jesus: scholars may dispute the historicity of his
miracles, "but the effects of the belief… are real enough." In his 1978 UN statement
(Appendix) he formalizes the point: "something is 'real' if enough people believe in it. The
UFO phenomenon has now reached this point. The question of knowing whether or not UFOs are
physically 'real' is becoming secondary in the mind of the public." Sociologist David Swift's
Epilogue restates it: "contact with space may become a social fact a long time before it is a
scientific reality."

## Contexts Within the Source

This claim licenses the entire second and third parts of the book, shifting analysis from the
object to the belief system. It underwrites
[[The belief in UFOs produces six identifiable social consequences]] and connects to
[[Myth is "truer than truth," and UFO belief is becoming the next form of religion]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Vallée stresses this is a statement about *social impact only* — "It still matters to me, of
course, as an individual scientist," and he continues to insist the physical phenomenon is
real and deserves study.
