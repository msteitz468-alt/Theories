## Claim Statement

Keel presents the case of Woodrow ("Woody") Derenberger — a Mineral Wells, West Virginia, appliance salesman who in November 1966 said a lamp-chimney-shaped craft blocked the highway and a grinning humanoid calling himself Indrid Cold stepped out and communicated telepathically — as an emblematic contactee encounter. Through Derenberger, Keel examines the contactee pattern: telepathic "space brothers" who deliver absurd, unverifiable, belief-confirming claims (Cold said he came from the planet Lanulos in the "galaxy of Ganymede"), the games and deceptions the entities play, and the chaos that engulfs the percipient afterward.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Chapter 5 describes Derenberger as "a tall, husky man with . . . an honest open face," whose mundane life was upended when an object "shaped like 'an old-fashioned kerosene lamp chimney'" stopped on the road and "a door slid open . . . and a man stepped out." On November 4, riding with a coworker, Derenberger "felt a tingling sensation in his forehead" and received telepathic thoughts: "Cold explained that he was from the planet of Lanulos which was in the 'galaxy of Ganymede,'" a world "very like the earth," where he "was married to a lady named Kimi," people lived "125–175 earth years," and "naturally there was no war, poverty, hunger, or misery." The entity later acquired a first name, Indrid.

Keel stresses the case's deceptive, "nonpeople" quality (Ch. 12): Derenberger's family also met "Indrid Cold and his colleagues from the planet Lanulos," whom Mrs. Derenberger thought "were engaged in something evil" and "were probably infiltrating the human race in large numbers"; the entities spread the absurd rumor that Derenberger was "pregnant" with a hybrid child. Keel records that the story "did not fit the mold of the usual UFO contact tale" — its "total physicality . . . seemed too real," leaving him to conclude Derenberger was "either . . . the world's most convincing liar" or had had "a very special set of experiences beyond the limits of ufology." A psychiatric and EEG examination (Dr. "Morgan") found "no evidence of mental disorder" and "a perfectly normal electroencephalogram."

## Contexts Within the Source

Keel uses Derenberger to argue that contactee messages are not loftier wisdom from a "superior culture" but tell percipients "the things we want to hear and believe," and that the phenomenon adapts its manifestations to confirm and escalate the believer's expectations — a core plank of his ultraterrestrial / control-system thesis.

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Keel is openly uncertain: he "suspected the whole thing was some kind of put-on," notes NICAP's newsletter "denounc[ed] it as a hoax" (misspelling Cold as "Kuld"), and leaves open that Derenberger was simply lying.
