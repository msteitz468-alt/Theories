## Claim Statement

Vallée argues the famous Fátima apparitions place the event "very clearly… in the perspective of UFO phenomena." The sequence — preceded by 1915–16 "Angel" sightings — featured a luminous flying globe/disk with falling-leaf motion, buzzing sounds, heat waves, "angel hair," thunderclaps, and a roaring departure, accompanied by the standard psychic component: paralysis, amnesia, prophecy, conversions, and healings.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He quotes the Church's 1930 verdict that the October 13 "Miracle of the Sun" "no astronomical observatory registered and which therefore was not natural," witnessed across social classes and up to thirty miles away — facts that "annul any explanation of collective illusion." He details the spinning silver disk casting "monochromatic sectors" of colored light, plunging in zig-zag toward the terrified crowd, then returning to the sun; the "falling petals" that shrink and dissolve "contrary to the laws of perspective"; and a skeptical priest's conclusion that it was "a heavenly vehicle." He notes the same seriousness/absurdity mix (the Lady at Lourdes telling Bernadette to perform "meaningless actions") and the Earth-as-"forbidden wasteland" motif.
