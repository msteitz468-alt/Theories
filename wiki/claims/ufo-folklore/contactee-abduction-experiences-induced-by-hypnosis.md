## Claim Statement

Vallée argues that the UFO "abduction" experience "is a constant that hypnosis can trigger in
almost anybody," casting doubt on the evidential value of hypnotic regression used by UFO
investigators. He cites the "imaginary abductees" experiment of English professor **Al
Lawson** and clinical hypnotist **Dr. William C. McCall**, in which volunteers with no UFO
sighting produced detailed abduction narratives strikingly similar to famous "real" cases.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "All of the imaginary subjects described many details which are identical to ones found in
> the literature. These patterns range from the obvious ('saucer-shaped') to rare and even
> obscure though well-established details of high strangeness." (Lawson, quoted)

Lawson screened out volunteers informed about UFOs; seven of eight selected subjects went into
deep trance and described a UFO appearing, going aboard, meeting occupants, being examined, and
receiving a message — paralleling the accounts of Betty Hill and Charlie Hickson. Vallée adds a
critique of lie-detector "evidence," quoting a U.S. Department of Justice list of nine reasons
polygraphs are unreliable, and notes "in no abduction case have the investigators taken
precautions against these problems."

## Contexts Within the Source

Supports [[Vallée argues UFOs are real and physical but not extraterrestrial spacecraft]] by
arguing the abduction content "may originate in the witness's mind," triggered "by suggestion,
hypnosis, drugs, and the physical tools of deception." Pairs with
[[Betty Hill's star map is "a map to nowhere" staged for the witness]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Vallée explicitly notes the study "does not demonstrate that UFO abductions are imaginary, or
that hypnotic regression of witnesses is always worthless" — only that "many aspects of the
experience may originate in the witness's mind." He maintains that real witnesses like the
Hills "were not lying, and they were not 'nuts.'"
