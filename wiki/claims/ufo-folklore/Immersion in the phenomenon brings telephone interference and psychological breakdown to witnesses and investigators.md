## Claim Statement

Keel claims that those drawn deeply into the phenomenon — witnesses, contactees, and the investigators themselves — suffer a characteristic toll: a plague of inexplicable telephone interference (eerie electronic sounds, Morse-like beeps, calls with no one on the line, cut-offs) and a spreading psychological derangement (paranoia, fanaticism, mental breakdown, even suicide). He treats both as manifestations of the phenomenon rather than ordinary malfunction or coincidence, and frames the spreading belief that "the U.S. government is being taken over by the space people" as a manufactured derangement.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

On telephone interference (Ch. 17, titled "Even the Bedouins Hate Their Telephone Company"): Keel records contactee Woodrow Derenberger switching to an unlisted number, only to get "crank calls" and calls "that consisted of nothing except eerie electronic sounds and codelike beeps"; Mrs. Kelly's telephone "behaving strangely, ringing when there was no one on the line, and sometimes emitting beeps like Morse code"; and the Scarberrys "plagued by strange sounds . . . [b]eeps and loud garbled noises like a speeded-up phonograph record." Keel's friend, zoologist Ivan T. Sanderson, anchors the theme of bizarre phone trouble besetting investigators.

On psychological toll (Ch. 16, "Paranoiacs Are Made, Not Born"): Keel describes how the immersed percipient can "become mentally unbalanced, abandon his family and his work, develop into a fanatic, and, in several unfortunate cases, end up with a nervous breakdown or commit suicide," likening the recurrence to LSD flashbacks. He records imposters and "strange ones" who "excite the latent paranoia of the UFO enthusiasts," contactee Calvin Parker suffering "a nervous breakdown," and the field as dominated by "paranoid-schizophrenics and obsessive-compulsive personalities," with UFO groups collapsing because "members often regarded their fellow members as 'agents of the air force.'" The chapter's refrain is the cry, "The U.S. government is being taken over by the space people!" Keel includes himself, recalling the "variation of this paranoia" he experienced when accused abroad of being a CIA agent.

## Contexts Within the Source

Keel uses these effects to support his control-system reading: the phenomenon does not merely appear, it acts on percipients — manufacturing belief, fear, and breakdown — which is why he interviews those "directly affected" rather than chance witnesses and tracks them for "changes in personality or outlook."
