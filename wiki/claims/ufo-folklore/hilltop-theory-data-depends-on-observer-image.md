## Claim Statement

Vallée's "Hilltop Theory" is the methodological principle that "the type of data collected by an investigator is a function of his image (the beliefs about him) among his audience." Scientists, the military, and amateur groups each receive a different, biased sample of the phenomenon according to how witnesses perceive them; "one can only learn more by changing one's own projected attitude."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "The Hilltop Theory could be summed up in the statement that the type of data collected by an investigator is a function of his image among his audience. It implies that scientists can only obtain a certain type of data (a biased sample)… One can only learn more by changing one's own projected attitude, and that is what I have done." (Ch. 5)

Vallée illustrates this with the reluctance of high-credibility witnesses (the engineer, Dr. X, the Berkeley woman who felt told "not to report this") to speak to scientists or authorities — meaning the most significant cases are systematically filtered out of official data.
