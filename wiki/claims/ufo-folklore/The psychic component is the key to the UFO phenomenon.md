## Claim Statement

Vallée argues that "no theory of UFOs can be deemed acceptable if it does not account for the reported psychic effects": space-time distortions, telepathic "messages" implanted in witnesses' minds, materializations/dematerializations, and — most importantly — lasting personality changes, healings, and newly acquired paranormal abilities in close-encounter witnesses. The key to understanding the phenomenon, he says, "lies in the psychic effects it produces (or the psychic awareness it makes possible) in its observers."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

He presents the case of an anonymous **engineer** abducted in 1961 (taken to a "teaching machine," returned with altered sleep, total recall, no illness, and a "mission" — yet who saw no occupant), and the heavily investigated **Case of Dr. X** (a French doctor, Nov. 1968: a merging twin-disk sighting, the spontaneous healing of his leg wound and old war injuries, a recurring triangular pigmentation on him and his infant son, and subsequent levitation and telepathy). He links these to Uri Geller and Edgar Cayce, and notes most such witnesses "choose to remain silent."

## Contexts Within the Source

The psychic component is the first of four elements Vallée says forced a new framework (with the triple coverup, the contactee belief patterns, and exploiting groups like UMMO). It underwrites [[UFOs are a thermostat-like control system that conditions human belief]] and connects to the religious cases — see [[The Fatima apparitions of 1917 were a UFO-type event]].
