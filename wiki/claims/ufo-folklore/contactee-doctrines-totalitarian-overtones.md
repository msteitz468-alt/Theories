## Claim Statement

Vallée argues that beneath the trivial verbiage, contactee and saucer-cult doctrines share a
consistent set of **authoritarian political themes**: that humans cannot solve their own
problems, that some people are of "superior" extraterrestrial descent, that civilization was
given by higher beings, and that democracy and money should be abolished in favor of a unified
world government. He compiles four recurring themes.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

From his interview-based catalogue (Ch. 5):

- **Intellectual abdication** — belief that humans are incapable of solving their problems and
  need extraterrestrial rescue "in spite of ourselves."
- **Racist philosophy** — "the pernicious suggestion that some of us… are of extraterrestrial
  descent and therefore constitute a 'higher race'" (he notes Adamski's Venusian and Bordas's
  visitor were "tall Aryan types with long blond hair").
- **Technical impotence** — that civilization came from higher beings, not human genius.
- **Social utopia** — "fantastic economic theories… that democracy should be abolished in favor
  of Utopian systems, usually dictatorial."

Concrete instances: the Order of Melchizedek leaflets ("All the money in the world is
obsolete… All religions are abolished… military service… is forbidden"); Mr. T.'s Space
Platform party; and Claude Vorilhon/Raël's Elohim instruction to "eliminate elections and
votes," create "a world government and a new monetary system," and end military service —
which Vallée compares to Hitler's "final solution" as a "simple" answer demanding faith.

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the engine of Part II ("The Manipulations") and feeds
[[The belief in UFOs produces six identifiable social consequences]] (effects 4 and 6). It is
why Vallée links contactees to extremist figures like [[William Dudley Pelley]] and
[[George Hunt Williamson]] in Chapter 10. Paris Flammonde is quoted: contactee philosophies are
"tinged, if not tainted, with totalitarian overtones."

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Vallée stresses most contactees "are sincere" and "not crazy," trapped by their beliefs rather
than conscious propagandists; he treats the doctrines as symptoms to be examined, not as proof
of a single organized conspiracy.
