## Claim Statement

Vallée states plainly that he believes "UFOs are real" and "technological," producing
genuine physical effects, but that they are **not** spacecraft carrying extraterrestrial
visitors. Instead he proposes "that the UFO we see is, among other things, a device which
creates a distortion of the witness's reality… for a purpose, which is to project images or
fabricated scenes designed to change our belief systems," and that the technology is "only
the incidental support for a worldwide enterprise of subliminal seduction."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "Let me summarize my conclusions thus far. UFOs are real. They are physical devices used
> to affect human consciousness. They may not be from outer space. Their purpose may be to
> achieve social changes on this planet." (Prologue)

Vallée describes three aspects of the phenomenon — **physical** (a ~10-meter region storing
large energy, pulsed light, microwaves that distort perception), **psychological**
(perception distortion, hallucination, post-hypnotic effects, personality change), and
**social** (spreading belief and its political consequences). In Chapter 1 he argues the
objects behave "like an image, or a holographic projection" — vanishing on the spot, fading
"like the Cheshire cat" — and that the too-human occupants (e.g., the "plain old G.I." at
Temple, Oklahoma) are "not genuine extraterrestrials."

## Contexts Within the Source

This reframes the "nuts and bolts" extraterrestrial hypothesis Vallée rejects throughout his
work; the Epilogue by sociologist David Swift notes the position "does not contradict our
present laws of physics." It connects to the Magonia thesis that occupants resemble folklore
denizens rather than planetary explorers (see [[Magonia]]).

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Vallée affirms the *physical* reality forcefully ("a phenomenon that leaves physical traces
must be taken seriously") and concedes the system "may well be able to locate itself in outer
space" — his objection is to the literal interplanetary-craft reading, not to the phenomenon's
existence.
