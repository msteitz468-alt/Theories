## Claim Statement

Vallée argues that whether or not UFOs are literally craft from space, the *belief* that they are is reshaping civilization — and that this belief is coalescing into "the next form of religion." Citing scholar Gordon Creighton's definition — "a myth is that which is TRUER THAN TRUTH" — he holds that if enough people believe in space visitors, "in some sense it will become truer than true, long enough for certain things to change irreversibly."

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

> "It has become very important for large numbers of people to expect visitors from outer space." (Conclusion)

He documents a growing subculture — middle-aged, employed people who drop out of city life on "instructions" received from space sources and "wait," seemingly happy — and warns it may be "a kind, benevolent trap." He describes the San Francisco psychic-fair scene (Allan the Messiah, Puharich, Charles Muses' image of the saucer offering humanity "a lift" over the chasm) and counsels skepticism: "Shouldn't we know something more about the helpful stranger before we jump on board?" He rejects Puharich's claim of benign space contact and the idea that Geller is a Messiah.

## Contexts Within the Source

This is the cultural payoff of the control-system thesis: the conditioning ([[UFO waves act as a Skinnerian schedule of reinforcement]]) shows up as an irreversible shift in belief. Vallée frames the religious apparitions ([[The Fatima apparitions of 1917 were a UFO-type event]]) as historical instances of the same process.
