## Claim Statement

Hynek mounts a detailed indictment of the **Condon Report** (the 1968 University of Colorado study, directed by Dr. Edward U. Condon, that led the Air Force to terminate Project Blue Book). His charges: (a) Condon's widely-publicized summary dismissed UFOs as unworthy of study while **the body of the 937-page report could not explain more than a quarter of the cases it examined**; (b) the committee **"studied the wrong problem,"** defining a UFO merely as anything that "puzzled a given observer" rather than as a report surviving competent screening; and (c) the committee's choice of data was poor — concentrating on a few current cases and ignoring the twenty-year worldwide pattern.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hynek (Ch. 12) describes the report as "a voluminous, rambling, poorly organized report of 937 pages of text, considerably less than half of which was addressed to the investigation of UFO reports," opening with "a singularly slanted summary by Dr. Condon, which adroitly avoided mentioning that … the committee had been unable to furnish adequate explanations for more than a quarter of the cases examined." He calls Condon's gesture toward future study "a masterpiece of throwing a scrap of political meat to the critic dogs."

He quotes the report against itself, citing buried admissions such as "the probability that at least one genuine UFO was involved appears to be fairly high" and "this sighting defies explanation by conventional means." His stated thesis: "(a) the subject matter for study by the Condon group was incorrectly defined, and (b) the committee studied the wrong problem." Because "the UFO was defined by Condon as merely something that puzzled a given observer" — with no screening by "technically aware persons" — the fact that "more than 25 percent of the cases studied were not assignable to natural causes simply means that only 25 percent of the cases studied were eligible for study as UFOs." He faults the data selection ("forty of the ninety cases studied were in the year 1967"; APRO and NICAP files largely unused) and notes the committee never examined many strong cases (e.g., the Beverly, Massachusetts and Philippines navy cases). Hynek also recounts that the committee "had its origin in 'swamp gas'" — his own widely-lampooned 1966 explanation for part of the Michigan sightings — and the ensuing Congressional hearing at which he called for a civilian scientific panel.

## Contexts Within the Source

The critique is the second prong of Hynek's broader argument that institutional science failed the UFO problem (the first being the Air Force's Blue Book history in Ch. 11). It supports [[The UFO phenomenon is a real empirical problem deserving scientific study]] and is an instance of [[Science and the Air Force dismissed UFOs a priori (the "laughter of science")]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hynek does not claim the Condon Report is worthless: read carefully, he says, "the report constitutes about as good an argument for the study of the UFO phenomenon as could have been made," and he credits Condon's personal scientific reputation and "courage in speaking out on controversial issues" even while attacking the summary.
