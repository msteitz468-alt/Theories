## Claim Statement

Hynek proposes that each screened UFO report be characterized by **two independent numbers** and plotted on a two-dimensional **S–P diagram**: a **Strangeness Rating (S)** — how many separate, common-sense-defying "information bits" the report contains — and a **Probability Rating (P)** — the credibility of the witnesses and the coherence of the account. Only reports scoring high on *both* axes (the upper-right region of the diagram) merit scientific attention.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hynek introduces the scheme in Ch. 4: "Each report that has satisfied the definition of UFO used in this book can be assigned two numbers: its Strangeness Rating and its Probability Rating." The Strangeness Rating is "a measure of the number of information bits the report contains, each of which is difficult to explain in common-sense terms" — a mere unexplained night light rates low (only its motion is strange), whereas a craft that descends to within 100 feet of a car, kills its engine, lights, and radio, leaves ground marks, and appears intelligently controlled "receives a high Strangeness Rating because it contains a number of separate very strange items."

The Probability Rating "is, of course, … a highly subjective matter," built from the assessed credibility of the reporters, internal and cross-report consistency, "the conviction transmitted by the reporter to the interrogator," and "that subtle judgment of 'how it all hangs together.'" Hynek reports his own working practice: he scores S "from 1 to 10," finds P harder to assign, never gives a single-witness report a P greater than 3 (and only for reporters of "very solid reputation"), and assigns P of 5 or more only when he "honestly cannot find reason to doubt the word of the reporters." He concludes that a report rated S = 3 and P = 5 "(or a combined index of SP = 35) … should command attention and challenge science."

## Contexts Within the Source

The S–P method is the analytic backbone underneath Hynek's six-fold observational classification (see [[Hynek classified UFO reports and coined the Close Encounter scale]]); it is the filter by which he separates the credible empirical residue from prosaic or low-credibility reports and so undergirds [[The UFO phenomenon is a real empirical problem deserving scientific study]].

## Opposing Information Within the Source

Hynek is candid about the method's limits: certainty (P = 10) is "not practically attainable," P = 0 is excluded by definition (such a report would never have been admitted), assignment of P is "highly subjective," and ideally "would require the judgment of more than one person" together with lie-detector, psychological, and medical input that "is rarely available."
