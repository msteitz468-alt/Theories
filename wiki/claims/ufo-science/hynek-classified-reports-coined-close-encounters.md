## Claim Statement

Hynek's signature contribution: a six-fold classification of UFO reports by observational circumstance — **Nocturnal Lights, Daylight Discs, Radar-Visual**, and three grades of **Close Encounter** (**First Kind**: close sighting; **Second Kind**: with physical effects/traces; **Third Kind**: with animate occupants) — coupled with a **Strangeness Rating** and a witness **Probability/credibility Rating** for weighing each case.

## Source Attribution and Direct Evidence

Hynek devotes a chapter to each category, building each from interrogated case files. He argues a report matters only when *both* its strangeness and the credibility/number of its witnesses are high — filtering out lone or prosaic reports. The "Close Encounter of the Third Kind" terminology entered popular culture via Steven Spielberg's 1977 film (Hynek served as a consultant and made a cameo).

The methodological backbone of [[The UFO phenomenon is a real empirical problem deserving scientific study]]; see the concept [[Hynek's UFO classification]]. The scheme became the standard vocabulary later used across ufology (cf. Vallée, Keel, and modern investigators).
