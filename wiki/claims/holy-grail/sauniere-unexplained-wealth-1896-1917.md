**Claim Statement**

Beginning in 1896, Bérenger Saunière — a rural parish priest whose annual income had averaged the equivalent of £6 sterling per year — began spending on a scale the source describes as equivalent to "several million pounds at least" by the end of his life in 1917. The source of this wealth was never explained.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

The source describes the expenditure: construction of the Tour Magdala (a tower), the Villa Bethania (an opulent country house he never personally occupied), restoration and bizarre redecoration of the village church, a modern road to the village, a zoological garden, an orangery, rare china, precious fabrics, antique marbles, and a magnificent library. Saunière also entertained on a lavish scale, hosting sumptuous banquets for parishioners.

Among notable visitors to the remote village: Emma Calvé (the famous opera diva and Paris esoteric socialite), the French Secretary of State for Culture, and the Archduke Johann von Habsburg (cousin of Emperor Franz-Josef). Bank statements revealed that Saunière and the archduke had opened consecutive accounts on the same day, and that the archduke had transferred a substantial sum to the priest. The archduke is subsequently identified by the source as Johann Salvator von Habsburg, who renounced his titles in 1889 and appears to have died in Argentina c.1910–11.

When Saunière's bishop attempted to call him to account, Saunière refused to explain his wealth, refused transfer orders, and was accused of simony. A local tribunal suspended him; he appealed to the Vatican, which exonerated and reinstated him. The source interprets this as evidence that the Vatican itself had "an urgent concern of its own" in the matter.

Saunière's housekeeper Marie Denarnaud, who was promised to reveal a "secret" that would make purchaser Noël Corbu "not only rich but also powerful," suffered a stroke in January 1953, rendering her speechless, and died carrying the secret. After World War II, when new currency registration would expose tax evaders, she burned vast quantities of old franc notes in the garden rather than disclose her revenues.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The authors argue that the wealth cannot be explained by gold or jewels alone: "Mere money, gold or jewels would not, in themselves, explain a number of facets to his story... They would not account for his introduction to Hoffet's circle... They would not explain the Church's intense interest in the matter... They would not explain a priest's refusal to administer the last rites." They propose that the wealth derived from knowledge — specifically, an "explosive secret" that could be used to extract payments from powerful interests, possibly including the Vatican.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**

The authors acknowledge they cannot identify a single definitive explanation for the wealth. They list several possible treasure sources (Visigoth treasure, Temple of Jerusalem treasure, Cathar treasure, Templar treasure, treasure of Dagobert II) but conclude the most likely scenario involves a secret rather than conventional treasure.
