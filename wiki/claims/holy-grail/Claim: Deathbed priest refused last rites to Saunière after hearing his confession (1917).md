**Claim Statement**

On January 17th, 1917, Bérenger Saunière suffered a sudden stroke. A neighbouring priest was called to hear his final confession and administer the last rites. After entering the sick-room and hearing Saunière's confession, the priest emerged visibly shaken and refused to administer extreme unction. According to eyewitness testimony, he "never smiled again" and lapsed into acute depression for several months.

Saunière died unabsolved on January 22nd, 1917. The following morning, his body was placed upright in an armchair on the terrace of the Tour Magdala in an ornate robe adorned with scarlet tassels, and unidentified mourners filed past, many plucking tassels from the dead man's garment. The ceremony has never been explained.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

"As Saunière lay on his deathbed, a priest was called from a neighbouring parish to hear his final confession and administer the last rites. The priest duly arrived and retired into the sick-room. According to eye-witness testimony, he emerged shortly thereafter, visibly shaken. In the words of one account he 'never smiled again'. In the words of another he lapsed into an acute depression that lasted for several months."

The source further notes that a receipt exists showing Marie Denarnaud ordered a coffin for Saunière on January 12th — five days before his stroke — when witnesses attest he appeared in excellent health. The date of January 17th also appears on the tombstone of the Marquise d'Hautpoul de Blanchefort, the stone Saunière had previously obliterated; and January 17th is the feast day of Saint-Sulpice.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The authors argue this event is incompatible with the explanation that Saunière merely discovered buried treasure. They suggest the priest refused the last rites because Saunière's confession revealed something of such magnitude that the priest deemed absolution inappropriate — possibly implicating the Church itself in something gravely compromising. The pre-ordered coffin implies the death was anticipated, adding to suggestions of foul play or a controlled end.
