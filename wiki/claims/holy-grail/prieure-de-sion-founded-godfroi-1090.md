**Claim Statement**

According to the "Prieuré documents" (Dossiers secrets deposited in the Bibliothèque Nationale), the Ordre de Sion was founded by Godfroi de Bouillon in 1090 (some documents give the date as 1099, after the fall of Jerusalem). Its official seat was the Abbey of Notre Dame du Mont de Sion, built at Godfroi's express command on Mount Sion just south of Jerusalem, on the ruins of an old Byzantine basilica dating from the fourth century. The Order's members were "Chevaliers de l'Ordre de Notre Dame de Sion." King Baudouin I, Godfroi's younger brother, "owed his throne" to the Ordre de Sion.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

The source confirms the abbey's existence through multiple external sources. A charter signed by Prior Arnaldus is dated July 19, 1116. Arnaldus's name appears alongside Hugues de Payen (first Grand Master of the Knights Templar) in a charter dated May 2, 1125. A Pope Alexander III Bull of 1178 officially confirms the Ordre de Sion's possessions across France, Italy, Spain, and the Holy Land.

A 1172 chronicler describes the abbey as "extremely well fortified, with its own walls, towers and battlements." A 1698 historian states: "There were in Jerusalem during the Crusades ... knights attached to the Abbey of Notre Dame de Sion who took the name of 'Chevaliers de l'Ordre de Notre Dame de Sion.'"

Until World War II, the Orleans municipal archives held no fewer than twenty charters explicitly citing the Ordre de Sion; seventeen were destroyed in the 1940 bombing of the city. The surviving three, and the Louis VII charters installing the Order at Orleans in 1152, can still be consulted at Orleans.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The source notes that a band of monks from Calabria arrived in the Ardennes Forest (part of Godfroi de Bouillon's domains) in 1070 — twenty-nine years before the First Crusade — under the patronage of Mathilde de Toscane, Godfroi's aunt. They established an abbey at Orval near Stenay (where Dagobert II had been killed) and then disappeared by 1108. The source proposes these monks may have become the Ordre de Sion. One of them may have been Peter the Hermit, proposed as Godfroi's personal tutor and co-instigator of the First Crusade.

When Jerusalem fell in 1099, an anonymous conclave met in secret — its leader described as "a certain bishop from Calabria" — and offered the throne to Godfroi. Godfroi declined the title of King, accepting "Defender of the Holy Sepulchre" instead. The source argues this conclave may have been the Ordre de Sion.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**

The source acknowledges no standard 20th-century work on the Crusades mentions any Ordre de Sion. It also acknowledges that whether the Order was formed before or after the abbey's construction cannot be determined from surviving documents.
