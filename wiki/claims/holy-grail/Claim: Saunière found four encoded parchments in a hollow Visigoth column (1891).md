**Claim Statement**

In 1891, while restoring the church at Rennes-le-Château, the parish priest Bérenger Saunière removed the altar-stone (which rested on two archaic Visigoth columns) and found one of the columns to be hollow. Inside it were four parchments preserved in sealed wooden tubes. Two were genealogies dating from 1244 and 1644 respectively. Two were composed in the 1780s by Saunière's predecessor, the Abbé Antoine Bigou, and superficially appeared to be pious Latin texts excerpted from the New Testament — but were in fact carriers of ingenious ciphers.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

The source states: "In the course of his endeavours he removed the altar-stone, which rested on two archaic Visigoth columns. One of these columns proved to be hollow. Inside the curé found four parchments preserved in sealed wooden tubes. Two of these parchments are said to have comprised genealogies, one dating from 1244, the other from 1644."

One encoded message, decoded from raised letters in one parchment, reads: *A DAGOBERT II ROI ET A SION EST CE TRESOR ET IL EST LA MORT* — "To Dagobert II, King, and to Sion belongs this treasure and he is there dead."

A second decoded message reads: *BERGERE PAS DE TENTATION QUE POUSSIN TENIERS GARDENT LA CLEF PAX DCLXXXI PAR LA CROIX ET CE CHEVAL DE DIEU J'ACHEVE CE DAEMON DE GARDIEN A MIDI POMMES BLEUES* — "Shepherdess, no temptation. That Poussin, Teniers, hold the key; peace 681. By the cross and this horse of God, I complete/destroy this daemon of the guardian at noon. Blue apples."

After this discovery, Saunière was immediately dispatched at his bishop's expense to Paris, where he met the Abbé Bieil (Director General of the Seminary of Saint-Sulpice) and Bieil's nephew Émile Hoffet, a specialist in linguistics, cryptography, and palaeography with connections to esoteric circles including Stéphane Mallarmé, Maurice Maeterlinck, and Claude Debussy.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The source uses this discovery as the foundation of the entire investigation. The authors note that some of the ciphers are "fantastically complex and unpredictable, defying even a computer," and that a cipher of this complexity would require a key that someone, somewhere, must possess. The authors suggest Saunière's trip to Paris and subsequent introduction to Hoffet's esoteric circle implies that the parchments contained information of religious or political sensitivity far exceeding mere local treasure.

The church itself was decorated bizarrely after Saunière's return: a statue of the demon Asmodeus (described as "custodian of secrets, guardian of hidden treasures and builder of Solomon's Temple") was placed at the entrance; the Stations of the Cross contained anomalous details (e.g. Station XIV depicts a nocturnal sky with a full moon during Jesus's burial, suggesting the burial occurred after nightfall or that the body is being carried *out* of the tomb).
