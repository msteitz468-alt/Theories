**Claim Statement**

In the early 1970s, an actual tomb was located on the outskirts of Arques (approximately six miles from Rennes-le-Château, three miles from the Château de Blanchefort) that is identical in setting, dimensions, proportions, shape, surrounding vegetation, and even in the circular outcrop of rock on which one of Poussin's shepherds rests his foot, to the tomb depicted in Poussin's famous painting "Les Bergers d'Arcadie" ("The Shepherds of Arcadia"). One of the peaks in the painting's background is identified as Rennes-le-Château itself.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

"In the early 1970s, however, an actual tomb was located, identical to the one in the painting — identical in setting, dimensions, proportions, shape, surrounding vegetation, even in the circular outcrop of rock on which one of Poussin's shepherds rests his foot. This actual tomb stands on the outskirts of a village called Arques — approximately six miles from Rennes-le-Château, and three miles from the chateau of Blanchefort."

The source notes that Anthony Blunt and other Poussin experts had previously asserted the landscape in the painting was wholly imaginary. The tomb had reportedly been standing since before living memory in the area, with possible mention in a 1709 memoir. Its land was owned until the 1950s by an American, Louis Lawrence of Boston, whose wife and mother-in-law were buried in it (he had opened it in the 1920s and found it empty).

During filming, the crew left the site for lunch and returned to find a crude and violent attempt had been made to smash into the sepulchre.

The painting's famous inscription "ET IN ARCADIA EGO" is noted to lack a verb. A television viewer suggested it was an anagram of *I TEGO ARCANA DEI* — "Begone! I conceal the secrets of God."

**Contexts Within the Source**

The painting's significance is further amplified by the 1656 letter from the Abbé Louis Fouquet to his brother Nicolas Fouquet (Superintendent of Finances to Louis XIV), describing a meeting with Poussin in Rome: "He and I discussed certain things, which I shall with ease be able to explain to you in detail — things which will give you, through Monsieur Poussin, advantages which even kings would have great pains to draw from him ... and which, according to him, it is possible that nobody else will ever rediscover in the centuries to come." Shortly after receiving this letter, Nicolas Fouquet was arrested, imprisoned for life, and his entire correspondence confiscated and personally reviewed by Louis XIV. The king subsequently went determinedly out of his way to acquire the original of Poussin's painting, which was then sequestered in his private apartments at Versailles.
