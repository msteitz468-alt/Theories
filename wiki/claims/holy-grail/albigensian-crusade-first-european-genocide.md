**Claim Statement**

In 1209, Pope Innocent III ordered a Crusade against the Cathar heresy in the Languedoc. The resulting war (lasting nearly forty years) was so devastating — entire populations slaughtered, towns razed, crops destroyed — that the source characterizes it as "the first case of 'genocide' in modern European history." At Béziers alone, at least 15,000 men, women, and children were massacred wholesale, many of them in a church. The papal legate Arnaud-Amaury allegedly replied to a commander asking how to distinguish heretics from true believers: "Kill them all. God will recognise His own."

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

"In 1209 an army of some 30,000 knights and foot-soldiers from Northern Europe descended like a whirlwind on the Languedoc ... In the town of Béziers alone, for example, at least 15,000 men, women and children were slaughtered wholesale — many of them in the sanctuary of the church itself. When an officer inquired of the pope's representative how he might distinguish heretics from true believers, the reply was, 'Kill them all. God will recognise His own.'"

The source notes the same papal representative wrote proudly to Innocent III: "neither age nor sex nor status was spared." Perpignan fell, Narbonne fell, Carcassonne fell, Toulouse fell. The Crusade lasted nearly forty years.

The source notes that Saint Bernard himself, visiting the Languedoc in 1145, was "less appalled by the heretics than by the corruption of his own Church. 'No sermons are more Christian than theirs,' he declared, 'and their morals are pure.'"

**Contexts Within the Source**

The source places the Albigensian Crusade in the context of the Languedoc's unique status: an independent principality whose culture was "the most advanced and sophisticated in Christendom" — with Jewish, Islamic, and Gnostic thought thriving alongside courtly poetry and the cult of *fin'amor* (courtly love). The Languedoc practiced religious tolerance and had largely rejected Roman Church authority. The crusade was motivated by both the Church's desire to extirpate the Cathar heresy (which threatened to displace Catholicism in the region) and northern European lords' covetousness of the Languedoc's wealth.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**

The source notes that the famous "Kill them all" quotation, "though widely reported, may be apocryphal. Nevertheless, it typifies the fanatical zeal and bloodlust with which the atrocities were perpetrated."
