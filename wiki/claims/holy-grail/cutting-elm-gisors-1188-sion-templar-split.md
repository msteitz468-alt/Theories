**Claim Statement**

In 1188 a formal separation occurred between the Prieuré de Sion (formerly Ordre de Sion) and the Order of the Temple. The "Prieuré documents" call this event "the cutting of the elm" and state it took place at Gisors. From 1188 onward, the two organizations were officially distinct. The Prieuré de Sion elected its first independent Grand Master: Jean de Gisors. It also modified its name (from Ordre de Sion to Prieuré de Sion) and adopted the subtitle "Ormus." The Rose-Croix is said to have been founded by Jean de Gisors in 1188.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

Both recorded history and local tradition confirm that a significant and violent incident at Gisors in 1188 did involve the cutting of an ancient elm. The elm stood in the Champ Sacré (Sacred Field) adjacent to the fortress of Gisors, a field deemed sacred since pre-Christian times. The tree was more than 800 years old and so large that nine men linking hands could barely encompass its trunk. During a meeting between Henry II of England and Philippe II of France, French forces (allegedly five squadrons commanded by distinguished lords) advanced on the tree with carpenters and sling-men. Richard Coeur de Lion participated in its defense. The tree was felled by the French.

The source cites Robert Denyau, curé of Gisors, who composed a history of Gisors in 1629 stating explicitly that the Rose-Croix was founded by Jean de Gisors in 1188 — a verbatim seventeenth-century confirmation of the Dossiers secrets claims.

Until 1188, the Ordre de Sion and the Order of the Temple shared the same Grand Master (e.g. Hugues de Payen and Bertrand de Blanchefort were allegedly Grand Masters of both simultaneously). After 1188, the Prieuré de Sion selected its own independent Grand Masters.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The source connects the 1188 rupture to the fall of Jerusalem in 1187, attributed in the Dossiers secrets to Gerard de Ridefort's "treason" (not merely ineptitude). With the fall of Jerusalem, the abbey on Mount Sion was lost and Sion returned en masse to France (to Orleans). The source speculates that the Ordre de Sion may have endorsed or even collaborated in the subsequent suppression of the Templars in 1307 — Guillaume de Gisors (Grand Master at the time) appears in Inquisition records as a colleague of the king's men who seized Templar property.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**

The source cannot externally confirm that the elm-cutting involved either the Templars or the Prieuré de Sion. It acknowledges: "There is no external confirmation that this event was related in any way to either the Knights Templar or the Ordre de Sion." The surviving accounts are described as "too vague, too scant, too incomprehensible, too contradictory."
