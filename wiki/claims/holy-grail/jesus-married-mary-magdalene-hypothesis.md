**Claim Statement**

The source's central hypothesis is that Jesus was married to Mary Magdalene, that they had children, and that after the Crucifixion the Magdalene (with at least one child) was smuggled to Gaul, where established Jewish communities would have provided refuge. This bloodline — the "Sang Real" (Royal Blood) — eventually intermarried with other Jewish, Roman, and Visigoth families and in the fifth century became allied with the Frankish royal line, producing the Merovingian dynasty.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

The source mounts several interlocking arguments:

1. **Argument from Jewish custom**: According to Dr. Geza Vermes of Oxford University, "There is complete silence in the Gospels concerning the marital status of Jesus... Such a state of affairs is sufficiently unusual in ancient Jewry to prompt further enquiry." The source argues that in first-century Judaism, celibacy was not merely unusual but "vigorously condemned" — one Jewish writer compared deliberate celibacy with murder. It was as obligatory for a Jewish father to find a wife for his son as to ensure his circumcision. The absence of any mention of celibacy (rather than marriage) is interpreted as evidence that Jesus conformed to convention and was married.

2. **The Rabbi argument**: Jesus is called "Rabbi" in the Gospels. The Jewish Mishnaic Law states explicitly: "An unmarried man may not be a teacher." If Jesus was a rabbi in the strict sense, marriage would have been virtually certain.

3. **The Wedding at Cana**: The source argues the wedding at Cana (John 2) may have been Jesus's own wedding. Mary his mother behaves "as if she were the hostess" — ordering Jesus to replenish the wine. The bride and groom remain anonymous throughout. Jesus is specifically "called" to the wedding before he has begun his ministry. The source argues that if it were someone else's wedding, Mary's imperious behavior would be inexplicable.

4. **The Beloved Disciple**: The source examines the figure of the "Beloved Disciple" in the Fourth Gospel and the special relationship between Jesus and the figure called the Magdalene. The anointing scenes (particularly the anointing of Jesus's feet and wiping them with her hair) are argued to follow the ritual of a Jewish bride anointing her husband.

5. **A retired Anglican priest's letter** (unsolicited): Following the 1972 BBC documentary, the authors received a letter asserting that the "treasure" of Rennes-le-Château consisted of "incontrovertible proof that the Crucifixion was a fraud and that Jesus was alive as late as A.D. 45." Although they found this claim "flagrantly absurd," they note the writer had connections through Canon Alfred Leslie Liney to Émile Hoffet — whom Saunière had visited in Paris.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The source frames this explicitly as a "tentative hypothesis" assembled from circumstantial, literary, and archaeological evidence — not as demonstrated fact. The authors state: "We began to test it at least as a tentative hypothesis." They acknowledge it is "preposterous" without further confirmation and proceed to explore whether evidence can support it.

The hypothesis is presented as explaining multiple otherwise anomalous elements of their investigation: the sacred status of the Magdalene and her cult significance during the Crusades; the fabulous dual-paternity birth legend of Merovee; the Church's pact with Clovis's bloodline; the assassination and erasure of Dagobert II; the Carolingians' obsession with legitimating themselves via claimed Merovingian descent; the Grail family romances; and Godfroi de Bouillon's triumphant capture of Jerusalem (which, if he were descended from Jesus, would have been reclaiming his own heritage).

**Opposing Information Within the Source**

The source acknowledges there is "no explicit statement" in the Gospels that Jesus was married. It also acknowledges the entire scenario of the Magdalene's flight to Gaul "rests on inference rather than direct evidence." The authors caution: "We were not prepared to accept [the Prieuré documents] on blind, unquestioning faith."
