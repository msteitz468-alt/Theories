**Claim Statement**

In many early manuscripts the Holy Grail is called the "Sangraal" or "Sangreal" (even Malory's later version calls it "Sangreal"). The source argues this single word was subsequently divided in the wrong place: rather than "San Graal" (Holy Cup) or "San Greal" (Holy Grail), it should be read as "Sang Raal" or "Sang Real" — in modern spelling, *Sang Royal* — meaning "Royal Blood." The Grail therefore pertains not to a physical cup but to a bloodline. The source identifies this bloodline as that of Jesus himself, transmitted through Mary Magdalene to the Merovingian dynasty.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

"In many of the earlier manuscripts, the Grail is called the 'Sangraal'; and even in the later version by Malory, it is called the 'Sangreal'. It is likely that some such form 'Sangraal' or 'Sangreal' was in fact the original one. It is also likely that that one word was subsequently broken in the wrong place. In other words 'Sangraal' or 'Sangreal' may not have been intended to divide into 'San Graal' or 'San Greal' but into 'Sang Raal' or 'Sang Real'. Or, to employ the modern spelling, Sang Royal. Royal blood."

The authors note that traditional associations of the Grail (the cup that caught Jesus's blood) would reinforce this supposition: the Grail has always been linked to blood. The emphasis throughout the Grail romances on genealogy, lineage, pedigree, and inheritance — and the protagonist's accession to the Grail by virtue of his lineage — is presented as further evidence that the Grail is fundamentally about bloodline.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The wordplay is presented not as definitive proof but as "taken in conjunction with the emphasis on genealogy and lineage, there is not much room for doubt." The source then raises the question: whose blood, and whose bloodline? This leads directly to the central hypothesis that the Grail bloodline is that of Jesus via Mary Magdalene and the Merovingians. The source notes that medieval traditions maintain the Grail was brought not to Britain by Joseph of Arimathea but to France by the Magdalene — and the church at Rennes-le-Château is itself consecrated to the Magdalene.

The source also notes that the Grail romances are set in Merovingian times (e.g. the *Queste del Saint Graal* states events occurred 454 years after the Resurrection, i.e. c.A.D. 487 — in the first flush of Merovingian power), and that Wolfram von Eschenbach insists Arthur's court is at Nantes, setting the romances in France rather than Britain.
