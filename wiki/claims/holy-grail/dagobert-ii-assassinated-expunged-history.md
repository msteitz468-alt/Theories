**Claim Statement**

On December 23, 679, Dagobert II — king of Austrasia and the last effective Merovingian king — was assassinated while sleeping under a tree near Stenay, supposedly by a lance thrust through the eye by a servant in the pay of Pépin d'Héristal, the Carolingian mayor of the palace. This assassination is alleged to have been endorsed or abetted by the Roman Church. Subsequently, Dagobert II was systematically excluded from official Frankish histories and royal genealogies for centuries — the source argues this erasure was deliberate, to hide the Church's complicity in the murder of what was (per the source's bloodline hypothesis) a lineal descendant of Jesus.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

The source connects Dagobert II directly to Rennes-le-Château: Dagobert was married to a Visigoth princess, and Rennes-le-Château was in that era a Visigoth stronghold. Documents speak of great wealth Dagobert amassed for military conquest, concealed in the environs of Rennes-le-Château — which would explain the parchment message's claim that "to Dagobert II, King, and to Sion belongs this treasure."

Stenay (formerly Sathanacum), where Dagobert was killed, appears repeatedly as a significant place in the "Prieuré documents." The source notes that it was near Stenay that the monks from Orval (the Calabrian band possibly connected to the founding of the Ordre de Sion) arrived in 1070 — some five hundred years after the assassination.

Dagobert was canonized as a saint by the Catholic Church in the eleventh century — despite being murdered with apparent Church complicity — which the source finds suspicious. If the bloodline hypothesis is correct, the Church's participation in Dagobert's murder would have constituted "not only regicide, but ... according to its own tenets, a form of deicide as well."

**Contexts Within the Source**

The source argues that Dagobert's erasure from history was not accidental but deliberate: the Carolingians, who supplanted the Merovingians, needed to delegitimize the dynasty they had deposed. Simultaneously, the Church — if it was party to the assassination — needed to bury the evidence. The source suggests the "Prieuré de Sion" was formed partly to preserve the memory of Dagobert and protect the surviving bloodline.

The Carolingians are noted to have been obsessed with legitimizing themselves by claiming Merovingian pedigree — Charlemagne's line claimed Merovingian descent. The source argues this obsession is only intelligible if the Merovingian bloodline was regarded as uniquely sacred.
