**Claim Statement**

The source's culminating hypothesis is that Jesus's bloodline — transmitted by Mary Magdalene to Gaul after the Crucifixion — became allied with the Frankish royal line in the fifth century, thereby producing the Merovingian dynasty. The Merovingians were thus allegedly descended from Jesus. This explains, in the source's argument, why the Merovingians were regarded as sacred "sorcerer kings" with miraculous powers, why the Catholic Church entered into an inviolable pact with Clovis's bloodline, and why the Carolingian usurpation of the Merovingians was felt to be so monstrous — amounting, in the Church's own terms, to a form of deicide.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

The source examines the semi-legendary birth of Merovee: "according to both the leading Frankish chronicler and to subsequent tradition, Merovee was born of two fathers. When already pregnant by her husband, King Clodio, Merovee's mother supposedly went swimming in the ocean. In the water she is said to have been seduced ... by an unidentified marine creature from beyond the sea." The source argues this dual-paternity legend may encode a historical memory of a blood intermingling — perhaps a lineage arriving "from beyond the sea" (i.e. from Palestine/Gaul via the Mediterranean) being merged with the Frankish royal line. The word "Merovee" echoes both the French word for "mother" (*mère*) and the French/Latin words for "sea" (*mer*/*mare*).

Merovingian kings were known as the "longhaired kings" — their power believed to reside in their hair (as Samson's did). They were called "sorcerer kings" or "thaumaturge kings," allegedly capable of healing by laying on of hands; their robes' tassels were said to have miraculous curative powers. They supposedly bore a distinctive birthmark — a red cross, either over the heart or between the shoulder blades (an "anticipation of the Templar blazon"). The source argues these attributes point to a claim of divine or semi-divine blood.

The Church's pact with Clovis (baptized c.496), binding the Church to the Merovingian dynasty in perpetuity, is argued to make sense only if the bloodline was regarded as uniquely sacred — i.e. directly descended from Jesus.

Dagobert II's assassination in 679 and subsequent erasure from history (he was missing from official Frankish records for centuries) is presented as evidence the Church participated in, and then sought to conceal, what would have been — if the bloodline hypothesis is correct — an act of deicide.

The source traces the bloodline's survival after the Carolingian usurpation through the line of Guillem de Gellone (Count of Razes), the Comte de Razès near Rennes-le-Château, and through subsequent noble families eventually connecting to the house of Lorraine and Godfroi de Bouillon.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The source acknowledges: "We cannot point to one man and assert that he is Jesus's lineal descendant. Family trees bifurcate, subdivide and in the course of centuries multiply into veritable forests." It names among contemporary families of alleged Merovingian lineage: Habsburg-Lorraine, Plantard, Luxembourg, Montpezat, Montesquieu, Sinclair (Britain), and various branches of the Stuarts. The Devonshire family is also mentioned.

The alleged political goal of the Prieuré de Sion — a theocratic United States of Europe ruled by a dynasty descended from Jesus — is presented as the contemporary practical implication of the bloodline's survival.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**

The authors caution throughout that the hypothesis rests on inference. They acknowledge: "Although [the hypothesis] explained many things, it could not yet in itself be supported. There were still too many holes in it, too many inconsistencies and anomalies, too many loose ends."
