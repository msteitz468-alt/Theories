**Claim Statement**

According to the "Prieuré documents" (Dossiers secrets), the founders of the Order of the Temple included not only the traditionally recognized Hugues de Payen and his companions, but also members of the Ordre de Sion: André de Montbard (Saint Bernard's uncle), Archambaud de Saint-Aignan, Nivard de Montdidier, Gondemar, and Rossal. King Baudouin I was "obliged" (the Dossiers secrets uses a verb with connotations of coercion) to negotiate the constitution of the Order of the Temple at Saint Leonard of Acre — one of the fiefs of the Ordre de Sion — in March 1117, and the Templars were made public in 1118. The source concludes the Ordre de Sion stood behind the Knights Templar as its parent organization.

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

The Dossiers secrets states: "Hugues de Payen, Bisol de St. Omer and Hugues, Comte de Champagne, along with certain members of the Ordre de Sion, André de Montbard, Archambaud de Saint-Aignan, Nivard de Montdidier, Gondemar and Rossal" as founders. A charter shows Arnaldus (Prior of Notre Dame de Sion) and Hugues de Payen together on a document dated May 2, 1125.

The source's own historical research confirms that the Templars were active at least as early as 1114 — four years before the conventionally accepted foundation date of 1118 — based on a letter from the bishop of Chartres to the Count of Champagne. Saint Bernard subsequently became the Templars' primary public advocate and wrote their official Rule, which is essentially the Cistercian Rule. André de Montbard's two brothers joined Bernard at Clairvaux simultaneously.

The source argues that both the Cistercian order (under Bernard) and the Templars began to prosper simultaneously between c.1115 and 1140, acquiring vast wealth. It proposes this simultaneous rise was not coincidental but the result of direction from the Ordre de Sion.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The source frames this as "a web of intricate, elusive and provocative connections" suggesting "not a number of essentially disconnected people, events and phenomena which just 'happened' ... to overlap" but rather "a plan of some sort, conceived and engineered by some human agency" — i.e. the Ordre de Sion. The count of Champagne's dual roles (donating land for Clairvaux, being lord of Troyes whence the Grail romances issued, being the first to contemplate joining the Templars) are presented as evidence of this coordinated design.

**Opposing Information Within the Source**

The source acknowledges this is inferential: "We could not but wonder whether this multitude of intricate connections was indeed wholly coincidental." It presents the pattern as highly suggestive but not definitively proven.
