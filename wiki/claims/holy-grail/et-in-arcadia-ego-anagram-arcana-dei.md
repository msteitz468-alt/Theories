**Claim Statement**

The inscription on the tomb in Poussin's "Les Bergers d'Arcadie" — *ET IN ARCADIA EGO* — lacks a verb, which the source considers suspicious. A television viewer proposed that the letters (being precise in number) were intended as an anagram, with the verb missing deliberately so the inscription would contain only the required letters. Rearranged, the letters form: *I TEGO ARCANA DEI* — "Begone! I conceal the secrets of God."

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

"One of our television viewers, in writing to us, suggested that this might indeed be so and then rearranged the letters into a coherent Latin statement. The result was: I TEGO ARCANA DEI (BEGONE! I CONCEAL THE SECRETS OF GOD). We were pleased and intrigued by this ingenious exercise. We did not realise at the time how extraordinarily appropriate the resulting admonition was."

The authors note that the codes in the parchments found by Saunière had relied heavily on anagrams, on the transposition and rearrangement of letters — making this interpretation contextually consistent.

**Contexts Within the Source**

This anagram is presented as one of several layers of concealment in the Rennes-le-Château mystery. The conventional interpretation of "ET IN ARCADIA EGO" is as a memento mori — Death speaking, announcing his presence even in the pastoral paradise of Arcadia. The source suggests the missing verb was deliberate and the inscription was designed to encode the hidden message about secrets of God. The authors describe the anagram as "extraordinarily appropriate" given what they later conclude the "secret" to be (the Jesus bloodline / Prieuré de Sion knowledge).
