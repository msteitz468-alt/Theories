**Claim Statement**

The Dossiers secrets (deposited in the Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris) include a list of alleged "Naut­onniers" (navigators/helmsmen — the official Prieuré de Sion title for Grand Master). The complete list as given:

1. Jean de Gisors 1188–1220
2. Marie de Saint-Clair 1220–66
3. Guillaume de Gisors 1266–1307
4. Édouard de Bar 1307–36
5. Jeanne de Bar 1336–51
6. Jean de Saint-Clair 1351–66
7. Blanche d'Évreux 1366–98
8. Nicolas Flamel 1398–1418
9. René d'Anjou 1418–80
10. Iolande de Bar 1480–83
11. Sandro Filipepi (Botticelli) 1483–1510
12. Léonard de Vinci (Leonardo da Vinci) 1510–19
13. Connétable de Bourbon 1519–27
14. Ferdinand de Gonzague 1527–75
15. Louis de Nevers 1575–95
16. Robert Fludd 1595–1637
17. J. Valentin Andréa 1637–54
18. Robert Boyle 1654–91
19. Isaac Newton 1691–1727
20. Charles Radclyffe 1727–46
21. Charles de Lorraine 1746–80
22. Maximilian de Lorraine 1780–1801
23. Charles Nodier 1801–44
24. Victor Hugo 1844–85
25. Claude Debussy 1885–1918
26. Jean Cocteau 1918–[present at time of writing]

**Source Attribution and Direct Evidence**

The list appears in the Dossiers secrets, a collection of documents assembled (or fabricated) in the 1960s by Pierre Plantard de Saint-Clair and associates and deposited in the Bibliothèque Nationale. The authors note their initial reaction was cynicism: "it immediately provoked our scepticism" because the famous names seemed to be "precisely the kind that twentieth-century organisations have often attempted to appropriate for themselves."

However, their subsequent research into each alleged Grand Master found: (a) most known figures on the list showed interest in Hermetic thought and/or secret societies; (b) virtually all had some contact — direct or through close mutual friends — with their predecessor and successor on the list; (c) most were either allied by blood to the house of Lorraine or associated with it; (d) many were associated with key sites in the investigation (Rennes-le-Château, Gisors, Stenay, Saint-Sulpice). The source describes this degree of consistency as unlikely to be coincidental.

For specific figures: Robert Fludd served as tutor to sons of the Duke of Lorraine. Johann Valentin Andréa (Fludd's successor) composed the "Rosicrucian manifestos" and confessed to writing *The Chemical Wedding of Christian Rosenkreuz*. Robert Boyle and Isaac Newton, while not conventionally associated with the occult, were steeped in Hermetic and alchemical thought. Victor Hugo was connected to esoteric circles. Claude Debussy was deeply immersed in Parisian occultism via Josephin Péladan and the Rosicrucian Order he founded; Debussy's name appears in connection with the Rennes-le-Château mystery independently (Émile Hoffet knew Debussy, and Hoffet met Saunière). Jean Cocteau was known to have been involved in occult affairs and controversially painted a mural in the church of Notre-Dame de France, London.

**Contexts Within the Source**

The source notes that the list alternates between two distinct types: (1) figures of "monumental stature" who influenced Western culture through esoterica, arts, or sciences; (2) members of a specific interlinked network of noble/royal families, many obscure. The source argues a forger seeking to create a prestigious lineage would have no reason to include obscure nobles like Édouard de Bar or Maximilian de Lorraine (who was "signally inept on the battlefield"), suggesting the list reflects genuine historical record rather than fabrication for prestige.
