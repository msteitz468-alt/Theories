## Identity and Nomenclature (from the source)

In Bramley's account, Freemasonry descends from the elite mason guilds that the Brotherhood sponsored in ancient Egypt — guilds of "free men" with "Warden" and "Master" ranks and mystical traditions that built temples (including Solomon's). He uses the white-lambskin **apron** (traced to the Melchizedek Priesthood, c. 2200 B.C., and to apron-wearing depictions of the Custodial gods) as the visible thread connecting Freemasonry to the ancient Brotherhood.

## Primary Textual Appearances

- Ch. 8 (Melchizedek's Apron): the guild origins, the apron lineage, the *Book of the Dead* "Grand Master" passage.
- Later chapters: Freemasonry as a politically powerful Brotherhood offshoot behind the knightly orders and the American and French revolutions.

## Source-Specific Portrait

Bramley treats Freemasonry as a sincere-membership organization sitting atop a corrupted, Custodian-influenced hierarchy — a continuation of the Brotherhood's symbol-coded, oral, initiatory tradition.
