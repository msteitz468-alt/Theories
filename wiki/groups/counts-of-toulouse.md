## Overview from Source

The source identifies the counts of Toulouse as one of the two dominant ruling families of the pre-Crusade Languedoc, alongside the House of Trencavel. The authors write that at the beginning of the thirteenth century the Languedoc "was ruled by a handful of noble families, chief of whom were the counts of Toulouse and the powerful house of Trencavel."

## Relationship to the Cathars and the Crusade

The source states that before the Albigensian Crusade, the counts of Toulouse — along with the Trencavel — "had been extremely friendly to the region's large indigenous Jewish population." Once Pope Innocent III launched the Crusade in 1209, this protection was withdrawn by order.

The source notes that in 1218 Simon de Montfort "was killed besieging Toulouse," and that the depredation of the Languedoc continued for another quarter-century, with Perpignan, Narbonne, Carcassonne, and Toulouse all eventually falling to the northern invaders.

## Raymond, Count of Toulouse, and Jerusalem

The source briefly mentions a "Raymond, Count of Toulouse" in connection with the aftermath of the First Crusade. When Jerusalem fell in 1099, Raymond reportedly had "a persuasive claim" to the throne of Jerusalem, but the mysterious conclave that elected Godfroi de Bouillon "promptly offered the throne to Godfroi" instead.

## Textual Appearances

The counts of Toulouse appear primarily in the source's discussion of Cathar society, the Albigensian Crusade (Chapter 2), and the fall of Jerusalem.
