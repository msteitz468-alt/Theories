## Identity and Nomenclature (from the source)

Per Quigley, the Bank for International Settlements (Basle, Switzerland) was intended to be "the apex" of "a world system of financial control in private hands" — "a private bank owned and controlled by the world's central banks which were themselves private corporations." Quigley presents it as the intended capstone of a system by which the central banks of the world would act "in concert by secret agreements arrived at in frequent private meetings and conferences."

## Primary Textual Appearances

- The famous "financial capitalism" passage (quoted by later authors including Griffin and Mullins).
- Quigley situates the BIS within the broader Rhodes-Milner / investment-banker network as the institutional mechanism for coordinated central-bank control.

## Source-Specific Portrait

Quigley — writing as an informed insider — describes the BIS not with alarm but as the logical apex of financial-capitalist coordination.

## Source-Specific Portrait — Talbot, *The Devil's Chessboard* (2015)

Talbot gives a darker wartime portrait: by 1940 the BIS was "effectively controlled by Hitler's regime" and laundered hundreds of millions in looted Nazi gold (some torn from concentration-camp victims). Its president **Thomas McKittrick** was a Wall Street friend of the Dulles brothers; five directors were later charged with war crimes (including IG Farben CEO Hermann Schmitz). Allen Dulles met McKittrick on arriving in Europe and collaborated with him against Roosevelt's **Project Safehaven** (which sought to confiscate hidden Nazi assets). New Dealers Henry Morgenthau and Harry Dexter White sought to abolish the BIS at the 1944 Bretton Woods Conference, but a Wall Street/State Department/Bank of England campaign blocked them.
