## Identity and Nomenclature (from the source)

Mullins presents the American Acceptance Council as the organization through which [[Paul Warburg]], after resigning from the Federal Reserve Board in 1918, continued to dominate Federal Reserve policy as Chairman of its Executive Committee, alongside his presidency of the Federal Advisory Council. The source ties it to the introduction of the acceptance system of credit into the United States — which Mullins calls a major and under-documented change in American commerce (the changeover "from the open-book system of credit to the acceptance system").

## Primary Textual Appearances

- Source's bibliography/Preface notes that the only material on the acceptance changeover came from American Acceptance Council pamphlets (1915–1928).
- The source states the acceptance business was "the private preserve of Paul Warburg," carried on by the Reserve Banks outside the control of the Open Market Committee and even the Federal Reserve Board (quoting Goldenweiser).
- William McChesney Martin's father is named as a close associate of Warburg in the Council.

## Source-Specific Portrait

Presented as the institutional mechanism by which Warburg monetized his role and retained control over a key, opaque part of Federal Reserve operations.
