## Identity and Nomenclature

The "Legacy Program" is Elizondo's term for the classified network of US defense and aerospace contractors that he asserts has held recovered nonhuman technology and biological materials since at least the late 1940s. The program reportedly migrated from direct government control into contractor Special Access Programs specifically to evade Congressional oversight, Inspector General review, and FOIA requests.

The named contractors (Chapters 11 and 13): Lockheed Martin, TRW, McDonnell Douglas, Northrop Grumman, Boeing, Raytheon, BAE Systems, Aerospace Corporation, and Monsanto (biological materials). TRW operated a crash-retrieval program at Wright-Patterson AFB.

## Source-Specific Portrait

Elizondo's portrayal draws on multiple sources: his own AATIP investigations, the Wilson/Davis memo (a 2002 meeting documented by Eric Davis in which Admiral Thomas Wilson described being denied access to a contractor-held UAP SAP), statements by an unnamed Legacy contractor who acknowledged to AATIP analysts that they held UAP materials, and David Grusch's 2023 Congressional testimony that the UAPTF was refused access to a crash retrieval program.

The Legacy Program is the central institutional obstacle in the disclosure narrative: it holds the physical evidence that would confirm UAP reality, but shields itself behind contractor status and Special Access Program classification beyond normal government oversight.
