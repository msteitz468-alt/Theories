## Identity and Nomenclature (from the source)

Mullins characterizes the Federal Reserve System as a central bank deliberately misnamed to avoid that term. Per the source, Paul Warburg coined "Federal Reserve System" to "allay suspicion in the popular mind that the bill was a central bank plan," while it would "still function as a central bank." The source insists the System is privately owned: its stock is held by member banks who draw profit and control the issue of money, and the Board of Governors' salaries are paid by the privately owned member banks.

## Primary Textual Appearances

The institution is the subject of the whole book. Key structural claims the source makes:
- Twelve regional Reserve Banks were a facade; the Federal Reserve Bank of New York is "the fountainhead," the other eleven "expensive mausoleums erected to salve local pride" (quoting Ferdinand Lundberg).
- Removal from Congressional control rendered it unconstitutional (Article 1, Section 8 vests coinage in Congress).
- Assets grew from $143 million (1914) to $45 billion (1949), accruing to private stockholders.
- The Banking Act of 1935 extended Governors' terms to fourteen years.
- The System "creates money" by purchasing government securities ("We created it ... out of the right to issue credit money" — Eccles).

## Source-Specific Portrait

Mullins portrays the System as controlled in its first fifteen years by [[Paul Warburg]] and [[Benjamin Strong]], later dominated by the Treasury (Mellon) and by international gold interests, and consistently operated against the interests of farmers, workers, and consumers. The source ties it to the Bank of England (Montagu Norman), the League of Nations, and later the [[Bretton Woods institutions were created to extinguish war credit and form an international government|Bretton Woods institutions]].
