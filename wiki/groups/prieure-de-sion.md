**Identity and Nomenclature**

Founded as the "Ordre de Sion" by Godfroi de Bouillon c.1090–1099, according to the "Prieuré documents" (Dossiers secrets deposited in the Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris, primarily in the 1960s). Originally housed in the Abbey of Notre Dame du Mont de Sion, Jerusalem. In 1188, following the fall of Jerusalem and the "Cutting of the Elm" at Gisors, the organization renamed itself the "Prieuré de Sion," selected its first independent Grand Master (Jean de Gisors), adopted the subtitle "Ormus" (used until 1306), and the subtitle "Ordre de la Rose-Croix Véritas." In 1956, a Prieuré de Sion was formally registered in France under the Law of Associations, with Pierre Plantard de Saint-Clair listed as secretary-general.

The Grand Master is given the official title "Nautonnier" (navigator/helmsman).

**Primary Textual Appearances**

The source's principal primary-level evidence for the Prieuré comes from:
1. The Dossiers secrets (Bibliothèque Nationale, Paris) — genealogies, lists of Grand Masters, internal documents
2. Pope Alexander III's Bull of 1178 confirming the Ordre de Sion's possessions
3. Louis VII of France's charters (still extant in Orleans municipal archives) installing the Order at Saint-Samson at Orléans in 1152
4. Inquisition records (1308) mentioning Guillaume de Gisors in connection with Templar property after the 1307 arrests
5. Robert Denyau's 1629 manuscript stating the Rose-Croix was founded by Jean de Gisors in 1188
6. Various charters bearing signatures of Sion priors (e.g. Prior Arnaldus, July 19, 1116)

**Source-Specific Portraits**

The source presents the Prieuré as an organization that: (1) may have orchestrated the First Crusade through Godfroi de Bouillon; (2) created and managed the Knights Templar as its armed and administrative wing; (3) formally separated from the Templars in 1188; (4) survived the Templars' suppression in 1307; (5) worked through successive Grand Masters (including, allegedly, Leonardo da Vinci, Isaac Newton, Victor Hugo, Claude Debussy, and Jean Cocteau) to preserve an esoteric tradition and the Merovingian bloodline; (6) has as its political goal the installation of a Merovingian dynasty (descended from Jesus) over a theocratic United States of Europe.

The source notes two "essentially distinct groups" among the alleged Grand Masters: (a) figures of monumental cultural/scientific stature and (b) members of an interlinked network of noble families, especially associated with the house of Lorraine. The Prieuré thus appears to function both as a repository of esoteric knowledge and as a dynastic caretaker.
