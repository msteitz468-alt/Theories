## Overview from Source

The source treats the house of Habsburg-Lorraine as a dynasty of paramount significance to the book's central thesis — namely that the Merovingian bloodline has persisted into the modern era through dynastic intermarriage. The authors write: "By virtue of its intermarriage with the Habsburgs, the house of Lorraine had actually acquired the throne of Austria, the Holy Roman Empire." When Marie Antoinette, daughter of François de Lorraine, became queen of France, "the throne of France, too, was only a generation or so away."

According to the source's account of the "Prieure documents," the Habsburg-Lorraine family is listed among those dynasties whose lineage is entwined with the Merovingian bloodline: "various other noble and royal families, past and present — Blanchefort, Gisors, Saint-Clair (Sinclair in England), Montesquieu, Montpezat, Poher, Luisignan, Plantard and Habsburg-Lorraine."

## The House of Lorraine and Merovingian Descent

The source argues that Godfroi de Bouillon, Duke of Lorraine, was a lineal descendant of the Merovingian dynasty, specifically of Dagobert II's son Sigisbert IV. The Lorraine bloodline — and by extension the Habsburg-Lorraine house — is therefore presented by the source as heir to this putative sacred lineage.

## Grand Masters from the Lorraine Line

According to the list of alleged Prieuré de Sion Grand Masters reproduced in the source (from the "Dossiers secrets"), two members of the Lorraine family held the Grand Mastership:

- **Charles de Lorraine** (1746–1780)
- **Maximilian de Lorraine** (1780–1801)

The source provides detailed biographical profiles of both in its appendix on the alleged Grand Masters.

### Charles de Lorraine

The source describes Charles de Lorraine (1712–1780) as a Marshal of the Austrian Empire, Governor General of the Austrian Netherlands, and Grand Master of the Teutonic Order. He was a patron of the arts and sciences, created an orangery and zoological garden, and assembled a magnificent library. An equestrian statue erected in his honour in Brussels was officially unveiled on January 17th — a date the authors note recurs repeatedly in the story (the date on Marie de Blanchefort's tombstone, Nicolas Flamel's first alchemical transmutation, and Saunière's fatal stroke).

### Maximilian de Lorraine

Maximilian de Lorraine (1756–1801) was Charles's favourite nephew and Maria Theresa's youngest son. After a riding injury ended his military career, he became bishop of Münster and archbishop and imperial elector of Cologne. On Charles's death in 1780 he also became Grand Master of the Teutonic Order. He was a patron of Haydn, Mozart, and the young Beethoven. The source notes that "although Maximilian declared that he was not a Freemason, this statement has often been questioned," and that he openly consorted with members of Masonic circles, including Mozart.

## The French Revolution as a Setback

The source argues that the French Revolution was "a devastating blow to Merovingian hopes and aspirations." Had the Revolution not intervened, the house of Habsburg-Lorraine — positioned through dynastic marriage on both the Austrian and French thrones — "might well, by the early 1800s, have been on its way to establishing dominion over all Europe."

## Connection to Rennes-le-Château

The source records that among the notable visitors to Saunière at Rennes-le-Château was "the Archduke Johann von Habsburg, a cousin of Franz-Josef, Emperor of Austria." Bank statements, the authors state, "subsequently revealed that Saunière and the archduke had opened consecutive accounts on the same day, and that the latter had made a substantial sum over to the former." The archduke is later identified in a footnote as Johann Salvator von Habsburg, who renounced all his rights and titles in 1889.
