## Overview from Source

The Urantia movement is built around *The Book of Urantia*, a 2,097-page revealed text
"mysteriously received in Chicago" and first printed in 1955 by the Urantia Foundation
("Urantia" is the cosmic name of Earth). Vallée examines it in Chapter 7 as a key example of how
a channeled cosmology becomes a religion, and because it makes the **Melchizedeks** a roaming
inter-planetary order — tying it to the broader [[Order of Melchizedek (contactee-occult network)]]
thread. He attended a Urantian study meeting in San Francisco.

## Description from Source

The book locates Earth as "the six hundred sixth inhabited world in the local system of Satania…
constellation of Norlatiadek… local universe of Nebadon," and describes "seraphic transports"
departing a base on Jerusem every three seconds — which Vallée asks, half-seriously, "Are these
our UFOs?" It teaches "Thought Adjusters" that live within human brains and "Trinity Teacher
Sons." Vallée notes its readable sections on religious history contrast sharply with its
"childish" cosmology.

## The Sadler testimony from Source

Vallée's main interest is **Dr. William S. Sadler**, the Chicago physician and surgeon who knew
the Urantia "channel" and studied him. Sadler — author of *The Physiology of Faith and Fear*
(1920) and *The Mind at Mischief* (1929), normally a debunker of mediumship and automatic
writing — confessed in an appendix that this one case (~250 night sessions over 18 years, from
1911) defied his explanations: a sleeping man, "utterly unconscious," produced consistent
communications "made by a vast order of alleged beings who claim to come from other planets,"
which "psychoanalysis, hypnotism, intensive comparison, fail to show… have origin in his own
mind." Sadler admitted "I find myself at the present time just where I was when I started." A
French branch of Urantia is among the largest, Vallée notes, contradicting the claim that such
cults are an "American aberration."
