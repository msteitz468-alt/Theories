## Identity and Nomenclature

Monroe describes the laboratory's name history in Chapter 2: "Originally named Whistlefield Research Laboratories, this was later changed to the Monroe Institute of Applied Sciences. Using the name Monroe was not an ego factor, but simply the quickest way to clear the title officially. The 'Applied Sciences' part was quite specific." The name Monroe Institute of Applied Sciences was formally established in 1973, with facilities at Afton, Virginia (the book's appendix biographical note also references Faber, Virginia, as the Institute's location by 1985). Gateway and Explorer research was conducted at laboratories west of Charlottesville, Virginia.

The Institute's formal statement of purpose: "(1) consciousness and the focusing thereof contain any and all solutions to the life processes that man desires or encounters; (2) greater understanding and appreciation of such consciousness can be achieved only through interdisciplinary approaches and coordination; (3) the results of related research effort are meaningful only if reduced to practical application."

## Primary Textual Appearances

The Institute appears across multiple chapters:

**Chapter 2**: Physical description of the laboratory — a one-story building with offices, lounge, research wing, control room, three isolation booths (each with a heated water bed, environmental control, acoustic/electric shielding, and physiological monitoring capacity), and a briefing room. Local volunteer staff included several M.D.s, a physicist, an electronic engineer, and psychiatric and social service workers.

**Chapter 3**: Site of the Gateway Program, later expanded to residential programs at the Faber, Virginia location using CHEC Units (an acoustically shielded 8-by-10 room with water bed, air conditioning, physiological monitoring electrodes, headphones, and a microphone).

**Chapters 4–8**: The control room and isolation booths (particularly "booth 2, everyone's favorite for some unknown reason") are the operational center for all Explorer Team research. The monitor communicates with the subject through a sound system while observing physiological readouts including eight-channel EEG, EMG, pulse rate, and body voltage.

**Appendices**: The Institute is acknowledged as partial funder of the Twemlow, Gabbard, Jones psychiatric OBE research.

## Source-Specific Portrait

Monroe describes the Institute's research base as primarily a volunteer operation conducted nights and weekends while all participants maintained other employment. He expresses gratitude for "the immense contribution that this group gave freely." The Institute received visits from academics, psychiatrists, psychologists, and government personnel; Monroe describes multiple government contacts and a visit to the Federal Rehabilitation Facility at Hot Springs, Arkansas, that did not produce follow-through on a proposal to train personnel in Hemi-Sync.

The Institute hosted at least one spontaneously produced phenomenon: a skeptical visiting psychologist placed in booth 2 immediately perceived ROMC's four non-physical helpers (who had arrived at their customary Wednesday 5 PM appointment time even though ROMC had cancelled her session).

## Early history (Journeys Out of the Body, 1971/1977)

In the 1977 foreword to *Journeys Out of the Body*, Monroe describes the Institute (then the **Monroe Institute of Applied Sciences**, based at [[Afton, Virginia]]) as a research facility "formed and became active in 1972." It attracted a board of advisers drawn from physicists, psychologists, biochemists, engineers, educators, psychiatrists, corporate presidents, and statisticians. A founding **Explorer Team of six** members generated data "faster than we can process it," and Monroe stressed the methodological weight of "consensus and agreement from six different explorers—each unaware of the other's experiences." By 1977 over seven hundred persons had taken part in its research and training program, the Institute hoped its training might "qualify for credit at the college and university level," and Monroe noted the flood of relieved mail from people who could finally discuss OBEs "without the need for sanity hearings."

## History (per Russell, 2007)

Russell's biography traces the Institute's growth from Monroe's private "Whistlefield" research (Ch. 4) to the building of the residential center on **Roberts Mountain** near Faber, Virginia (Ch. 6), and the maturing of the **Gateway** and **Lifeline** residential programs. He credits a conversation with [[Charles Tart]] (about Andrew Neher's work on driving EEG with acoustic stimuli) as the likely seed of the Hemi-Sync idea, and notes the Institute survived Monroe's death in 1995 to continue his work.
