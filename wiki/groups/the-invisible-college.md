## Identity and Nomenclature (from the source)

Vallée's term (adopted with J. Allen Hynek) for the informal, international network of about 100 scientists in five or six countries who take the UFO phenomenon seriously and study it discreetly. The name deliberately echoes the 17th-century "Invisible College" of natural philosophers who met clandestinely before the Royal Society was chartered (1660s) — scientists then "suspected of being in league with the Devil."

## Primary Textual Appearances

- Introduction: Vallée's account of being drawn into the group via Hynek's invitation to apply computer science to Project Blue Book's statistics; the group "provided discreet support to groups of amateurs," safeguarded records, and used "every available piece of scientific equipment."

## Source-Specific Portrait

Presented as a serious-but-stigmatized research community challenging both the extraterrestrial-hypothesis believers and the debunkers, and willing to entertain "no theory… no matter how fantastic." Its members encouraged Vallée to publish the control-system thesis. Its discreet methods relate to Vallée's [[The Hilltop Theory holds that an investigator's data depends on his image among his audience]].
