## Overview from Source

According to the authors, at the beginning of the thirteenth century the Languedoc was an independent principality ruled by a handful of noble families, "chief of whom were the counts of Toulouse and the powerful house of Trencavel." The Languedoc's culture was, the authors assert, "the most advanced and sophisticated in Christendom, with the possible exception of Byzantium."

## Role in the Albigensian Crusade

The source states that before the Albigensian Crusade, many Languedoc nobles — "especially the influential houses of Trencavel and Toulouse — had been extremely friendly to the region's large indigenous Jewish population." This protection was forcibly withdrawn once the Crusade commenced.

The source notes in a footnote that "the preceptor of the Temple at Toulouse at the beginning of the Albigensian Crusade was of the Cathar Trencavel family," suggesting a Trencavel connection to the Knights Templar. In a separate footnote the source states that "the lord of Blanchefort had fought at the side of Raymond-Roger Trencavel, the Cathar leader," and that Bertrand de Blanchefort (fourth Grand Master of the Temple), "often in conjunction with the earlier Trencavel, was involved in donations of money and property to the Templars."

## Raymond-Roger Trencavel

The source identifies Raymond-Roger Trencavel as a specific Cathar leader who fought alongside the lord of Blanchefort during the Crusade. The index entry reads: "Trencavel, Raymond-Roger, 44–5."

## Textual Appearances

The Trencavel family appears primarily in Part One of the source, in the discussion of Cathar society and the Albigensian Crusade (Chapter 2 of the source). The family is cited again in footnotes discussing Templar connections to the Cathar world.
