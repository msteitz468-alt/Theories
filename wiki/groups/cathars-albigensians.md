**Identity and Nomenclature**

The Cathars were not a single coherent church but "a multitude of diverse sects." The source notes: "It is therefore virtually impossible to present a coherent and definitive summary of what actually constituted 'Cathar thought.'" The name "Cathar" appears to derive from a Greek root; "Albigensian" comes from Albi, where they were condemned in 1165. In Italy they were called Patarines. Their initiated leaders were called *parfaits* ("perfected ones") and were of both sexes.

**Source-Specific Portraits**

**Theology**: The Cathars were dualists who recognized two gods of roughly equal status: a "good" God of pure spirit (principle of love) and a "usurper God" — Rex Mundi ("King of the World") — who was the creator of the material world, which was therefore intrinsically evil. They believed in reincarnation, recognized the feminine principle in religion, and emphasized direct personal knowledge (*gnosis*) over faith accepted at second hand.

On Jesus: Cathar views varied. Some deemed Jesus "wholly incorporeal, a phantasm, an entity of pure spirit" who could not have been crucified. Others regarded him as "a prophet no different from any other — a mortal being who ... died on the cross." All Cathars "vehemently repudiated the significance of both the Crucifixion and the cross." The word AMOR (love) inverted becomes ROMA — the Cathars saw the Church of Rome as a palpable embodiment of Rex Mundi's sovereignty on earth.

**Practice**: Cathars condemned procreation (serving Rex Mundi) but not sexuality — birth control and abortion are suggested as their practical resolution. They were strict vegetarians (fish allowed), held services in the open air or any available building, practiced meditation, and when traveling always moved in pairs (giving ammunition to opponents' charge of sodomy). The Consolamentum — the Cathar "sacrament" — bound one to chastity but was usually administered only on the death-bed.

**Scale**: By 1200, there was "a very real prospect of this heresy displacing Roman Catholicism as the dominant form of Christianity in the Languedoc." About 30 per cent of all parfaits were drawn from Languedoc nobility. Even Saint Bernard, visiting in 1145, declared: "No sermons are more Christian than theirs, and their morals are pure."

**Fall of Montségur (1244)**: The last major Cathar fortress surrendered after a long siege. The source notes that four Cathars escaped the night before the mass burning, reportedly carrying with them the "Cathar Treasure" — believed by some to be the Holy Grail. Rennes-le-Château was an important Cathar town and suffered greatly in the Albigensian Crusade.
