## Identity and Nomenclature (from the source)

Per Quigley, the Royal Institute of International Affairs (Chatham House) was founded in 1919 by the Rhodes-Milner Round Table group, "for which the chief financial supporters were Sir Abe Bailey and the Astor family (owners of The Times)." Quigley states that "similar Institutes of International Affairs were established in the chief British dominions and in the United States (where it is known as the [[Council on Foreign Relations and the Round Table network|Council on Foreign Relations]]) in the period 1919–1927."

## Primary Textual Appearances

- Presented as the public-facing institutional successor to the secret Round Table Groups.
- Interlocked with the Institute of Pacific Relations (after 1925) and, in each country, with the local Round Table Group.
- Quigley notes the group "dominated The Times from 1890" and was sometimes called the "Cliveden Set" after the Astor country house.

## Source-Specific Portrait

Quigley treats the RIIA and CFR as the semi-public arms of the Rhodes-Milner network, through which it shaped imperial and foreign policy and opinion.
