## Nature of the Dispute

*[[The Case Against Reality (Why Evolution Hid the Truth from Our Eyes)]]* is built around a live scientific dispute over whether natural selection shapes perception to **report objective reality accurately** or to **track fitness payoffs** that hide reality. Donald Hoffman presents both sides in detail because his case depends on overturning a standing consensus.

## Positions Presented in the Source

### Veridical-perception consensus (the view Hoffman opposes)
- **Francis Crick** — metaphysical realist; the "Astonishing Hypothesis" assumes neurons-in-themselves exist and cause perception. In 1994 letters to Hoffman he held the thing-in-itself is a useful (if untestable) standard hypothesis "underlying all science."
- **David Marr** (Hoffman's late doctoral advisor) — in *Vision* (1982): "usually our perceptual processing does run correctly (it delivers a true description of what is there)," with larger brains moving "toward... representing progressively more objective aspects of the visual world."
- **Robert Trivers** — "our sensory systems are organized to give us a detailed and accurate view of reality."
- **Stephen Palmer** — "visual perception is useful only if it is reasonably accurate."
- **Zygmunt Pizlo** — "veridicality is an essential characteristic of perception and cognition... absolutely essential"; symmetry we see reveals real symmetry.
- Also Geisler & Diehl, Noë & O'Regan, Jack Loomis, and (with model-dependent realism) Hawking & Mlodinow.

### Interface theory (Hoffman's position)
Backed by the **[[Fitness-Beats-Truth (FBT) Theorem]]** and the **[[Invention of Symmetry Theorem]]** (both proved by [[Chetan Prakash]]): selection drives true perception to extinction; perception is a fitness-tracking interface. Allied quotations from **Steven Pinker** ("not to commune with correctness") and **Robert Trivers** himself (the "very naïve view of mental evolution" remark) are enlisted on this side. See [[Natural selection favors perception of fitness, not perception of truth]] and [[Perception is a species-specific interface, not a window on objective reality]].

### Critics engaged in the text
Hoffman quotes and answers objections from **Michael Shermer** (*Scientific American*: mimicry requires an objective reality to mimic; why frame it as either/or?), philosopher **Jonathan Cohen** (perception must have content before it can be judged veridical), and psychologist **Rainer Mausfeld** (natural selection may not be the major force in evolution).

## Resolution

Per the wiki's strict-extraction protocol, this page records the positions as the source presents them and does **not** adjudicate. The source is, of course, advocacy: Hoffman argues his side prevails and that each objection fails, but he presents the opposing consensus as genuine, widely held, and not yet abandoned by the field.
