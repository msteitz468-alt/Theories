**Nature of the Dispute**

The source explicitly addresses the question of whether the "Prieuré documents" are authentic historical records or modern fabrications. The authors cannot definitively resolve this question but present evidence on both sides.

**Position 1 — Fabrication**

The Dossiers secrets were deposited in the Bibliothèque Nationale primarily in the 1960s, associated with Pierre Plantard de Saint-Clair. The Grand Masters list includes famous names (Leonardo, Newton, Hugo, Cocteau) that could easily be appropriated to lend prestige — a tactic employed by modern organizations like the California Rosicrucians. Some claims (e.g. the Prieuré as "Ordre de la Rose-Croix" in 1188, Ormus) cannot be externally confirmed. The documents' very existence and provenance are suspicious.

**Position 2 — Genuine Inside Information**

The source argues the documents demonstrate "astonishing accuracy" on verifiable points:
- The list of Templar Grand Masters is assessed as more accurate than any published version, suggesting access to records unavailable to historians
- Specific abbots, land transactions, and dates cited in the documents are confirmed by independent archival sources (Orleans municipal archives, Inquisition records, etc.)
- The inclusion of obscure nobles alongside famous names argues against fabrication for prestige purposes alone
- A 1629 manuscript by Robert Denyau, curé of Gisors, independently confirms that the Rose-Croix was founded by Jean de Gisors in 1188 — matching the Dossiers secrets claim
- The documents are too internally consistent and historically precise to have been casually invented

**The Source's Position**

The authors decline to adjudicate definitively: "We did not just dismiss them out of hand. We felt obliged to reserve judgment." They proceed to treat the documents as potentially reliable, confirming what can be confirmed and remaining agnostic on what cannot.
