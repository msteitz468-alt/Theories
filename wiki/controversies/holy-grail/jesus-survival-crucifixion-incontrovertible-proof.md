**Nature of the Dispute**

Following the 1972 BBC documentary on Rennes-le-Château, the authors received an unsolicited letter from a retired Anglican priest asserting — "with categorical certainty and authority" — that the "treasure" was not gold but "incontrovertible proof that the Crucifixion was a fraud and that Jesus was alive as late as A.D. 45."

**Position 1 — Incontrovertible Proof Exists**

The priest asserted the claim baldly, "with apparent indifference as to whether we believed him or not." When personally interviewed, he was reticent but added one detail: the claim had been divulged to him by Canon Alfred Leslie Liney (died 1940), who had worked in Paris, maintained contacts with the Catholic Modernist Movement (based at Saint-Sulpice), and in his youth had been acquainted with Émile Hoffet — the very person Saunière had met in Paris. This chain of connection — Saunière → Hoffet → Liney → anonymous priest — renders the claim, however absurd, not entirely dismissible.

**Position 2 — The Claim Is Absurd**

The authors' own response: "This claim sounded flagrantly absurd. What, even to a convinced atheist, could possibly comprise 'incontrovertible proof' that Jesus survived the Crucifixion? We were unable to imagine anything which could not be disbelieved or repudiated." They note the priest refused to expand on what form the "proof" might take.

**The Source's Position**

The source treats this as a data point rather than a conclusion. It is not central to the authors' own bloodline hypothesis — which posits a marriage and bloodline rather than Jesus's survival — but the claim is noted as another thread in the pattern of evidence suggesting the Rennes-le-Château secret is of religious rather than merely financial significance.
