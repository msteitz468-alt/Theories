**Event Overview**

In 1188 — one year after the fall of Jerusalem to Saladin and Gerard de Ridefort's alleged "treason" — an incident at Gisors, Normandy, involved the cutting of an ancient elm in the Champ Sacré (Sacred Field). Medieval chronicles confirm this event, though surviving accounts are contradictory, vague, and seem to conceal something. The "Prieuré documents" call this event "the cutting of the elm" and identify it as the moment of formal separation between the Ordre de Sion and the Order of the Temple. From this date, the renamed Prieuré de Sion selected its own independent Grand Masters.

**Participants and Their Roles**

According to medieval chronicles: Henry II of England and Philippe II of France met for negotiations under the elm, which afforded the only shade. On the third day tempers broke and the French attacked; Richard Coeur de Lion participated in defending the tree. Philippe subsequently cut it down with five squadrons of men armed with carpenters' tools.

The "Prieuré documents" add a layer not in the chronicles: that this event was the symbolic ritual marking the Prieuré de Sion's formal separation from the Knights Templar, who had been Sion's military and administrative arm since their founding. Jean de Gisors — lord of the fortress at Gisors — became the Prieuré's first independent Grand Master.
