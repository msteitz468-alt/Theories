**Event Overview**

In 1891, while undertaking a modest restoration of the village church (consecrated to the Magdalene), Saunière removed the altar-stone, which rested on two archaic Visigoth columns. One column was hollow and contained four parchments in sealed wooden tubes. Two were genealogies (1244 and 1644). Two were ostensibly pious Latin texts composed by his predecessor Abbé Antoine Bigou in the 1780s — but were in fact carriers of complex ciphers.

With the consent of the village mayor, Saunière took the parchments to his bishop, who immediately dispatched him to Paris to present them to the Abbé Bieil (Director General of the Seminary of Saint-Sulpice) and Émile Hoffet. Saunière spent three weeks in Paris, was warmly received into Hoffet's esoteric circle, and purchased reproductions of three paintings from the Louvre: a portrait of Pope Celestine V, a David Teniers work, and Poussin's "Les Bergers d'Arcadie." On his return, he continued his excavations and obliterated the inscribed tombstone of the Marquise de Blanchefort — a stone that itself contained an encoded version of the parchment message.
