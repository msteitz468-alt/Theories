**Event Overview**

In 1209, Pope Innocent III — following the murder of his legate Pierre de Castelnau (blamed on the Cathars though committed by anti-clerical rebels) — ordered a Crusade against the Cathar heresy in the Languedoc. A crusading army of approximately 30,000 knights and foot-soldiers descended on the Languedoc, launching a war that lasted nearly forty years.

The pretext was heresy extirpation, but the source argues the crusade was also driven by northern European lords coveting the wealth of the Languedoc, and by the Roman Church's desire to reassert authority over a region that had largely rejected it.

**Participants and Their Roles**

- **Pope Innocent III**: Ordered the Crusade; appointed Arnaud-Amaury as legate
- **Arnaud-Amaury (Abbot of Cîteaux)**: Military commander and papal legate; allegedly replied "Kill them all. God will recognise His own" at Béziers (source notes this "may be apocryphal")
- **Cathars**: The heretic targets; non-violent in doctrine but deemed existential threat to Roman authority
- **Counts of Toulouse and house of Trencavel**: The regional nobility, many of whom sheltered Cathars and resisted the crusade

**Key Claims Arising from This Event**

- [[Claim: Albigensian Crusade (1209–1244) may constitute the first genocide in modern European history]]
- At Béziers: at least 15,000 killed wholesale; after the siege of Montségur, more than 200 *parfaits* were burned alive
- The crusade destroyed what the source calls "the most advanced and sophisticated culture in Christendom" at the time
- German troops during the 1940 occupation allegedly undertook excavations near Rennes-le-Château "following in Wagner's wake" — in search of the Cathar Treasure
