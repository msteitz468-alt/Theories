**Event Summary**

The Siege of Montségur (1243–1244) was the final major military action of the Albigensian Crusade. The fortress-village of Montségur in the Pyrenean foothills was the last significant Cathar stronghold and refuge for parfaits (Cathar perfecti). After a ten-month siege by French royal forces, the garrison surrendered in March 1244. More than 200 Cathar parfaits who refused to recant were burned alive at the base of the mountain.

In *The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail*, Montségur is treated as symbolically significant — the Cathars are portrayed as possible custodians of a secret treasure or knowledge, and four parfaits reportedly escaped the night before the surrender, allegedly carrying "the treasure of the Cathars." The nature of this treasure is left unspecified in the source.
