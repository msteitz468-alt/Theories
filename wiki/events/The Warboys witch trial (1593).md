## Summary
The trial and execution of Alice Samuel (Mother Samuel), her husband, and daughter for allegedly bewitching the children of Robert Throckmorton of Warboys, Huntingdonshire — one of the best-documented and most influential English witch cases discussed by Keith Thomas in [[Religion and the Decline of Magic (Thomas)]]. The case illustrates several of Thomas's recurring analytical points: the diagnosis of witchcraft originated with a physician, Dr Barrow of Cambridge, after his own remedies for the Throckmorton children's fits had failed; Lady Cromwell's presumptuous cutting of a lock of Mother Samuel's hair for counter-magic, and Mother Samuel's protest ("I never did you any harm as yet"), was treated as a damning admission; and the case shows the pattern in which bystanders initially resisted the accusation because they could think of no clear grudge Alice Samuel bore the family. The witches' forfeited goods (forty pounds) went to the lord of the manor, Sir Henry Cromwell, who used the proceeds to endow an annual sermon on witchcraft at Huntingdon — one of the very few English cases where Thomas finds even a trace of a financial motive behind a prosecution.
