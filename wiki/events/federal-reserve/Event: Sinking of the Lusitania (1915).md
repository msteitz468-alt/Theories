## Event Overview (from the source)

Griffin (Ch. 12, "Sink the Lusitania!") presents the 1915 sinking of the British liner *Lusitania* as a deliberately engineered provocation to bring the United States into World War I. Per the source, the *Lusitania* — owned by Cunard, operating outside J. P. Morgan's shipping cartel — "was built to military specifications and was registered with the British Admiralty as an armed auxiliary cruiser," carrying passengers "as a cover" while transporting "contraband war materials from the United States": "virtually a floating ammunition depot."

Griffin states this was "known to Wilson and others in his administration," and that when the German embassy tried to publish warnings to American passengers, "the State Department intervened and prevented newspapers from printing it" (except one ad in the *Des Moines Register*). As the ship entered waters where a German U-boat was known to operate, "First Lord of the Admiralty, Winston Churchill, ordered her destroyer protection to abandon her," and she had been ordered to reduce speed. After one torpedo, "a mighty second explosion from within ripped her apart," sinking her in under eighteen minutes with 195 Americans aboard.

## Participants and Their Roles

Per the source: Winston Churchill (withdrew the escort); J. P. Morgan & Co. (shipping cartel and war-bond/purchasing profits); Woodrow Wilson and Colonel House (aware; pursuing the secret agreement to enter the war); the German Embassy (suppressed warnings). Lord Mersey chaired the official inquiry, which Griffin calls "a coverup" blaming the captain; Mersey reportedly called it "a damn dirty business."

## Source-Specific Portraits

Griffin frames the sinking as an instance of the [[The Rothschild Formula]]: war engineered to protect Morgan's threatened war-bond cash flow and to enable Federal Reserve war finance. Within days of the US declaration, "Congress voted $1 billion in credit for England and France," with $200 million applied to the Morgan account.
