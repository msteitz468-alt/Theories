## Event Overview (from the sources)

Both Griffin and Mullins present the October 1929 crash as an engineered outcome of Federal Reserve policy rather than a market accident. Griffin (Chs. 20, 23) argues that during the nine years before the crash the Fed conducted "a massive expansion of the money supply," a primary motive being "to assist the government of Great Britain" — by devaluing the dollar and depressing U.S. interest rates so investment would flow to England (where [[Montagu Norman]] headed the Bank of England and worked secretly with [[Benjamin Strong]]).

Griffin asserts "circumstantial evidence" that the Bank of England and the Fed concluded at "a secret meeting in February of 1929" that a collapse was inevitable and "the best action was to let nature take its course," after which "advisory warnings" went to preferred customers to exit the market while the public was assured the economy was sound. On August 9, the Fed "applied the pin to the bubble" — raising the loan rate and selling securities — and on October 29 "the stock market collapsed," with forewarned insiders converting to cash beforehand and then buying.

## Participants and Their Roles

Benjamin Strong / the New York Fed and Montagu Norman / Bank of England (engineered the preceding expansion and the reversal); preferred insiders (warned in advance); the public (assured of soundness). See also Mullins's [[Event: Federal Reserve–European central bank conference (1927)]].
