## Event Overview (from the source)

Griffin (Ch. 5) presents the 1944 Bretton Woods conference of "global financiers and politicians" as the founding of the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank. Their "announced goals were to facilitate international trade and to stabilize the exchange rates of national currencies"; their "unannounced goals," per the source, "were the elimination of the gold-exchange standard ... and the establishment of world socialism."

Griffin names the conference's two guiding theoreticians as [[John Maynard Keynes]] (a Fabian Socialist) and [[Harry Dexter White]] (Assistant Secretary of the U.S. Treasury, later first U.S. Executive Director of the IMF, and — per the source — "a Member of a Communist espionage ring"). The source frames the IMF as evolving into "a central bank for the world" and the World Bank as "its lending arm," transferring wealth from industrialized to underdeveloped nations via loans that "never be paid back."

Mullins's account ([[Bretton Woods institutions were created to extinguish war credit and form an international government]]) treats the same institutions as devices to extinguish WWII credit and form international government.

## Participants and Their Roles

Keynes (Fabian theoretician); White (Communist-linked U.S. Treasury official, first IMF U.S. Director); the industrialized nations as capital contributors (the U.S. largest).
