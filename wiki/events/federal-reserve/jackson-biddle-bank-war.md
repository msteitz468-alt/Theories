## Event Overview (from the source)

Griffin (Ch. 17, "A Den of Vipers") recounts President Andrew Jackson's destruction of the Second Bank of the United States — the nation's third central bank — as a precedent for the dangers of central banking. Per the source, Bank head [[Nicholas Biddle]] sought early renewal of the charter in 1832, expecting Jackson would avoid controversy in an election year. Jackson vetoed the renewal (calling the Bank a "monster") and made it the central campaign issue; he was re-elected "by a large margin."

Jackson then removed federal deposits from the Bank and placed them in regional banks. Griffin states Biddle "counterattacked by contracting credit and calling in loans," deliberately triggering "a national panic-depression" to frighten voters, publicly blaming Jackson's deposit removal. Biddle is quoted: "All other banks and all the merchants may break, but the Bank of the United States shall not break." When his strategy "leaked out, it backfired"; the Senate censure of Jackson was rescinded and the Bank's charter expired in 1836.

## Participants and Their Roles

Andrew Jackson (vetoed renewal, removed deposits); Nicholas Biddle (engineered the credit contraction/panic). Jackson is quoted: "I am ready with the screws to draw every tooth and then the stumps."

## Source-Specific Portraits

Griffin presents the episode as proof that a central banker can deliberately create a depression for political leverage — a precedent for his reading of [[Benjamin Strong]] and the 1929 crash.
