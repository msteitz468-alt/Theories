## Event Overview (from the source)

Mullins (Chs. 4–6) recounts the final passage of the Federal Reserve Act. After the Aldrich Plan was discredited, the substantially identical bill was promoted by Woodrow Wilson and the Democratic Party as the Owen-Glass / Federal Reserve Act. William Jennings Bryan whipped Congress into line; the Senate restored Aldrich-Plan provisions struck in the House. The Act passed the Senate on December 22, 1913.

Per the source, Wilson at first refused to sign over the Class B Directors provision, until [[Bernard Baruch]] hurried to the White House to assure him it could be "fixed up later." Wilson signed on December 23, 1913. Mullins comments: "On that day, the Constitution ceased to be the governing covenant of the American people, and our liberties were handed over to a small group of international bankers." The source quotes Congressman Lindbergh on the floor that day: "This Act establishes the most gigantic trust on earth ... the invisible government by the Monetary Power will be legalized."

## Participants and Their Roles

Wilson (signer); Carter Glass (House sponsor); Paul Warburg (substance/author, via Colonel House); William Jennings Bryan (whip); Bernard Baruch (intervenor on the Class B provision). The source notes a contemporaneous *Nation* item: the New York stock market "began to rise steadily upon news that the Senate was ready to pass the Federal Reserve Act."

## Source-Specific Portraits

Mullins juxtaposes the public framing (a "people's banking bill" freeing the nation from Wall Street) against quoted admissions and the stock market's positive reaction, presenting the signing as the consummation of the Jekyll Island plan.
