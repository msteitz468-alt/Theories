## Event Overview (from the source)

Mullins (Ch. 3) describes the Pujo Committee — chaired by Congressman Arsene Pujo of Louisiana, with [[Samuel Untermyer]] as Special Counsel — convened in 1912 to investigate the control of money and credit (the "Money Trust"). The hearings ran five months and filled four volumes of some six thousand pages.

The source argues the hearings were a deliberate "farce": the eleven congressmen barely questioned witnesses; Senator LaFollette and Congressman Lindbergh, who had forced the investigation, were not invited to participate; and Untermyer's questioning let Jacob Schiff and J. P. Morgan avoid disclosing anything damaging. Mullins quotes J. P. Morgan's testimony ("Money is a commodity"; that he weighed a man's "character" above collateral) and notes Morgan died a few months later. The net effect, per the source, was to convince the public a money monopoly existed — which was then used to argue that the Federal Reserve Act was the cure.

## Participants and Their Roles

Untermyer (Special Counsel, conducting the questioning); Pujo (chair); the New York bankers as witnesses; LaFollette and Lindbergh (excluded instigators).

## Key Claims Arising from This Event

- [[The Pujo Committee Money Trust hearings were a controlled sham]]

## Source-Specific Portraits

Mullins frames the hearings as theatre that softened public opinion to accept the Federal Reserve Act, with Untermyer — a Wilson campaign donor and co-author of the administration bill — protecting his own class.
