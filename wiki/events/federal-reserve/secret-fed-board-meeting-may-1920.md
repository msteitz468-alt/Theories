## Event Overview (from the source)

Mullins (Ch. 9) describes a secret all-day meeting of the Federal Reserve Board on May 18, 1920, whose minutes (he says) ran sixty printed pages and appear in Senate Document 310 (Feb. 19, 1923). Per the source, the Class A Directors and the Federal Reserve Advisory Council attended, but the Class B Directors (business, commerce, agriculture) and Class C Directors (the public) were not present or invited.

The source, quoting Senator Robert L. Owen, states the meeting produced a contraction of credit and a raise of the discount rate to 7% on agricultural and livestock paper, which "had the effect the next year of reducing the national income fifteen billion dollars, throwing millions of people out of employment, and reducing the value of lands and ranches by twenty billion dollars" — the Agricultural Depression of 1920–21. Mullins argues the purpose was to break the rural/state banks that had refused to join the System and to recover the money paid to farmers and workers during WWI.

## Participants and Their Roles

The Board, the Federal Advisory Council (Warburg as its president, per the source), and Carter Glass (then Treasury Secretary and ex-officio member). Senator Brookhart's testimony is quoted alleging Warburg pushed a resolution to seek railroad-rate increases at the same meeting.

## Source-Specific Portraits

Mullins presents the meeting as documentary proof (via the 1923 Senate hearings and *Manufacturers' Record*) that the Board deliberately engineered a depression targeting farmers while protecting manufacturers and merchants with increased credit.
