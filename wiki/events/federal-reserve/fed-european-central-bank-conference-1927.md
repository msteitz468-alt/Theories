## Event Overview (from the source)

Mullins (Ch. 11) reconstructs, from the House Stabilization Hearings of 1928, a 1927 meeting at which the heads of three European central banks — Montagu Norman (Bank of England), Hjalmar Schacht (Reichsbank), and Charles Rist (Bank of France) — conferred with officials of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York and then with the Federal Reserve Board in Washington (a luncheon hosted by the Governors). Governor Adolph Miller's testimony, quoted at length, acknowledges the visitors "were all pretty concerned with the way the gold standard was working" and "desirous of seeing an easy money market in New York and lower rates, which would deter gold from moving from Europe to this country."

Per the source, the Board then lowered the discount rate and conducted open-market purchases (~$80 million in August), easing money and forcing roughly $500 million in gold out to Europe. Mullins argues this credit expansion fueled the 1927–29 stock boom, and that the subsequent reversal to high rates (after Norman's February 1929 visit to Mellon) precipitated the Crash and the Great Depression. Miller is quoted agreeing the action "stabilized the European currencies, and upset ours" and that "Yes, that was what it was intended to do."

## Participants and Their Roles

Benjamin Strong and the New York Fed; the Board of Governors; Montagu Norman, Hjalmar Schacht, Charles Rist (European central banks); the Open-Market Policy Committee.

## Source-Specific Portraits

Mullins treats Governor Miller's congressional testimony as an admission that Federal Reserve policy was set in the interest of European gold needs and the League of Nations' gold-standard project, at the deliberate expense of US monetary stability.
