## Event Overview (from the source)

Mullins opens the book with this meeting. On the night of November 22, 1910, a group of highly placed bankers and Senator [[Nelson Aldrich]] boarded a sealed private railway car at Hoboken, New Jersey, with drawn shades, and travelled roughly a thousand miles to the Jekyl Island Hunt Club in Georgia — an exclusive club owned by J. P. Morgan and a small group of New York bankers. The club's regular staff were given two weeks off and new servants brought in; the men used only first names ("Henry, Ben, and Paul") to conceal their identities, later formalizing this as the "First-Name Club."

Over about nine days the group drafted the central-bank legislation that the [[National Monetary Commission]] had been charged to prepare. Per the source, [[Paul Warburg]] did most of the drafting; Aldrich ensured it could pass Congress; the others advised on banking problems. The plan was first presented to Congress as the "Aldrich Plan" and later, in substantially the same form, passed as the Federal Reserve Act of 1913.

## Participants and Their Roles

Per the source (corroborated by Mullins's quotation of J. Laurence Laughlin): Senator Aldrich; Henry P. Davison and Charles D. Norton (J. P. Morgan / First National Bank); Paul Warburg and (in Mullins's narration) Benjamin Strong of the Kuhn, Loeb / Morgan orbit; Frank Vanderlip of the National City Bank; and A. Piatt Andrew, Assistant Secretary of the Treasury.

Mullins treats participants' own later accounts as confirmation: Warburg's memorandum ("The matter of a uniform discount rate was discussed and settled at Jekyl Island"), Vanderlip's *Saturday Evening Post* article and autobiography, and Laughlin's history all naming the attendees and the two-week secret meeting.
